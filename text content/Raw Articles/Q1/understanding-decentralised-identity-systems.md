# Understanding decentralised identity systems

*By Jonny Fry | *

The aim of [decentralised identity systems](https://www.techtarget.com/whatis/definition/decentralized-identity) is to transform digital identity (DID) creation, storage and sharing. DIDs use distributed networks instead of central authorities, taking data from social media platforms, email providers and government databases. Blockchain technology and cryptographic keys enable self-sovereign identification so allowing the user of a DID to verify themselves without disclosing sensitive data. DIDs are based on self-sovereignty where users decide what information to reveal and with whom; DIDs reduce data breaches by decentralising data storage and employing distributed ledgers. These solutions also prioritise privacy by design, sharing sensitive data only with user agreement to comply with [GDPR](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/g/general-data-protection-regulation-gdpr.asp).

A decentralised identification system operates using key components to ensure secure and private identity verification. Furthermore, by leveraging blockchain technology, this system eliminates the need for centralised ledger whereby allowing users to authenticate their identities without exposing personal information. Users receive cryptographically signed credentials (including qualifications and professional certificates) from trusted sources. These credentials are stored in identity wallets similar to cryptocurrency wallets which securely manage and exchange identity data when needed. By leveraging blockchain technology, this system eliminates the need for centralised authorities so ensuring that DIDs and credentials remain tamper-proof, verifiable and privacy-preserving. Traditional password-based systems are vulnerable to assaults due to their centralised storage and also require that the users both remember them and, ideally, do not have one password for multiple companies, websites, etc. DIDs decrease [phishing](https://www.cloudflare.com/en-gb/learning/access-management/phishing-attack/), [brute force attacks](https://www.fortinet.com/uk/resources/cyberglossary/brute-force-attack#:~:text=A%20brute%20force%20attack%20is,and%20organizations'%20systems%20and%20networks.) and credential breaches by eliminating passwords and by using cryptographic authentication. Therefore, by sharing only the minimum authentication information, DIDs protect privacy and security - users can give a credential proving they are over a specific age instead of their birthdate. For example, [Yoti uses AI technology to verify age](https://www.yoti.com/business/digital-id/) through facial analysis, enabling confirmation of being over a certain age without revealing a date of birth; this degree of data minimisation prevents identity theft and protects personal information. Moreover, decentralised identity systems eliminate third-party middlemen and allow users complete control over their digital identities - users may manage their credentials without external systems by way of this change from centralised to user-controlled identity management. Credentials such as academic credentials and career experience verified by reliable institutions facilitate a decrease in fraud using DIDs. According to [Top Univesrities.com](https://www.topuniversities.com/blog/lying-your-cv-facts#:~:text=According%20to%20a%20UK%20Higher,make%20up%20a%20degree%20altogether.): “ _UK Higher Education Degree Datacheck claim graduate data fraud, around 33% of graduates or job seekers falsify important information on their CVs every year. Amongst the culprits, 40% exaggerate their academic qualifications, while 11% make up a degree altogether.”_ Essentially,__ these DID credentials cannot be changed using cryptographic signatures, so making identity fraud harder. DIDs also provide seamless authentication across different platforms without the need for distinct accounts and passwords for each service.

Blockchain provides a decentralised, tamper-proof framework for DID storage and credential verification with people utilising Ethereum, Hyperledger Indy and Sovrin to promote decentralised identification systems. In addition, public-key cryptography protects identification credentials throughout generation, storage and distribution, so giving users privacy and control. The [World Wide Web consortium ](https://accessibe.com/glossary/world-wide-web-consortium#:~:text=The%20World%20Wide%20Web%20Consortium,standards%20websites%20should%20adhere%20to.)(WC3) also provides standards for decentralised IDs and verified credentials, assuring platform and system compatibility. The potential applications of decentralised identity systems are many and span multiple industries - in finance, for example, DIDs can streamline [know your customer (KYC)](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/k/knowyourclient.asp) processes, allowing financial institutions to verify user identities without storing sensitive personal data. This reduces compliance costs, enhances security and improves the overall user experience. Meanwhile, in [healthcare](https://www.healthdatamanagement.com/articles/why-theres-potential-to-use-decentralizing-identity-in-healthcare), decentralised identity systems can give patients more control over their medical records; patients can share their health information with providers securely, ensuring that sensitive data remains private. This also enhances interoperability between different healthcare systems, making it easier for healthcare professionals to access important patient information without compromising privacy. Governments are also exploring decentralised identity for a range of applications, including digital IDs, voting systems and public service access. [Estonia’s e-Residency](https://www.xolo.io/zz-en/e-residency) program is a notable example, utilising blockchain-based digital identities to offer secure, cross-border services to individuals around the world. In education, decentralised identities allow universities and training institutions to issue verifiable credentials for diplomas, certifications and transcripts. These credentials are tamper-proof and can be easily shared with employers or other institutions, reducing the risk of fraud and simplifying credential verification. For example, universities in the US, such as [Stanford and MIT, are already issuing diplomas as verifiable credentials](https://www.technologyreview.com/2018/04/25/143311/digital-diplomas/). In the workplace, DIDs simplify employee onboarding by enabling workers to present verified credentials such as employment history or certifications, without the need for lengthy manual checks. This not only speeds up the process but also improves the accuracy of the information provided. In the world of airline travel, it is estimated that the number of flight passengers is set to rise from [4billion to over 40billion by 2024. Delta Airlines and the Government of Aruba](https://indicio.tech/how-decentralized-digital-identity-is-creating-simpler-more-streamlined-air-travel/) have developed an identity verification solution to enable travellers to obtain pre-authorisation before departing, and then go through border controls simply by looking at a camera - could this herald the end of long queues and inconvenience at passport control?

However,**** decentralised identification systems face various obstacles despite their potential, such as:

> · **scalability** \- to promote DID adoption, blockchain networks must effectively process high transaction volumes. Without scaling, these systems may struggle to meet global demand.
> 
> · **lack of uniform standards** \- interoperability across solutions may be constrained without established protocols in the emerging decentralised identity ecosystem. DIDs must function across platforms and services to be widely adopted.
> 
> · **regulatory compliance** \- maintaining privacy while complying with AML regulations is difficult. Decentralised identification systems must comply with regulations and safeguard user privacy.
> 
> · **user adoption matters** \- DIDs succeed when users understand the benefits and have easy-to-use tools to manage their digital identities. Users may be hesitant to transition from traditional identity management without sufficient education and simple apps.
> 
> · **decentralised systems** \- these are safe; however, identity wallets and credential implementation might be vulnerable. Maintaining system confidence requires strong security and risk reduction.

Essentially, future digital ecosystems will need decentralised identity methods; DIDs provide safe and seamless authentication for dApps, making them important in Web3 development. Multi-industry groups are developing decentralised identification standards whereby enhancing collaboration, and the [Decentralised Identity Foundation (DIF)](https://identity.foundation/) is promoting the technology with stakeholders. Zero-knowledge proofs (ZKPs) are being added to DIDs to improve privacy so that, with these technologies, users may authenticate identify without providing personal information whereby boosting privacy and security. Meanwhile, governments worldwide are exploring decentralised identity technologies for e-governance, public service delivery and secure digital IDs. Therefore, as use grows, these technologies may improve digital efficiency, security and usability across sectors. Additionally, decentralised identity systems are reshaping how we verify identity, so shifting control from institutions to individuals. And by leveraging blockchain and cryptographic security, these systems eliminate centralised gatekeepers, as a result promising fraud-resistant, tamper-proof credentials - eliminating the need for passwords, then identity theft and phishing could become obsolete, creating a future of seamless, cross-border authentication. But, unsurprisingly, with this promise comes uncertainty. Without clear governance, decentralised identity could become fragmented, limiting interoperability, and regulatory concerns loom as governments weigh privacy against oversight, raising the question of whether DIDs will truly liberate users or simply offer a new avenue for surveillance. Furthermore, security remains a critical challenge - whilst the removal of intermediaries enhances privacy, it also introduces risks if identity wallets are compromised.

Ultimately, as Web3 evolves, decentralised identity could become the foundation of a more secure and efficient digital world, or it could introduce unforeseen vulnerabilities. The core question remains: are we decentralising identity for greater freedom, or are we decentralising risk without adequate safeguards?
