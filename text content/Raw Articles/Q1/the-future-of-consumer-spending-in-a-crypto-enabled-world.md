# The future of consumer spending in a crypto-enabled world

*By Jonny Fry | *

Traditional financial companies (TradFi) and crypto companies are integrating digital assets to meet customer demand for faster, more flexible financial services - an example being Visa recently [partnering with Coinbase](https://cryptopotato.com/visa-partners-with-coinbase-to-enable-real-time-cash-outs/) to enable real-time cashouts. Earlier in 2024, [Visa had also partnered with Transak](https://www.kiplinger.com/personal-finance/credit-cards/visa-transak-crypto-partnership), intending to allow crypto-to-fiat conversions within thirty minutes for use at any Visa-accepting business, supporting both crypto and NFT markets. All the while, [Paysafe is expanding its crypto-to-cash reach in Latin America with Skrill and Neteller](https://fintechmagazine.com/articles/paysafe-the-value-of-partnerships-cryptos-growth-in-2024), which _“enables millions of users to convert cash into online spending.”_ Meanwhile, Bitpanda has [joined forces with Deutsche Bank](https://cointelegraph.com/news/bitpanda-deutsche-bank-payments) and even [SWIFT](https://www.ledgerinsights.com/japans-big-3-banks-to-use-stablecoins-swift-for-cross-border-payments/) (which is used by over 11,000 TradFi firms globally) has been working with three of Japan’s biggest banks that are creating stablecoins - and intend to use SWIFT’s infrastructure to make cross border payments. Partnerships such as that of Visa’s with Coinbase signal a broader trend toward “mainstreaming” crypto; a trend that shows consumer spending is poised for a transformation driven by speed, accessibility and inclusivity. And, as [traditional finance (TradFi) and crypto companies converge](https://thepaypers.com/expert-opinion/the-convergence-between-crypto-and-traditional-financial-markets--1266996), consumers gain seamless access to crypto-based financial services previously limited to fiat currencies. This shift is notably reshaping the pattern of how, where and why people spend. Real-time crypto cashouts are increasingly accessible through several mechanisms besides [crypto debit cards](https://www.paymentsjournal.com/consumers-can-spend-crypto-at-checkout-with-card-from-metamask-mastercard/), with these innovations aiming to both improve liquidity and bridge the gap between digital assets and fiat currencies whereby enhancing the usability of cryptocurrencies for everyday transactions. Some of these include:

> · **crypto ATMs** \- these allow users to convert cryptocurrencies into fiat currencies such as USD, EUR, or GBP. Users can deposit crypto into the ATM, which then dispenses cash. This is an easy way for crypto holders to access liquidity in real-time, especially in areas with limited traditional banking infrastructure. Companies such as Bitcoin Depot and Coinme are [expanding crypto ATM networks](https://www.paymentsdive.com/news/bitcoin-depot-cryptocurrency-atm-crypto-coo-scott-buchanan/707498/) globally, making it simpler for users to cash out their holdings instantly.
> 
> · **peer-to-peer platforms** \- LocalBitcoins, Paxful and Binance P2P allow users to sell their crypto directly to other individuals in exchange for fiat. These platforms often feature instant payment systems via bank transfers, PayPal or cash, providing a real-time cashout option without relying on centralised exchanges. P2P platforms enable users to bypass certain exchange fees and enjoy a more flexible, decentralised method of cashing out.
> 
> · **crypto payment processors** \- BitPay and CoinGate enable merchants to accept crypto payments and convert them into fiat currency instantly. This means merchants or individuals can convert crypto directly into local currencies (often within minutes) using these services. This model facilitates real-time transactions, improving liquidity for both businesses and customers.
> 
> · **centralised digital asset exchanges with instant withdrawals** \- Coinbase, Kraken and Gemini offer users the ability to cash out instantly through their integration with traditional payment systems (e.g. [ACH for US users](https://support.blockchain.com/hc/en-us/articles/4584838422300-How-to-deposit-USD-via-ACH) or [SEPA for EU users](https://blockchain.news/news/binance-zero-fee-euri-sepa-transfer)). These exchanges provide services that allow direct withdrawal of funds to a bank account, with transactions often processed in real time or within a few hours depending on the method.
> 
> · **stablecoins for instant conversion** \- USDC, Tether (USDT) and DAI allow users to convert their crypto into a digital currency pegged to a stable asset (usually the US dollar) which can then be easily converted back to fiat at any time via exchanges or wallet services. This enables quick and predictable conversions from volatile cryptocurrencies into stable digital assets, providing liquidity without the traditional delays of bank transfers or external withdrawal processes.

Innovations, such as listed above, indicate a new business model where consumers are shifting from saving crypto assets to using them for day-to-day transactions - this model signifies a departure from the early “hold and invest” mindset toward a “spend and circulate” approach. It also represents an era where crypto platforms and traditional financial companies work together, leveraging each other’s strengths, meaning the market reach expansion for these institutions in an ever-expanding field like crypto. But what does this mean for the industry?

The decentralised nature of digital assets, free from government and bank controls, offers significant advantages such as lower transaction fees and faster processing. These are particularly appealing in markets where banking systems are slow or expensive​. On the other hand, the emergence of digital assets in this context could also disrupt traditional banking services. Banks, which have long been intermediaries in financial transactions, might see a decline in the demand for certain services (such as cross-border payments or remittance services) which are now being served more efficiently by blockchain technologies​.

Furthermore, whilst necessary for trust, regulatory complexities such as AML and KYC standards could slow the widespread adoption of real-time cashout services - these impacts consumers by limiting access in regions with stricter compliance requirements. Also, banks are cautious about reputational risks associated with volatile digital assets. In response, many banks implement partnerships with strict terms, often requiring digital asset platforms to meet specific operational standards before collaboration. This conservatism stems from the high-risk profile of cryptocurrencies, which are subject to market volatility and, in some cases, misuse for illicit activities. Consequently, building trust through proven security practices and transparency is vital for crypto firms aiming to collaborate with established banks. Some banks are taking steps to integrate digital asset services, such as offering custody services or providing crypto trading through their platforms in order to remain competitive​. HSBC has [partnered with Ripple](https://finbold.com/ripple-launches-crypto-custody-storage-for-banks/) to offer custodial services whilst in the US, Anchorage Digital [provides institutional-grade crypto custody services and settlement solutions](https://www.anchorage.com/). Even Taiwan is not left out - starting in 2025, it is set to [put to trial](https://en.cryptonomist.ch/2024/10/09/taiwan-ready-to-experiment-with-crypto-custody-services-in-banks/) cryptocurrency custody services through its local banks as part of a regulatory initiative by the Financial Supervisory Commission.

In conclusion, the shift from “hold and invest” to “spend and circulate” highlights a fusion of traditional finance and crypto which is undoubtedly redefining consumer spending. Partnerships such Visa with Coinbase and Deutsche Bank with Bitpanda accentuates a move toward seamless crypto-to-fiat integration, so enabling real-time access and transactions. In turn, this evolution promises speed, lower costs and financial inclusion, especially in underserved regions. However, regulatory challenges, reputational risks and market volatility pose hurdles. The question is not whether TradFi and crypto can co-exist, but how their synergy can create a hybrid financial system that combines the stability of banks with the innovation of digital assets - this collaboration could transform finance whereby offering flexibility and accessibility for a new era of consumer spending. The future is not simply about one system replacing the other - it is about them evolving together to build a more inclusive financial ecosystem.
