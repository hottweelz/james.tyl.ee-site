# Wealth creation democratised using digital assets

*By Jonny Fry | *

Digital assets and blockchain revolutionise wealth generation dividing typically high value assets into smaller units/shares and so enable smaller investors to participate - i.e. democratise wealth and provide new investment opportunities for a broader range of investors. This wealth democratisation includes funds typically reserved for wealthier investors such as private, equity, hedge funds, private credit, IP, fine art, collectibles and property, and these fractional units/shares are typically represented as blockchain tokens thus allowing safe and transparent transactions to be possible 24/7. Blockchain technology makes token trading on numerous platforms efficient whereby allowing participants to buy, sell and exchange their shares like few assets before. The concept of fractional ownership is not new - collective investment schemes were first introduced by the [Dutch in 1774](https://www.robeco.com/files/docm/docu-summer-read-a-concise-financial-history-of-europe.pdf) and now many jurisdictions you will find a range of funds available for smaller investors as well as those designed for larger investors such as institutions and high net worth investors where the minimum investment can be in excess of £500,000. Fractional ownership lowers investor entry hurdles and boosts market liquidity because there are more investors and blockchain-powered tokens allowing for faster trading, 24/7. Investor trust also increases because blockchain makes ownership and transactions visible and immutable, and fractional ownership allows investors to diversify across many more asset classes, hopefully reducing a portfolio’s volatility and concentration and therefore improving returns. [Securitize was one of the first companies](https://securitize.io/learn/press/securitize-kkr-tokenized-fund) to tokenise a private equity fund, allowing investors access to the KKR’s Health Care Strategic Growth Fund II (HCSG II). “ _Tokenisation has the potential to address many of the biggest challenges for individual investors seeking to participate in private market investing by enabling technological and product innovations that were not possible before”_ reports Securitize CEO, Carlos Domingo, further adding that _“this new fund is an important step toward democratizing access to private equity investments by delivering more efficient access to institutional quality products.”_ Moreover, fractional ownership is making waves across a variety of industries, and not only in the asset management and funds sector. Real estate has long been considered a stable and profitable investment but its high entry costs have often excluded smaller investors. The first Real Estate Investment Trust (REIT) was [set up in 1960s ](https://www.reit.com/what-reit/history-reits)as a means to provide smaller investors with the ability to invest in real estate. The resultant problem is that many REITs trade at a discount to Net Asset Value. By tokenising properties, each token represents a fractional share of ownership, which can be bought and sold on digital exchanges - this creates more liquidity in the real estate market and offers property developers new opportunities for funding. Platforms such as [RealT](https://www.request.finance/use-cases/realt) and [Propy](https://propy.com/home/) are at the forefront of real estate tokenisation, allowing a broader range of people to participate in property investments.

Historically, art investment has been reserved for the wealthy with iconic pieces often fetching millions of dollars at auctions; fractional ownership is now making it possible for everyday investors to own shares in high-value art and collectibles. Tokenising artwork allows investors to buy shares in famous pieces with ownership and provenance being recorded on a blockchain, ensuring transparency and reducing fraud. In turn, this has opened up the art market to a wider audience, providing opportunities for both artists and collectors to benefit from new revenue streams. Platforms such as [Masterworks](https://www.masterworks.com/) and [Artory](https://www.artory.com/) are leading the charge in this space. Meanwhile, intellectual property (IP) such as patents, copyrights and trademarks is another area where fractional ownership is creating new opportunities for both creators and investors. By tokenising IP assets, creators can sell fractional shares to investors who may then receive royalties or other income generated by the intellectual property. This model not only increases revenue for creators but also provides a more accessible way for individuals to invest in innovative technologies and products. In addition, companies such as [IPwe are leveraging blockchain technology](https://www.lfdecentralizedtrust.org/blog/2023/02/02/ipwe-launches-revolutionary-platform-and-deployment-of-25-million-patent-nfts-on-hybrid-casper-blockchain-and-hyperledger-fabric-network) to facilitate the tokenisation and trade of intellectual property. In the renewable energy sector, investments in renewable energy projects such as solar farms have typically been dominated by institutional investors. Fractional ownership is disrupting this trend by enabling individual investors to participate in sustainable projects. Tokenising renewable energy assets allows investors to own a stake in these projects and share in the returns generated from energy production and sales. This model offers new funding opportunities for green initiatives and promotes broader participation in sustainable investments, and platforms such as [Power Ledger](https://powerledger.io/) and [Energy Web Token](https://www.energyweb.org/) are pioneering this form of fractional ownership in the energy sector.

Fractional ownership is also assisting luxury goods such as high-end watches, rare wines and designer jewellery which can be bought in fractional portions by tokenising them. These tokens can sometimes be traded or even exchanged for real property, and tokenising luxury goods enhances provenance tracking, boosts market liquidity and reduces fraud - the potential market size for luxury goods that could be [tokenised is $2.1trillion](https://manimama.eu/tokenization/luxury-units-tokenization/). An example of tokenisation in the art markets is [Octarr’s](https://www.octarr.com/project-georges-braques) presentation of a Georges Braque painting - an artist who, like Pablo Picasso, pioneered the cubism art movement. Essentially, the benefits of fractional ownership are numerous and wide-ranging, making it a compelling option for both investors and asset owners, such as:

> · **democratising wealth creation** \- lowering financial barriers to investment possibilities for people from varied economic backgrounds is one of fractional ownership's biggest benefits. This wealth generation democratisation might level the playing field and provide more people access to high-value assets.
> 
> · **improved liquidity** \- many buyers and sellers of real estate and fine art experience long wait periods and hefty transaction expenses. By tokenising assets, fractional ownership increases liquidity and blockchain technologies allow fractional share trading so making investments easier to enter and leave.
> 
> · **increased transparency** \- blockchain's transparency records all transactions on an immutable ledger that parties may verify. Since every asset activity is public and unchangeable, this builds confidence and eliminates fraud.
> 
> · **global accessibility** \- with digital token-powered fractional ownership, investors worldwide may invest; global accessibility opens markets to more investors, overcoming geographical and financial obstacles.
> 
> · **portfolio diversification** \- by investing in a variety of assets, fractional ownership diversifies portfolios and this may lower risk and increase rewards. Instead of investing extensively in one or two high-value assets, investors can own fractional interests in several assets so reducing risk.

However, despite its many advantages, fractional ownership also faces several challenges that could hinder widespread adoption because the regulatory landscape for fractional ownership remains unclear in many jurisdictions. Governments have yet to fully define how fractionalised assets should be treated legally and there is a need for clear regulations to protect investors and ensure market stability. The lack of a unified regulatory framework is a significant barrier to the growth of fractional ownership. And whilst blockchain technology provides the foundation for fractional ownership, there are still technological challenges to overcome. Issues related to scalability, interoperability and the overall infrastructure supporting tokenised assets must be addressed before fractional ownership can achieve mainstream adoption. For fractional ownership to thrive, investors, businesses and regulators need to be educated about its benefits and potential risks - a lack of understanding can result in hesitancy and slow adoption. Therefore educational efforts are crucial for building trust and encouraging participation. Fractional ownership offers improved liquidity compared to traditional asset ownership but it still relies on well-functioning secondary markets. If these markets fail to operate efficiently, investors may face difficulties selling their fractional shares. Furthermore tokenised assets are still susceptible to cybersecurity risks - the protection of these assets from hacking and fraud is paramount in ensuring investor confidence. And, as with any emerging technology, the security of blockchain platforms must be a priority to prevent potential threats. So, as fractional ownership continues to evolve, several key trends are shaping its future, including:

> · **institutional adoption** \- large financial institutions are beginning to recognise the value of fractional ownership and are exploring how it can be integrated into their investment offerings. This could lead to broader acceptance and more widespread adoption.
> 
> · **integration with decentralised finance (DeFi)** \- DeFi platforms are incorporating fractionalised assets, creating new opportunities for innovative financial products and services. The fusion of fractional ownership with DeFi could result in more flexible and dynamic investment solutions.
> 
> · **expansion of asset classes** \- the range of assets available for fractional ownership is likely to expand, with new industries such as healthcare, education and sports being explored for tokenisation. This will provide even more opportunities for investors to diversify their portfolios.
> 
> · **regulatory evolution** \- as fractional ownership becomes more mainstream, regulatory bodies are working to establish clear guidelines that will balance innovation with investor protection. This evolving regulatory landscape will be crucial to the growth and sustainability of the fractional ownership model.
> 
> · **technological advancements** \- ongoing advancements in blockchain technology, such as scalability solutions and enhanced interoperability, will make fractional ownership platforms more efficient and accessible. As the technology matures, a smoother and more seamless experience for both investors and asset owners can be expected.

So, is fractional ownership the future of wealth or a risky illusion? Without doubt, tokenisation is tearing down the walls of elite investing, so turning private equity, real estate, fine art and intellectual property into accessible, tradeable assets for the masses. With blockchain enabling 24/7 liquidity, transparency and global access, fractional ownership promises to democratise wealth creation like never before. Yet, with opportunity comes uncertainty. Regulatory blind spots, cybersecurity risks and fragmented secondary markets could undermine trust before this revolution takes off. And if anyone can own a piece of a Picasso or a skyscraper, then what happens when liquidity dries up? Essentially, is fractional ownership the key to unlocking financial inclusion, or merely another bubble waiting to burst? Surely the answer lies in whether technology, regulation and market adoption can align before speculation outpaces reality.
