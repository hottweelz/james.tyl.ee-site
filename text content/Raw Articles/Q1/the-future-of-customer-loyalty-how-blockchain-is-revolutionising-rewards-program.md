# The future of customer loyalty: how blockchain is revolutionising rewards programs

*By Jonny Fry | *

For years, loyalty programs have been a cornerstone of customer retention, engagement and revenue generation but traditional models often lack efficiency, interoperability and customer flexibility. However, with blockchain technology gaining traction, businesses are re-imagining loyalty schemes to enhance security, transparency and user experience. [PYMNTS Intelligence](https://www.pymnts.com/tracker_posts/from-transaction-to-transformation-blockchains-loyalty-proposition/) projects that the loyalty market will surpass $24 billion in revenue within five years as more brands adopt blockchain technology to build adaptable and profitable customer reward programs. Indeed, as reported in the [Harvard Business](https://hbr.org/2024/09/why-loyalty-programs-fail) review: “ _A recent Bain & Company and ROI Rocket survey of nearly 870 US consumers found that 63% of respondents said they make buying decisions based on loyalty programs they participate in.” _By tokenising rewards, companies can offer customers new ways to earn, trade and redeem points, fostering deeper engagement whilst opening new revenue streams. Certainly, this innovation is already gaining traction - as demonstrated by ten-time GRAMMY nominee country singer, [Eric Church](https://single.xyz/blogs/blog/eric-church-future-proofs-fandom-solana-based-digital-deeds-nashville-bar), who has issued over 100,000 blockchain-supported digital collectibles to his most loyal fans. Nonetheless, although blockchain’s potential in the loyalty sector is clear, its implementation presents both opportunities and challenges that must be navigated carefully.

Essentially, blockchain technology is transforming loyalty programs by addressing key inefficiencies and unlocking new opportunities for businesses and consumers alike. One of the biggest challenges in traditional loyalty programs is fraud, including the misuse of points, counterfeit reward and system manipulation. By leveraging blockchain’s decentralised and tamper-proof ledger, businesses can significantly reduce fraudulent activity whilst enhancing customer trust. In addition, interoperability is another major advantage since many loyalty programs restrict points to a single brand, limiting their value for customers. Blockchain technology enables tokenised rewards that can be used across multiple merchants, creating a more flexible and seamless experience. A prime example is [Singapore Airlines’ KrisPay](https://mothership.sg/2018/07/krispay-singapore-airlines/#:~:text=Singapore%20Airlines%20\(SIA\)%20has%20just%20announced%20a,to%20use%20their%20miles%20for%20daily%20purchases.), which allows frequent flyer miles to be converted into digital credits redeemable at various partner merchants, from retail stores to gas stations. And the ability to process transactions in real time further enhances customer experience, eliminating delays in earning, trading and redeeming loyalty points. [American Express has explored blockchain-powered solutions](https://www.ledgerinsights.com/amex-blockchain-rewards-american-express/) that enable merchants to issue real-time incentives, streamlining reward distribution. Furthermore, blockchains enable the creation of secondary markets where customers can trade, sell or even rent their loyalty rewards, increasing engagement and maximising value. In turn, smart contracts automate the process by triggering reward distribution based on predefined conditions. For instance, a customer who reaches a spending milestone can automatically receive tokenised points, removing the need for manual approvals. Hospitality group, [Chanticleer Holdings, has already implemented a blockchain-based loyalty system](https://www.nrn.com/restaurant-technology/chanticleer-holdings-to-launch-cryptocurrency-based-loyalty-program) where customers earn cryptocurrency for dining at their restaurants, demonstrating how this technology can drive innovation in customer engagement.

However, whilst blockchain-powered loyalty programs offer numerous advantages, they also present several challenges that businesses must consider. High implementation costs can be a major barrier, especially for SMEs. Large corporations, such as American Express and Singapore Airlines, have the resources to integrate blockchain technology into their loyalty programs but smaller businesses may struggle with the infrastructure and technical expertise required. Complex user experiences and adoption barriers also pose challenges. Many consumers are unfamiliar with digital wallets, private keys and blockchain transactions, creating friction in adoption. For example, [Blackbird, a blockchain-based restaurant loyalty platform, requires customers to interact with a crypto-based app](https://www.coindesk.com/tech/2025/02/26/blackbird-blockchain-restaurant-loyalty-app-goes-live-with-flynet-mainnet), which may be intimidating for non-tech-savvy users. A further challenge is the regulatory and compliance uncertainty that complicates blockchain loyalty adoption; tokenised rewards may be subject to taxation, AML compliance and consumer protection laws, depending on the jurisdiction. The EU’s MiCA regulation, for instance, imposes strict guidelines on how blockchain-based loyalty programs operate in Europe, which has led to [Coinbase closing its loyalty program](https://www.ccn.com/news/crypto/coinbase-axes-usdc-rewards-eu-mica/) for EU residents. Scalability and transaction speed issues remain a concern as public blockchains such as Ethereum can suffer from congestion, slow transaction processing and high gas fees. Businesses may need to adopt [Layer 2 solutions](https://www.starknet.io/blog/layer-2-scaling-solutions/#:~:text=Rollups%20are%20L2%20solutions%20that,time%2C%20at%20a%20lower%20cost.) (such as Polygon or Optimism) to ensure cost-effective and efficient transactions at scale. Security risks and smart contract vulnerabilities also need to be addressed; whilst blockchain technology provides enhanced security, bugs in smart contracts can lead to loss or theft of loyalty points. Additionally, if customers lose access to their private keys, they may permanently lose their rewards - robust security measures, smart contract audits and user education are essential for mitigating these risks. Furthermore, a lack of standardisation and interoperability between different blockchain networks limits the transferability of tokenised rewards across ecosystems. For instance, whilst Singapore Airlines’ KrisPay allows point conversions within its merchant network, customers cannot transfer these rewards to other blockchain-based loyalty programs whereby reducing flexibility. Finally, environmental concerns must be considered particularly for blockchains using proof-of-work (PoW) consensus mechanisms (such as Bitcoin) which consume significant amounts of energy. And companies with ESG commitments must evaluate the sustainability of their blockchain choice - Ethereum’s shift to proof-of-stake (PoS) has substantially reduced its energy consumption, highlighting the importance of selecting eco-friendly blockchain solutions.

Yet, despite the challenges, blockchain presents exciting opportunities for revolutionising customer loyalty. Companies looking to leverage this technology can explore hybrid models that combine traditional loyalty points with tokenised rewards, easing customer adoption whilst gradually introducing blockchain’s benefits. Educational initiatives, such as user-friendly guides, tutorials and in-app support, can improve blockchain literacy and encourage participation so reducing friction in onboarding. Building collaborative ecosystems by partnering with multiple brands can enhance customer flexibility and increase the attractiveness of loyalty programs, making rewards more versatile and valuable. Additionally, selecting sustainable blockchain solutions that prioritise energy efficiency can help businesses align with ESG goals while ensuring scalability and operational efficiency. Hence, by addressing these key areas, businesses can unlock blockchain’s full potential in transforming loyalty programs and driving customer engagement in the digital era. So, is blockchain the future of customer loyalty, or just another overhyped tech experiment? Tokenised rewards promise security, flexibility and real-time transactions, but businesses will need to overcome the high costs and complex regulations. Will customers embrace decentralised loyalty programs, or will crypto-based rewards remain niche and inaccessible to the average consumer? Brands such as American Express and Singapore Airlines are pioneering blockchain-powered incentives, but traditional loyalty models could potentially become obsolete. And with concerns around scalability and sustainability, will blockchain loyalty programs truly enhance customer engagement, or are they merely another digital trend struggling to find real-world adoption?
