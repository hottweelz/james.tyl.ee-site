# New Year, new scams

*By Jonny Fry | *

In 2024 the Web3 ecosystem grew, driven by innovations such as real-world assets tokenisation, Euro-backed stablecoins, and Bitcoin's milestone of reaching a price of $100,000. The vices in the industry persist. [According to Cyvers](https://cryptopotato.com/crypto-industry-lost-2-3b-to-cyber-threats-in-2024-cyvers-report/), 2024 saw over $2.3 billion lost to cyber threats - which amounts to a 31.6% year-on-year increase in value stolen. This predicament needs to be amended so that the wins of Web3 can be consolidated. Phishing, a scam as old as the internet, brought in almost half the money lost to cyber-attacks; what makes these scams even more insidious is the adoption of AI by bad actors, making them increasingly difficult to detect.

Worryingly, phishing attacks have been rebranded for today’s remote economy. Russian-linked [hackers exploiting](https://www.binance.com/en-ZA/square/post/18178345934402) fake Zoom meeting links have shown that online meetings and their participants have become hotspots for scammers - similarly, a $300,000 theft through a malicious work meeting link has been [tied to BingX](https://cryptopotato.com/crypto-user-loses-300k-in-phishing-scam-via-malicious-work-meeting-link/). However, credit/debit card fraud globally is estimated in 2024 to exceed $34billion; in 2025, it is estimated that credit card fraud in the US alone will be in excess of [$15billion.](https://merchantcostconsulting.com/lower-credit-card-processing-fees/credit-card-fraud-statistics/)

Another type of scam to watch is the impersonation sort, especially now that it is harder to establish entities apart online. AI-generated content has been used repeatedly to mimic legitimate entities - the ["BlackRock token scam" ](https://www.blackrock.com/uk/solutions/insights/scam-notice#:~:text=BlackRock%20identity%3A%20Illegal%20and%20fraudulent%20use%20of%20BlackRock's%20name,including%20scams%20relating%20to%20cryptocurrency.)saw fraudsters using fake content which was impersonating BlackRock to appear legitimate so as to promote a fictitious “BlackRock Token (BRT)” presale. Other examples show real-life entities giving these scammers all the legitimacy they desire - such as [allegations against Barcelona and Real Madrid legends](https://cryptopotato.com/barcelona-and-real-madrid-legends-accused-of-endorsing-a-e3-billion-crypto-ponzi-report/) for endorsing a €3 billion cryptocurrency Ponzi scheme. And when they are unable to persuade these public figures to agree, the scammers then hack their accounts to promote scams. News outlet, CNBC, has [disclosed that crypto scammers](https://www.cnbctv18.com/cryptocurrency/crypto-scammers-coindcx-twitter-locked-hacking-verified-accounts-new-exploit-14772741.htm) had exploited a new method to hack verified Twitter accounts, including that of CoinDCX, a prominent cryptocurrency exchange. These hackers gain control of verified accounts to promote fraudulent cryptocurrency schemes, misleading followers into potential scams. In another attack, Animoca Brand’s co-founder, Yat Siu's, X account [was hacked](https://www.chaincatcher.com/en/article/2159270) where the attackers promoted a fraudulent token and stole nearly $500,000 from over 15 crypto accounts. [Telegram verification bots also spread malware threats](https://cointelegraph.com/news/telegram-verification-bots-carry-crypto-stealing-malware) which scammers use to steal cryptocurrencies. These scammers begin by impersonating popular people of influence, which drives people to their Telegram bots hoping to join their Telegram groups. The bots trick users into sharing sensitive data by mimicking official verification processes whilst the malware collects credentials and crypto wallet details. And where no honour exists among thieves, it does not end there - even in Web3. Cyber security firm, Kaspersky, has revealed that [scammers now set traps for each other](https://cryptopotato.com/crypto-scammers-bait-thieves-with-public-seed-phrases-in-new-scheme-kaspersky/) where they bait their colleagues by posting fake seed phrases publicly (typically on YouTube comments) posing as inexperienced users. These scammers aim to trick opportunistic thieves into trying to steal funds from multi-signature wallets, rendering them inaccessible to their colleagues. The scam primarily targets criminals, creating a trap where even thieves fall victim to the setup. The result? Web3 users who indulge in greed could also fall victim to this scam scheme.

However, it is not only the greedy but also the rich who are targeted by these Web3 scammers; which is what Casa Nick exposed after [his encounter with a member of a scamming ring](https://cryptopotato.com/casa-ceo-exposes-shocking-phishing-scam-targeting-wealthy-crypto-users/) specifically targeting wealthy crypto users. Impersonating Coinbase support, these scammers used sophisticated tactics (including fake emails and [auto-doxxing tools](https://www.wired.com/story/credit-bureau-dox-americans-security-roundup/)) to steal sensitive information. The scammer even bragged about the vastness of its scamming ring operation. Certainly, one such ring was brought to justice as authorities in [Nigeria arrested nearly 800 individuals](https://www.efcc.gov.ng/efcc/news-and-information/news-release/10584-efcc-bursts-syndicate-of-792-cryptocurrency-investment-romance-fraud-suspects-in-lagos-arrests-193-chinese-arabs-filipinos-others) linked to a large-scale cryptocurrency and romance fraud syndicate - among them are 193 suspects in Lagos, including Chinese, Arabs and Filipinos. The group used fake investment schemes and romantic relationships to deceive victims worldwide, stealing substantial sums of money. In addition, when identified as part of an international operation, the syndicate had exploited these tactics to target victims across multiple countries (investigations are ongoing to prosecute those involved). Scammers also have their eyes on Google ads where have also been [reports that scammers now use fraudulent Google ads](https://www.bitgetapp.com/news/detail/12560604457114) to impersonate and steal crypto assets. Usual Protocol was the subject of one such ad; these malicious advertisements appear at the top of Google search results for "Usual Protocol," mimicking the platform's branding to deceive users. Meanwhile, Scam Sniffer [flagged a similar issue](https://cryptopotato.com/scam-sniffer-exposes-google-ad-for-sonys-soneium-blockchain-as-crypto-wallet-drainer/) \- a Google ad pretending to promote Sony's blockchain platform, Soneium, was exposed as a phishing scam to drain cryptocurrency wallets. It used a nearly identical domain name and a deceptive landing page to trick users, bypassing Google's security measures. When users clicked on these ads, they were redirected to fake websites that attempted to access their digital assets by requesting wallet connections or prompted the signing of malicious transactions. According to a [report](https://cryptopotato.com/certik-report-reveals-december-crypto-losses-dropped-to-2024s-lowest-at-28-6m/) by blockchain security company [Certik](https://www.certik.com/), the space witnessed about $28.6 million in losses in December of last year. One such loss was $300,000 for a user who clicked a malicious KakaoTalk link shared on X whereby compromising the user’s Ethereum and Solana wallets and showing that, whilst hacks are usually directed at organisations and centralised bodies, individuals cannot protect themselves from that. Scams on the other hand often target individuals and, as such, users must take extra caution when defending themselves. Best practices for users include:

> · avoid sharing sensitive information
> 
> · verify sources before clicking on links or engaging with offers
> 
> · if it sounds too good to be true, it is too good to be true (exploitation of emotional vulnerability and FOMO (fear of missing out)
>

Undoubtedly, great power and technology (i.e. artificial intelligence) necessitates a profound responsibility to counter evolving scams and their sinister applications. As Web3 continues to advance so, too, do the tactics of nefarious actors - undoubtedly underscoring the urgent need for robust safeguards, increased vigilance and collaborative efforts across the industry. An example of this can be seen in Binance’s fight against scams through its [AI and machine learning efforts](https://qacc.giveth.io/news/binance-ai-ml-crypto-security-2024) in 2024 which significantly enhanced crypto scam detection, blocking $129 million in fraudulent transactions and recovering $9 million for victims. On the other hand, Chainalysis provides blockchain analytics to track stolen funds and identify scam operations.

The year 2024 has shown both the promise and peril of Web3 technologies, with various significant milestones eclipsed by hacks and scams. As we navigate 2025 and beyond, individuals, organisations and developers must collaborate in building a safer Web3 ecosystem. Certainly, by staying vigilant, adopting best practices and leveraging advanced security tools, we can protect Web3's gains and ensure its future is one of opportunity, and not exploitation.
