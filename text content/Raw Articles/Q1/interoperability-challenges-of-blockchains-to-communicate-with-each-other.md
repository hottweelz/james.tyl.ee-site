# Interoperability challenges of blockchains to communicate with each other

*By Jonny Fry | *

In the context of blockchain technology, “interoperability” refers to the ability of different blockchain networks to connect, exchange data and transfer assets without relying on intermediaries. The idea is to overcome the isolation between networks, creating a more cohesive and efficient ecosystem. Furthermore, interoperability enables seamless communication between blockchain platforms, allowing them to work together in a way that optimises both performance and functionality. Key components of blockchain interoperability include the exchange of data across networks, the transfer of assets such as digital tokens and the establishment of common standards to facilitate communication.

Blockchain interoperability is crucial for several reasons, including the fact that it enhances efficiency by eliminating redundancies and allowing networks to specialise in different functions. For example, one blockchain might be optimised for fast transaction processing whilst another could offer superior data storage capabilities; connecting these strengths can optimise the performance of decentralised systems. Interoperability also improves scalability by distributing workloads across multiple blockchains - this reduces congestion and alleviates bottlenecks, thus enhancing the overall user experience. Moreover, it fosters innovation and collaboration by enabling developers and businesses to build more complex applications that leverage the unique capabilities of various blockchains. And this can lead to the creation of multi-chain applications (mApps) where different components of a single app run on separate networks, each chosen for its specialised features. Another significant advantage of blockchain interoperability is its ability to facilitate cross-industry use cases. For example, supply chain networks could connect with financial systems to streamline trade financing, or healthcare blockchains could securely share patient records with insurance providers. Interoperability thus extends the potential applications of blockchain technology across various sectors, further embedding it into the global economy. Several projects and technologies are helping bridge the gap between blockchain networks, allowing them to communicate and share assets seamlessly, including:

> · **Cosmos: the internet of blockchains**

[Cosmos](https://cosmos.network/) aims to create an interconnected network of blockchains through its [Inter-Blockchain Communication (IBC) protocol](https://tutorials.cosmos.network/academy/3-ibc/1-what-is-ibc.html). The Cosmos Hub acts as a central platform that connects various blockchains, enabling the smooth transfer of assets and data between them. In this ecosystem, developers can build customised blockchains (zones) that can interoperate with others. The Cosmos network also promotes shared security, allowing smaller or newer blockchains to benefit from the security provided by the more established zones.

> · **Polkadot: enabling cross-chain communication**

Polkadot offers a unique approach to blockchain interoperability through its [relay chain](https://www.ledger.com/academy/glossary/relay-chain) and [parachains](https://decrypt.co/resources/what-are-parachains-polkadots-secret-weapon-explained). The relay chain serves as Polkadot’s central hub, whilst parachains are individual blockchains connected to it. This structure enables cross-chain communication, allowing data and assets to be transferred between parachains and external blockchains. The system is highly scalable, as parachains can operate independently, distributing workloads and reducing congestion. Furthermore, Polkadot's shared security model ensures that all parachains connected to the relay chain benefit from a unified security structure.

> · **Chainlink: decentralised oracles**

Chainlink plays a critical role in blockchain interoperability by providing decentralised oracles. These oracles connect blockchain networks with external data sources and other blockchains. Chainlink’s infrastructure enables smart contracts to interact with off-chain data and execute cross-chain functions. The company is also developing a [Cross-Chain Interoperability Protocol (CCIP)](https://docs.chain.link/ccip), designed to facilitate universal messaging between different blockchains, further enhancing the ability to transfer data and assets across networks.

> · **bridges: connecting isolated networks**

Blockchain bridges serve as connectors between different blockchain networks. They enable the transfer of assets by creating pegged representations of tokens on destination chains. For example, Wrapped Bitcoin (WBTC) allows Bitcoin to be used on the Ethereum network, creating a cross-chain bridge between two otherwise separate systems. Similarly, platforms such as [Multichain (formerly Anyswap](https://pontem.network/posts/an-overview-of-multichain-multi)) and [Wormhole](https://cryptoslate.com/products/wormhole-bridge/) provide bridges that allow token and data transfers across multiple networks, e.g. Solana, Ethereum and Binance Smart Chain.

Meanwhile,**** the ability for blockchain networks to interact with one another is already transforming various industries, including:

> · **decentralised finance (DeFi)** \- interoperability is playing a pivotal role in the growth of DeFi, where users can access lending, borrowing and trading services across different blockchain networks. By connecting liquidity pools from multiple chains, DeFi platforms can offer users access to a broader range of assets and services without having to switch between networks. Projects such as SushiSwap and Aave are exploring cross-chain functionality to create a seamless experience for users.
> 
> · **supply chain management** \- blockchain interoperability is also revolutionising supply chain management; by connecting different blockchain networks used by stakeholders in a supply chain, data can be shared securely and transparently. For example, a food supply chain blockchain could integrate with a logistics network to ensure end-to-end traceability, allowing all parties to access real-time data and improve efficiency.
> 
> · **healthcare** \- in the healthcare sector, blockchain interoperability enables the secure exchange of patient data between providers, insurers and other stakeholders. Through interoperable systems, patients can maintain control over their data while giving authorised parties streamlined access to necessary information. This has the potential to improve the quality of care and reduce administrative inefficiencies.
> 
> · **gaming and NFTs** \- in the world of gaming and NFTs, interoperability allows players to transfer in-game assets across different platforms. Non-fungible tokens (NFTs) can be used across multiple blockchains, expanding their market reach and usability. This flexibility enhances the value of digital assets and improves the overall experience for users in gaming and collectibles.

Yet, whilst significant progress has been made, several challenges remain in achieving full blockchain interoperability - one of the primary issues is security. Cross-chain interactions expose networks to new vulnerabilities (particularly in the case of bridge exploits) which can lead to the loss of assets. The complexity of designing and maintaining interoperable systems also presents a significant challenge - these systems require sophisticated protocols and technologies, making them difficult to implement and sustain. Another barrier is the lack of standardisation - the absence of universally accepted protocols makes it difficult for different blockchains to integrate seamlessly. Additionally, regulatory concerns pose a challenge, particularly in industries such as finance and healthcare, where cross-border data and asset transfers are subject to complex legal frameworks. Blockchain interoperability is redefining the possibilities of decentralised systems, allowing networks to seamlessly share data and assets whilst unlocking unprecedented efficiency, innovation and scalability. By connecting specialised blockchains, it is powering multi-chain applications and transforming industries such as DeFi, healthcare and gaming. Yet, this interconnected future comes with risks - security vulnerabilities, regulatory hurdles and a lack of standardisation threaten progress. With ongoing research and development, the future of blockchain interoperability is poised to create a more interconnected and efficient digital economy. The question remains: can we overcome these challenges to realise a truly cohesive blockchain ecosystem, or will fragmentation hinder the full potential of this transformative technology?
