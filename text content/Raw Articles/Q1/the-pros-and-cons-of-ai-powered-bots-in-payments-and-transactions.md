# The pros and cons of AI-powered bots in payments and transactions -

*By Jonny Fry | *

**The pros and cons of using AI for payments**

The integration of AI-based bots in financial systems is dramatically altering global payment infrastructures, bringing both speed and complexity to transactions. These bots are able to carry out transactions at impressive speeds with great accuracy, fundamentally altering financial interactions for both businesses and consumers. Major concerns about this shift include the effects on payment speed, security and ethical dilemmas. Through their automation of decisions and management of real-time transactions, AI bots are likely to foster demand for new digital money forms, such as CBDCs and sophisticated stablecoins.

A notable development in this space is [Siemens, becoming the first client of JP Morgan to use the bank’s new programmable payment functionality with JPM Coin](https://www.ledgerinsights.com/ubs-crypto-etfshong-kong/), its blockchain-based platform. This innovation allows companies to establish complex payment rules, moving beyond traditional conditional payments. Through a new “if-this-then-that” interface, JP Morgan’s online treasury portal enables programmable payments that can trigger actions (i.e. topping up accounts based on certain conditions) such as margin calls for the derivatives market or paying, in almost real time, for securities once a trade has been executed. Siemens views this as a crucial step toward optimising working capital, supporting digital business models and enhancing scalability for their treasury operations. However, this shift towards increased automation does also raise important concerns about accountability. As bots take over more tasks, will resultant increased automation in payments then lead to a rise in litigation, particularly regarding errors made by bots and the distribution of liability? Certainly, this evolving landscape invites critical discussions on both the benefits and challenges posed by the growing role of AI in financial transactions.

In the world of payments and financial transactions, AI-powered bots are now automating processes through [advanced algorithms that analyse, execute and manage transactions instantly](https://www.circle.com/blog/enabling-ai-agents-with-blockchain). These bots pull data from a range of sources (such as market behaviour and customer activity) to make decisions. They can automatically initiate transactions, trigger payments when certain criteria are met and ensure settlements are completed on time. Additionally, they evaluate potential risks, ensure compliance with regulations and manage cash flow without human intervention. Hence, by combining AI with blockchain technology, bots can enable faster, more secure and transparent transactions (minimising errors and lowering operational expenses) thus helping regulated firms achieve same day settlement when buying and selling equities and bonds, i.e. T0. Moreover, because AI bots continuously improve their performance by learning from transaction data, they will theoretically increase both efficiency and accuracy the more payments they make; this automation boosts the speed, scalability and dependability of financial systems so making them highly attractive for businesses to streamline operations and optimised capital management. A key area where AI-powered bots have demonstrated remarkable impact is in the stablecoin space. [Visa's recent research finds that bots account for 90% of stablecoin transactions](https://www.pymnts.com/news/banking/2024/wells-fargo-selling-san-francisco-hq-in-eastward-expansion/), whereby reflecting the growing role of automation in financial processes. The study also reveals that fewer than 10% of these transactions come from real users, with only $149 billion of the $2.2 trillion total transactions last month driven by organic payments. This also suggests that, whilst stablecoins show promise, they are still in the early stages of becoming a widely used form of payment. However, McKinsey forecasts that [the tokenised market capitalisation across various asset classes could reach $2 trillion by 2030](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/financial-services/our-insights/from-ripples-to-waves-the-transformational-power-of-tokenizing-assets) and tokenised assets will gravitate to digital programmable payment solutions powered by AI. Whilst Visa’s research signals a nascent stage for stablecoins as a payment method, it also points to their long-term potential. The market's limited demand and technological adoption, as noted by industry experts, underscore the challenge of making AI-powered systems and digital currencies more user-friendly and accessible.

In addition, the payments and transactions space is being revolutionised by AI-powered bots, offering several benefits that boost efficiency, security and accessibility. As financial services evolve, AI is emerging as a key tool for automating processes, cutting down costs, enhancing fraud detection and providing personalised experiences. One of the most notable benefits of AI-powered bots is their ability to streamline payment operations, resulting in faster and smoother transactions. AI systems remove human delays, optimising payment flows and significantly reducing transaction processing time. For example, [PayPal’s AI-driven fraud detection system processes over $1 trillion in payments annually with few errors](https://emerj.com/artificial-intelligence-at-paypal/); by leveraging extensive data, PayPal’s AI can identify fraudulent activities in real-time, offering speed and security. This further prompts the question: can traditional banks, restricted by legacy infrastructure, compete with the speed and efficiency of AI systems? In addition to enhancing speed (by automating tasks once performed by human workers or intermediaries) means the cost of a transaction ought to be lower, thereby making financial services more affordable. For example, [Stripe utilises AI-powered automation to optimise recurring payments](https://stripe.com/newsroom/news/sessions-2024), enabling businesses to cut administrative costs whilst boosting efficiency. However, it is also worth considering whether these savings come with risks - although AI can reduce costs, issues such as system malfunctions or security breaches must be carefully managed. Therefore, businesses implementing AI should weigh the potential financial savings against these emerging risks.

Meanwhile, AI’s skill in detecting patterns and abnormalities in data makes it an essential asset for fraud prevention - by analysing transaction data in real-time, AI can identify suspicious behaviours and prevent fraud before it takes place. [Mastercard’s Decision Intelligence system](https://www.mastercard.com/globalrisk/en/resources/all-resources/detect.html#:~:text=Mastercard%20Decision%20Intelligence%20is%20a,authorization%20decisions%20in%20order%20to) is a perfect illustration of this since it reduces false declines and boosts fraud detection accuracy. However, the constant evolution of fraud presents a significant challenge: can fraudsters learn to bypass AI systems? As AI technology continues to improve, ensuring it stays one step ahead of fraudsters is vital for the protection of financial transactions - AI-powered bots such as Google Pay and Apple Pay go beyond fraud prevention by offering 24/7 availability, executing transactions and handling payments around the clock. This ‘always-on’ capability is especially [advantageous for global platforms such as Aave](https://aave.com/), a decentralised finance (DeFi) service that needs real-time transaction processing. However, the constant operation of AI systems undoubtedly comes with potential risks. Excessive reliance on these systems may create weaknesses, particularly if there are cyberattacks or system breakdowns and, as AI continues to play a key role in financial services, maintaining its reliability and security will be essential.

AI’s integration with blockchain technology streamlines financial transactions, enhancing both efficiency and transparency. Smart contracts enable AI bots to automate processes such as loan management, carrying out transactions automatically based on set conditions. [Ethereum’s smart contracts are a prime example](https://m.economictimes.com/markets/cryptocurrency/understanding-smart-contracts-the-backbone-of-secure-transactions-in-defi/articleshow/111630573.cms), allowing bots to oversee complex financial agreements without intermediaries. However, as programmable money becomes more common, it raises important questions about user autonomy. The growing automation of financial systems may result in a hyper-automated ecosystem that diminishes individuals’ control over their financial choices. Furthermore, AI bots are offering highly personalised financial services by analysing how users behave and delivering recommendations that align with individual preferences. A good example is [Bank of America’s Erica chatbot](https://cointelegraph.com/learn/articles/bank-of-america-erica-ai-powered-chatbot), which gives personalised savings advice to users although this personalisation comes at the cost of data collection, so leading to privacy concerns. Ultimately though, is how much personal data are users willing to disclose for the sake of personalisation? Striking a balance between the advantages of tailored financial guidance and user privacy will be vital as AI continues to evolve in finance. Meanwhile, one significant challenge of AI-powered systems is their inability to match the nuanced judgment that human decision-makers bring to complex financial choices. AI is proficient in processing large amounts of data quickly but struggles in situations that require human intuition or subjective assessment. A noteworthy case from [2018 involved Wells Fargo’s AI system mistakenly rejecting mortgage applications](http://privacyinternational.org/examples/2736/wells-fargo-software-bug-penalises-mortgage-holders) from qualified borrowers, showing the limitations of AI in handling high-stakes decisions that require empathy or discretion. And this raises the important issue: should AI be trusted with critical financial decisions, or should human oversight remain essential to ensure fairness and accuracy? Another challenge that AI-powered digital payments face is security vulnerabilities; whilst automated bots can enhance efficiency, they are susceptible to hacking if their underlying code contains vulnerabilities. [In 2021, the Poly Network hack exposed these risks, with hackers exploiting flaws in AI-driven bots to steal over $600 million in cryptocurrencies](https://www.reuters.com/technology/how-hackers-stole-613-million-crypto-tokens-poly-network-2021-08-12/). Hence, this event brings forth an important question: does the efficiency and speed of AI-powered payments make financial systems more vulnerable to cyberattacks?

In addition to this, the adoption of AI in financial services faces substantial regulatory hurdles; whilst AI is transforming the payments sector, global regulations governing its application remain in early stages. [The European Union's Artificial Intelligence Act](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/news/en/press-room/20240308IPR19015/artificial-intelligence-act-meps-adopt-landmark-law) and [ongoing US discussions](https://www.whitecase.com/insight-our-thinking/ai-watch-global-regulatory-tracker-united-states#:~:text=As%20noted%20above%2C%20there%20is,Responsible%20innovation%20and%20development) reflect the regulatory uncertainty surrounding AI-driven financial systems. With regulations varying across different regions, financial institutions operating internationally may face significant compliance challenges such as Binance and Kraken. And this brings up an important issue: how will inconsistent regulations influence the scalability and effectiveness of AI-powered financial systems? Privacy, too, is a vital concern when it comes to AI systems in financial transactions since these systems often rely on large quantities of consumer data to operate effectively, raising potential risks to privacy and data security. An example of this is [Facebook's Libra project](https://www.ft.com/content/a88fb591-72d5-4b6b-bb5d-223adfb893f3), which was heavily criticised over privacy concerns, leading to its re-branding as Diem (before the whole project was eventually cancelled). Hence, the key challenge is balancing AI's efficiency and personalised service offerings with the need to protect consumer privacy - but can AI-powered payment systems secure consumer data while delivering customised experiences?

Moreover, the effectiveness of AI systems hinges on the quality of the data they are trained with. Poor-quality or biased data can result in unfair decisions, potentially contributing to financial exclusion. A prime example of this was [Amazon's AI recruitment tool, which was discarded after it was discovered that it favoured male candidates](https://www.reuters.com/article/world/insight-amazon-scraps-secret-ai-recruiting-tool-that-showed-bias-against-women-idUSKCN1MK0AG/). Similarly, AI-powered financial systems could unintentionally reinforce discrimination whether the data used was incomplete or biased, posing a serious barrier to financial equity. Indeed, a speech, [‘Monsters in the deep’](https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/speech/2024/may/jon-hall-speech-at-the-university-of-exeter), by Jonathan Hall (a member of the Financial Policy Committee at the Bank of England) highlighted: _“Significant recent advances in the capabilities of artificial intelligence (AI) raise the question of whether increasing use of AI models could have a negative impact on financial stability”_. Over-reliance on automation also introduces the risk of unintended consequences, especially within financial markets. A key instance of this was the [2010 “Flash Crash," when algorithmic trading bots caused a swift market collapse, erasing $1 trillion in equity in minutes](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/228261350_The_Flash_Crash_The_Impact_of_High_Frequency_Trading_on_an_Electronic_Market). As a consequence, it leads to important questions: what dangers do unsupervised AI bots present in financial markets, and how can we minimise such risks moving forward? Job displacement is certainly becoming an increasing concern as AI steps in to handle tasks once managed by humans, particularly in customer service and routine banking operations. [JPMorgan Chase, for instance, has reduced its workforce due to the improved efficiency of automated systems](https://www.livemint.com/companies/news/jpmorgan-ceo-jamie-dimon-says-ai-reduce-workweeks-to-three-and-half-days-tech-always-replaced-jobs-but-benefits-us-news-11732676143354.html). And, as AI continues to dominate more areas of finance, societies need to prepare for the potential loss of jobs and the broader social impact of this shift. Finally, accountability presents a major issue when AI systems make errors. [The Tesla autopilot accidents](https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2024/apr/26/tesla-autopilot-fatal-crash) have sparked significant legal questions about who is liable for mistakes made by autonomous systems. In the financial sector, a similar issue arises regarding responsibility when AI bots make errors - should it be the developers, the financial institutions that use the technology, or the users themselves?

So, as AI-driven payments grow in popularity, co-operation between developers, regulators and financial institutions will be essential to building a secure and efficient future. In addition, achieving the right balance between speed, efficiency and security will determine whether AI-powered bots enhance or disrupt the financial system. The advancement of AI in payments presents tremendous opportunities but also challenges that need to be addressed with careful oversight. As we embrace this shift, one question remains: can we develop a financial ecosystem that utilises AI's power whilst maintaining trust, security and fairness?
