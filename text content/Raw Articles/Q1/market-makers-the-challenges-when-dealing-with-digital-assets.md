# Market makers: the challenges when dealing with digital assets

*By Jonny Fry | *

Market makers are essential for ensuring liquidity in traditional financial (TradFi) and decentralised finance (DeFi) markets by continuously providing buy and sell orders, which helps keep assets tradable. Many of the traditional market makers that have served TradFi markets and exchanges are now active in the DeFi sector, providing liquidity through [automated market-making](https://www.coinbase.com/en-gb/learn/advanced-trading/what-is-an-automated-market-maker-amm#:~:text=Automated%20Market%20Makers%20\(AMMs\)%20are,DeFi%20ecosystem%20through%20liquidity%20pools.) (AMM) algorithms and [liquidity pools](https://www.kraken.com/learn/crypto-liquidity-pool): automated market makers (AMMs) are decentralised exchange protocols that use mathematical algorithms to price assets and provide liquidity, replacing traditional order books with user liquidity pools.

These pools which contain paired tokens (e.g., ETH/USDC) enable traders to swap assets directly, adjusting prices dynamically based on supply and demand. Operating without intermediaries, AMMs such as Uniswap, SushiSwap and PancakeSwap exemplify DeFi principles so allowing users to trade directly from wallets whilst democratising liquidity provision - think of them as crowdsourced stock exchanges. However, running these “stock exchanges” is not as easy as it sounds…. Web3 market makers face complex challenges, starting with 'impermanent loss' - this term refers to the temporary losses that liquidity providers may experience when the value of assets in a pool diverges from holding them individually. Although this can discourage participation in decentralised exchanges, it is important to note that the loss is only realised when withdrawing funds - and prices can recover, sometimes very quickly. This can present a challenge especially for traders who historically use simple stop losses since, whilst automated stop-losses are critical risk management tools, impairment losses and extreme volatility make them highly susceptible to forced liquidations at suboptimal prices in crypto markets where prices can dramatically fall as well as rise.

Regulatory uncertainty compounds these risks and smart contract risks further threaten stability with vulnerabilities in code potentially leading to exploits, hacks or fund losses - as seen in [high-profile DeFi breaches](https://www.coindesk.com/business/2022/09/20/crypto-market-maker-wintermute-hacked-for-160m-says-ceo), necessitating rigorous audits and security protocols. Meanwhile, [liquidity fragmentation](https://arxiv.org/html/2307.13772v5) across an ever-growing number of decentralised exchanges and blockchains can give added challenges to market makers, who must deploy capital efficiently while competing for yields in diluted pools. Finally, market manipulation (such as [front-running](https://www.nasdaq.com/glossary/f/front-running) via bots exploiting blockchains’ transparency) undermines fair trading environments whereby eroding trust, which is why such practices have been banned in TradFi markets for years. Some market makers have been accused of unethical practices such as wash trading (which some estimate as being in excess of [$2.5billion](https://www.chainalysis.com/blog/crypto-market-manipulation-wash-trading-pump-and-dump-2025/) in trading volume) also front-running (i.e. Trading on their own account before executing trades for clients). Whilst some manipulate token prices through loan option models - to boost trading volumes and influence market metrics artificially. Token projects sometimes hire these market makers to create higher volumes and more attractive price movements, even if it means resorting to tactics that would be considered illegal in traditional markets. But, whilst legitimate market makers focus on maintaining healthy order books and tight spreads, the industry's darker side undermines trust. It could prompt stricter regulatory oversight, as seen with frameworks (such as the EU's MiCA) that aim to [curb such manipulative behaviours.](https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20241212677489/en/Kairon-Labs-and-Solidus-Labs-Partner-to-Redefine-Ethical-Market-Making-Under-MiCA) Bitwise has [reported](https://cointelegraph.com/news/bitwise-calls-out-to-sec-95-of-bitcoin-trade-volume-is-fake-real-market-is-or) that 95% of Bitcoin volume in 2019 was fake and there have been several further high-profile stories in recent years.

In 2024, [Fracture Labs sued Jump Crypto](https://cointelegraph.com/news/game-developer-jump-crypto-pump-and-dump), a prominent market maker, claiming it had manipulated the price of the DIO token (a popular cryptocurrency) causing a pump-and-dump. The lawsuit alleges Jump inflated the price and sold off tokens, crashing the value and freezing $1.4M in funds. In 2023, the [SEC charged Moonwalker, a Crypto market maker,](https://hackernoon.com/sec-charges-crypto-market-maker-in-a-pump-and-dump-of-a-worthless-token) with a pump-and-dump of a worthless token. In late 2023, Binance's market surveillance team [reportedly found evidence](https://www.wsj.com/finance/currencies/binance-investigation-suspicious-trading-dwf-5f481f9d) of DWF Labs engaging in manipulative activities, affecting the prices of tokens like Yield Guild Games (YGG) and several others, including $300 million in wash trading, but deemed it insufficient for action. Binance also got the heat for _“[firing the investigator”](https://coingeek.com/binance-denies-firing-investigators-who-exposed-market-maker-manipulation/)_ who exposed this market’s manipulation, which it denied - both Binance and [DWF Labs denied the claims](https://cryptoinput.com/dwf-labs-navigating-controversy-and-commitment-to-integrity/). In addition, the latter has also been criticised for [emphasising price management](https://www.theblock.co/post/267354/how-dwf-labs-makes-deals-and-its-tendency-to-talk-about-price) in deals and raising concerns about transparency and ethics. [Peers have also criticised](https://www.theblock.co/post/225705/dwf-labs-biggest-crypto-investors?) DWF for being a Web3 venture capitalist amongst other things; Wintermute, a leading market maker, is one such [critic](https://www.chaincatcher.com/en/article/2091187). In 2023, criticism of Wintermute intensified, with [some comparing](https://cryptoslate.com/wintermute-the-new-crypto-behemoth-rising-from-the-ashes-of-ftx-and-alameda/) it to FTX/Alameda and questioning its role in the Terra-Luna collapse - Wintermute is said to have benefitted from the crash. Concerns were also raised about Arbitrum’s governance after its foundation spent $1 billion in ARB tokens before a DAO vote - [Wintermute received $40 million](https://coingeek.com/arbitrum-wintermute-and-more-defi-lies/) of this and, furthermore, these events fuelled scepticism about DeFi's transparency. In 2025, Wintermute’s CEO, Evgeny Gaevoy, [responded](https://www.chaincatcher.com/en/article/2165791) in strong language, rejecting market manipulation claims, explaining the firm’s liquidity strategies and dismissing accusations of price exploitation. [Gaevoy](https://www.trendx.tech/news/the-founder-of-wintermute-responded-to-the-market-manipulation-question-treat-me-as-a-false-enemy-don-39-t-blame-me-to-spray-you-1339320) when questioned about manipulation recently said: “ _Alas, we do not manipulate the price. One is that we do n’t know how to manipulate; the other is that our core business is enough to make money, so we do n’t do stupid things illegal; the third is that I live in London and like to travel to the United States. I do n’t want to move to Dubai.”_

Meme coins have become a prime example of this shift towards venture capital - institutional investments have surged nearly 300% to $295 million, with the Avalanche Foundation launching [a $100M fund.](https://cointelegraph.com/news/avalanche-foundation-memecoin-eligibility-criteria-one-month-old) DWF Labs also [introduced a $20M “Meme Fund”](https://www.coinspeaker.com/web3-market-maker-dwf-labs-unveils-20-million-meme-fund/) to back projects such as Floki and Barsik, securing Binance listings. With structured support from market makers, meme coins are evolving beyond hype attracting millions of users globally - even [President Trump and his wife have launched meme coins](https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/articles/c98y47vrv2jo). Whether they should be doing this or not is another conversation waiting to be had.Integrating venture capitalist functions into Web3 market makers can accelerate innovation by funding and supporting promising projects while leveraging market insights. However, it also raises conflict-of-interest concerns as these firms might favour projects that boost their portfolios, potentially skewing market fairness and inviting regulatory scrutiny. But the thin line between market manipulation and market making is hard to tell - a [2022 NBER study](https://cointelegraph.com/news/70-of-unregulated-exchange-transactions-are-wash-trading-nber-study) puts 70% of the volume on regulated exchanges as fake, a 25% decline from [2019](https://cointelegraph.com/news/bitwise-calls-out-to-sec-95-of-bitcoin-trade-volume-is-fake-real-market-is-or). Hopefully, the numbers will decrease as regulatory frameworks and industry members hold themselves to higher standards. These, paired with AI-driven liquidity solutions and transparent audits, must balance innovation with accountability so ensuring a future where decentralised finance achieves its promise of fairness and transparency.

Consequently, market makers are essential for liquidity but also increasingly controversial in the Web3 space. Whilst AMM protocols democratise trading, challenges such as impermanent loss, smart contract vulnerabilities and liquidity fragmentation pose risks. Yet, the darker side of market making is raising concerns - allegations of wash trading, front-running and pump-and-dump schemes have surfaced, with lawsuits and regulatory scrutiny intensifying as potentially the blurred line between liquidity provision and manipulation. So, as AI-driven liquidity solutions emerge and regulatory frameworks tighten, can Web3 market makers evolve into responsible financial architects - or will they repeat the excesses of TradFi’s worst actors?
