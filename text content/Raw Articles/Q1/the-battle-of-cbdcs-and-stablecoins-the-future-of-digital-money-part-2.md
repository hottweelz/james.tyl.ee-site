# The battle of CBDCs and stablecoins: the future of digital money (Part 2)

*By Jonny Fry | *

Stablecoins have grown rapidly as serious competitors to CBDCs, offering a blend of speed, transparency and blockchain integration. Stablecoins backed by fiat such as USDC and Tether peg their value to traditional currencies whilst capitalising on blockchain’s efficiency. This competitive edge has fuelled their rising popularity, with large financial institutions recognising their potential. For instance, [JP Morgan](https://www.jpmorgan.com/kinexys/documents/mCBDCs-Unlocking-120-billion-value-in-cross-border-payments.pdf), the [world’s fifth-largest bank](https://gfmag.com/award/worlds-safest-banks-2024-biggest-banks/), introduced the JPM Coin for immediate settlements for institutional clients whilst Visa and PayPal have adopted stablecoin transactions, demonstrating growing mainstream acceptance. And the [advantages of stablecoins over CBDCs are evident in several key areas](https://fintechtakes.com/articles/2025-01-10/how-to-think-about-stablecoins/). First, stablecoins leverage blockchain networks whereby allowing transactions to be completed in seconds, unlike retail CBDCs which may be slowed down by existing banking systems. Furthermore, stablecoins help drive financial inclusion by providing unbanked populations with access to digital currency without the need for traditional banking accounts. Additionally, the programmability of stablecoins through smart contracts enables the automation of transactions based on specific conditions. This is therefore transforming business processes, particularly in areas such as supply chain settlements and loan repayments where efficiency and automation are essential. However, although stablecoins offer numerous benefits, they also encounter significant obstacles. One of the biggest challenges is regulatory uncertainty as governments and financial institutions seek to define appropriate rules for their use. Additionally, stablecoins must prove their ability to remain stable during financial turbulence in order to maintain trust and ensure long-term viability. The future success of stablecoins will largely depend on the evolution of regulatory frameworks and their practical applications in the global financial landscape.

**Programmable payments: the future of finance**

A significant advantage of stablecoins and blockchain-based digital money lies in their ability to facilitate programmable payments. [Chris Colson, a payments expert at the Atlanta Fed,](https://www.atlantafed.org/blogs/take-on-payments) emphasises that _"programmable payments are reshaping the future of finance by offering automation, simplicity and flexibility."_ This innovation is intrinsically poised to transform how financial transactions are conducted, making them more efficient and adaptable to various needs. And the key technologies enabling programmable payments are smart contracts and application programming interfaces (APIs). Smart contracts are self-executing agreements that automatically initiate payments when specific conditions are fulfilled, such as an insurance payout being processed after a claim is verified and APIs allow financial platforms to communicate with each other, automating tasks such as payroll and invoice reconciliation, whereby streamlining operations and minimising human error. The benefits of programmable payments are substantial. First, they introduce automation, enabling businesses to eliminate manual tasks, cut down on errors and improve efficiency. Additionally, blockchain-based payments offer transparency, so providing a clear and immutable audit trail that minimises the risk of fraud. Moreover, programmable payments enable faster settlements by processing transactions instantly without relying on traditional banking intermediaries, which can often cause delays.

Looking ahead, the internet of things (IoT) is poised to take programmable payments to new heights. As more devices become connected, transactions could be managed automatically - e.g. taxes, transport costs, harbour fees being requested and paid once an IoT device on a ship triggers a request for payment based on the geolocation of the goods. Alternatively, a smart fridge ordering and paying for food items by replenishing groceries when supplies get low. This seamless flow between devices and payments could revolutionise everyday financial interactions whereby offering new opportunities for financial automation and efficiency. Meanwhile, the rivalry between CBDCs and stablecoins will be a critical factor in determining the future of digital finance; each has its own strengths and challenges and their success will depend on regulatory frameworks and the pace of adoption. The central issue in this competition is adoption and trust. Central banks must reassure the public and financial institutions that CBDCs offer real benefits (such as improved efficiency and financial inclusion) whilst safeguarding privacy. Furthermore, stablecoins must navigate regulatory challenges surrounding stability and security, all whilst maintaining their decentralised appeal, and successfully balancing these factors will be essential for both CBDCs and stablecoins to thrive.

Another critical consideration is interoperability - for both CBDCs and stablecoins to achieve widespread use, they must seamlessly integrate with existing payment networks and financial systems. Wholesale CBDCs, designed for interbank settlements, show significant promise in improving the efficiency of cross-border transactions and large-scale payments. In contrast, stablecoins are dominating the retail and cross-border transaction space where their speed, transparency and programmability provide tangible benefits for both consumers and businesses. And the role of regulation and policy is key to the ongoing debate. Governments globally are struggling to regulate stablecoins in a manner that promotes innovation whilst protecting both consumers and the overall financial system. Central banks, on the other hand, need to ensure that CBDCs do not threaten financial stability or interfere with the operations of commercial banks. A well-balanced regulatory approach is vital for sustaining competitiveness, ensuring user confidence and promoting the growth of both CBDCs and stablecoins. The ongoing battle between CBDCs and stablecoins is not merely about technological advancement but also about control, efficiency and promoting financial inclusion. Whilst retail CBDCs raise concerns over privacy and potential disruptions to the banking sector, wholesale CBDCs hold the potential to revolutionise cross-border payments and enhance financial infrastructure. Consulting firm, [McKinsey, has reported](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/financial-services/our-insights/global-payments-in-2024-simpler-interfaces-complex-reality): _“In 2023, the global payments industry handled 3.4 trillion transactions, accounting for $1.8 quadrillion in value and a revenue pool of $2.4 trillion._ ”

Stablecoins, on the other hand, continue to provide a viable alternative, supported by strong institutional backing and organic market adoption and are increasingly targeting global payments as a faster and cheaper alternative tool. **With a $9.9million of fresh capital, US-based,**[Cedar Money](https://www.pymnts.com/news/investment-tracker/2025/cedar-money-raises-9-million-dollars-cross-border-payment-solution-using-stablecoins/)**, is on a mission to bridge the financial divide between emerging and developed markets, offering an alternative to the barriers that have long stifled international money flows. The timing could not be better - stablecoins are rapidly becoming the backbone of global payments with businesses relying on them for efficiency, cost savings and stability. But this shift is not just market-driven - it is political. The US presidential election has re-ignited discussions on digital currencies, so accelerating the need for regulated and scalable payment solutions. However, as Cedar Money rewires the global financial system with stablecoin infrastructure, the real question emerges: if money moves faster, cheaper and more securely on stablecoins, why keep using banks at all?** Looking toward 2025 and beyond, it is unlikely that one will emerge as the definitive winner. Instead, we may see a hybrid future of alternative digital programmable money payment providers work along existing banking rails. As the financial landscape evolves rapidly, institutions that fail to adapt to these changes risk obsolescence. Hence, a further critical question remains: will central banks embrace collaboration with the private sector, or will regulatory battles ultimately determine the future of digital money?

**The battle for the future of money: will stablecoins render banks obsolete?**

The global financial system is at a crossroads. Central banks are racing to develop CBDCs as a means of maintaining control over digital payments whilst stablecoins (faster, programmable and increasingly backed by institutions such as JPMorgan and PayPal) are emerging as the superior alternative. Stablecoins offer seamless cross-border transactions, financial inclusion without banks and automated smart contracts - all without the bureaucratic delays and privacy concerns tied to CBDCs. Meanwhile, the rise of stablecoin-powered fintech solutions (such as Cedar Money) is proving that businesses no longer need traditional banking rails to operate globally. As programmable money reshapes finance, the real question is not simply whether CBDCs or stablecoins will dominate - but whether traditional banks will even be relevant in the new digital economy. If money can move faster, cheaper and more securely on stablecoins, why use banks at all? The battle between CBDCs and stablecoins is not merely about technology - it is about control, efficiency and financial inclusion. Central banks see CBDCs as a way to modernise finance, yet concerns over privacy, banking disruption and public adoption remain unresolved. Meanwhile, stablecoins are already proving their value, so offering faster, cheaper and programmable payments. However, regulatory uncertainty threatens their expansion.

So, the real question is not as to whether one will replace the other - it is whether governments will collaborate with private innovation or fight to retain dominance. A future where CBDCs and stablecoins co-exist could unlock global financial inclusion and efficiency. But if central banks fail to adapt, stablecoins may outpace them whereby forcing a redefinition of financial sovereignty. In the race for the future of money, will governments shape innovation - or will innovation shape governments?
