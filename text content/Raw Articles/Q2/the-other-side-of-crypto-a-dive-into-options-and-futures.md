# The other side of crypto: a dive into options and futures

*By Jonny Fry | *

Crypto futures are standardised contracts obliging traders to buy or sell an asset at a set date and price, and options give the holder the right (but not the obligation) to buy or sell at a specified price. Think of them as insurance or a one‑sided bet on future price moves. Perpetual contracts are similar to futures with no expiry - they trade continuously and are maintained by periodic funding payments between longs and shorts so allowing constant leverage without settlement dates. On the other hand, futures have fixed-term contracts to trade Bitcoin, Ether, etc. at a future date, and are often used for hedging or speculation. Finally, option contracts grant an option to buy (calls) or sell (puts) at a strike price (a pre-agreed price in the future). Buyers pay a premium but are not obliged to buy or sell the security on which they have taken an option if the trade proves to be unprofitable. Major venues for these products that are linked to cryptocurrencies include Binance, Deribit, OKX and the regulated CME Group. And the scale is massive; for example, combined open interest on Binance and Deribit futures now exceeds tens of billions of dollars. Deribit alone was reported to hold [about $30 billion in futures and options](https://siliconangle.com/2025/05/08/coinbase-agrees-acquire-major-crypto-derivatives-platform-deribit-2-9b/#:~:text=Deribit%20holds%20a%20major%20position,Coinbase%20said%20in%20its%20announcement) open positions by early 2025. Such depth of liquidity (and leverage) underscores why derivatives dominate crypto trading volumes.

**Recent events shaping the derivatives market**

> · **spot Bitcoin ETFs** \- the January 2024 US approvals of multiple spot BTC ETFs ignited a derivatives boom. CME data shows Bitcoin futures [open interest hit an ](https://www.cmegroup.com/openmarkets/equity-index/2024/Bitcoin-Risk-Management-Moves-to-the-Fore.html#:~:text=Indeed%2C%20open%20interest%20in%20CME,the%20space%20beyond%20Bitcoin%20futures)_[all-time high](https://www.cmegroup.com/openmarkets/equity-index/2024/Bitcoin-Risk-Management-Moves-to-the-Fore.html#:~:text=Indeed%2C%20open%20interest%20in%20CME,the%20space%20beyond%20Bitcoin%20futures)_[ on 12 January](https://www.cmegroup.com/openmarkets/equity-index/2024/Bitcoin-Risk-Management-Moves-to-the-Fore.html#:~:text=Indeed%2C%20open%20interest%20in%20CME,the%20space%20beyond%20Bitcoin%20futures) (the very day 10 spot ETFs were approved). Trading volume spiked too, [roughly 66,000 BTC futures contracts](https://www.cmegroup.com/openmarkets/equity-index/2024/Bitcoin-Risk-Management-Moves-to-the-Fore.html#:~:text=Moreover%2C%20an%20average%20of%2066%2C000,according%20to%20CME%20Group%20data) changed hands daily that month (up 50% month-on-month). In practice, institutions have rushed to arbitrage between the new ETFs and futures and analysts note a surge in _cash-and-carry_ trades: buying actual Bitcoin or ETF shares and selling futures to lock in a profit. In short, ETF approvals drove record CME volumes and an influx of institutional hedging.
> 
> · **Ethereum Dencun upgrade** \- in March 2024, Ethereum’s “Dencun” upgrade (proto-danksharding) loomed large. Derivatives traders crowded into ETH futures and options with on-chain data showing Ethereum futures open interest hitting a new [$14 billion peak just ahead of Dencun (a 3% jump in 24h)](https://coingape.com/eth-price-set-for-5000-after-ethereum-dencun-upgrade-derivatives-data/#:~:text=The%20Ethereum%20futures%20OI%20hits,major%20buying%20from%20institutional%20investors). ETH options interest also exploded, with [total open interest reaching $14.8 billion](https://coingape.com/eth-price-set-for-5000-after-ethereum-dencun-upgrade-derivatives-data/#:~:text=In%20addition%2C%20Ethereum%20options%20OI%20has%20also%20hit%20a%20new%20all%2Dtime%20high%20of%20%2414.77%20billion.%20Call%20options%20open%20interest%20is%20at%2065.87%25%20and%20put%20options%20open%20interest%20is%20at%2034.13%25%2C%20supported%20by%20massive%20volumes%20for%20calls.). Notably, call options dominated the flow: [about 66% of ETH option OI were calls](https://coingape.com/eth-price-set-for-5000-after-ethereum-dencun-upgrade-derivatives-data/#:~:text=Deribit%20data%20shows%20a%20put,for%20%245000%20before%20March%2029) and tens of thousands of bullish calls were bought (e.g. 44.8K calls at $4,500 and 50.9K at $5,000 strikes before the upgrade). Traders were clearly positioned for a big rally around the hard fork.
> 
> · **Exchange adjustments** \- crypto venues have also been reducing the number of leverage products such as futures and options under regulatory pressure. Binance, for instance, has recently delisted some high-leverage perpetual contracts. In late 2024, it announced the [removal of USD-margined perps for tokens like NEM (XEM), Orbs (ORBS) and Loom (LOOM)](https://www.binance.com/en/square/post/16884293511866#:~:text=Binance%20announced%20the%20delisting%20of,reflecting%20market%20concerns). These tiny-coin markets were shut amid low liquidity and compliance reviews (prices promptly fell 5-7% on the news). Binance likewise continues to trim maximum leverage on certain perps and amend margin tiers to reduce volatility. Such moves show regulators effectively forcing exchanges to limit extreme leverage on crypto derivatives.
> 
> · **Bitcoin halving (April 2024)** \- the [fourth BTC halving on 20–21 April 2024](https://www.chainalysis.com/blog/bitcoin-halving-2024/#:~:text=In%20the%20months%20leading%20up,halving%20price%20surge) (cutting miner rewards from 6.25 to 3.125 BTC per block) was another volatility catalyst. Historically, markets _“price in”_ the supply shock well ahead of halvings, often driving big swings. Indeed, in 2024, Bitcoin climbed from the mid-$60K range into the $100K region in the months after the halving. Analysts observed that post-halving behaviour _[“reflects patterns observed after the April 2024 halving.”](https://cointelegraph.com/news/market-volatility-indicator-still-points-to-135-k-bitcoin-within-100-days-analyst)_ In practice, the mere expectation of reduced issuance had already ramped up futures open interest and trading ranges in the spring, exemplifying how protocol events feed into derivatives-driven volatility.

Meanwhile, many institutions obtain their exposure to crypto futures and options contracts on regulated exchanges such as CME, which is based in the US. As one [CME executive stated](https://www.cmegroup.com/openmarkets/equity-index/2024/Bitcoin-Risk-Management-Moves-to-the-Fore.html#:~:text=%E2%80%9CWe%20see%20regulation%20as%20a,and%20sophisticated%20investors%20to%20our): “ _We see regulation as a feature, not a bu_ g.” CFTC oversight attracts global institutional capital and with US ETFs available, sophisticated traders deploy cash-and-carry arbitrage, buying spot Bitcoin (or ETF shares) and selling futures to lock in yields. As a result, [basis trades](https://finimize.com/content/the-bitcoin-trade-you-can-copy-from-the-pros) have become a routine part of institutional desks. Meanwhile, the CME’s deep liquidity and surveillance makes it attractive for hedging, especially relative to unregulated crypto markets. On the other side, crypto-savvy retail investors tend to use pereptuals and leverage. Exchanges have been adding exotic perpetuals for meme-coins - e.g. in January 2024, Binance launched a WIF (Dogwifhat) perpetual with [up to 50x leverage](https://www.binance.com/en/support/announcement/detail/5c6cb594550247aea7c93a47c0156b17#:~:text=Binance%20Futures%20Will%20Launch%20USD%E2%93%88,With%20Up%20to%2050x%20Leverage). Social-media-fuelled traders have piled into these - for example, a recent report highlighted a single _“whale”_ placing a [$27.5 million 10x leveraged long on meme-coin PEPE](https://beincrypto.com/whale-pepe-leverage-on-hyperliquid/#:~:text=,makes%20the%20bet%20increasingly%20unstable) on the new Hyperliquid derivatives platform. That trade nearly blew up and attracted further liquidations. In general, retail traders push out risk by crowding into high-beta coins. Long memecoin bets and punting on alt futures keep funding rates buoyant on leverage - until a significant change in price wipes them out.

**Derivatives as price drivers (not merely reflections)**

Open interest and leverage can drive prices, not simply follow them. For instance, late January 2025 saw a vicious sell-off in Ethereum. A rapid drop to sub-$2,200 triggered [over $1 billion in long liquidations](https://www.galaxy.com/insights/perspectives/price-action-triggers-liquidation-cascade-slashing-funding-rates-and-wiping/#:~:text=Image%3A%20Ethereum%20) across crypto. Ethereum perpetual open interest plunged from [circa $5 billion to under $4 billion](https://www.galaxy.com/insights/perspectives/price-action-triggers-liquidation-cascade-slashing-funding-rates-and-wiping/#:~:text=Ethereum%27s%20open%20interest%20dropped%20sharply,in%20just%20a%20few%20days) in days. Those forced liquidations themselves pushed prices lower - a classic cascade effect of leveraged futures unwinding. In other words, when open interest is high, a marginal tilt in sentiment can snowball into outsized moves. Funding rates also impact sentiment. If perpetual funding is strongly positive (e.g. +5% annualised), it means long positions are paying shorts a bullish tilt. By contrast, when funding turns negative (longs _receive_ payment), it signals heavy shorting and caution. In January 2025, [BTC/ETH/SOL funding rates flipped negatively](https://www.galaxy.com/insights/perspectives/price-action-triggers-liquidation-cascade-slashing-funding-rates-and-wiping/#:~:text=January%20proved%20to%20be%20a,cash%20rates%20across%20the%20market) as prices fell. Conversely, earlier in the year, Solana perps briefly traded at [8% annualised funding](https://cointelegraph.com/news/solana-network-and-de-fi-activity-suggest-sol-price-rally-will-continue#:~:text=To%20gauge%20whether%20traders%20are,to%20keep%20their%20trades%20open), reflecting optimistic longs paying up. Thus, funding snapshots can be thought of as a mood gauge: extreme funding often foreshadows [mean-reversion](https://quantpedia.com/trend-following-and-mean-reversion-in-bitcoin/#:~:text=In%20other%20words%2C%20the%20trend,buy%20low%20and%20sell%20high.) when the cycle turns. Options and volatility plays add another dimension to pricing. Traders frequently use straddles/strangles (long call+put packages) around big events to profit from volatility rather than direction. We witnessed this before Dencun: ETH’s implied volatility rose sharply and open interest soared (nearly $14.8 billion total, with 66% in calls). Many buyers were clearly betting on a large move. In general, the options market can amplify moves - e.g. heavy put buying at a strike can set a soft price ceiling, whilst mass liquidations of short options can fuel volatility squeezes. In short, derivatives are not simply passive hedges, they frequently lead to accelerated price action through liquidations and volatility strategies.

**Regulatory scrutiny and global fragmentation**

Regulation is a central theme. In the US, derivatives exchanges such as CME operate under CFTC rules, giving institutions confidence in market integrity. By contrast, many crypto firms trade on offshore platforms (Bybit, Bitget, etc.) with lighter oversight. Coinbase itself framed its [$2.9 billion Deribit acquisition as a way to challenge](https://siliconangle.com/2025/05/08/coinbase-agrees-acquire-major-crypto-derivatives-platform-deribit-2-9b/#:~:text=Deribit%20holds%20a%20major%20position,Coinbase%20said%20in%20its%20announcement) _“offshore crypto exchange giants such as Binance and OKX.”_ The point? US authorities are keen to channel major flows into regulated venues whilst offshore platforms remain popular for unregulated leverage trading. One quirky consequence, however, is the Friday effect. In crypto, weekly options (and some futures) often expire on Fridays, so every week brings a large settlement event. These expiries can trigger outsized volatility into Friday’s close. For example, on 24 Dec 2024, nearly [146,000 BTC options](https://www.coindesk.com/markets/2024/12/24/record-14-b-bitcoin-options-expiry-looms-as-market-looks-highly-levered-up#:~:text=%2A%20Friday%27s%20expiry%20represents%2044,is%20more%20bearish%20for%20ETH) (notional $14 billion) expired on Deribit - about 44% of all BTC options open interest on the platform. Unsurprisingly, that expiry weekend saw wild price swings. Such ‘quadruple witching-style’ Fridays are now well-acknowledged in crypto markets. Regulators are also sending stern signals. In April 2025, the [SEC’s corp. finance division issued guidance on crypto securities offerings](https://www.sullcrom.com/insights/memo/2025/April/SEC-Staff-Issues-Statement-Regarding-Securities-Offerings-Crypto-Asset-Markets#:~:text=On%20April%2010%2C%202025%2C%20the,during%20the%20pendency%20of%20deliberations), emphasising that token issuances or lending programs could trigger full disclosure requirements. In practice, some firms have reportedly received SEC “warning letters” or requests to register in lieu of enforcement. Meanwhile, Europe’s MiCA regime has taken effect, imposing strict obligations on crypto exchanges and asset issuers. [MiCA aims to harmonise rules across the EU](https://iongroup.com/blog/markets/crypto-derivatives-a-comprehensive-guide/#:~:text=The%20EU%20has%20introduced%20a,assets%20Regulation%20%28MiCA), covering transparency, consumer protection and risk management. And, although MiCA does not explicitly cap leverage, it brings crypto platforms closer to traditional exchange standards (drawing on MiFID II precedent). All told, the patchwork of US, UK and EU rules means crypto derivatives are becoming increasingly fragmented by jurisdiction. Firms must navigate both CFTC-enforced platforms (e.g. CME, Eris) and offshore/DeFi venues, each with its own rules and leverage policies.

The next development looks to be on-chain derivatives and new volatility instruments. DeFi protocols are racing to offer trustless perps and options. For example, [Synthetix has deployed its ](https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20240124505362/en/Synthetix-Deploys-First-Major-Perpetuals-Protocol-on-Base-Mainnet-Marking-the-Completion-of-the-Synthetix-V3-Upgrade)_[Perps V3](https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20240124505362/en/Synthetix-Deploys-First-Major-Perpetuals-Protocol-on-Base-Mainnet-Marking-the-Completion-of-the-Synthetix-V3-Upgrade)_[ contracts on Coinbase’s Base network](https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20240124505362/en/Synthetix-Deploys-First-Major-Perpetuals-Protocol-on-Base-Mainnet-Marking-the-Completion-of-the-Synthetix-V3-Upgrade), becoming _“the first to introduce a major perpetuals product for crypto”_ on that Layer 2. Likewise, [GMX (a DeFi perp DEX on Arbitrum/Avalanche) recently launched GMX V2](https://www.globenewswire.com/news-release/2023/10/03/2753351/0/en/Decentralized-Perpetual-Exchange-GMX-Adds-Support-for-Bitget-Wallet.html), adding new markets (e.g. AAVE, ATOM, NEAR) and faster execution to its decentralised exchange. Perhaps most ambitiously, dYdX is rolling out its V4 protocol on its own Cosmos-based chain, aiming for fully decentralised perpetuals with on-chain order books. These developments blur the line between TradFi and DeFi derivatives, creating 24/7 infrastructure where collateral, execution and margin live on public ledgers. Into the bargain, a new class of volatility products is emerging; traders are treating volatility itself like an asset. One example is the [crypto volatility index (CVI) by COTI](https://cryptobriefing.com/what-is-the-cvi-how-was-it-developed-what-are-future-plans-for-the-product/#:~:text=CVI%20was%20developed%20and%20launched,the%20CVI%20board%20of%20advisors): a DeFi-native “crypto VIX” for ETH and BTC developed with Professor Dan Galai (creator of the VIX). CVI lets users hedge or speculate on 30-day implied volatility in a decentralised way. Exchanges and DeFi platforms also offer structured notes and volatility tokens (essentially straddles packaged as tokens) to meet this demand. Even traditional structured products desks are tailoring products around crypto volatility. In effect, volatility has become commodified in its own right.

In essence, derivatives are fast becoming the plumbing of the crypto market. From regulated futures and options on CME, to meme-coin perps on Binance and to automated perp markets on blockchains, these instruments help discover price, drive liquidity and amplify moves. The line between reflection and causation is thin: derivatives not only mirror trader expectations, but they can also amplify them. As the market matures, expect more innovation (and scrutiny) in this space, with volatility itself treated as a full-fledged asset class by savvy traders.
