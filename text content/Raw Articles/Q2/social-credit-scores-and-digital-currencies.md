# Social credit scores and digital currencies

*By Jonny Fry | *

The rapid digitisation of finance has paved the way for state-controlled digital currencies, with [central bank digital currencies (CBDCs)](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/central-bank-digital-currency-cbdc.asp#:~:text=CBDCs%20are%20government%2Dbacked%20digital%20currencies%20that%20use%20blockchain%20or,costs%20of%20current%20monetary%20systems.) emerging as a revolutionary financial tool. Simultaneously, the rise of social credit systems, initially popularised in China, has introduced a new layer of government oversight on individual citizens behaviour. As governments explore ways to integrate CBDCs with behavioural tracking mechanisms, questions surrounding privacy, civil liberties and financial autonomy are becoming more pressing than ever. Whilst China has pioneered this integration, other nations are also exploring similar frameworks. Indeed, not so long ago in Canada, President Justin Trudeau used emergency powers to [freeze the bank accounts of Canadian truckers](https://www.cato.org/blog/emergencies-act-after-two-years) who were blocking traffic in and out of the US. Therefore, could the future of finance be one where state-controlled digital money is directly tied to social behaviour and government-imposed metrics? And if so, what are the implications for personal freedoms and financial independence?

In essence, a social credit system is a framework designed to assess the trustworthiness of individuals and institutions based on their behaviours, financial history and compliance with legal and societal norms. Whilst most people associate social credit with China’s model, variations of behavioural scoring exist in several countries - particularly in areas such as creditworthiness, criminal records and digital reputation. Introduced in the 2010s, [China’s social credit system](https://velocityglobal.com/resources/blog/chinese-social-credit-system/) assigns scores to individuals and organisations based on behaviours such as debt repayment, legal compliance and even on-line speech. High scores bring rewards such as access to loans, priority in public services and travel benefits. Conversely, low scores can result in restrictions such as flight bans, reduced internet speeds and exclusion from certain jobs. So, whilst China’s model is the most extensive, elements of social credit-style assessments exist in other nations, including:

> · **the United States** \- private companies, such as credit agencies and social platforms, collect extensive data on individuals. Some insurance companies use behavioural data to set rates whilst employers increasingly use AI-driven reputation scores.
> 
> · **the European Union** \- the [GDPR](https://gdpr.eu/what-is-gdpr/) regulates data collection, but social scoring still exists in limited forms such as “digital footprints”, affecting job hiring and banking.
> 
> · **India** \- [the Aadhaar biometric ID system](https://www.uidai.gov.in/en/16-english-uk/aapka-aadhaar/14-what-is-aadhaar.html#:~:text=Aadhaar%20is%20a%2012%20digit%20individual%20identification%20number%20issued%20by,and%20address%2C%20anywhere%20in%20India.) is often linked to financial and social services, raising concerns over potential future expansions into social scoring.

Meanwhile, CBDCs represent state-issued digital currencies, designed to replace or complement physical cash. But, unlike cryptocurrencies such as Bitcoin, CBDCs are intended to be fully regulated and centralised. According to [Atlantic Council](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/cbdctracker/#:~:text=134%20countries%20%26%20currency%20unions%2C%20representing,advanced%20stages%20of%20CBDC%20exploration.): “ _134 countries & currency unions, representing 98% of global GDP, are exploring a CBDC. In May 2020 that number was only 35. Currently, 66 countries are in the advanced phase of exploration - development, pilot or launch. Every G20 country is exploring a CBDC, with 19 of them in the advanced stages of CBDC exploration.” _There are a number of benefits that CBDCs offer - they can provide banking services to unbanked populations and hence foster greater financial inclusion; citizens can use a digital currency simply by having access to the internet; digital payments are faster and less susceptible to counterfeiting and can function 24/7, as well as being able to be executed in a programmable manner therefore removing the need for intermediaries such as lawyers when purchasing real estate; governments can track transactions so reducing illicit activities, as opposed to cash which typically does not leave a trail for authorities to trace; and authorities can program CBDC with conditions such as expiration dates or usage restrictions.__ However, there are concerns and challenges that need to be addressed before widescale adoption of CBDCs is seen. There are grave concerns regarding privacy because transactions are traceable, so potentially eliminating financial anonymity and enabling the state to know what you are spending, where, and on what. Governments could potentially manipulate money flow, enforce spending restrictions or penalise dissidents, plus a centralised digital financial system could present a prime target for hackers. Equally, a government could manipulate CBDCs for economic or social engineering purposes - for example, low-credit individuals might receive devalued digital currency or be restricted from certain purchases.__ Furthermore, as digital currencies gain traction, their integration with behavioural tracking systems are being explored by several governments and this fusion could lead to state-controlled financial environments where spending power is directly linked to an individual’s compliance with state-mandated behaviour:

> · **China: the digital yuan and social credit system**
> 
> China has been testing its [digital yuan (e-CNY)](https://www.clydeco.com/en/insights/2024/09/digital-yuan-a-global-game-changer-in-the-era-of-c) since 2020. Whilst officials claim it is not explicitly linked to the social credit system, the infrastructure enables seamless integration. If merged, the digital yuan could become a tool for enforcing social compliance by restricting financial privileges based on an individual’s credit score.
> 
> · **Russia: digital ruble and political control**
> 
> Russia is developing its digital ruble, aiming for full implementation by 2025. Critics worry that, given the nation’s track record of suppressing dissent, a digital ruble linked to political behaviour could serve as a tool to limit financial access for opposition figures and activists.
> 
> · **European Union: privacy concerns and regulatory balancing**
> 
> The EU is designing its digital euro with a focus on privacy. However, European regulators are also considering ways to combat fraud and tax evasion, which could lead to partial monitoring of transactions. Whilst a full-fledged social credit system is unlikely, targeted behavioural tracking is possible.
> 
> · **United States: balancing innovation with liberties**
> 
> In the US, discussions around a digital dollar remain ongoing. Whilst officials emphasise privacy protections, the potential for integration with taxation, law enforcement and financial institutions raises concerns about increased government oversight. The Trump administration is anti-CBDC, but very keen to encourage stablecoins.

Meanwhile, other nations exploring social credit-like financial systems include India’s digital rupee which could link with Aadhaar ID for social welfare distribution potentially affecting financial privileges; Nigeria’s eNaira could be used to control monetary policy and ensure compliance with government directives; and, Brazil’s digital real which is expected to have programmable spending capabilities, possibly influencing social policies.

**Potential safeguards and solutions**

> · **decentralisation and privacy protections** -**** implementing privacy-enhancing technologies, such as zero-knowledge proofs, could enable financial transparency without compromising individual privacy.
> 
> · **independent oversight and regulation** \- creating independent agencies to oversee CBDCS could prevent government overreach. International organisations could also play a role in setting ethical guidelines.
> 
> · **opt-in social credit features** -**** rather than mandatory social credit scoring, individuals could opt into voluntary trust networks that enhance financial credibility without state control.
> 
> · **offline transactions and cash alternatives** \- ensuring that digital currencies have offline capabilities and preserving physical cash options would protect against full financial control.

Undoubtedly, the intersection of social credit systems and CBDCs presents a double-edged sword. Whilst the potential for increased financial inclusion and security is evident, the risks of surveillance, government overreach and political manipulation cannot be ignored - nations must tread carefully, implementing safeguards to protect civil liberties whilst embracing financial innovation. The future of finance should not be one where compliance dictates access to money, but rather a balanced system that upholds both security and personal freedoms. Surely nations need to question whether we a**re building the infrastructure for financial innovation, or the rails for digital authoritarianism.** If CBDCs are rolled out without privacy safeguards, regulatory oversight and offline options, the global monetary system could tilt toward surveillance and control. Hence, the question is not just _how_ CBDCs will be used - but _who_ gets to decide.
