# Tokenised Time

*By Jonny Fry | *

Consider a world where time, your most valuable asset, can be used like money. Imagine exchanging digital tokens for your talents, labour and service hours without banks or intermediaries. This vision has become reality. Blockchain and time-tokenisation could change economic engagement in rural areas across the world - they could create new prosperity by valuing their time and abilities. Globally, rural communities often struggle with limited access to banks or formal financial services, frequent cash shortages and unstable local currencies. Indeed, according to the [World Bank](https://digitalfinance.worldbank.org/), there are over 1.4billion people (many in rural areas) who are unbanked - yet 60% of the unbanked have access to the internet. Moreover, under-employment persists despite abundant skills and willing labour, and many rural residents are left dependent on external aid or volatile market forces. But, although many have talents such as carpentry, farming, teaching, sewing and caregiving, they often lack formal ways to monetise the skills consistently. And without bank accounts, credit histories or financial literacy, they remain trapped outside the economic mainstream and fall prey to the array of charlatans who exist when looking for credit of investment to grow their enterprises. Traditional solutions such as microfinance have helped somewhat, but these solutions can also introduce new problems such as high-interest debt cycles. What rural economies need is an alternative system that values people, not by their financial capital but by their time, creativity and work.

Time tokenisation involves representing units of human time, such as one hour of work, as a cryptographic digital asset on a blockchain. People earn tokens by providing services such as fixing a roof, teaching a child or helping farm a neighbour’s land and these tokens can then be spent to “purchase” other services within the community. This system itself shares similarities with [Time Banks,](https://www.timebanks.org/) a social movement where community members exchanged hours of work instead of money. The key difference is that blockchain technology brings security, transparency, portability and scalability to the model, making tokenised time systems viable because it ensures decentralisation, transparency, trust and security. Decentralisation means there is no need for a central authority to manage exchanges. Transparency comes from recording all transactions on an immutable ledger, trust is built because smart contracts automatically ensure fair exchanges without relying solely on personal relationships, and security is ensured because tokens cannot be forged or duplicated.

By tokenising time on a blockchain, rural communities can create local economies backed by human labour rather than cash. They can securely exchange services (even among strangers), store value in a form resistant to inflation or currency devaluation and empower individuals to monetise their skills without the need for formal employment. In practical terms, a tokenised time economy would begin with individuals listing the services they can offer - services might include tailoring, tutoring, plumbing or farming. This information would be recorded on a decentralised platform accessible through mobile phones or community hubs. For instance, when someone provides a service such as three hours of tutoring, three time tokens would be earnt. These tokens could then be spent to receive services from others, perhaps two hours from a plumber and one hour of gardening. Smart contracts would ensure fairness by automatically verifying service completion, potentially through peer confirmations or digital proofs such as signatures or photos, before transferring tokens. Additionally, communities could set up optional marketplaces where members list their services and bid for tasks based on available tokens. In this model, the economy circulates skills for skills - rather than cash for goods.

In addition, tokenised time economies could offer financial inclusion without the need for banks. There would be no credit checks, interest rates, or bank fees; anyone who can work can earn. During periods of economic downturns or cash shortages, communities could continue functioning and thriving based on mutual service exchange. Such systems would also recognise informal labour; tasks such as caregiving, housework and teaching, often undervalued in cash economies, would gain tangible worth. They would help retain local wealth instead of allowing money to leak out to external suppliers and, most importantly, tokenised time would empower individuals and preserve their dignity. People would feel valued for their contributions, regardless of their educational level, age or financial background. Consider a rural village in Kenya, Nepal or Brazil adopting a tokenised time system. Maria, a midwife, could earn tokens by helping deliver babies and use them to pay Joseph, a mechanic, for motorcycle repairs. Tunde, a young farmer, might earn tokens teaching elderly women how to use smartphones, which he then spends hiring extra hands during the harvest season. Aisha, a skilled seamstress, could accumulate tokens by making school uniforms, later exchanging them for building supplies from a local carpenter. The entire community would become an interconnected web of services and trust without needing a single dollar to change hands.

Blockchain might sound high-tech, but practical solutions tailored to rural realities already exist - lightweight, low-bandwidth blockchains such as [Celo](https://celo.org/), [Stellar](https://stellar.org/) or [Mina Protocol](https://minaprotocol.com/) can operate with minimal internet access. Mobile-first interfaces ensure that apps work on basic smartphones whilst offline solutions allow transactions to be recorded offline and later synced when connectivity becomes available. Community validators (trusted local members verifying transactions) can replace expensive computational proof-of-work since it is important that technology must serve the people, not overwhelm them. Notably, although this may still seem an emerging idea, there are existing models and experiments pointing the way forward. [TimeBanks USA](https://www.timebanks.org/) has shown that communities can successfully exchange time without money for decades, even without using blockchains. In Kenya, [the Sarafu Network by Grassroots Economics](https://www.grassrootseconomics.org/pages/sarafu-network) has developed blockchain-based community currencies, allowing rural Kenyans to trade goods and services without relying on national currency. Meanwhile, [Circles UBI](https://handbook.joincircles.net/docs/developers/whitepaper/), in Germany, has created personal blockchain-based currencies showing how decentralised currency systems can thrive without central banks. These examples certainly prove that alternative value systems are not only possible but practical and blockchain simply supercharges these efforts by making them more secure, scalable and efficient.

But, like any new system, [tokenised time economies](https://blockchain-today.medium.com/crypto-and-time-the-concept-of-tokenized-time-as-a-valuable-asset-5e63eded0d19) face challenges, including:

> · **trust** \- building trust is essential because new systems often meet scepticism, hence community education and small pilot programs showcasing successful trades can help build confidence.
> 
> · **skill imbalance** \- this might arise if too many people offer the same services or if some skills are more in demand than others. To address this, communities can encourage diversified skills training and adjust token values slightly for high-skill tasks if necessary.
> 
> · **service delivery** \- verifying that a service was delivered properly could also be an issue. Solutions include peer review systems, digital signatures and simple proof submissions such as photos or witness confirmations. Older or less tech-savvy individuals might struggle with blockchain apps, but this can be overcome by providing simplified mobile apps, local tech ambassadors to assist users and offline-first options.
> 
> · **scalability** \- could pose a challenge when moving from a single village to a regional network. A federated model, where local token economies link together whilst maintaining autonomy, can allow sustainable growth.

However, irrespective of these challenges, the potential is immense. Imagine villages linking up to trade skills regionally via time tokens; local governments could recognise time tokens as complementary currencies; development programs could fund skill training initiatives that reward time contributions rather than handing out cash; and, co-operative projects such as building clinics, irrigation systems or schools could be funded and organised through tokenised labour pools. This approach would empower rural people economically, rebuild social cohesion, reduce dependency on external aid and foster self-determined development. Tokenised time could become a new pillar of economic sovereignty for the world's most underserved populations. In a world dominated by traditional money systems, rural communities often find themselves marginalised, trapped by lack of access rather than lack of value - hence blockchain-powered tokenised time offers a profound alternative. It shifts the definition of wealth from how much cash one has to how much one can contribute, recognising skills, effort and humanity as true assets. By transforming hours of work into secure, tradable digital tokens, rural economies can thrive based on collaboration, mutual respect and local empowerment.

The technology to make this possible already exists. If you would like to find out how to be more involved in time banks, [Capital Frontiers](https://fastercapital.com/topics/how-to-get-involved-in-a-time-bank.html) has plenty of information and practical guides. What is needed now is vision, education and commitment to build a future with reduced poverty, because everyone's time is valued. In this brave new economy, your time is not merely precious - it becomes your currency.
