# Decentralised social media: new build or tweak existing platforms?

*By Jonny Fry | *

The push for decentralised social media has taken two distinct approaches. On one hand, native decentralised platforms such as [Mastodon](https://joinmastodon.org/), [Bluesky](https://bsky.app/) and [Steemit](https://steemit.com/) have emerged, built entirely on Web3 structures to offer alternatives to mainstream social media. On the other hand, established platforms have attempted to integrate Web3 elements into their existing frameworks. Twitter (now X) introduced Bitcoin tipping and NFT profile pictures, Meta briefly supported NFTs and attempted to launch the [Diem (formerly Libra)](https://edition.cnn.com/2022/02/01/tech/facebook-diem-association-dissolving/index.html) a digital currency, [Reddit introduced Ethereum-based Community Points and NFT avatars](https://decrypt.co/107021/reddit-to-build-community-points-on-arbitrums-new-ethereum-scaler), Telegram developed the [TON blockchain](https://www.ledgerinsights.com/report-telegram-reliant-on-crypto-revenues/) and [Discord considered Ethereum wallet integration](https://blockworks.co/news/discord-ceo-hints-at-crypto-wallet-integration-some-users-boycott).

Yet, despite these efforts, many of these initiatives either failed or faced strong resistance. [Meta](https://giecrypto.com/traditional-companies-quietly-abandon-web3-and-metaverse-projects/) and [Telegram struggled with regulatory pressures](https://www.coindesk.com/policy/2020/05/14/telegrams-ton-was-built-on-sand-its-failure-isnt-all-bad-for-crypto) whilst Reddit and [Discord encountered user backlash](https://techcrunch.com/2021/11/10/discord-nfts-crypto-jason-citron/). Nevertheless, the broader shift toward decentralisation continues to gain momentum. Reddit’s [co-founder, Alexis Ohanian, joined Project Liberty's](https://africa.businessinsider.com/news/reddit-cofounder-alexis-ohanian-says-he-wants-to-buy-tiktok-and-set-a-new-standard-in/xel1dmd) bid in 2025 to acquire TikTok's US operations. The company hopes to redesign TikTok around a blockchain-based technology, echoing the zeitgeist. Hence, it seems no matter the hurdles that present themselves, having a mainstream decentralised social media will be achieved, one way or the other. The present solution seems to be either by building from scratch or by modifying existing social media. Certainly, an understanding of the pitfalls that surround both should provide some insight into the future of decentralised social media - and the best way forward since new decentralised platforms often struggle to achieve mainstream adoption due to several barriers, including:

> · **network effects** \- platforms such as Mastodon and Bluesky lack the massive user bases that make centralised social media appealing.
> 
> · **user experience and onboarding** \- managing wallets, signing up for servers, handling private keys and dealing with tokens create friction that [discourages casual users](https://cointelegraph.com/news/friend-tech-decentralized-social-media-onboarding-problem&ved=2ahUKEwiIqKugxpOMAxUATkEAHQJfKooQFnoECFUQAQ&usg=AOvVaw1Z2g0VivFp-IUhamYubwgj). A simple Google sign-in could ease access, but that level of convenience often requires centralisation.
> 
> · **monetisation challenges** \- traditional social media thrives on adverts whilst decentralised platforms rely on token economies - an approach that can be volatile and unattractive to mainstream advertisers.

In view of this, unless decentralised networks provide seamless experiences and clear advantages over existing platforms, large-scale adoption remains unlikely. Re-vamping mainstream social media with blockchain has not been widely successful due to a variety of reasons. Many users see no compelling reason to switch to decentralised alternatives whether they are built from scratch or existing social media revamped, some blockchains continue to struggle with scaling in order to accommodate billions of users, and many Web3 integrations (such as NFT profile pictures) are seen as gimmicks rather than meaningful improvements. Nonetheless, hopefully TikTok’s future owners succeed where others have failed, albeit past failures suggesting that merely adding blockchain features is not sufficient enough. There are often conversations on why citizens need to own their data, and it could be argued that most are ambivalent about this. But it does ultimately affect everything else, however, for example, after TikTok's 2025 US ban over Chinese data concerns, many users wilfully migrated to [RedNote (Xiaohongshu)](https://www.xiaohongshu.com/explore) to make a point to the US government which had banned TikTok because of its concern over privacy. RedNote is another Chinese-owned platform aligned with state regulations, hence this shift revealed that engagement, entertainment and usability often outweigh data privacy fears therefore challenging assumptions about user priorities and raising questions about digital security trade-offs. It is not sufficient for corporations to strive to revamp existing social media platforms - the natives of these platforms must also be carried along on this development, otherwise we would probably witness a repeat of the Discord/Reddit backlashes. Of note, whilst this has been taking place, native decentralised social media platforms have been gaining numbers with [Bluesky reportedly now with over 25 million users](https://www.theverge.com/2024/12/13/24320334/bluesky-now-has-more-than-25-million-users) \- X (formerly known as Twitter) has been responsible for this and it shed some light on the landscape. Elon Musk's acquisition of Twitter in 2022 was initially formed by co-founder Jack Dorsey as a step toward decentralisation, but that vision never materialised. Instead, X remains tightly controlled, with erratic policies and algorithmic favouritism toward paid users. Frustrated by the instability, users are increasingly migrating to decentralised alternatives such as Mastodon, Bluesky and [Farcaster](https://www.farcaster.xyz/) \- platforms that offer the community governance and data autonomy once promised by Twitter, but which it failed to deliver. So, this migration proves that interest in decentralisation persists and also contrasts with the RedNote scenario. However, relying on events such as Musk’s blunders is not a sustainable strategy. Human behaviour can be nuanced, not black and white. Decentralised social media is fragmented, yet interoperability offers a solution. Bluesky and Mastodon are not the 'new Twitter,' but [their resemblance to the old one](https://m.economictimes.com/tech/technology/bluesky-isnt-the-new-twitter-but-its-resemblance-to-the-old-one-is-drawing-millions-of-new-users/articleshow/116035949.cms) could also be a contributing factor for the easy move, solving the UI/UX problem even though some continue to find decentralisation’s server system [confusing](https://www.buzzfeednews.com/article/katienotopoulos/mastodon-twitter-replacement). Meanwhile, federated networks such as [Mastodon’s ‘ActivityPub](https://docs.joinmastodon.org/spec/activitypub/)’ and [Bluesky’s ‘AT Protocol](https://docs.bsky.app/docs/advanced-guides/atproto)’ enable cross-platform interaction, countering the “network effect” that keeps users on centralised platforms. [Meta’s ‘Threads’ joining the Fediverse](https://engineering.fb.com/2024/03/21/networking-traffic/threads-has-entered-the-fediverse/) certainly signals a hybrid future but challenges remain, such as can federation scale without compromising core values? And, rather than competing in silos, decentralised platforms must organise themselves like the giants they seek to disrupt. Given these points, TikTok’s blockchain experiment presents a fascinating intersection between decentralisation and virality, raising crucial questions about data ownership, moderation and platform governance. So, whilst decentralisation promises greater user control, history has shown that adoption hinges on usability and incentives rather than ideology alone.

It appears that decentralised social media is gaining momentum - but is it gaining _meaning_? As native Web3 platforms such as Mastodon and Bluesky grow, and giants such as TikTok explore blockchain overhauls, we are left asking whether users really care about owning their data or do convenience and entertainment always win? Users jumped from TikTok to another Chinese-owned app after a data-privacy-driven ban, but why? Therefore, can decentralised platforms ever overcome the UX friction and network- effect moat of incumbents and, if mainstream platforms bolt on blockchain features, is that genuine innovation or merely decentralisation theatre? If native Web3 platforms cannot onboard the masses, they could be destined to remain digital communes on the fringe. So, could TikTok’s blockchain pivot finally bridge virality with decentralisation - or will it repeat the failures of Libra and TON? Ultimately, we could be building toward user empowerment, or we could simply be tweaking the same power structures with shinier tech…
