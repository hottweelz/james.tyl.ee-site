# Blockchain for digital preservation: saving humanity's ephemeral data

*By Jonny Fry | *

In an increasingly digital world, the vast and rapidly growing ocean of data we generate - from scientific breakthroughs and artistic creations to personal memories and historical records - is paradoxically fragile. [Unlike the tangible artifacts of the past, digital information is susceptible to degradation, corruption, obsolescence and deliberate alteration.](https://www.fountaindale.org/understanding-digital-decay/#:~:text=Mechanical%20failures%2C%20overheating%20or%20unexpected,confines%20of%20silicon%20and%20metal) The loss of this “ephemeral data” poses a significant threat to our collective memory whereby hindering scientific progress, distorting historical narratives and, ultimately, eroding our understanding of ourselves and our journey. Hence, the challenge lies in creating robust and trustworthy systems for digital preservation - systems that can guarantee the long-term integrity, accessibility and authenticity of digital assets. Traditional approaches, often relying on centralised institutions and fallible technologies, face inherent limitations. Single points of failure, the costs of continuous maintenance and migration and the potential for censorship or accidental data loss all contribute to the precariousness of our digital heritage. Amazon (AWS), Microsoft (Azure) and Google have a [63% share of the cloud infrastructure worldwide](https://www.cloudzero.com/blog/cloud-service-providers/#:~:text=Amazon%20Web%20Services%20\(AWS\):,market%20positions%20as%20of%202025:), all of which are US-based companies. Without doubt, the robustness of those businesses that are using cloud technology needs to be questioned.

Whilst blockchain technology is widely known for its use to power and issue cryptocurrencies, blockchain's immutability, decentralisation, transparency and cryptographic security provide a compelling paradigm shift for digital preservation; blockchains can strengthen, trust and preserve humanity's digital history by harnessing these qualities. But, before exploring blockchain's potential, it is crucial to understand the multifaceted threats facing [digital preservation](https://www.dpconline.org/handbook/organisational-activities/storage). One major concern is [Bit Rot and Data Degradation](https://www.datacore.com/glossary/bit-rot/), where physical storage media such as hard drives are not immune to decay over time, so corrupting data. This necessitates regular, costly migration to newer storage technologies which, itself, carries risks of data loss. Compounding this, [software and hardware obsolescence](https://www.dpworkshop.org/dpm-eng/oldmedia/obsolescence2.html) means digital files often become inaccessible as the specific software and hardware used to create and access them become outdated. The result is the incredibly difficult process of retrieving and interpreting old data, potentially an impossible task. Another significant vulnerability stems from “centralised points of failure” - [traditional digital archives often rely on single institutions managing centralised servers and databases,](https://www.dxtalks.com/blog/news-2/unveiling-the-difference-blockchain-vs-traditional-databases-529) leaving them susceptible to technical failures, cyberattacks, natural disasters or even institutional collapse which can result in catastrophic data loss. This centralisation also opens the door to “data tampering and censorship” since malicious actors or controlling entities can alter or delete data for personal gain, political manipulation or historical revisionism, whereby undermining the authenticity and trustworthiness of preserved information. Furthermore, a pervasive lack of trust and transparency often plagues centralised archival systems; their opacity means users have limited visibility into how their data is stored and protected, raising concerns about data integrity and potential misuse. [Finally, the sheer cost and sustainability of maintaining digital archives pose an ongoing challenge](https://www.oracle.com/big-data/what-is-big-data/) \- significant and continuous financial investment in infrastructure, personnel and technological upgrades is required, making long-term preservation efforts difficult to sustain.

Blockchain technology offers unique and strong digital preservation solutions that are more reliable than traditional approaches - immutability, the foundation of preservation, is its strength. Blockchains are append-only distributed ledgers, therefore changing or removing data without network consensus is nearly impossible. The intrinsic quality guarantees data integrity over time like no other and this implies that blockchain records or those with cryptographic hashes may be verified as original and un-tampered for digital preservation. Blockchain eliminates single points of failure through decentralisation with its networks spanning several, often geographically distant computers or nodes. The system is more robust to technological failures, cyberattacks and censorship due to its decentralised nature - if one node fails, the data is still accessible and safe on the others. For digital preservation, its dispersed structure assures that losing a single person or server will not erase important data. Transparency and verifiability are other strengths of the technology, so building trust; most blockchain networks are public, displaying all transactions. Although participants are pseudonymised, the data is auditable; cryptographic hashing and consensus procedures provide data integrity, allowing anybody to independently verify data integrity; transparency and verifiability boost digital preservation confidence; provenance and timestamping complement these characteristics with a blockchain's timestamp recording when data was added to each block (essential for digital asset authentication and provenance). A permanent audit trail may be created by storing metadata regarding a digital object's creation, ownership and alterations on the blockchain. The potential applications of blockchain technology for digital preservation are vast and span various domains, including:

> · **archiving scientific data** \- research data, including experimental results, datasets and publications, is often lost or becomes inaccessible over time. Blockchain can provide a secure and immutable repository for scientific data, ensuring its long-term availability and facilitating reproducibility. Initiatives such as [Scienceroot](https://scienceroot.com/) aim to use blockchain to create a decentralised platform for scientific collaboration and data preservation.
> 
> · **preserving historical records** \- from government documents and legal archives to personal letters and photographs, historical records are vital for understanding the past. Blockchain can offer a tamper-proof and accessible means of preserving these records, protecting them from loss, alteration or censorship. Companies such as [Mattereum](https://mattereum.com/) are exploring the use of blockchain for verifying and tracking the provenance of physical and digital historical artifacts.
> 
> · **safeguarding cultural heritage** \- museums, libraries and cultural institutions face the challenge of preserving digital representations of art, artifacts and cultural performances. Blockchain can provide a secure and transparent system for managing the ownership, authenticity and long-term preservation of digital cultural assets, potentially utilising non-fungible tokens (NFTs) for unique digital items.
> 
> · **personal data archiving** \- individuals are increasingly generating vast amounts of digital data, including photos, videos, messages and personal documents. Blockchain-based solutions could empower individuals to securely store and preserve their personal digital heritage, granting them control over their data and ensuring its longevity. Platforms such as Kleros offer decentralised dispute resolution which could be relevant for verifying the authenticity of personal archives.
> 
> · **intellectual property protection** \- creators of digital content, such as artists, musicians and writers, can use blockchain to timestamp and register their work, establishing clear proof of ownership and provenance. This can aid in protecting their intellectual property rights and tracking the usage of their creations over time.  
>   
> 

Conversely, however, whilst blockchain technology offers significant promise for digital preservation, several challenges and considerations need to be addressed for its widespread adoption, including:

> · **scalability** \- some blockchain networks have limitations in terms of the volume of data they can process and store. Preserving large datasets, such as entire scientific archives or national historical records, may require more scalable blockchain solutions or hybrid approaches.
> 
> · **cost of storage** \- whilst the blockchain itself might primarily store metadata or cryptographic hashes of the actual data, the cost of storing the original digital files in a secure and redundant manner still needs to be factored in. [Decentralised storage solutions such as IPFS (InterPlanetary File System)](https://coinmetro.com/learning-lab/decentralized-file-storage-solutions) can complement blockchain by providing content-addressed storage.
> 
> · **data governance and access control** \- determining who has the authority to add data to a preservation blockchain and who can access the preserved information are crucial governance considerations. Different use cases may require different access control mechanisms.
> 
> · **long-term sustainability of blockchain networks** \- the longevity and stability of the underlying blockchain network are essential for long-term digital preservation. Factors such as the network's governance model, community support and economic incentives need to be carefully considered.
> 
> · **legal and regulatory frameworks** \- the legal and regulatory implications of using blockchain for digital preservation are still evolving. Issues related to data ownership, legal admissibility of blockchain-recorded evidence and cross-jurisdictional compatibility need to be addressed.
> 
> · **interoperability and standards** \- ensuring interoperability between different blockchain-based preservation systems and establishing common standards for metadata and data formats will be crucial for the widespread adoption and usability of these solutions.
> 
> · **energy consumption** \- some blockchain technologies, particularly those using proof-of-work consensus mechanisms, can have significant energy consumption. More energy-efficient consensus mechanisms and sustainable blockchain solutions are needed for environmentally responsible digital preservation.  
>   
> 

Meanwhile, as society hurtles deeper into the digital era, a silent crisis looms: the gradual erosion of our collective memory. Unlike books or stone tablets, digital records - from scientific breakthroughs to cultural heritage - are vulnerable to decay, manipulation and institutional collapse. Cloud servers can fail, code becomes obsolete and power shifts erase inconvenient truths. Enter blockchain, not as hype but as **a durable, decentralised ledger of human experience.** Its immutability resists tampering, its decentralisation guards against single points of failure and its transparency fosters trust where centralised systems falter.

Imagine a future where historical records, art, scientific data and even personal legacies are preserved on distributed networks immune to censorship, war or decay. Blockchain becomes not merely a ledger but a **cultural insurance policy** , encoding memory in consensus and timestamping truth before it can be rewritten. However, it does require energy on an on-going basis to preserve this digital record - unlike a physical record which, provided it is stored in the correct manner, once created, does not produce a carbon footprint for ever. Yet this vision is not guaranteed. Challenges such as interoperability, governance, cost and sustainability loom large. But so does the urgency: **as our world digitises, what we fail to preserve may be lost forever**.**** In the end, blockchain offers more than storage - it offers the possibility of **a trust less memory infrastructure** for a trust-deficient world one where history cannot be hacked. And truth has a timestamp…
