# Digital asset colonialism: a financial revolution or a new form of economic domination?

*By Jonny Fry | *

The rise of digital currencies has sparked intense debate over whether they are truly democratising finance or merely reinforcing existing economic hierarchies - a phenomenon often referred to as [crypto colonialism](https://muse.jhu.edu/article/39112/summary#:~:text=While%20much%20recent%20literature%20has,cases%2C%20while%20geographically...). On one hand, decentralised cryptocurrencies offer financial access to unbanked populations, bypassing traditional financial gatekeepers and challenging the dominance of Western-controlled monetary systems. Digital assets, such as Bitcoin, stablecoins and decentralised finance (DeFi), enable cross-border transactions without reliance on intermediaries and clearing houses and are outside the influence of the International Monetary Fund) (IMF) or World Bank (WB) whereby empowering users in emerging economies to control their wealth.

Advocates argue that digital assets create new opportunities for financial inclusion. According to the World Bank, [nearly 1.4 billion adults remain unbanked worldwide](https://www.worldbank.org/en/topic/financialinclusion/overview#:~:text=The%20expansion%20of%20digital%20financial,owning%20an%20account%20by%202021.), with many residing in developing nations. Digital assets, particularly stablecoins, offer an alternative to volatile local currencies which, in some countries, depreciate because of economic mismanagement by their governments causing high inflation rates. Stablecoins allow people to save, send and receive money without a needing to have a traditional bank account and store money in less volatile fiat currencies. In regions with unstable banking systems, Bitcoin and other digital currencies serve as a financial lifeline, protecting wealth from hyperinflation and capital controls. In Venezuela, for example, [hyperinflation reached at 686.4% in 2021](https://www.investing.com/news/economy/venezuelas-inflation-hit-6864-in-2021--central-bank-2733884?), rendering the national currency almost worthless. As a result, [many citizens have turned to Bitcoin and stablecoins](https://cryptoforinnovation.org/crypto-usage-grows-in-venezuela-despite-government-mismanagement/) to preserve their savings and conduct everyday transactions. Similarly, cryptos have surged: between July 2021 and June 2022, **sub-Saharan Africa** saw [$100.6 billion in crypto transactions](https://www.chainalysis.com/blog/sub-saharan-africa-cryptocurrency-geography-report-2022-preview/) and **MENA** received [$566 billion](https://cryptoslate.com/emerging-markets-dominate-chainalysis-2022-geography-of-cryptocurrency-report/), whilst **Central & Southern Asia and Oceania (CSAO)** led with over [$750 billion](https://www.chainalysis.com/blog/central-southern-asia-crypto-adoption-2024/#:~:text=Similar%20to%20last%20year%2C%20Central,studied%2C%20at%20approximately%20$157.1%20billion.) between July 2023 and June 2024. Yet, despite the decentralised ethos of blockchain technology, the reality is that much of the cryptocurrency industry remains dominated by Western venture capital, centralised exchanges and regulatory frameworks shaped by major economies. The largest crypto firms - such as Binance, Coinbase and [FTX (before its collapse)](https://www.investopedia.com/what-went-wrong-with-ftx-6828447) \- have dictated much of the market’s direction, often reinforcing existing financial power structures. Moreover, many blockchain networks rely on infrastructure developed and maintained by Western tech firms, further entrenching economic dependencies - a prime example is the dominance of dollar-pegged stablecoins such as [Tether (USDT)](https://tether.to/) and [USD Coin (USDC)](https://www.circle.com/en/usdc). And, whilst these assets offer stability compared to volatile local currencies, they also reinforce the global supremacy of the US dollar - [over 90% of all stablecoin transactions are in USD-backed assets](https://www.bis.org/publ/work1219.pdf), so raising concerns about whether digital assets are truly decentralising finance or simply extending the reach of existing financial hegemons.

Certainly, cryptocurrencies offer financial independence, but at a cost: extreme volatility. So, whilst Bitcoin is often praised as a hedge against inflation, its price fluctuations offer little comfort for those who rely on it as a store of value. As [iShares reminds us](https://www.ishares.com/us/insights/bitcoin-volatility-trends#:~:text=Bitcoin%20has%20exhibited%20strong%20performance,gold%20and%20global%20equities%2C%20respectively.): _“Bitcoin's volatility remains elevated at 3.9 and 4.6 times that of gold and global equities, respectively.”_ The [2022 crypto crash](https://www.weforum.org/stories/2023/04/2022-was-a-hard-year-for-cryptocurrencies-but-it-may-have-been-just-what-the-industry-needed/) wiped out over $2 trillion in market value, disproportionately affecting retail investors in developing nations who bought in at high prices. Additionally, the rise of speculative trading and yield farming in DeFi platforms has led to new forms of economic exploitation. Many DeFi projects promise high returns, but their sustainability is often questionable, leading to [rug pulls](https://www.coinbase.com/en-gb/learn/tips-and-tutorials/what-is-a-rug-pull-and-how-to-avoid-it) and financial losses for uninformed investors. In some cases, developing nations have become testing grounds for unregulated financial experiments, with little recourse for those who suffer losses. Another contentious issue is the environmental impact of proof-of-work (PoW) mining which powers Bitcoin and many other cryptocurrencies. Bitcoin mining is notoriously energy-intensive, with some estimates suggesting it [consumes around 121 terawatt-hours (TWh) per year - more than the whole of Argentina](https://www.bbc.com/news/technology-56012952). This therefore raises ethical concerns about whether digital assets are truly a sustainable alternative to traditional finance, although, according to [Bitcoin ESG Forecast, over 54%](https://forkast.news/bitcoin-minings-green-mile-54-5-sustainable-energy-use/#google_vignette) of the energy used by Bitcoin miners is now from renewable energy sources. Furthermore, other blockchains have attempted to address this issue by adopting more energy-efficient consensus mechanisms such as proof-of-stake (PoS), used by Ethereum after its [Merge upgrade](https://ethereum.org/en/upgrades/merge/) \- this transition [reduced Ethereum’s energy consumption by 99.95%](https://www.ey.com/en_ch/insights/technology/how-does-the-ethereum-merge-help-the-real-and-virtual-world-save-energy#:~:text=By%20eliminating%20the%20need%20for,has%20reduced%20this%20amount%20significantly.&text=Tests%20run%20on%20the%20Beacon,than%20proof%2Dof%2Dwork.), marking a significant shift toward sustainability. Meanwhile, regulatory uncertainty continues to shape the global crypto landscape - whilst some governments embrace digital assets, others impose strict regulations or outright bans. China, for example, has cracked down on cryptocurrency trading and mining, whilst countries such as El Salvador have [adopted Bitcoin as legal tender](https://www.nber.org/digest/202207/el-salvadors-experiment-bitcoin-legal-tender) whereby sparking debates about financial sovereignty. Western regulatory bodies, particularly in the US and EU, exert significant influence over the industry with the implementation of policies such the Markets in Crypto-Assets (MiCA) framework in Europe and SEC enforcement actions in the US shaping how digital assets are used globally. And, whilst regulation is necessary to prevent fraud and protect consumers, it often benefits established financial institutions whilst creating barriers for smaller players in emerging markets.

Essentially,**** the question of whether digital assets democratise finance or perpetuate economic hierarchies remains open-ended. On one hand, cryptocurrencies empower individuals by providing financial alternatives outside traditional banking systems but, on the other, the industry’s dependence on Western capital, infrastructure and regulatory frameworks suggests that economic dependencies are merely being reshaped rather than eliminated. Ultimately, the impact of digital assets will depend on how they are developed, regulated and adopted in the coming years. If true financial decentralisation is to be achieved, more inclusive governance structures, better education and sustainable economic models will be necessary to ensure that blockchain technology benefits all rather than reinforcing existing inequalities.
