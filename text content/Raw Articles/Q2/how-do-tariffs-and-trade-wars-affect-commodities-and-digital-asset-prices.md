# How do tariffs and trade wars affect commodities and digital asset prices?

*By Jonny Fry | *

Tariffs are taxes imposed by governments on imported goods and services. They are typically used to protect domestic industries, reduce trade deficits, or as leverage in political negotiations. When multiple countries retaliate by imposing tariffs on each other’s goods, a trade war ensues. Trade wars disrupt global supply chains, increase the cost of goods and slow economic growth, creating widespread uncertainty across markets. Historically, tariffs and trade wars have impacted traditional asset classes (stocks, bonds and commodities) but their influence now extends to emerging asset classes such as cryptocurrencies and other digital assets due to globalisation and the digitisation of finance. As noted by [the House of Lords Library](https://lordslibrary.parliament.uk/trump-and-us-tariffs-what-might-the-impact-be-on-the-uk/#:~:text=One%20criticism%20of%20tariffs%20is,incentive%20to%20become%20more%20productive.): _“One criticism of tariffs is that they raise prices for consumers and firms buying goods from abroad, so fuelling inflation. Another is that tariffs mean domestic firms face less competition from imports, reducing their incentive to become more productive.”_ At the same time, higher production costs and retaliatory tariffs can reduce international trade volume, so hampering GDP growth. Hence, in response to rising inflation, central banks may raise interest rates to stabilise the economy. Moreover, businesses and consumers may delay investment and consumption decisions amid uncertainty, favouring safer assets and so resulting in increased risk aversion. Finally, trade wars can destabilise fiat currencies, particularly if one country’s exports are severely impaired, creating currency volatility. These factors collectively influence the behaviour of both traditional and digital assets, albeit in complex and sometimes contradictory ways.

Meanwhile, risk assets are investments that offer a higher potential return in exchange for higher risk. Cryptocurrencies, NFTs and other digital assets fall squarely into this category due to their volatility and speculative nature. When tariffs spur inflation and prompt higher interest rates, the attractiveness of yield-bearing assets (such as bonds) increases, reducing the appeal of volatile, non-yielding assets such as cryptocurrencies. Higher interest rates discourage speculative investment and result in a rotation away from cryptocurrencies and other digital assets. Furthermore, rising borrowing costs drain liquidity from the markets, curbing essential inflows into high-risk sectors. Cryptocurrencies also experience heightened price volatility during economic policy shifts as investor sentiment swings sharply in response to rate hikes or inflation fears. In this way, tariffs and trade wars can act as indirect brakes on the price growth of cryptocurrencies and digital assets by tightening economic conditions.

Therefore, whilst tariffs and trade wars can initially suppress demand for risk assets, prolonged trade wars may lead to long-term systemic shifts that _benefit_ cryptocurrencies. If trade wars cause chronic inflation, deficits and currency devaluation, individuals and institutions may seek alternative stores of value. Bitcoin and other cryptocurrencies that are decentralised and not controlled by any single nation-state make them attractive hedges against national economic mismanagement. Their limited supply, such as Bitcoin's 21 million coin cap, contrasts sharply with fiat currencies that central banks can expand at will. Additionally, cryptocurrencies offer global accessibility, allowing people worldwide to seek refuge from localised economic turmoil. Historical examples, such as Bitcoin’s rising adoption in Venezuela and Argentina amid hyperinflation, highlight how cryptocurrencies can act as hedges against fiat instability. Thus, over extended periods of economic stress caused by trade wars, demand for cryptocurrencies could increase as investors seek to diversify away from traditional assets. Meanwhile, commodities are among the first asset classes affected by tariffs and trade wars. Safe-haven commodities, such as gold, often benefit during times of economic uncertainty and periods of high inflation as investors seek a reliable store of value. Gold prices tend to rise as risk appetite diminishes during trade wars. Conversely, industrial commodities such as oil, copper and aluminium often suffer, as trade wars typically depress global manufacturing growth and reduce demand for industrial inputs. If tariffs make intermediate goods more expensive, production slows, so reducing the need for raw materials. Agricultural commodities are also highly sensitive. During the US-China trade war, China imposed retaliatory tariffs on US soybeans, causing a significant collapse in American agricultural exports and price declines. Therefore, commodities react differently depending on their characteristics: safe havens thrive whilst industrial and agricultural goods often face downturns.

Beyond cryptocurrencies, the broader digital asset ecosystem including NFTs, stablecoins and tokenised assets is also influenced by economic conditions stemming from trade wars. Highly speculative assets such as NFTs rely heavily on disposable income and enthusiastic investment sentiment. Tariff-induced recessions (by reducing consumer and investor confidence) can dramatically lower participation in NFT markets. Higher interest rates further reduce speculative appetite, likely leading to lower valuations and sales volumes. On the other hand, stablecoins pegged to strong fiat currencies (e.g. USD-pegged stablecoins) may experience increased usage during periods of economic turmoil, particularly if local currencies weaken. However, governments may also increase regulatory scrutiny on stablecoins to maintain control over monetary systems during economic crises. Meanwhile, tokenised real-world assets, such as digital representations of gold or real estate, might find favour as investors seek tangible, inflation-resistant stores of value. For instance, tokenised gold could benefit from the same safe haven dynamics that physical gold enjoys during periods of uncertainty. Thus, whilst the speculative sectors of the digital asset world may contract, assets tied to stability and real-world value could see growing demand.

A key determinant of how digital assets and commodities respond to trade wars is the policy response from governments and central banks. If trade wars trigger recessions, governments may respond with massive fiscal and monetary stimulus, injecting liquidity into the system. Based on the chart above it appears that China has already begun to stimulate its economy by pumping trillions into its money supply. Such actions, especially when combined with low interest rates and money printing, can fuel inflation and benefit cryptocurrencies and commodities as investors seek hedges against currency debasement. Conversely, protectionist measures and economic nationalism may accelerate de-dollarisation efforts, as countries look to reduce their reliance on the USD; decentralised cryptocurrencies could indirectly benefit from this global monetary realignment. However, policymakers could also impose stricter regulations on cryptocurrencies in an effort to control capital flows during periods of heightened instability and such regulatory actions could temporarily suppress crypto prices despite broader supportive macroeconomic trends. Hence, market outcomes depend not merely on tariffs and trade wars themselves, but also on how policymakers react to the resulting economic stresses.

The US-China trade war provides a practical case study of these dynamics. As mentioned previously, commodities such as soybeans suffered dramatically as China imposed retaliatory tariffs on US agricultural exports, leading to price declines. Industrial metals such as copper and aluminium also experienced significant weakness due to fears of slowing global demand but gold, as expected, performed well during this period, benefiting from increased investor uncertainty. Meanwhile, Bitcoin and other cryptocurrencies showed increased volatility, with price spikes often coinciding with moments of heightened trade tensions, suggesting that some investors turned to digital assets as alternative stores of value during periods of instability. Overall, the trade war illustrated that financial markets are complex ecosystems where multiple factors (including economic policy, market sentiment and technological change) interact in shaping asset prices. Tariffs and trade wars exert a powerful indirect influence on the financial market, affecting commodities and digital assets by altering the broader economic environment. In the short term, tariffs increase inflationary pressure, prompt higher interest rates and suppress demand for riskier assets such as cryptocurrencies and NFTs. Commodities react differently depending on their nature - safe haven assets such as gold tend to thrive, whilst industrial commodities suffer under reduced global demand. In the long term, however, prolonged trade wars may undermine confidence in fiat currencies and traditional financial systems, boosting demand for decentralised digital assets as hedges against currency devaluation and systemic risk. Certainly, understanding these intricate dynamics is essential for investors navigating an increasingly volatile and interconnected financial world.
