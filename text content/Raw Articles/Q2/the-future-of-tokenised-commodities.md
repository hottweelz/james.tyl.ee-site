# The future of tokenised commodities

*By Jonny Fry | *

Imagine owning part of a gold bar in a high-security vault halfway around the world, or a barrel of oil that can be swapped or traded immediately with a push of a key on a computer. A blockchain-based digital token lets you verify the asset's existence, authenticate your ownership and utilise it in financial transactions without you needing to worry about seeing, storing or insuring it. Commodity tokenisation converts tangible goods into digital representations that may be stored, transferred and traded with anyone in almost real time. Technically, tokenisation creates a digital token for an item such as gold, oil or agricultural commodities. Each blockchain-based token is backed by a physical object and has the transparency, speed and programmability of decentralised finance. Take [PAXG](https://www.withtap.com/gr/blog/what-is-pax-gold-paxg), for instance. This token represents one Troy ounce of gold, stored in an independent vault. When you hold a PAXG token, you legally own a slice of the physical gold itself and you are free to trade it globally, 24/7, without the need for brokers or banks. Tokenising commodities digitises assets and opens new opportunities to invest in and engage with global economic building blocks and for investors, it means eliminating friction and accessing previously unattainable markets. When asked, Paul Taylor, who is launching Token X (a MICA Compliant SRT stablecoin backed by a portfolio of European Critical Mineral Assets), said: “ _The traditional funding mechanisms for critical resources are broken - junior listed markets are starved of liquidity, leaving vital projects stranded. MiCA offers a rare breath of progress: a clear, forward-thinking framework that unlocks new capital flows through digital assets, finally giving essential ventures a credible path to funding_.” Taylor further added that: “ _Critical minerals are at the heart of the global power struggle. As geopolitical tensions rise and supply chains fragment, access to these essential resources is no longer just an economic issue - it’s a matter of national security.”_

But tokenised commodities are doing more than digitising physical assets - they are unlocking a new financial ecosystem where participation is broader, faster and more transparent. Historically, commodities such as gold, nickel or oil have been difficult to access for average investors - tokenisation changes that by allowing[fractional ownership](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/f/fractionalownership.asp), enabling someone to own 0.001 of a gold bar or a part of a grain shipment. This democratises access and brings new participants into traditionally closed markets.**** Commodity trading is no longer restricted to five days a week and hours dictated by different exchanges and so, with tokenised assets powered by blockchains, investors can trade anytime, anywhere, whereby meeting the demands of a global investor base. Tokens can be traded on digital marketplaces with fewer barriers and faster settlement times. This boosts liquidity, making it easier for investors to enter or exit positions compared to traditional commodity contracts or physical asset transactions.**** And because very token movement is recorded on a blockchain, transparency is improved offering a clear, immutable trail of ownership and transaction history. For commodities, where trust and traceability are crucial, this level of transparency is a major advantage and helps to increase confidence in the integrity of trading. Finally, by eliminating the need for brokers, custodians and clearing agents, tokenised commodity platforms reduce transaction costs and complexity. Essentially, smart contracts automate trust and enforcement whereby making processes faster and more cost-effective.

Tokenised commodities are no longer merely an emerging concept, they are ‘live’ and gaining traction across industries. From gold vaults to energy markets to agricultural supply chains, real-world examples are showing just how powerful tokenisation can be. Tokens such as PAX Gold (PAXG) and [Tether Gold (XAUT)](https://academy.yellowcard.io/topics/what-is-tether-gold-xaut/) represent physical gold bars stored in secure vaults, each backed 1:1 by a tangible asset. These tokens allow investors to tap into the value of gold without the logistical headaches of storage or transport, offering both the security of a traditional hedge and the fluidity of a digital asset. Energy markets are embracing tokenisation to streamline and decentralise trading, such as[ Sunified](https://www.sunified.com/) which is looking to issue tokens backed by the production of solar energy and carbon credits. Enabling fractional ownership and more agile trading strategies. with smart contracts automating settlement between producers and buyers in real time. Food and crop markets are also moving over to being digital thanks to companies such as [Agridex](https://thepaypers.com/payments-general/agridex-introduces-the-all-in-one-agricultural-payment-solution--1273191) which is tokenising wine, olive oil, cocoa, coffee and livestock, therefore giving farmers quicker access to capital and creating transparent, traceable supply chains. Tokens can represent both physical inventories and forward contracts, making them valuable tools for both producers and speculators; financial giants such as JPMorgan and Goldman Sachs are experimenting with blockchain for commodity settlement too. Meanwhile, startups and consortiums are testing tokenised grain exchanges, oil-backed [stablecoins](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/stablecoin.asp) and DeFi integrations - all paving the way for broader adoption. So, how do physical commodities such as gold bars or barrels of oil become digital tokens you can trade online? It all comes down to asset backing, blockchain infrastructure and smart contracts working behind the scenes:

> · **asset verification and custody -** before anything becomes tokenised, the physical asset must be authenticated and stored by a trusted custodian, whether it is a vault for gold, a silo for wheat or a tank for oil. This ensures the token truly represents something tangible.
> 
> · **issuance of digital tokens** \- once**** verified, a digital token is minted on a blockchain. Each token is typically pegged 1:1 to a specific quantity of the commodity (e.g., 1 token = 1 gram of gold). The token is often created by a regulated issuer who guarantees the asset is held in reserve.
> 
> · **blockchain and smart contract integration** \- smart contracts handle everything from issuing tokens to enabling peer-to-peer trading, enforcing rules without intermediaries. They also make ownership transfers transparent, secure and irreversible.
> 
> · **trading and settlement** \- once issued, the tokens can be traded on exchanges or integrated into decentralised finance (DeFi) platforms. Investors can buy, sell or use them as collateral, with settlements happening virtually instantly compared to traditional commodity markets.

Without doubt, tokenised commodities are opening the doors to a broader, more inclusive investment ecosystem. Whether you are a retail investor looking for inflation protection or an institutional player diversifying your portfolio, the opportunities are multiplying. Traditionally, investing in commodities required navigating futures markets or working with brokers, often out of reach for smaller investors but tokenisation changes that. Now, anyone with a digital wallet can gain exposure to gold, oil or even agricultural products through fractionalised, blockchain-based tokens. Furthermore, tokenised commodities make it easier to build a more diversified portfolio. These tokens can sit alongside crypto, equities or DeFi positions, without requiring storage, transport or complex settlement. And unlike[ ETFs](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/e/etf.asp) or [futures](https://www.ig.com/en/futures-trading/what-are-futures-how-do-you-trade-them), tokenised commodities offer direct, often asset-backed exposure with faster settlement, 24/7 trading and fewer intermediaries. Hence, whilst ETFs are still powerful tools, tokens unlock real-time flexibility and global access; commodities such as precious metals and gems such as diamonds have long been a safe haven during market turbulence. Tokenised versions carry the same fundamental properties, whilst giving investors a modern, digital channel to access them - whether through [centralised exchanges](https://www.investopedia.com/tech/what-are-centralized-cryptocurrency-exchanges/) or DeFi protocols.

However, irrespective of all the above, tokenised commodities are difficult to adopt and although there are legal and technological hurdles, regulation in many jurisdictions is unclear; many tokenised asset initiatives are uncertain concerning their legal status since they combine securities and commodities. Custody of tokenised assets of any kind is another challenge. Currently, the amount of trading in tokenised commodities is low so liquidity can be challenging because traditional investors need to learn to use digital wallets and new digital marketplaces. Finally, security can be problematic as smart contracts used by tokenised commodities may be hacked. So, to mitigate risk, projects use third-party audits, bug bounties and open-source development to ensure code quality before release. Nonetheless, tokenised commodities are quickly moving from niche innovation to real-world application. As regulations take shape and platforms mature, these digital assets are becoming a key part of the evolving financial landscape. From tokenised gold in DeFi to blockchain-backed oil trading pilots, the use cases are growing. What started as a way to fractionalise assets is becoming a blueprint for modernising global trade. In the coming years, expect smoother integration between traditional markets and blockchain networks, making the trading of tokenised commodities more accessible, liquid and programmable. The shift is clear: we are heading toward a future where ownership is not merely physical - it is digital, transparent and borderless.
