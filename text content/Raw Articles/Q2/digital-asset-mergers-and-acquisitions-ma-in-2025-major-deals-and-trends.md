# Digital asset mergers and acquisitions (M&A) in 2025: major deals and trends

*By Jonny Fry | *

Crypto firms opened 2025 with a burst of dealmaking, therefore reflecting a push for strategic growth and cross-sector expansion. In May, [Coinbase announced a $2.9 billion acquisition of Deribit](https://www.reuters.com/markets/deals/coinbase-acquire-deribit-29-billion-deal-wsj-reports-2025-05-08/#:~:text=May%208%20%28Reuters%29%20,into%20the%20crypto%20options%20markets), gaining a major foothold in crypto options and futures markets, particularly outside the US. A few months earlier, [Kraken agreed to buy NinjaTrader for $1.5 billion](https://blog.kraken.com/news/kraken-to-acquire-ninjatrader#:~:text=We%20are%20excited%20to%20announce,to%20certain%20purchase%20price%20adjustments), boosting its presence in regulated futures and paving the way into equities and payments. [Ripple followed in April with a $1.25 billion deal to acquire Hidden Road](https://www.reuters.com/markets/deals/crypto-firm-ripple-buy-prime-broker-hidden-road-125-billion-2025-04-08/#:~:text=April%208%20%28Reuters%29%20,acquisitions%20by%20the%20crypto%20company), aiming to become the first crypto firm to own a global prime brokerage, and with the move enhancing its institutional services and strengthening the use case for its XRP-linked stablecoin. Meanwhile, [MGX, an Abu Dhabi-based AI fund, committed $2.0 billion to acquire a stake in Binance](https://www.ledgerinsights.com/abu-dhabis-state-linked-mgx-invests-2-billion-in-binance-crypto-exchange/), settling the deal in stablecoins; the investment deepens Binance’s ties with the UAE, a growing digital asset hub. In mid-2024, on the AI front, [Fetch.ai, Ocean Protocol and SingularityNET merged to form the ASI Alliance](https://fetch.ai/blog/june-13-merger#:~:text=We%20are%20pleased%20to%20announce,projects%20dominated%20by%20Big%20Tech), issuing a single token and combining their decentralised AI networks. Elsewhere, [KaJ Labs signed a $1.5 billion letter of intent to acquire the assets of bankrupt broker, Genesis Global Capital](https://www.newsfilecorp.com/release/152105/Lithosphere-LITHO-Developer-KaJ-Labs-Announces-Letter-of-Intent-to-Acquire-Genesis-Global-Capital-DCG-Assets-For-1.5-Billion#:~:text=January%2023%2C%202023%201%3A00%20AM,Source%3A%20Kaj%20Labs) whereby targeting its prime brokerage business and client base. In NFTs, [Candy Digital and Palm NFT Studio merged under the Candy brand](https://www.coindesk.com/web3/2023/06/29/gary-vaynerchuk-backed-candy-digital-and-web3-production-company-palm-nft-studio-announce-merger#:~:text=Digital%20collectibles%20company%20Candy%20Digital,sports%2C%20entertainment%2C%20art%20and%20culture), combining entertainment IP from partners such as MLB, Netflix and DC Comics. Certainly, taken together, these deals show crypto firms expanding beyond spot trading into futures, AI, prime brokerage and entertainment so seeking to build full-stack ecosystems through acquisition.

Meanwhile, many digital asset native companies are now eyeing traditional banks, brokerages and asset managers with the aim being to plug into existing finance infrastructure and gain credibility. For example, Kraken’s NinjaTrader acquisition allows it to it tap into the regulated futures market and leverage NinjaTrader’s US broker licence. Likewise, Ripple’s Hidden Road deal brings Ripple into the core of Wall Street’s prime brokerage (clearing, lending and execution). Even [JPMorgan, Bank of America, CitiGroup and Wells Fargo](https://www.theblock.co/post/355495/major-us-banks-in-early-talks-for-joint-stablecoin-venture-wsj?utm_campaign=website&utm_medium=referral&utm_source=newsletter) (which account for over 21% of global FX) are rumoured to be joining other banks to create a US$ stablecoin - certainly, with favourable policies in 2025, analysts expect a [wave of bank‑charter deals](https://www.bankingexchange.com/news-feed/item/10275-trump-administration-policy-spurs-crypto-company-and-fintech-firm-interest-in-becoming-banks?Itemid=533#:~:text=Also%2C%20companies%20are%20also%20looking,major%20trend%20beginning%20in%202025). Digital asset native and FinTech firms in the US are looking at acquiring community banks to obtain national bank licenses, and a cited trend report notes that “ _analysts expect [acquiring a community bank for a charter] to be a major trend beginning in 2025._ ” In the UK and Europe, we may see similar moves as exchanges pursue regulated entity status (Coinbase in the UK, Kraken in EU, etc.). This crossover means that exchanges, for example, could offer savings accounts, payments and trading in fiat and securities. In addition, increasingly digital companies such as [Circle’s Payment Network](https://www.circle.com/cpn) are also stepping into payments and remittance. Many already issue stablecoins and debit cards, and they are also exploring merchant networks. For instance, some exchanges and wallet providers are in talks with payment processors (such as Mastercard, PayPal or local remittance firms) to integrate digital wallets into everyday payments. As an example of the trend, the MGX‑Binance deal highlights how traditional investors see crypto exchanges as part of a wider payments ecosystem. Stablecoin networks themselves are evolving into payment rails; smaller stablecoin issuers might find merger or partnership options attractive in order to meet emerging regulation. And crypto and fintech are blurring lines with some crypto companies, possibly even seeking full payment licenses or buying established remittance platforms to offer crypto‑fiat payment services globally.

Artificial intelligence and data analytics are also high on the radar, with the merger of Fetch.ai, Ocean Protocol and SingularityNET to form the ASI Alliance [fetch.ai](https://fetch.ai/blog/june-13-merger#:~:text=We%20are%20pleased%20to%20announce,projects%20dominated%20by%20Big%20Tech) being a notable example: three blockchain‑based AI labs uniting to pool talent, data and tooling. This sets a template - crypto platforms acquiring or merging with AI startups to embed machine learning into their networks. Similarly, decentralised data platforms (e.g. oracles, storage or indexing services) are attractive targets and we are

seeing some movement here, too. Chainalysis, a blockchain analytics firm, has been active in M&A ([acquiring AI fraud detector Alterya for $150 million early in 2025](https://www.coindesk.com/business/2025/01/13/chainalysis-buys-israeli-fraud-detection-startup-alterya-for-150-m#:~:text=Blockchain%20analytics%20company%20Chainalysis%20said,million%2C%20according%20to%20Business%20Insider)) so combining on‑chain intelligence with off‑chain security AI. In the broader market, we may also witness exchanges or crypto hedge funds buying into AI talent or data‑science companies to enhance trading models. Likewise, data layer projects (The Graph, Flipside Crypto, etc.) may consolidate via acquisition. The key is that as crypto becomes more data‑driven, then owning AI and data infrastructure becomes a priority. Compliance and security technology firms are natural M&A candidates. As regulation tightens, crypto companies need strong KYC/AML, on‑chain monitoring and fraud detection - for example, the Chainalysis‑Alterya deal combines blockchain analytics with AI‑powered fraud prevention. Chainalysis itself (backed by crypto incumbents such as Coinbase and Square) is expanding its services through acquisitions and others, such as Elliptic or TRM Labs, could either be acquired or procure their own deals. Digital asset exchanges and wallets will look to integrate advanced security tools so acquiring identity verification services, wallet‑security vendors or open‑source auditor firms (smart‑contract auditors) could be strategic.

The broader point: any compliance or security technology that bridges DeFi and Trad Fi is ripe for takeovers as firms strive to meet institutional standards and regulatory demands. This covers a broad category: system software, middleware, APIs and platforms that underlie crypto. Recent examples include exchanges buying white‑label platforms, or wallet vendors merging with custody firms; in 2025, we may see consolidation among crypto infrastructure stacks. Digital wallet providers (custodial and non‑custodial) are key components. For example, major custodians such as BitGo or Fireblocks could be targets, or smaller wallet apps might be absorbed by larger players to bolster user numbers. Tokenisation platforms (issuance and secondary trading of tokenised assets) are another focus - think tokenised stocks, funds or real‑world assets. Start-ups in that space, such as Securitize or tZERO, might be joined under crypto exchange umbrellas. Furthermore, exchanges themselves remain in play, especially regional ones. So, apart from Coinbase/Deribit, we may see deals such as large Western exchanges buying Middle Eastern or Asian exchanges to access local licenses. Middleware providers such as API gateways, blockchain data indexers (The Graph, Covalent) or DeFi liquidity aggregators could also change hands as projects vie to offer full‑stack services.

**Media, research and analytics:** Information and research companies are also turning acquisitive. Blockchain data providers, crypto research outlets and analytics platforms all have buyer appeal. A notable 2025 deal was Chainalysis’s $150M purchase of fraud‑analytics start-up, Alterya, reflecting high demand for AI‑driven crypto analytics.**** Investment research firms (CoinDesk, Messari, CryptoCompare, etc) may join forces or be bought by larger financial media houses to provide comprehensive crypto coverage. On‑chain data companies such as Glassnode, Dune Analytics or Nansen are already scaling quickly. As tokenised assets grow, there will be a premium on analytics tools that value and track these assets. We might see crossover acquisitions, too (for example, traditional stock analytics firms buying into crypto data, or crypto media merging with equity research outlets).**** Together, these trends highlight that companies are seeking to become one‑stop shops: trading, data and payments __ all under one roof. This convergent M&A environment offers both opportunity and complexity.

**Market makers are next on the radar:** DWF Labs reports daily trading volumes of around [$3.14 billion](https://www.coingecko.com/en/categories/dwf-labs-portfolio), whilst Wintermute handles roughly [$2.24 billion](https://alphapoint.com/blog/top-crypto-market-makers/). As tokenised assets expand, from digital bonds to real-world assets, the need for deep liquidity will grow. Exchanges may move to acquire market makers to control spreads and secure liquidity in-house. Others such as GSR, Cumberland, Kairon Labs, Alpathea, Bluesky Capital, Algoz and Acheron Trading remain active (despite undisclosed volumes) and could soon attract similar interest.

**Stablecoin issuers may consolidate:** As US regulations tighten with proposed rules on reserves and compliance, smaller or offshore stablecoin firms may seek mergers to survive. Larger players such as Circle and Paxos are already expanding. Consolidation could help issuers meet audit and capital requirements whilst securing broader distribution through exchanges and payment networks. Expect a wave of dealmaking as stablecoins race to scale under new regulatory standards.

**Digital asset infrastructure is fast becoming a hotspot for acquisitions:** Wallet providers are drawing interest, with exchanges eyeing both custodial and non-custodial options to grow their user base and enhance custody offerings. A popular mobile wallet or secure multi-chain tool could offer instant distribution. Tokenisation platforms are also in demand - as more assets move on-chain, firms enabling the issuance and management of tokenised securities are likely to be bought by traditional asset managers seeking digital capabilities. A good example of this is the recent acquisition of[ Tokeny, a company that has tokenised over $32billion of assets](https://www.ledgerinsights.com/fund-administrator-apex-to-acquire-tokenization-firm-tokeny/) by Apex, a global third party fund admin platform. And smaller or regional digital exchanges also remain attractive targets. Established firms such as Coinbase, Kraken or even trading houses such as Jane Street may look to acquire these platforms to gain local licenses, regulatory access and new users in emerging markets. Analytics and valuation providers are another key focus; Chainalysis’s acquisition of Altery reflects rising demand for high-quality data. Other firms such as Glassnode, Skew and Coin Metrics may also see increased M&A interest as institutions seek better tools to assess risk and asset value. Altogether, crypto companies are no longer merely targeting each other - they are buying into banking, fintech, analytics and even entertainment, blurring the lines between industries.

Nonetheless, for digital asset focused businesses and investors navigating the current wave of mergers and acquisitions, several practical strategies stand out. Firstly, staying closely aligned with evolving regulation is essential _._ With new rules emerging around banking, stablecoins and market conduct, acquisitions that bring regulatory credibility, such as a payment licence or a custodial trust can offer both strategic and operational advantages. Building complementary services is another key focus; rather than re-inventing the wheel, firms should look for acquisitions that fill product or capability gaps. For instance, a centralised exchange might acquire a crypto analytics startup to enhance research offerings whilst a DeFi project could target a gaming studio to broaden user engagement. But, the success of any deal hinges on execution. Cultural and technical integration should be prioritised early _,_ as the true value often lies in cross-selling and operational scale. Whether it is offering crypto trading alongside savings tools or combining NFT drops with embedded payments, synergy matters.

That said, not every trend demands a buyout. Thoughtful diversification means targeting businesses that support a long-term vision, be it bridging into traditional finance through brokerages or expanding into frontier tech such as AI or the metaverse - in some cases, full acquisitions may be premature _._ Strategic partnerships, joint ventures or minority stakes can offer exposure with reduced risk, especially when working across sectors such as banking, media or big tech. Ultimately, the M&A momentum of 2025 reflects a deeper shift: the digital asset industry is no longer a standalone sector, but one that is actively merging into the wider digital economy. Firms that stay nimble and deliberate will be best placed to shape and benefit from this convergence.
