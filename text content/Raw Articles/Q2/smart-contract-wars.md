# Smart contract wars

*By Jonny Fry | *

Blockchain technology has undoubtedly transformed industries by offering decentralisation, transparency and security. However, governments have recognised its potential not only for economic benefits but also for enforcing regulatory compliance. Smart contracts (self-executing agreements written in code) are being used by governments to automate taxation, monitor financial activity and even help in judicial enforcement in some jurisdictions. An example is the [Keleros blockchain](https://kleros.io/) (otherwise known as the Justice protocol) which is being used in arbitration proceedings in Argentina and Mexico whereby freeing up expensive court time. By using blockchains, governments can eliminate intermediaries, reduce fraud and ensure real-time regulatory enforcement. However, these advancements raise serious ethical concerns, in particular with regards to privacy, financial autonomy and the risks of over-automation. Governments see smart contracts as a way to increase efficiency, reduce corruption and ensure compliance because they enable financial transactions and regulatory enforcement to be programmed into code, eliminating the need for manual oversight. In addition, traditional tax systems rely on voluntary reporting, audits and penalties but smart contracts introduce a new paradigm: automated tax collection and compliance verification. Governments such as [Sweden, Germany and Estonia](https://www.ciat.org/blockchain-in-tax-administrations/?lang=en#:~:text=In%20Sweden%2C%20blockchain%20is%20being,%2C%20health%2C%20and%20business%20records.) are all experimenting with blockchain-based taxation systems where tax deductions are programmed directly into digital transactions. For instance, digital payments could automatically trigger tax deductions before funds reach the recipient - this would ensure real-time tax compliance, reducing evasion and fraud. In addition, the [EU is exploring how to deploy blockchain technology with VAT tracking](https://www.vatcalc.com/eu/eu-cesop-payment-system-blockchain-to-detect-vat-fraud/) to prevent tax fraud and streamline regulatory compliance. However, whilst these innovations enhance efficiency, they also pose risks to financial privacy; citizens and businesses may find themselves under constant financial surveillance, with little room to contest or negotiate tax obligations.

Moreover, smart contracts are being used to automate legal compliance and law enforcement and governments are exploring blockchain solutions to impose fines and penalties instantly upon detecting a violation. Traffic fines, environmental regulations and corporate compliance issues could be managed through automated systems - for example, a business failing to meet environmental standards could automatically incur a fine through a smart contract. Similarly, government agencies could freeze assets linked to money laundering or illicit financial activities without requiring court intervention. However, once again, whilst these measures improve enforcement efficiency, they also raise due process and privacy concerns. The lack of human oversight could lead to wrongful penalties, and citizens might have limited options for legal recourse. Meanwhile, blockchain-based digital identities are gaining traction as governments seek to modernise identity verification and public service delivery. Countries such as Estonia have pioneered blockchain-driven national ID systems, linking them to banking, healthcare and voting services (smart contracts could further automate these services). For instance, social welfare programs in the [UK are looking to use blockchain technology](https://blockchaintechnology-news.com/news/govcoin-aims-disrupt-uk-welfare-system/) to ensure that benefits are distributed fairly and transparently. A person eligible for financial aid would receive it automatically through a smart contract, so reducing bureaucratic delays and corruption. However, such systems give governments greater control therefore raising concerns regarding data privacy and government overreach, i.e. citizens could lose anonymity and be subjected to excessive monitoring.

The EU is cautiously integrating blockchain into financial regulation with the [Markets in Crypto-Assets (MiCA)](https://www.esma.europa.eu/esmas-activities/digital-finance-and-innovation/markets-crypto-assets-regulation-mica) framework aiming to standardise rules for digital assets and ensure compliance without stifling innovation. Several EU countries are exploring blockchain-based tax collection and fraud prevention and the European Central Bank is looking to introduce the digital euro; the European Central Bank’s digital euro initiative incorporates elements of programmable money whilst maintaining strict data privacy rules. However, the EU faces challenges in implementing a uniform regulatory approach across member states, with some adopting blockchain-friendly policies whilst others remain sceptical. On the other side of the pond, the US government has taken a fragmented approach to blockchain governance with agencies such as the [IRS](https://www.irs.gov/), [SEC](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/sec.asp) and [CFTC ](https://www.cftc.gov/)independently regulating different aspects of digital assets. The GENIUS Act, an acronym for the [Guiding and Establishing National Innovation for US Stablecoins of 2025 Act](https://www.hagerty.senate.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/GENIUS-Act.pdf), is set to introduce limitations on non-permitted stablecoins, even as it allows their ownership and sale in the US. Whilst these stablecoins remain tradable, the Act imposes functional restrictions that curb their integration into mainstream finance. Some states (such as Wyoming) have embraced blockchain innovation, offering legal clarity for smart contract use having passed [thirty pieces of legislation](https://content.govdelivery.com/accounts/WYGOV/bulletins/3d6ce34#:~:text=Governor%20Gordon%20has%20signed%20over,and%20digital%20assets%20in%20Wyoming.) regarding the treatment and use of blockchain technology and digital assets in Wyoming. However, federal authorities remain cautious, focusing on anti-money laundering (AML) measures and taxation enforcement. Furthermore, the US, like other jurisdictions, faces the challenge of balancing blockchain innovation with regulatory oversight so ensuring security without stifling economic growth. Certainly, despite the efficiency gains, the widespread use of smart contracts in governance raises ethical and legal dilemmas, including:

> · **over-automation and lack of human oversight** \- automated systems can execute financial penalties, freeze accounts and enforce regulations without human intervention. Whilst this reduces corruption, it also eliminates the flexibility of human discretion. Individuals wrongly penalised by a smart contract may have limited avenues for appeal.
> 
> · **privacy concerns and financial surveillance** \- smart contract-driven governance can lead to pervasive financial surveillance. If all transactions are recorded and regulated via a blockchain, governments gain unprecedented access to citizens’ financial activities. Whilst transparency reduces fraud, it also undermines financial privacy; individuals and businesses may lose autonomy over how they manage their finances, raising concerns about state overreach.
> 
> · **programmable money and social control** \- CBDCs with smart contract capabilities introduce the possibility of government-controlled financial behaviour. Governments could restrict how individuals spend money, block certain transactions or impose expiration dates on funds. Such control mechanisms, if misused, could erode economic freedoms and create a system where citizens’ financial autonomy depends entirely on government policy.

Without doubt, as blockchain adoption increases, the debate over government-controlled smart contracts will intensify and several key trends will shape the future of digital governance. Governments will need to establish standardised frameworks to regulate blockchain compliance, so ensuring transparency and accountability. As centralised blockchain enforcement expands, decentralised solutions are likely to emerge to counter excessive state control and regulatory bodies may introduce measures to ensure that smart contract enforcement respects due process and individual rights. There will also be the inevitable challenges of integrating different technology alongside blockchains - future smart contracts could incorporate artificial intelligence and the internet of things (IoT) to create even more advanced compliance mechanisms. Governments need to strike a balance between leveraging blockchain for efficiency and safeguarding individual freedoms and the battle between state control and personal autonomy will define the evolution of smart contract governance. Certainly, smart contracts are transforming governance, offering new ways to automate compliance and financial oversight and governments worldwide are leveraging blockchain to enhance tax collection, law enforcement and digital identity systems. But unsurprisingly, these advancements come with ethical concerns, particularly regarding financial privacy, over-automation and government overreach. Hence, the challenge lies in balancing efficiency with personal freedoms, ensuring that blockchain-driven governance serves society without undermining fundamental rights.

So, as the "smart contract wars" unfold, the future of digital governance will depend on regulatory decisions, technological advancements and public discourse. Whether smart contracts empower individuals or increase state control will be a defining issue of the digital age. Therefore, are we set to see governments weaponising smart contract - that is, automating taxes, enforcing fines and tracking financial activity in real time? The clash is clear: efficiency vs. personal liberty. As blockchain reshapes governance, the fight is not simply about technology - it is about who holds the power. Ultimately, will smart contracts empower citizens or create a financial system where every transaction is monitored, controlled and enforced by code…?
