# Beyond transactions: exploring non-payment applications of CBDC infrastructure

*By Jonny Fry | *

[The global exploration of central bank digital currencies (CBDCs) has primarily focused on their potential to revolutionise payment systems.](https://www.imf.org/en/Publications/fintech-notes/Issues/2024/08/27/Cyber-Resilience-of-the-Central-Bank-Digital-Currency-Ecosystem-554090) From enhancing financial inclusion and streamlining cross-border transactions to providing a resilient alternative to cash, the payment-centric benefits of CBDCs are widely discussed. However, to truly grasp the transformative potential of CBDC infrastructure, it is crucial to look beyond mere transactions and delve into its broader applications. The underlying technology and design principles of a well-conceived CBDC could serve as a foundational digital public infrastructure, enabling a host of non-payment functions that could significantly improve governance, public services and citizen well-being.

The fundamental characteristics of a CBDC such as its direct liability of the central bank, its potential for programmability, together with the secure, verifiable nature of its underlying ledger technology (whether centralised or distributed), open doors to innovative applications far beyond simply moving money. These non-payment uses leverage the inherent trust, efficiency and data integrity that a sovereign digital currency infrastructure could provide.

**Digital identity verification: a foundational layer for trust**

One of the most compelling non-payment applications of CBDC infrastructure lies in revolutionising digital identity verification. [In an increasingly digital world, a robust and trustworthy digital identity system is paramount for accessing services, participating in the economy and combating fraud.](https://accesspartnership.com/digital-authentication-trust-mechanisms-dpi-southeast-asia/#:~:text=Robust%20digital%20identification%20and%20authentication,Asia%20Pacific's%20growing%20and%20evolving) CBDC infrastructure could provide a secure and verifiable backbone for such a system - a CBDC system holds immense potential for transforming digital identity verification, offering a secure and reliable alternative to current fragmented approaches. Furthermore, this infrastructure could fundamentally support verifiable credentials and self-sovereign identity, enabling individuals to control and selectively share digital proofs of their identity attributes (such as age or professional qualifications) without exposing sensitive underlying personal data. And instead of relying on insecure, disparate databases, a CBDC ledger could record cryptographic proofs or hashes of these attributes. This shift empowers individuals with greater control over their personal information, aligning with the principles of self-sovereign identity. [Beyond empowering individuals, a CBDC-backed digital identity system could significantly enhance KYC/AML processes for financial institutions and other regulated entities.](https://www.tookitaki.com/compliance-hub/challenges-in-anti-money-laundering-compliance) By providing a single, trusted source for identity verification, the complexities and costs associated with know your customer (KYC) and anti-money laundering (AML) compliance would be streamlined so reducing onboarding times and boosting the effectiveness of financial crime prevention. This robust identity linkage would also lead to reduced fraud and identity theft; each online transaction or service access could be cryptographically tied to a verified identity whereby creating an auditable trail that naturally deters malicious activities. Finally, a well-designed CBDC system integrated with digital identity offers a powerful pathway to financial inclusion. For unbanked and underbanked populations (often hindered by a lack of verifiable identity documents), this infrastructure could provide a legitimate and accessible means to establish a digital footprint whereby opening doors to essential financial services like credit and insurance.

**Government service delivery: programmable, efficient and transparent**

CBDC infrastructure is programmable, enabling an innovative approach to government service delivery that departs from old, difficult and opaque approaches. Automated social welfare payments might employ programmable CBDCs to target, rapidly distribute and even earmark payments for food or healthcare. This drastically lowers administrative overhead, leakage and fraud, and guarantees funds reach intended beneficiaries efficiently and transparently with the option to program money for expenditure solely at certain suppliers. Transparent and efficient grant disbursal would handle government funds to businesses, non-profits and schools with unprecedented transparency and accountability. Programmable CBDCs might automate reporting and auditing to decrease administrative work and build public trust by ensuring grant money are spent as intended. Fiscally, CBDC infrastructure might improve tax collection and rebates; sensitive, smart contracts might streamline tax processes, reduce mistakes and administrative expenses, and instantly provide tax refunds to citizens so boosting economic activity during downturns. CBDC infrastructure with tokenisation of products and services might create transparent and immutable procurement records for public procurement and supply chain management. This would automate payment release after delivery or service completion, improving efficiency and reducing corruption. If privacy and security are prioritised, the ledger technology could be used for digital voting and civic engagement to improve electoral integrity, participation and costs. Finally, license and permit issuance might be simplified - digital licenses might be provided as verified credentials on a secure CBDC ledger whereby minimising complexity and fraud in many government permits.

**Other potential non-payment applications**

Beyond digital identity and government services, the foundational capabilities of CBDC infrastructure hint at an even wider array of non-payment applications:

> · **tokenisation of assets** \- a CBDC could serve as the settlement layer for the tokenisation of various real-world assets, such as real estate, intellectual property and even commodities. This could enhance liquidity, transparency and fractional ownership, opening up new investment opportunities and streamlining asset transfers.
> 
> · **smart contracts for legal agreements** \- the programmability of CBDC could extend to various legal agreements, enabling self-executing contracts where certain conditions trigger automated actions including non-monetary obligations. This could be applied to escrows, wills and other contractual agreements, so reducing the need for intermediaries and legal disputes.
> 
> · **data integrity and provenance** \- the immutable nature of a CBDC ledger could be leveraged to timestamp and verify the integrity of critical data, such as academic records, medical histories or environmental data. This provides an undeniable record of authenticity and prevents tampering.
> 
> · **disaster relief distribution** \- in times of crisis, efficient and targeted distribution of aid is crucial. Programmable CBDCs could ensure that relief funds reach affected individuals quickly and are used for essential needs whereby minimising diversion and maximising impact.

However, whilst the non-payment applications of CBDC infrastructure hold immense promise, their realisation is not without significant challenges - [integrating sensitive personal data with a CBDC raises significant privacy concerns.](https://www.elibrary.imf.org/view/journals/063/2024/004/article-A001-en.xml#:~:text=Data%20leakage.&Such%20leakage%20can%20lead%20to,trust%20in%20the%20CBDC%20system.) Central banks and governments must implement robust privacy-by-design principles, including pseudonymity, data minimisation and strong cryptographic techniques (e.g. zero-knowledge proofs), to ensure individual privacy is protected. Building and integrating such expansive systems requires significant technical expertise and careful planning for interoperability between different government agencies and private sector platforms. Moreover, determining who controls access to data, who can issue verifiable credentials and how disputes are resolved within these complex ecosystems will be critical and require robust governance frameworks. Essentially, for any non-payment CBDC application to succeed, public trust in the underlying technology and the institutions managing it is paramount. Transparent communication, robust cybersecurity and clear legal frameworks will be essential. In addition, one needs to consider the existing legal and regulatory frameworks which may need significant adaptation to accommodate the new paradigms introduced by these non-payment CBDC applications, particularly regarding data ownership, liability and the legal status of digital credentials. Clearly these applications could disrupt established industries, such as traditional identity verification services or some aspects of legal and financial services. Managing this transition ethically and equitably will be important.

CBDCs are quietly evolving from mere payment innovations into **nation-scale digital infrastructure** tools not simply for money movement, but for identity, governance and civic trust. This shift reframes money as programmable public infrastructure _,_ where the same system that settles transactions could also issue licenses, disburse disaster relief, automate taxes and verify identities. It is the transformation of central banks from monetary gatekeepers into **digital architects of citizenship.** A CBDC-linked identity layer could eliminate inefficiencies and fraud in KYC/AML, reduce reliance on siloed databases and give citizens sovereignty over their digital credentials. At the same time, programmable money could offer governments tools to execute policy with surgical precision while raising critical questions of surveillance, data ownership and institutional trust. In this vision, CBDCs become more than state-backed currency - they become the **operating system of the modern state,** redefining how services are delivered, how trust is verified and who is included in the digital economy. The risk is not merely in rushing implementation - it is in **failing to recognise that the future of money may also be the future of public infrastructure**.
