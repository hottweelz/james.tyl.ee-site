# Smart savings circles: trustless rotating savings and credit associations (ROSCAs) on a blockchain

*By Jonny Fry | *

The Rotating Savings and Credit Association (ROSCA) are one of the oldest informal financial institutions that thrives on co-operative pooling of resources dependant on shared trust - typically created by and for a close-knit community and also designed to offer mutual support for the community. A group of individuals agree to contribute a fixed sum regularly, typically on monthly basis, and this establishes a pool of money that others can access _._ Anthropologist and researcher,__[Shirley Ardener](https://www.ijassjournal.com/2021/V4I1/4146575401.pdf), gives a more general definition by saying that _“ROSCA is an association formed upon a core of participants who agree to make regular contributions to a fund which is given, in whole or in part, to each contributor in a rotation._ ” At their core, [ROSCAs are based on mutual trust](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/r/rotating-credit-and-savings-association.asp), i.e. a group of individuals agrees to contribute a fixed amount of money regularly. Meanwhile, according to [Money Helper.or.uk](https://www.moneyhelper.org.uk/en/savings/types-of-savings/unregulated-savings-schemes), ROSCAs are sometimes referred to as: _“Informal schemes [which] exist under many different names depending on where in the world they were originally based. For example, pardna (from the Caribbean), susu (from Africa), chit (south Asia) and kou (Japan).”_

ROSCAs have many advantages: they encourage saving, offer lump-sum financing without interest, build strong social ties and are particularly popular in developing economies where access to formal financial services is limited. Yet traditional ROSCAs have significant vulnerabilities: organisers might embezzle funds, recordkeeping errors can spark disputes and members might miss payments - all potentially causing disruptions. In worst-case scenarios, circles collapse entirely with severe financial consequences for participants. Hence, the entire system hinges on interpersonal trust (a scarce and fragile resource) and trust is one thing that blockchain-powered platforms can foster due to the transparency and immutability it offers. Blockchain technology can eliminate the need for trust by using [smart contracts to automate](https://www.ibm.com/think/topics/smart-contracts) ROSCA operations. Smart contracts can be used to collect contributions, enforce rules, schedule payouts and record everything transparently. More importantly, once deployed, no single participant can tamper with the process; no individual person can disappear with the group’s funds; and payouts occur according to pre-agreed conditions. Hence, this approach transforms ROSCAs into trustless savings circles - systems where participants do not need to personally trust each other or a central organiser. Ultimately, trust is shifted from individuals to tamper-proof code. Moreover, blockchain's immutability ensures that every action (such as payment deposits and withdrawals) is permanently recorded, providing a clear audit trail that prevents disputes. A blockchain-based ROSCA would typically have several essential components, with each of these elements works together to create a seamless, tamper-resistant savings experience, including:

> · smart contracts to enforce the group’s rules.
> 
> · [a digital wallet for each participant to hold and transfer funds](https://www.techtarget.com/whatis/definition/digital-wallet).
> 
> · [group governance protocols](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/corporategovernance.asp) defining how membership is handled, how payouts are scheduled and how defaults are managed.
> 
> · transparency tools to allow members to monitor contributions, payouts and group status in real time.

Setting up a blockchain ROSCA would begin with the creation of a smart contract that specifies the group size, contribution amount, payment frequency, payout order (randomised or voted) and rules for defaults.

Participants would deposit their contributions into the smart contract at each cycle. The smart contract would verify that all required contributions have been made before releasing the pooled funds to the designated recipient. If a participant fails to pay, penalties (such as forfeiting participation rights or previous contributions) could be automatically enforced according to the preset rules. Smart contracts could also randomise the pay-out order to prevent favouritism or allow the group to vote. Alternatively, reputation systems could reward prompt contributors with earlier payouts. All participants would have real-time access to the ROSCA dashboard, allowing them to verify payments, monitor the fund’s status and receive automatic notifications without needing to manually track anything. In addition, blockchain ROSCAs offer several powerful advantages over traditional models. They drastically reduce fraud risk by eliminating the need for human fund managers and relying on secure, autonomous code. They ensure transparent recordkeeping by recording every transaction permanently on the blockchain (making disputes virtually impossible); they can handle cross-border participation, allowing friends or family members in different countries to join the same savings circle; they provide customisable governance, enabling groups to set up specific rules to match their cultural preferences and needs; they offer financial inclusion by bringing secure savings mechanisms to people without access to banks; and finally, they can integrate with decentralised finance (DeFi) platforms to offer participants additional benefits, such as earning interest on pooled funds while waiting for payouts.

However, despite their promise, blockchain ROSCAs face hurdles of which, firstly, is technical complexity - many rural or low-income communities may find blockchain technology intimidating or inaccessible so user-friendly mobile apps and education initiatives will be critical to overcoming this barrier. Another issue is internet access - in many of the places where ROSCAs are most needed, connectivity can be unreliable. Solutions might include lightweight blockchains that work with intermittent connections or offline transaction capabilities. Trust in technology also remains a challenge - people who are used to face-to-face agreements may be reluctant to trust “invisible” digital systems. Therefore, community demonstrations, pilot programs and hybrid models (partially automated but still locally overseen) could bridge this trust gap. A lack of regulatory clarity is another challenge and some countries might impose restrictions on cryptocurrency use, whereby complicating blockchain ROSCA deployments. Hence, careful legal navigation and local partnerships would be necessary. Finally, smart contract vulnerabilities themselves pose a risk as bugs in code can cause serious losses. Therefore, open source, audited smart contracts would be essential for community adoption.

Nonetheless, blockchain-powered ROSCAs could evolve to beyond simple savings groups and into robust, decentralised financial ecosystems. Communities could layer-in insurance pools, investment clubs or emergency loan funds - all governed by transparent smart contracts. Participants could build credit reputations based on their ROSCA history, enabling them to access wider financial opportunities without needing traditional banks. Governments and NGOs might partner with blockchain ROSCAs to deliver aid more efficiently, channelling funds directly into smart contracts for secure disbursement among community members. Ultimately, blockchain-powered ROSCAs offer a model of finance based on solidarity, transparency and mutual empowerment - not exploitation or gatekeeping. For centuries, rotating savings circles have helped people meet urgent financial needs without banks or loans, however, the fragility of human trust has always been a risk. Therefore, blockchain technology offers a compelling solution - by automating ROSCAs through smart contracts, communities can preserve the power of collective saving while eliminating the dangers of fraud, delay and mismanagement. Smart savings circles on the blockchain could usher in a new era of financial resilience, where trust is built not on human promises but on transparent, tamper-proof code. Essentially, the future of community finance may not simply be traditional - but trustless, borderless and unstoppable.
