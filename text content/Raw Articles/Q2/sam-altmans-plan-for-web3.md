# Sam Altman's plan for Web3

*By Jonny Fry | *

AI has often been portrayed as stripping humans out of the workplace, replacing people with bots. However, the biggest name in AI - Sam Altman, CEO of ChaptCPT - is on a journey of countering this effect with Web3 through Worldcoin, recently rebranded as World (a Web3 AI biometric system), and its supporting ecosystem. World aims to create a universal “proof of personhood” system to distinguish humans from AI bots, addressing a critical challenge as AI-generated content and deepfakes proliferate.

By leveraging blockchain technology, World provides a decentralised identity layer that enables secure, privacy-preserving verification without relying on centralised databases. Altman has envisioned World as infrastructure for distributing universal basic income (UBI) in an AI-dominated job market, ensuring funds reach verified humans among many other things, which include:

> · **privacy-first digital identity** \- unlike centralised systems, Worldcoin’s decentralised identity uses zero-knowledge proofs to verify humanity without exposing personal data.
> 
> · **financial inclusion** \- Worldcoin (WLD) targets the unbanked by rewarding orb verification with WLD tokens. These tokens grant access to decentralised finance (defi) via the world app whilst partnerships with visa and stripe bridge crypto and traditional finance, enabling cross-border payments in underserved regions.
> 
> · **secure governance and voting** \- World ID could enable fraud-resistant, anonymous voting in decentralised organisations (DAOS) or elections, ensuring “one person, one vote”.
> 
> · **fraud prevention** \- from welfare programs to NFT airdrops, World ID’s uniqueness verification could curb duplicate claims.

With 26 million users and 12 million verified across 100+ countries, World is scaling rapidly, partnering with giants [such as Stripe, Visa and Match Group](https://techcrunch.com/2025/04/30/world-partners-with-tinder-visa-to-bring-its-id-verifying-tech-to-more-places/) to integrate its ID system into dating apps, gaming platforms and financial service. It does all these with World Chain, its Ethereum blockchain designed to scale World’s infrastructure. The company behind World (Tools for Humanity (TFH) and co-founded by Altman in 2019) has been aggressively scaling; by mid-2023, it had [raised about $250 million](https://www.quora.com/How-did-Worldcoin-manage-to-raise-250-million-from-investors-like-a16z-and-Bain-Capital-Crypto) in venture funding. This includes a [$25 million seed round in October 2021](https://www.binance.com/en/square/post/2023-10-26-worldcoin-project-receives-25-million-wld-tokens-from-market-makers-1508939), followed by $100 million within six months, boosting its valuation to $3 billion. In May 2023, the company [secured another $115 million in a Series C round](https://www.reuters.com/technology/openais-sam-altman-raises-115-mln-worldcoin-crypto-project-2023-05-25/) led by Blockchain Capital. Other participants included Andreessen Horowitz, Bain Capital Crypto and Distributed Global. TFH also [explored raising up to $50 million](https://www.binance.com/en/square/post/1174518459178) through discounted WLD token sales, although this was still under discussion in late 2023. These efforts underscore TFH’s push to scale its digital identity and crypto initiatives amid regulatory scrutiny, and, whilst the US has not cracked down, countries where Worldcoin previously launched (such as Kenya) have raised serious concerns - the project's past continues to cast a shadow on its future. Worldcoin focused recruitment efforts in countries such as Indonesia, Kenya and Sudan, offering cash incentives for iris scans. In Kenya, participants were given about 7,000 Kenyan shillings in cryptocurrency in exchange for their biometric information. But many did not understand the project’s goals, with some believing it was a government aid program. In Indonesia, villagers were mis-led about the purpose of the scans, and terms of service were provided only in English. Local officials were reportedly paid to facilitate sign-ups, raising ethical red flags, hence these actions have called the project’s intent and methods into question.

So, in 2024, [Kenya closed its probe into Worldcoin](https://techcrunch.com/2024/06/20/worldcoin-in-kenya-2/) for operating illegally - allowing the company to relaunch its orbs after a year-long suspension despite some calling for its shutdown. However, with the latest rollout, Kenya, Germany, Spain, Portugal, South Korea and Hong Kong have all suspended or banned Tools for Humanity due to the ways it collected biometric information from citizens. In 2025, Justice Aburili Roselyne of the Kenyan High Court ruled that the [Worldcoin Foundation violated Kenya’s Data Protection Act](https://allafrica.com/stories/202505060224.html) of 2019 and [ordered all data be erased](https://www.financemagnates.com/cryptocurrency/worldcoin-ordered-to-delete-biometric-data-in-kenya-over-privacy-breach/) within seven days. An MIT investigation found that Worldcoin collected more personal data (such as emails and phone numbers) than it publicly acknowledged (many users had not provided meaningful consent), and TFH’s legal team admitted that it did not engage sufficiently with regulators before launch. Thomas Scott, the company’s Chief Legal Officer, [acknowledged](https://www.vanguardlawmag.com/case-studies/thomas-scott-tools-for-humanity/) that the team failed to explain its intentions clearly in Kenya, leading to widespread distrust.

Much of Vitalik Buterin’s 2023 [criticism still holds true in 2025,](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=28998065) with his four key concerns including privacy, accessibility, centralisation and security; he warned that even hashed iris scans could expose sensitive personal data. Furthermore, the requirement to access a physical Orb limits participation, especially in remote or underserved regions. Edward Snowden also voiced concern - he warned that even hashed biometric data could be used to track individuals, stating: “ _Don’t catalogue eyeballs_.” However, although Worldcoin claims that raw iris images are deleted, MIT Technology Review [found the company retained biometric data longer than disclosed](https://www.technologyreview.com/2022/04/06/1048981/worldcoin-cryptocurrency-biometrics-web3/) during testing. The project relies on Trusted Execution Environments for security but these are not universally trusted for meeting strict privacy laws, such as the EU’s GDPR. Vitalik also criticised the project’s reliance on proprietary hardware, which undermines decentralisation and opens the door to fraud; potential issues include fake identities, coercion, resale of IDs and attempts to spoof the system using fake or falsified biometric data. In 2023, hackers [stole Orb operator credentials](https://techcrunch.com/2023/05/12/hackers-stole-passwords-of-worldcoin-orb-operators/) using malware whereby gaining access to internal systems. In Kenya and Cambodia, some users sold iris data for as little as $30. Moreover, a black market for World IDs [emerged in China,](https://www.biometricupdate.com/202305/worldcoin-may-have-a-biometric-data-black-market-problem) undermining the system’s claim to ensure “proof of uniqueness”. And, although a “trail of bits” [audit revealed no direct](https://cointelegraph.com/news/worldcoin-trail-of-bits-audit-no-vulnerability-orb-software) vulnerability in the Orb software, the surrounding risks remain significant.

World presents an ambitious vision to distinguish humans from AI through privacy-first digital identity and financial inclusion. However, its execution has been marred by ethical missteps, regulatory pushback and unresolved privacy concerns. The project's reliance on proprietary biometric hardware, opaque data practices and controversial recruitment methods in vulnerable regions has eroded public trust. Furthermore, despite strong investor backing and rapid global expansion, World’s long-term success will depend on its ability to address these criticisms transparently. It must comply with global data protection standards and build a truly inclusive and secure identity infrastructure to realise its full promise.
