# Cold storage, hot competition: inside the digital asset custody revolution

*By Jonny Fry | *

The global market for custody of securities is vast; as reported by [Flow.db:](https://flow.db.com/securities-services/the-future-of-custody-roadmap) “ _PwC projects global assets under management will reach US$171trn by 2028.”_ But, in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, custody of securities had evolved from banks offering custody for shares, initially providing physical safekeeping of paper certificates in their vaults. The modern concept of global custody services - where banks also handle settlement, administration and cross-border asset servicing - [emerged in the 1970s](https://www.globalcustody.net/industry_history/), notably after the founding of both the Depository Trust Company (DTC) in 1973 and the passage of the US Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA) in 1974 in order to address the paperwork crisis in securities markets. Furthermore, the use of custodians increased in the 1980s as markets became more global. This included innovations such as the introduction of Certificateless Registry for Electronic Share Transfer (CREST) as a project sponsored by the Bank of England in 1993 and led to [CREST, beginning in 1996,](https://www.bis.org/cpmi/publ/d53p14uk.pdf) which enabled electronic settlement of UK equities and replaced paper certificates. De-materialised holdings managed by custodians banks such [BNY Mellon](https://www.bny.com/corporate/global/en/about-us/about-bny.html), [State Street](https://www.statestreet.com/us/en) and [Citigroup](https://www.citigroup.com/global) built global reputations by ensuring the safe storage and transfer of securities, management of settlements and preservation of client trust. Yet, in the digital asset world, the notion of custody has been transformed. Indeed, in May 2025, [Paul Maley at Deutsche Bank](https://flow.db.com/securities-services/the-future-of-custody-roadmap) predicted: “ _One of the biggest systemic changes we’re likely to_

 _see in the next couple of years is real-time, 24/7 transfer of value becoming a real thing”,_ adding that,_“for our industry, that means perpetual balance sheet velocity suddenly becoming a reality. Adapting to these changes, and what that means for our collective business model, will be significant_.”

Owning a digitised asset does not mean holding a certificate or ledger entry but controlling a private cryptographic key. Lose that key, and the asset is gone forever. In this context, custody is no longer a matter of physical safekeeping but of technological precision and operational resilience. Therefore, to protect these keys, custodians now deploy systems built on [hardware security modules (HSMs)](https://www.entrust.com/resources/learn/what-are-hardware-security-modules), [multi-party computation (MPC)](https://www.fireblocks.com/what-is-mpc/) and [cold storage solutions](https://www.supermicro.com/en/glossary/cold-data-storage). Alongside these technical defences, they must navigate complex layers of regulatory compliance, insurance and cybersecurity oversight. The result is a new discipline where technology and trust intersect, and whomever can command both will define the future of institutional digital finance. After years of hesitation, the world’s largest financial institutions are entering digital asset custody with force as can be seen by Citibank’s recent announcement that it is to start offering digital asset custody services. Moreover, the catalyst has been both regulatory and reputational, a combination of clearer legal frameworks and a string of crypto-native failures that rattled institutional investors.

Across the pond, [US Bancorp recently revived its institutional Bitcoin custody service, targeting ETF issuers and asset managers](https://www.reuters.com/sustainability/boards-policy-regulation/us-bancorp-revives-institutional-bitcoin-custody-service-2025-09-03/). The move followed the repeal of Staff Accounting Bulletin 121 (SAB 121), a rule that had forced banks to treat custodied crypto as a liability on their balance sheets - an accounting burden that effectively froze traditional custodians out of the market. Its removal re-opened the door for regulated participation, signalling a policy shift toward normalisation. Other titans are following suit. BNY Mellon launched its own custody platform, State Street Digital continues expanding crypto services, and Citigroup has developed digital asset units focusing on tokenisation. In Europe, [Standard Chartered’s, Zodia Custody, and Societe Générale’s, Forge division](https://www.reuters.com/business/finance/stanchart-forms-new-entity-digital-assets-custody-services-eu-2025-01-09/), are rolling out services for institutional investors whilst [Nomura’s Komainu in Japan integrates traditional trust principles with blockchain infrastructure.](https://www.reuters.com/article/technology/nomura-and-partners-launch-digital-asset-custodian-komainu-idUSKBN23O2AK/) For institutional clients, banks offer three compelling advantages: reputation, regulation and resilience. Their long histories in asset protection, deep capital reserves and embedded compliance structures make them natural

custodians for cautious investors. And, unlike many crypto firms, they can provide insured custody and audited reporting that satisfies fiduciary and regulatory standards. Yet legacy institutions face steep adaptation costs; their technology stacks are outdated, internal approval cycles slow and blockchain expertise remains scarce - integrating key management and smart contract functionality into existing systems can take years. Nonetheless, for the first time, banks view crypto custody not as an experiment but as an obligation to remain relevant in a tokenised world. Whilst banks play catch-up, crypto-native custodians have spent years building the infrastructure of the on-chain economy. Firms such as [Anchorage Digital](https://www.bitstamp.net/learn/company-profiles/what-is-anchorage-digital/), [BitGo](https://www.bitgo.com/), Coinbase Custody and Gemini Custody pioneered institutional-grade storage systems that catered to funds, exchanges and high-net-worth investors. These companies offered agility that legacy institutions could not match - their systems were built for multi-chain compatibility, automated staking, API integration and rapid token support. Many provide on-chain governance and DeFi participation options - features unthinkable within bank risk models. However, the same flexibility that fuelled their rise also exposed vulnerabilities; the Prime Trust collapse in 2023, involving mismanagement of wallets and commingling of client funds, revealed the fragility of under-regulated custodial structures ([State Street Digital Digest](https://www.statestreet.com/lu/en/insights/digital-digest-july-2025-digital-asset-custody?utm_source=chatgpt.com)). Such failures eroded institutional confidence, prompting calls for bank-level oversight across the sector. Even so, crypto-native custodians maintain their technological edge. They move faster, support new protocols instantly and integrate with decentralised networks that banks still treat with caution. Their clients often value this dynamism over the conservative safety of traditional custody. In many cases, innovation remains their strongest currency.

Meanwhile, the custody wars are being fought as much in courtrooms and regulatory chambers as in codebases and boardrooms. The question of who is allowed to hold digital assets legally remains unresolved in several jurisdictions, and the outcome could decide the winners. In the US, [the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) maintains that many crypto exchanges are not ](https://www.investopedia.com/sec-chair-says-crypto-exchanges-may-not-be-qualified-custodians-7182200?utm_source=chatgpt.com)_[“qualified custodians,”](https://www.investopedia.com/sec-chair-says-crypto-exchanges-may-not-be-qualified-custodians-7182200?utm_source=chatgpt.com)_[\- a legal category essential for institutions managing client assets](https://www.investopedia.com/sec-chair-says-crypto-exchanges-may-not-be-qualified-custodians-7182200?utm_source=chatgpt.com). Without that designation, registered investment advisers risk violating compliance rules if they store funds on such platforms. Meanwhile, Europe has surged ahead with the Markets in Crypto-Assets (MiCA) regulation, which introduces clear custody and capital requirements for digital asset service providers. Asia, too, has become a hub for compliant innovation - Singapore, Japan and Hong Kong are licensing digital asset custodians under frameworks that mirror traditional banking rules. However, the [repeal of SAB 121 in the US](https://www.fireblocks.com/blog/the-repeal-of-sab-121-a-new-chapter-for-u-s-leadership-in-crypto/) marked a major inflection point. By removing a punitive accounting rule, it enabled banks to compete directly with crypto custodians for the first time in years. Analysts expect a wave of regulated custody services tied to Bitcoin ETFs, tokenised bonds and on-chain settlement platforms. For crypto-native firms, stricter oversight may compress margins and raise barriers to entry, but it also legitimises the sector whereby filtering out weak operators and building long-term trust.

Institutional investors now face a nuanced choice between traditional and crypto-native custodians. Risk appetite, governance requirements and investment strategy often dictate the decision. Large asset managers, pension funds and insurers (subject to fiduciary oversight) tend to favour regulated banking custodians or hybrid models. Their priorities are clear - legal certainty, insurance and operational continuity. Moreover, hedge funds and digital asset funds with higher risk tolerance gravitate toward crypto-native custodians for their flexibility, token coverage and yield-generation services. According to a recent PwC report, [over 70% of traditional custodians plan to expand their digital asset offerings by 2026, often through strategic partnerships rather than in-house development](https://www.pwc.com/it/it/publications/assets/docs/pwc-digital-asset-custody-survey.pdf?utm_source=chatgpt.com). The logic is simple: banks provide compliance and client access; crypto firms provide

technology and speed. In Asia, [DBS Bank and Standard Chartered’s, Zodia Custody, are already demonstrating how this model works](https://asia.viseo.com/industries/institutional-crypto-custody-in-asia-viseos-collaboration-with-a-british-bank-to-craft-next-gen-platforms/?utm_source=chatgpt.com). Their operations integrate blockchain-native tools within the comfort of regulated frameworks, creating a blueprint for what might become the global standard. Ultimately, the custody wars are driven by technology as much as trust. Notably, both camps are investing heavily in next-generation cryptographic solutions to outdo one another in security and scalability. In addition, multi-party computation (MPC) and threshold signature schemes now enable key fragmentation across multiple entities, removing single points of failure and cold-to-hot automation frameworks are improving liquidity management whilst maintaining offline safety. Another frontier is tokenisation, where real-world assets - from government bonds to real estate -Interestingly, are issued on blockchains. Custodians must be able to handle these hybrid instruments, blending legal claims with digital tokens. Interestingly, banks may hold a structural edge here since they already manage trillions in traditional securities and can extend existing legal custody models into the blockchain era. Crypto-native custodians, in contrast, are pioneering programmable custody smart contract-based systems that enforce compliance, reporting and transaction logic automatically. Certainly, these architectures hint at a future where custodianship is no longer passive safekeeping, but an active, automated process embedded directly into the asset itself. However, whilst the competition fosters innovation, it also introduces systemic risks; concentrating custody among a few global banks could recreate the too-big-to-fail problem in digital finance. A single cyber breach or regulatory dispute could freeze billions in tokenised assets across markets but, conversely, over-reliance on under-regulated crypto custodians may amplify counterparty risk, fraud exposure and jurisdictional uncertainty. Harmonisation of global standards, particularly around AML/KYC, capital requirements and bankruptcy treatment, will be essential to prevent a fragmented and fragile custody landscape. At a philosophical level, custody strikes at the heart of crypto’s ethos and self-custody, the idea that individuals should control their own assets, remains a core tenet of decentralisation. Yet for institutions constrained by compliance obligations, it is impractical. This tension between personal sovereignty and institutional trust will define the custody debate for years to come. In the end, the digital asset custody wars may not produce a clear victor. Instead, the market is likely to evolve toward convergence, a dual ecosystem where banks dominate regulated institutional custody, whilst crypto-native firms power innovation and infrastructure beneath the surface.

Undoubtedly, hybrid partnerships are already blurring traditional boundaries; custody-as-a-service platforms, co-branded trust entities and white-label wallet solutions are emerging to bridge compliance with decentralisation. Over time, these alliances could yield a regulated yet programmable financial system, where blockchain transparency co-exists with institutional trust. Custody, once a backstage function of finance, is now at centre stage - it determines who holds the keys, literally and figuratively, to the next era of wealth. Whether those keys rest in a bank vault or a cold wallet, one truth is clear: custody is not merely about security, it is about sovereignty, stability and the future of trust itself.
