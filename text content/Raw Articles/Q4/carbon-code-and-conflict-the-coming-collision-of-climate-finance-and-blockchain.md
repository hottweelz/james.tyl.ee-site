# Carbon, code and conflict: the coming collision of climate finance and blockchain regulation

*By Jonny Fry | *

In the unfolding story of global climate action, finance and technology are converging in unexpected ways. The urgency to fund decarbonisation has created a trillion-dollar ecosystem of carbon markets, green bonds and sustainability-linked investments. Yet, as this ecosystem expands, it is colliding with a parallel revolution: the rise of blockchain technology and digital assets. What began as an experimental attempt to record carbon credits on-chain is rapidly evolving into a regulatory and geopolitical flashpoint. Governments, corporations and crypto innovators now find themselves negotiating not only the future of climate finance, but also the digital infrastructure through which that finance will flow.

Blockchain’s initial allure in climate finance was transparency. For decades, voluntary carbon markets have been plagued by opacity, double counting and questionable verification. Indeed, the [Centre for Strategic & International Studies](https://www.csis.org/analysis/whats-plaguing-voluntary-carbon-markets#:~:text=These%20challenges%20point%20to%20problems,to%20Address%20the%20Credibility%20Challenge) has reported:_“Voluntary carbon markets (marketplaces in which buyers voluntarily purchase and trade in offsets generated from emissions reduction or removal projects) have long been the center of a divisive debate.”_ Credits were often issued under inconsistent standards, tracked through fragmented registries and traded with limited auditability. Blockchain technology appeared to offer an elegant solution - by recording every issuance, transfer and retirement of a carbon credit on a public ledger then fraud could, in theory, be eliminated. Tokenisation could enable a carbon credit to exist as a digital asset with immutable provenance, traceable from project origin to final buyer. And this vision quickly captured attention. Projects such as [Toucan](https://toucan.earth/), [KlimaDAO](https://www.klimadao.finance/) and [Flowcarbon ](https://www.flowcarbon.com/)attempted to bridge off-chain carbon registries with on-chain tokens. Institutional players soon followed, exploring blockchain as a means to digitise renewable energy certificates, nature-based offsets and [ESG-linked financial instruments](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/e/environmental-social-and-governance-esg-criteria.asp). The rationale was straightforward: transparency and efficiency could restore trust to carbon markets, attract liquidity, and channel capital more effectively toward verified climate projects. However, the very openness that made blockchain-powered platforms attractive also unsettled regulators. Climate finance operates under strict oversight - from the [United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC)](https://unfccc.int/process-and-meetings/united-nations-framework-convention-on-climate-change) to regional emission trading schemes, such as the [EU ETS](https://climate.ec.europa.eu/eu-action/carbon-markets/eu-emissions-trading-system-eu-ets_en) (these systems rely on centralised verification and compliance reporting). [Tokenised carbon credits](https://www.carbonmark.com/post/carbon-credit-tokenization-the-digital-driver-of-real-world-environmental-impact), by contrast, challenge those hierarchies - when a carbon offset is represented as a blockchain token, jurisdictional boundaries blur. A single token might originate from a forest project in Brazil, be tokenised in Singapore and traded by investors in London, all without passing through traditional compliance channels. Regulators quickly recognised the implications, including:

> · who certifies the validity of the tokenised credit?
> 
> · who ensures that a retired on-chain credit is not reissued elsewhere off-chain?
> 
> · and perhaps most importantly, who holds legal liability when a blockchain-based credit is discovered to be fraudulent?

Certainly, the absence of clear answers has led to mounting friction. [The Verra registry](https://verra.org/registry/overview/), one of the world’s largest certifiers, suspended the tokenisation of its credits in 2023 after concerns over double-counting. Similar caution has emerged among Western regulators wary of decentralised platforms that bypass compliance frameworks. Essentially, this regulatory uncertainty has left the tokenised carbon sector in limbo - technically sophisticated, yet institutionally distrusted. At the heart of this tension lies a deeper challenge: data integrity. Blockchain may ensure transparency, but it cannot guarantee truth. A carbon token is only as reliable as the off-chain data that supports it - satellite imagery, project audits, emission baselines and community reports. If these inputs are flawed, tokenisation merely digitises bad information and the blockchain becomes an immutable record of errors rather than accountability. However, AI-powered verification is beginning to address this problem - new climate-data protocols use machine learning to analyse deforestation patterns, methane leaks and renewable energy generation in real time. When combined with blockchain, these systems could form a more reliable infrastructure for measuring and verifying environmental impact. Yet this convergence introduces new regulatory concerns: AI

models themselves require validation, data sources may be proprietary and integrating them into compliance-grade systems raises questions of transparency and intellectual property rights.

In short, solving the verification problem through code creates another layer of complexity for climate regulators already struggling to keep pace. Climate finance has never been politically neutral, and blockchain is amplifying that reality. As global powers compete to define the digital future of money, they are also vying to control the data and standards that underpin green finance. [The European Union’s MiCA regulation](https://www.legalnodes.com/article/mica-regulation-explained) is positioning Europe as the standard-setter for sustainable tokenisation, embedding environmental disclosures into crypto frameworks. The United States, meanwhile, approaches the space through a fragmented regulatory lens, focusing on securities law and consumer protection. China, for its part, is integrating carbon data into its national digital infrastructure, potentially linking its emissions-trading system with the digital yuan and, in doing so, Beijing could export a model of state-supervised green tokenisation, whereby aligning environmental reporting with [central-bank digital currency (CBDC)](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/central-bank-digital-currency-cbdc.asp) infrastructure. Yet, these diverging approaches threaten to fragment the emerging market for digital carbon assets - a carbon token recognised under one regime may not be legally valid in another. The result could be a bifurcated ecosystem, one driven by Western private-sector standards and another shaped by state-directed digital currencies and compliance systems.

Nonetheless, despite the friction, the potential scale of blockchain-enabled climate finance is enormous. [The global carbon credit market is projected to surpass $100 billion by 2030, whilst ESG investment already exceeds $30 trillion](https://www.carbontide.co/post/global-carbon-market-strategy-2025). Tokenisation could unlock new forms of liquidity, enabling retail and institutional investors alike to participate directly in environmental markets. This helps to explain why financial giants such as [JP Morgan are active in bringing carbon credits on-chain](https://www.jpmorgan.com/payments/newsroom/kda-blockchain-carbon-market-tokenization#:~:text=assets%20and%20information.-,Kinexys%20by%20J.P.%20Morgan%2C%20the%20firm's%20industry%2Dleading%20blockchain%20business,International%20Carbon%20Registry%20\(ICR\).). Yet this growth invites a different kind of risk: the capture of climate finance by speculative capital. The collapse of early “green crypto” projects, such as KlimaDAO, exposed the volatility of blending environmental goals with token incentives. Prices detached from underlying carbon fundamentals, undermining credibility among environmental stakeholders. The challenge is ensuring that blockchain serves climate outcomes, not merely trading profits, and this requires robust governance frameworks, clear standards for token issuance, verification and retirement, as well as interoperability with recognised registries. Without them, the sector risks repeating the same credibility crisis that traditional carbon markets sought to escape. Governments and standard-setting bodies are now racing to bring order to the chaos. [The World Bank](https://www.worldbank.org/ext/en/home), [OECD](https://www.oecd.org/en.html) and various national regulators are exploring frameworks for digital measurement, reporting and verification (MRV), compatible with blockchain systems. In the European Union, the [European Blockchain Services Infrastructure (EBSI)](https://ec.europa.eu/digital-building-blocks/sites/spaces/EBSI/pages/447687044/Home) is developing public-sector blockchain solutions for green reporting and emissions tracking. [The Taskforce on Scaling Voluntary Carbon Markets (TSVCM)](https://www.iif.com/tsvcm) is likewise engaging with tokenisation standards to ensure interoperability between traditional and digital registries. However, at the same time, regulators are signalling caution - the [Financial Stability Board (FSB)](https://www.fsb.org/) and the [International Organization of Securities Commissions (IOSCO)](https://www.iosco.org/) have both emphasised the need for guardrails to prevent the misuse of tokenised climate assets in speculative or fraudulent schemes.

The deeper question is philosophical: can blockchain’s ethos of decentralisation co-exist with the centralising imperatives of climate governance? Climate action requires co-ordination, verification and accountability - principles that seem at odds with the permissionless nature of DeFi. Yet this conflict may be overstated. Decentralisation does not inherently mean deregulation; rather, it offers new architectures for coordination. Smart contracts could embed compliance directly into

code, ensuring that a carbon token cannot be traded or transferred without meeting specific criteria. Decentralised verification networks could complement, rather than replace, traditional

auditors. Regulators, too, could use blockchain’s transparency to enhance oversight rather than relinquish it. The challenge is not whether code or law will prevail, but how they can be made to converge in a manner that preserves integrity and trust. A functional future for blockchain-based climate finance will require several foundational steps, including:

> · **standardisation** \- industry participants must converge on shared data and verification protocols to ensure that digital carbon assets are fungible and trustworthy.
> 
> · **interoperability** \- tokenised credits must integrate seamlessly with both existing registries and future compliance markets, avoiding fragmentation.
> 
> · **governance** \- decentralised systems must incorporate transparent governance models, whether through multi-stakeholder DAOs or public-private partnerships, that prevent manipulation while maintaining openness.
> 
> · **regulatory engagement** \- innovators must work proactively with policymakers to co-design frameworks that protect market integrity without stifling technological progress.

Only by aligning these pillars can blockchain transition from a niche experiment to a credible backbone of global climate finance. The collision of carbon, code and regulation is inevitable, and necessary. The world’s de-carbonisation challenge demands financial systems that are both transparent and scalable, capable of channelling trillions of dollars into verified climate solutions. Blockchain offers the tools to achieve that transformation, but only if integrated responsibly within existing governance structures. If mishandled, tokenised carbon markets could replicate the very failures they aim to address - opacity, speculation and distrust. However, if designed with rigor and cooperation, they could mark the next great leap in environmental accountability, where every ton of carbon reduced and every renewable megawatt produced is recorded immutably and verified globally. The stakes are unquestionably not only about the financials. They are ecological, ethical and geopolitical. The code that governs the next generation of carbon markets will shape not simply the efficiency of transactions, but the integrity of humanity’s fight against climate change.
