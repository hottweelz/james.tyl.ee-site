# One currency, four rulebooks: the global stablecoin divide

*By Jonny Fry | *

When the [first stablecoin, BitUSD](https://www.withtap.com/blog/what-weve-learned-in-a-decade-of-stablecoins#:~:text=they%20got%20there.-,The%20first%20wave%20\(2014\),for%20the%20stablecoins%20that%20followed.), launched in 2014, it was tethered to Bitcoin. USDT (Tether) launched in the same year and it was tethered to the US dollar - this marked stablecoins’ first interaction with the real world. As the years have gone by, the world has interacted with stablecoins and now regulatory oversight is finally coming into the picture. Four frameworks have emerged: MiCA in Europe, the GENIUS Act in the US, VARA in Dubai and MAS in

Singapore. These four regulatory frameworks for stablecoins balance both innovation with monetary sovereignty and consumer protection - each with distinct approaches to reserve requirements, interest payments and asset segregation. [MiCA mandates](https://www.innreg.com/blog/mica-regulation-guide) 30% bank deposits whilst [GENIUS prohibits](https://www.lw.com/en/insights/the-genius-act-of-2025-stablecoin-legislation-adopted-in-the-us) long-maturity bonds and requires separate entities for bank-issued stablecoins. Interest payments are [banned under MiCA and GENIUS](https://www.twobirds.com/en/insights/2025/useu-regulatory-divergence-in-cryptoassets-the-strategic-implications-of-the-genius-act-and-micar-fr) but potentially allowed by VARA and Singapore. In addition, reserve requirements vary from GENIUS’s strict sub-93-day treasuries to VARA’s 100% cash equivalents or 90-day sovereign debt. MiCA requires third-party custodians, whilst GENIUS grants senior creditor status. [Singapore’s MAS framework](https://www.mas.gov.sg/news/media-releases/2023/mas-finalises-stablecoin-regulatory-framework), established in 2023, focuses on credibility for single currency stablecoins with strict custodian rules and monthly attestations. Dubai’s [VARA provides](https://charltonsquantum.com/dubai-virtual-assets-regulation/) a dedicated virtual asset regulator, [MiCA prioritises](https://amlwatcher.com/blog/mica-regulation-how-the-eu-is-shaping-the-future-of-crypto-asset-compliance/) financial stability and euro protection whilst GENIUS aims for dollar dominance. Singapore and Dubai offer flexible, business-friendly regimes. The ultimate winner remains uncertain, however, the allure of stablecoins is increasingly being understood and this helps explain why we are seeing a plethora of stablecoins being issued in a variety of jurisdictions.

The GENIUS Act is likely to dominate through network effects, not regulatory superiority.[ ](https://www.elliptic.co/blog/mica-what-does-the-latest-data-tell-us-about-stablecoin-compliance)US dollar stablecoins account for [99% of total market capitalisation](https://www.jpmorgan.com/insights/global-research/currencies/stablecoins), whilst euro stablecoins remain [below €350 million](https://www.ecb.europa.eu/press/blog/date/2025/html/ecb.blog20250728~e6cb3cf8b5.en.html), and[ US firms, Tether and Circle](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/tether-now-holds-more-us-180110469.html), dominate the $300+ billion stablecoin market and became the [third-largest purchasers of US Treasury bills in 2024](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/tether-now-holds-more-us-180110469.html). MiCA’s failure stems from re-distributing dollar-stablecoin market share rather than challenging dollar dominance. Despite Tether’s market share falling from [70% to 59.9%](https://coinfomania.com/stablecoin-market-faces-competition-as-tether-loses-ground/) due to MiCA enforcement, USDT’s supply surged from [$89.1 billion to $180.9 billion](https://cryptoslate.com/tethers-181b-paradox-how-usdt-keeps-growing-as-its-market-share-collapses-under-mica/), whilst Circle’s USDC grew from [$24.3 billion to $76.3 billion](https://www.circle.com/pressroom/usdc-circulation-soars-78-year-over-year-circle-unveils-2025-state-of-the-usdc-economy-report). Euro stablecoins total only $350 million [versus](https://www.forbes.com/digital-assets/categories/stablecoins/#:~:text=The%20Stablecoins%20market%20cap%20today,$1.15) $280 billion for dollar stablecoins - a 450:1 gap. And Singapore and Dubai will thrive as arbitrage havens but reinforce, not challenge, dollar hegemony; they are building elegant frameworks for distributing US monetary dominance. But the race is over as first-mover advantage meets geopolitical strategy. Proponents assert the federal stablecoin framework will expand the market to [$2 trillion by 2030](https://www.reuters.com/business/global-markets-stablecoins-jpm-2025-10-08/) and secure dollar dominance globally.

The implementation of MiCA in the EU and Hong Kong’s ordinance may lead to arbitrage opportunities. Investors might diversify stablecoin holdings with non-US exposure as firms such as Binance or Bitstamp could gain from less restrictive regulations in emerging markets. Non-EU entities might exploit regulatory gaps by partnering with EU intermediaries, creating arbitrage risks where non-EU stablecoins could destabilise the eurozone during market stress. Significant differences in the intensity of stablecoin regulation across jurisdictions have created opportunities for issuers to engage in regulatory arbitrage, with[ experts advocating for major economies to adopt unified requirements on reserve asset audits, legal definition of redemption rights and anti-money laundering standards](https://amlwatcher.com/blog/mica-regulation-how-the-eu-is-shaping-the-future-of-crypto-asset-compliance/). Yet,[ the Financial Stability Board has found](https://www.fsb.org/2025/10/thematic-review-on-fsb-global-regulatory-framework-for-crypto-asset-activities/) that implementation progress remains incomplete, uneven and inconsistent whereby creating opportunities for regulatory arbitrage and complicating oversight of the inherently global and evolving crypto-asset and stablecoin market. Arbitrage is complicating oversight of the inherently global and evolving crypto-asset and stablecoin market - yet stablecoins have emerged as one of the fastest-growing segments in global finance, acting as foundational scaffolding underpinning a new infrastructure for payments, settlement and value transfer across borders [\- the total stablecoin market capitalisation reaching $300 billion in September 2025](https://www.jpmorgan.com/insights/global-research/currencies/stablecoins). True scaling of stablecoins will require a shift in the prevailing paradigm that requires most transactions to settle in local currency and, if the majority of customers choose to retain their funds in stablecoins, this could have far-reaching consequences for the demand for underlying reserves and implications for the deposit funding and revenue models of financial institutions. The foundations of global payments infrastructure [were laid over 60 years ago](https://www.fireblocks.com/report/state-of-stablecoins) and remained largely unchanged until now, with stablecoins becoming a key component of modern payment rails for payment providers, banks and FinTechs looking to move faster, reach new markets and stay competitive. Worthy of note, [FV Bank’s CEO views](https://www.pymnts.com/blockchain/2025/this-bank-ceo-says-stablecoins-are-the-newest-payment-rail/) stablecoins as complementary to traditional payment rails such as ACH, Fedwire and Swift, stating that where stablecoins offer superior benefits, customers will naturally gravitate toward them.

Stablecoin adoption [increased significantly in 2025](https://coinlaw.io/stablecoin-statistics/#:~:text=Stablecoins%20are%20increasingly%20used%20for,offer%20returns%20below%205%25%20APY.), with some platforms reporting over 50% of their remittance volume powered by stablecoins together with stablecoin-powered remittance services reducing fees by 30-60% in some cases, with average global fees around 2-3% compared to 6% with traditional banks. [Nigeria leads this adoption globally](https://businessday.ng/technology/article/nigeria-tops-global-stablecoin-adoption-as-africas-digital-asset-booms/) with customers using it to hedge against inflation and access global financial services. Emerging market businesses, from large corporations to SMBs, are employing stablecoins to mitigate currency risk, settle international invoices and pay local salaries tied to the US dollar whereby replacing traditional foreign exchange or offshore banking with digital wallets. Stablecoins act as the base pairs in the majority of crypto trading, lending and yield farming with estimates by the US Federal Reserve Board indicating that more than [80% of trade volume](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/financial-services/our-insights/the-stable-door-opens-how-tokenized-cash-enables-next-gen-payments) on major centralised crypto exchanges involves stablecoins as part of the traded pair. Meanwhile, blockchain-based telecom payments platform, Zeebu, used stablecoins to scale its cross-border settlement system, [processing $5.7 billion in transactions](https://www.fireblocks.com/report/state-of-stablecoins) and settling 99,000 B2B invoices across 139 active carriers.

For a stablecoin to be taken seriously today, it does not need to brand itself with a name such as “realcoin” as Tether did in 2014. The convergence of legislative action, executive promotion and surging institutional interest has unequivocally legitimised stablecoins within the global financial infrastructure. The passage of the GENIUS Act, coupled with critical milestones such as Circle’s IPO and the US President’s executive order, has provided the regulatory certainty and definitional clarity that institutional investors sought. With a burgeoning crypto market and significant investment from financial giants, stablecoins are no longer a nascent technology but a foundational element reshaping traditional finance. This period marks a pivotal shift where stablecoins are not merely regulated but fully embraced, so paving the way for these four horsemen to ride. Everything else follows....
