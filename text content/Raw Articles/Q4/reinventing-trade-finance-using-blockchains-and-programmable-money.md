# Reinventing trade finance using blockchains and programmable money

*By Jonny Fry | *

Global supply chains link manufacturers, suppliers, distributors and retailers across continents, fuelling global trade. According to [the latest Global Trade Update](https://unctad.org/publication/global-trade-update-march-2025) by UN Trade and Development (UNCTAD): “ _Global trade reached over**$33 trillion in 2024, expanding 3.7% ($1.2 trillion).”**_**** Inefficiencies, laborious processes, opaque transactions and liquidity limitations plague these supply chains' financial architecture. Yet traditional supply chain financing solutions, whilst essential for commerce, frequently include late payments, lack of transparency and higher credit risks - especially for SMEs.

[Supply chain finance with embedded payments and tokenised inventory](https://www.comviva.com/blog/the-future-of-payments-embedded-and-tokenized/) (SCF-EPTI) changes everything. Inventory tokenisation on a distributed ledger allows real-time tracking, verifiable ownership and smooth transferability in this blockchain-enabled solution. Payment flows are incorporated in the supply chain process and triggered by smart contracts at predetermined milestones. Programmable money in the form of central bank digital currencies (CBDCs) or stablecoins provide new global trade finance efficiency, liquidity and confidence. But the problem with traditional [supply chain financing (SCF)](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/supply-chain-finance.asp) solutions is that they optimise cash flow for buyers and suppliers by extending payment periods and allowing suppliers to obtain early payments. The goal is to reduce chain-wide working capital demands - but the reality is much more complicated.

Manual reconciliations, paper invoicing and intermediaries make most SCF procedures inefficient and expensive. Lack of real-time inventory and shipping visibility causes payment disputes, credit concerns and protracted payment cycles. Global supply chains depend on SMEs, which face liquidity constraints, increased financing costs and restricted trade finance facilities due to these inefficiencies. And, due to their reliance on major purchasers' creditworthiness, classic SCF models typically exclude smaller participants without sufficient collateral or credit ratings. Decoupling physical supply chains from financial flows causes systemic friction - financial institutions need days or weeks to reconcile and finalise payments for products that move in physical space. This temporal mismatch causes cash flow constraints, higher capital costs and systemic risks.

However, by using blockchain technology it is now possible to tokenise inventory thus transform physical goods into digital assets. Inventory token information includes product kind, origin, production specs, ownership status and enables real-time location using IoT devices to be tracked. This digital token provides a single source of truth for supply chain stakeholders by proving ownership and validity immutably. Tokenised inventory provides unmatched transparency and traceability - as products travel through manufacturing, warehousing, shipping and delivery, the blockchain updates inventory token statuses in real time. This constant visibility therefore removes supply chain blind spots, so allowing buyers, suppliers, lenders and regulators to precisely track items. Additionally, tokenisation allows partial ownership and collateralisation - even before selling or delivering merchandise, suppliers can tokenise a batch and use the tokens as collateral for financing. This feature helps SMEs overcome credit constraints by accessing working cash based on real-time inventory value.

Furthermore, it is now possible using blockchain- powered programmable payments to automate transactions 24/7 conditional on certain actions - for example, when goods are delivered. In SCF-EPTI, payments are integrated into supply chain workflows rather being handled manually. Programmable smart contracts create payment criteria and trigger automated financial transfers at milestones. A smart contract might pay a supplier 30% upon production completion, 40% upon shipping dispatch and 30% upon final delivery and inspection confirmation. According to [Global Trade Review](https://www.gtreview.com/news/global/late-payment-issues-worsening-in-global-supply-chains-taulia-data-reveals/): “ _A growing number of firms reporting delays of more than 45 days and over 515 of companies are paid late._ ” However, using self-executing code eliminates the need for manual intervention or third-party escrow banking arrangements. Settlement with programmable money such as CBDCs or stablecoins improves efficiency whereby enabling near-instantaneous, low-cost settlements. The [“pay-as-you-go” technique of embedded payments](https://www.digitalroute.com/resources/glossary/payg/) aligns cash flow with goods movement. Synchronising physical and financial flows decreases supply chain working capital pressures, payment default risks and liquidity inequality.

Essentially, one of SCF-EPTI's biggest effects is reducing counterparty and operational risks. Suppliers traditionally take credit risk, depending on purchasers' payment discipline and financial intermediaries' efficiency. SCF-EPTI reduces payment delays and disputes by integrating payment triggers in the supply chain process to automatically pay for objective, verifiable criteria. The immutability of blockchain records boosts stakeholder trust. Every transaction, inventory change and payment execution is time-stamped and cryptographically encrypted, so creating a clear and tamper-proof audit trail. Furthermore, verifiability improves confidence, speeds reconciliation and lowers fraud and data manipulation; this transparent ecology benefits financial institutions. Lenders can better analyse risk profiles and offer borrowers better lending conditions with real-time inventory and payment flow insight and SMEs benefit from trade finance democratisation since they may get funding based on asset-backed, real-time data rather than credit ratings.

Moreover, interoperability with supply chain management systems and IoT devices is crucial to SCF-EPTI's success. IoT sensors that give real-time data on product location, condition and movement improve inventory tokenisation. IoT-enabled smart contracts can notify or conditionally pay for temperature-sensitive shipments. SCF-EPTI platforms may also be created with APIs to integrate warehouse management and logistics tracking systems - this allows stakeholders to use the blockchain-based solution without revamping their IT infrastructure, easing onboarding and acceptance. Furthermore, using IoT data and blockchain tokenisation, every supply chain asset has a “digital twin” that represents its real-time state. This integration of physical and digital realities improves inventory management, decision-making and supply chain agility. Clear regulatory frameworks that recognise tokenised inventories as financial assets and specify smart contract legal enforceability in commercial transactions are needed to grow SCF-EPTI. Governments and financial authorities must define digital asset classification, ownership, taxation and dispute resolution norms. There is also the need to provide regulatory clarity and legal certainty when using programmable money in order that such digital payments can indeed be accepted as legal tender.

There are certainly signs this is beginning to happen in the US - [The Block](https://www.theblock.co/post/365554/sec-staff-guidance-stablecoins) has reported that the new guidance from the SEC indicates stablecoins pegged to the US dollar could be treated as cash. Testing the SCF-EPTI model in controlled conditions in regulatory sandboxes and pilot programs will help stakeholders discover issues, improve standards and enhance public confidence. Interoperable tokenised supply chain finance standards from international trade agencies and standards organisations can provide cross-border recognition and compatibility. Hence, tokenised inventories and embedded payments ought to become standard trade finance tools in a globally harmonised environment. Furthermore, the introduction of SCF-EPTI will alter global trade; the concept decreases the capital costs of supply chain enterprises by speeding up payment cycles, increasing liquidity and democratising trade finance. Due to collateral limits and credit rating requirements, SMEs are typically excluded from traditional funding channels and so this improved liquidity benefits them. Low administrative expenses, faster order fulfilment and increased supply chain resilience result from real-time inventory management, automated payments and decreased reconciliation. Businesses can adjust faster to demand changes, supply interruptions and geopolitical shocks, so creating a more competitive trading ecosystem. SCF-EPTI promotes blockchain, digital currencies and IoT infrastructure macroeconomically. Countries that actively adopt this strategy will boost trade competitiveness, SME ecosystems and financial services innovation. SCF-EPTI's transparency and auditability allow tracking of commodities' origin, carbon footprints and ethical sourcing compliance, whereby supporting sustainability programs. In a global market where customers and regulators expect more corporate accountability and environmental stewardship, traceability is crucial.

However, SCF-EPTI's mass adoption is challenging despite its transformational promise. Public blockchains must manage enormous transaction volumes without compromising speed or security, making technological scalability an issue. But layer-2 scaling protocols and private-permissioned blockchains will help solve these performance issues by managing data privacy and cybersecurity threats carefully. Blockchain systems prioritise openness, yet sensitive commercial data including contract terms, pricing structures and shipment information must be encrypted and secured. Another obstacle is regulatory ambiguity, notably surrounding digital assets, digital payments and smart contracts. To facilitate worldwide adoption and ensure tokenised transaction legality, cross-border regulatory harmonisation is essential. Moreover, stakeholder buy-in and cultural aversion to technology change may impede adoption. Awareness, pilot initiatives and public-private collaborations will help overcome these hurdles. By tokenising inventory on blockchains and embedding programmable payments directly into supply chains, financial and physical movements can finally be synchronised. Goods are no longer simply shipments on a ledger but digital tokens carrying proof of ownership, real-time location and collateral value. Smart contracts can trigger payments automatically when milestones are reached, using programmable money such as CBDCs or stablecoins to settle instantly and transparently. And the implications are profound. Liquidity flows faster and more fairly across global supply chains; SMEs gain access to working capital they could never unlock under legacy systems. Moreover, trust between buyers, suppliers and lenders is reinforced by immutable records and verifiable transactions. Financial institutions can price credit with greater accuracy whilst supply chains become more resilient to shocks.

Yet the challenge is not the vision but the execution. Regulatory clarity, technological scalability and global standards will decide whether SCF-EPTI remains a pilot project or becomes the new backbone of trade finance. If realised, it marks a shift from opaque, friction-filled systems to a real-time, trust-driven architecture where money and goods move together at the speed of trade.
