# How AI is re-engineering the psychology of wealth

*By Jonny Fry | *

As [$124 trillion](https://fortune.com/2025/07/23/great-wealth-transfer-124-trillion-bigger-than-ever-millennials-gen-x/) of wealth transfers to a new generation, artificial intelligence and changing attitudes reshape the investment landscape. If fear is the investors’ greatest enemy, artificial intelligence may prove their most valuable ally. Robo-advisors now manage over [$1 trillion globally and, in the US alone, 6million people](https://coinlaw.io/robo-advisors-market-statistics/) rely on Robo advice, with [PwC estimating](https://www.pwc.com/gx/en/news-room/press-releases/2023/pwc-2023-global-asset-and-wealth-management-survey.html#:~:text=PwC%20predicts%20assets%20managed%20by,global%20ETF%20market%20by%202027.) this could reach $5.9 trillion by 2027. But AI’s role extends far beyond simple portfolio allocation - the most sophisticated systems are becoming psychological coaches, gently steering investors away from fear-driven mistakes whilst encouraging disciplined, systematic strategies.

One of AI’s most powerful applications lies in enabling systematic £-cost/$-cost/€-cost averaging (PCA) into volatile assets. The concept is simple - invest a fixed amount at regular intervals, i.e. weekly/monthly, regardless of market conditions. The execution, however, requires emotional discipline that few humans naturally possess. Consider Bitcoin, which experienced multiple 50%+ drawdowns over the past decade. An investor who attempted to “time the market” likely experienced significant fear-driven errors. According to [Hartford Funds](https://www.hartfordfunds.com/practice-management/client-conversations/managing-volatility/timing-the-market-is-impossible.html#:~:text=If%20you%20missed%20the%20market's,returns%20by%20an%20astonishing%2083%25.&text=Past%20performance%20does%20not%20guarantee%20future%20results.,-For%20illustrative%20purposes), having examined the S&P 500 stock market performance: “ _If you missed the market’s 10 best days over the past 30 years, your returns would have been cut in half. And missing the best 30 days would have reduced your returns by an astonishing 83%.”_ By contrast, an AI-managed system executing £500 monthly investments since 2018 would have navigated every crash and rally without emotional interference.

The “manual PCA (attempted)” category reflects reality: humans plan to invest systematically but often pause during downturns, precisely when continued buying offers greatest value. [AI systems eliminate this emotional interference](https://www.rapidinnovation.io/post/robo-advisors-transforming-investment-advice-with-ai). Perhaps AI’s most sophisticated application involves dynamic asset allocation as portfolios grow. Consider a young professional beginning with £10,000: with limited capital and decades until retirement, higher volatility makes sense. That same investor , twenty years later with £500,000 accumulated, needs different exposure. Advanced Robo-advisors now employ [machine learning algorithms](https://www.yieldstreet.com/blog/article/ai-robo-advisors/) that adjust risk exposure based on multiple factors, including:

> · portfolio size and growth trajectory
> 
> · time until stated financial goals
> 
> · market volatility levels
> 
> · correlation between assets
> 
> · tax efficiency considerations.

This creates a “glide path” that systematically reduces volatility exposure as wealth accumulates. Early in one’s investment journey, AI might advocate 80% equities and 20% bonds - as capital grows and goals approach, this shifts towards 60/40, then 50/50 and potentially 40/60 for those approaching retirement. Indeed,__[studies show](https://coinlaw.io/ai-powered-robo-trading-statistics/) _[](https://coinlaw.io/ai-powered-robo-trading-statistics/)_[that AI-managed portfolios](https://coinlaw.io/ai-powered-robo-trading-statistics/) achieve 35% better risk-adjusted returns over 10-year periods, primarily by eliminating emotional decision-making during market stress. And the largest wealth transfer in human history is under way: [baby boomers will pass approximately $124 trillion to younger generations](https://fortune.com/2025/07/23/great-wealth-transfer-124-trillion-bigger-than-ever-millennials-gen-x/) in the next 25 years. Moreover, this is not merely a transfer of capital but a fundamental shift in investment psychology and service requirements. Younger investors who inherit wealth demonstrate markedly different attitudes

towards volatility and fear than those who accumulated wealth gradually. Research from [Bank of America’s Private Bank](https://www.ml.com/articles/great-wealth-transfer-impact.html) reveals striking generational differences:

These differences have profound implications; traditional wealth management firms built around ‘golf course’ relationships and quarterly face-to-face reviews face existential challenges. [Research suggests](https://integrated-financial-group.com/resources/the-great-wealth-transfer-2025-building-multigenerational-relationships/) that 70% of inherited wealth leaves the family’s current financial advisor within 18 months. Inherited wealth creates a unique psychological dynamic - those who built fortunes through decades of work, saving and investment develop deep emotional attachments to their capital. Every £/$/€ represents sacrifice, discipline and delayed gratification but inheritors, by contrast, receive windfalls they did not directly earn. This creates what behavioural economists call the _“house money effect”_ \- a tendency to take greater risks with “found” money than with earned income. Early evidence suggests younger inheritors do, indeed, exhibit lower fear responses to volatility. A [survey of millennials expecting inheritance](https://fortune.com/2025/07/18/great-wealth-transfer-gen-x-1-4-trillion-millennials-boomers/) found that 55% planned to increase allocation to growth assets, 42% intended to explore alternative investments and 38% would increase crypto exposure beyond what their parents held. Meanwhile, financial services firms are scrambling to adapt to younger clients’ expectations. The traditional model, built around high-touch personal service and quarterly reviews, no longer resonates with digital natives comfortable managing investments via smartphone. What younger inheritors demand are:

> · **technology-first platforms** \- [millennials expect](https://www.hashstudioz.com/blog/how-ai-is-transforming-financial-planning-through-robo-advisors/) real-time portfolio access, instant rebalancing capabilities and AI-driven insights delivered through elegant mobile interfaces. Annual fees of 1% for quarterly meetings feel antiquated when Robo-advisors offer similar services for 0.25%.
> 
> · **values alignment** \- environmental, social and governance (ESG) considerations are not add-ons, but core requirements. [Bank of America data shows](https://www.ml.com/articles/great-wealth-transfer-impact.html) that 73% of millennial inheritors screen investments for ESG compliance, compared to only 32% of boomers.
> 
> · **education over advice** \- rather than being told what to do, younger investors pursue tools to make informed decisions. Interactive financial planning software, educational content and transparent fee structures matter more than personal relationships with advisors who they may meet twice yearly.
> 
> · **flexibility and customisation** \- the old model offered three risk profiles: conservative, moderate and aggressive. Modern platforms offer dozens of customisation options,
> 
> allowing investors to express specific views on sectors, geographies and asset classes whilst maintaining AI-managed discipline.

The solution is not simply pure Robo-advice or traditional relationships, but intelligent hybrids. [Leading firms are developing](https://www.neosofttech.com/blogs/ai-wealth-management-robo-advisors/) platforms that combine AI-driven daily portfolio management, access to human advisors for major decisions or complex situations, educational content tailored to experience level, community features allowing peer learning together with integrated tax planning and estate considerations. Vanguard’s Digital Advisor exemplifies this approach, blending algorithmic portfolio management with access to certified financial planners for $300 annually, plus 0.15% of assets under management. More than [90% of investors](https://coinlaw.io/robo-advisors-market-statistics/) under the age of forty in 2025 have preferred Robo-advisors due to their lower fees and ease of use - more importantly, the providers of Robo-advice retention rates for leading platforms exceeded 85% in 2025. Ultimately, successful wealth transfer requires addressing fear at multiple levels. Older generations fear their life’s work being squandered through reckless speculation: younger generations fear missing opportunities or being constrained by outdated thinking and a lack of exposure to alternative investments. But AI and modern portfolio theory offer a middle path - systematic strategies can accommodate higher risk exposure whilst maintaining discipline. And dynamic allocation allows portfolios to evolve as circumstances change. Transparent fee structures and real-time reporting build trust between generations who may hold very different views about money. Practical strategies for inheritors include:

> · **pause before acting** -**** Nasdaq reported [that waiting 6-12 months](https://www.nasdaq.com/articles/suze-orman-how-long-you-must-wait-spending-or-investing-your-inheritance#:~:text=Why%206%20Months?,readjust%20to%20your%20new%20normal.) after receiving inheritance leads to better outcomes than immediate changes - therefore, use this time to educate yourself and clarify goals.
> 
> · **implement gradually** \- rather than re-allocating entirely to new strategies, test approaches with 10-20% of assets first. This reduces regret if choices prove sub-optimal.
> 
> · **leverage AI for discipline** \- set up automated systems for rebalancing and systematic investing. Let algorithms execute strategies whilst you focus on major decisions.
> 
> · **maintain emergency reserves** -**** keep 12-18 months of expenses in liquid, conservative investments. This buffer eliminates forced selling during downturns.
> 
> · **separate growth and income** \- consider a “barbell” strategy: conservative assets generating income for near-term needs, aggressive assets for long-term growth.

**Key insight -**[advisors who successfully navigate](https://www.bankrate.com/investing/the-great-wealth-transfer/) the wealth transfer engage entire families in planning, not merely the primary wealth holder. Family meetings involving multiple generations reduce conflict and build shared understanding.

Looking ahead, the convergence of AI, tokenised assets, prediction markets and generational change points towards a more democratic yet sophisticated investment landscape. AI will continue reducing emotional decision-making whilst enabling more personalised strategies. As [Robo-advisor assets reach](https://coinlaw.io/robo-advisors-market-statistics/) an estimated $479billion by 2032, these tools will become primary rather than supplementary investment vehicles. Tokenisation will unlock liquidity in traditionally illiquid and alternative assets, reducing the fear of being trapped into an investment. The ability to exit

positions in real estate, private credit or collectibles within minutes rather than months fundamentally changes risk calculations. Prediction markets will mature from speculation venues

into serious information sources of sentiment of crowds, helping investors gauge probability distributions around key events affecting their portfolios. Most importantly, younger investors

entering the market with different expectations and lower fear responses to volatility may reshape investing culture itself. If millennials and Gen Z maintain higher risk tolerance through their peak earning and investing years, market dynamics could shift significantly. Investment fear is not something to eliminate but to manage. Those who feel no fear often take reckless risks that destroy wealth - those paralysed by fear never build it. The goal is calibrated fear: enough to maintain discipline but not enough to prevent action. Technology, properly deployed, helps achieve this balance - AI removes emotional interference whilst maintaining systematic strategies. Prediction markets aggregate information, reducing uncertainty and tokenisation provides liquidity escape hatches. Moreover, education builds confidence through understanding.

As $124 trillion changes hands over coming decades, the investors who thrive will combine traditional wisdom, modern tools and intergenerational perspective - they will use AI to execute strategies that human emotion would derail; they will leverage crowd intelligence whilst maintaining independent thinking; and they will embrace new asset classes whilst respecting fundamental principles. The fear of investing may be eternal, but our tools for managing it have never been more powerful. The question is not whether to feel fear, but what to do with it. And increasingly, the answer involves letting intelligent systems handle execution whilst humans focus on goals, values and the long game. In the end, successful investing across generations requires accepting that markets will always be volatile, predictions will often be wrong and fear will never disappear entirely. What changes is our ability to continue investing wisely despite these constants - and that is where technology, education and evolving attitudes combine to create opportunity from uncertainty.

_The future of investing is not fearless - it is intelligently afraid_
