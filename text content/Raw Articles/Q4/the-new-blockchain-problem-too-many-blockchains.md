# The new blockchain problem: too many blockchains

*By Jonny Fry | *

As institutions finally embrace blockchain technology, they are creating an ecosystem even more fragmented than the decentralised one they sought to tame; the original Web3 vision championed decentralisation as liberation from centralised gatekeepers. Bitcoin, Ethereum and their descendants offered a unified dream: interoperable networks where assets move seamlessly without asking permission. Web3 also promised a decentralised future where value flows freely across borderless networks. However, as banks and traditional financial institutions have embraced blockchain technology, they have brought their institutional instincts with them - and those instincts favour control over collaboration.

Today, JPMorgan has its own blockchain, [Onyx](https://onyx.org/), Goldman Sachs is [building its own digital asset platform](https://www.goldmansachs.com/pressroom/press-releases/2024/announcement-18-nov-2024) whilst HSBC, BNY Mellon and dozens of other banks are each constructing proprietary blockchain infrastructures. And rather than converging on shared rails, the institutional adoption of blockchain technology has spawned a Cambrian explosion of isolated, potentially incompatible chains. Despite years of industry consortia, working groups and “collaborative initiatives”, financial institutions have largely retreated to their own corners - there is no single chain that Bank of America, Citigroup and Deutsche Bank all use. Hence, no shared infrastructure allows JPMorgan’s digital dollar to seamlessly interact with HSBC’s tokenised securities, and, for stablecoins, this has created an opportunity for Ubyx which has raised 410million to create a platform to enable different stablecoins to be settled.

Meanwhile, there are other initiatives such as the [Canton blockchain](https://www.canton.network/) in Switzerland and the [Regulated Liability Network](https://www.ukfinance.org.uk/tokenised-sterling-deposits) in the UK for tokenised deposits but, in essence, the institutional reality is centralised yet fractured by competing implementations. We have moved from one form of fragmentation to another, and it is unclear which is worse. The proliferation of isolated bank blockchains threatens to create a cross-border payments nightmare worse than the correspondent banking system it aims to replace. When JPMorgan, HSBC, BNY Mellon and dozens of other institutions each operate proprietary chains with different protocols, governance models and technical standards, then every cross-border transaction requires custom integration work, multiple settlement layers and reconciliation processes that negate blockchain’s efficiency gains.[ The OMFIF warns](https://www.omfif.org/2025/11/the-missing-piece-of-the-fragmentation-problem/) that fragmentation is now “the missing piece” of the digital finance puzzle whilst[ the New York Fed highlights](https://libertystreeteconomics.newyorkfed.org/2025/03/interoperability-of-blockchain-systems-and-the-future-of-payments/) how blockchain fragmentation undermines the future of payments. Instead of money flowing seamlessly across borders, we are building digital borders between bank chains that are harder to cross than physical ones. The cruel irony is that blockchain was supposed to eliminate correspondent banking relationships - now, each proprietary chain becomes its own mini-correspondent bank, requiring new relationships, new APIs and new trust frameworks to connect.[ Cross-border payments face a connectivity crisis in an era of payments fragmentation](https://gfmag.com/transaction-banking/promoting-cross-border-connectivity-in-an-era-of-payments-fragmentation/) and bank blockchains are making it worse, not better.

Banks are moving from blockchain pilots to production infrastructure because the technology has finally matured beyond the hype cycle, regulatory frameworks are crystallising and competitors are forcing their hand.[ JPMorgan’s announcement](https://fortune.com/2025/05/15/jpmorgan-public-blockchain-standard-institutional-finance-cfo/) about public blockchain standards for institutional finance signals that blockchain is no longer experimental - it is becoming competitive infrastructure. After years of proof-of-concepts that demonstrated cost savings of as much as [80% for cross boarder payments](https://www.phoenixstrategy.group/blog/how-blockchain-reduces-cross-border-payment-fees#:~:text=Business%20Examples%20and%20Case%20Studies%20Real%2Dworld%20examples,settlement%20times%2C%20leading%20to%20better%20operational%20efficiency.) (as well as for settlement operations and near-instant transaction finality),[ the business case for blockchain in banking is now undeniable](https://www.pymnts.com/blockchain/2025/5-blockchain-projects-the-worlds-biggest-banks-are-behind). The shift is also driven by fear: banks that do not build blockchain capabilities risk being disintermediated by those that do, and[ institutional adoption hinges on the DevOps capabilities that banks are now acquiring](https://devops.com/enterprise-blockchain-adoption-hinges-on-devops/). Furthermore,[ stablecoins are forcing banks’ hands](https://treasurup.com/stablecoins-strategic-playbook-banks-2025/) as these digital currencies threaten to route around traditional banking infrastructure entirely unless banks offer competitive blockchain-based alternatives. Undoubtedly, the experimental phase is over because the existential threat is here; banks either build now or become an obsolete infrastructure in someone else’s financial system.

Yet, despite this, institutional-focused public blockchains have not lost the battle; they have only been systematically excluded from it by regulatory requirements and institutional risk appetites that demand permissioned environments.[ Public versus private blockchain debates](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5420016#:~:text=Public%20block%20chains%2Dcharacterized%20by,specific%20security%20and%20operational%20requirements.) consistently reveal that financial institutions prioritise compliance, data privacy and governance control over the censorship resistance and permissionless innovation offered by public chains. Whilst[ Ethereum advocates argue the network is ready for institutional adoption](https://blockchainreporter.net/blockchain-is-fast-enough-for-institutional-adoption-but-what-else-does-it-need), banks are not convinced they can meet know-your-customer requirements, anti-money-laundering standards or data protection regulations on transparent public ledgers. A further challenge of using a blockchain such as Ethereum is because it has nodes in a variety of jurisdictions. In the future, for example, will the US demand a % of any transaction fees on a payment that was made between two parties in Europe, simply because some of the Ethereum nodes are located in the US? Furthermore, do the courts of England and Wales have legal certainty on nodes located outside of England and Wales? [Blockchain adoption rates show that enterprises](https://douglevin.substack.com/p/the-state-of-blockchain-adoption) value different properties than crypto-natives - they pursue privacy over transparency, permissioned access over permissionless innovation and institutional control over algorithmic neutrality. Public chains optimised for DeFi and retail crypto users, built the wrong product for the institutional market, they solved for censorship resistance when banks wanted compliance infrastructure, for permissionless access when institutions demanded gatekeeping, and for transparency when enterprises required confidentiality.The battle was lost not on technical grounds but on fundamental value alignment: public chains and traditional finance require money to be under blockchain technology.

Certainly, money cannot be truly fungible when it exists as incompatible digital representations across fragmented blockchain networks - each with different settlement mechanisms, smart contract languages and governance structures.[ The interoperability crisis is now recognised as a major problem](https://www.coindesk.com/opinion/2024/12/31/blockchain-fragmentation-is-a-major-problem-that-must-be-addressed-in-2025) that must be solved for blockchain to fulfil its promise; even the World Economic Forum [notes that interoperability remains “the missing piece”](https://www.weforum.org/stories/2025/05/digital-finance-assets-interoperability/) of the digital finance puzzle. When a US dollar tokenised on JPMorgan’s chain cannot natively interact with a euro tokenised on Deutsche Bank’s chain, then cross-border commerce requires conversion mechanisms, trusted bridges and additional counterparty risk: exactly the friction that blockchain was supposed to eliminate.[ The challenges extend beyond technical compatibility](https://londonblockchain.net/blog/expert-perspective/blockchain-interoperability-challenges-and-solutions/) to legal enforceability, regulatory jurisdiction and liability frameworks that differ across chains. The result is that “tokenised money” today behaves less like fungible currency, more akin to incompatible foreign exchange instruments that require conversion infrastructure. The real winners in the fragmented bank blockchain divergence will not be the banks themselves but the infrastructure providers who make incompatible chains talk to each other - and[ SWIFT is positioning itself to own this layer](https://www.swift.com/news-events/press-releases/swift-add-blockchain-based-ledger-its-infrastructure-stack-groundbreaking-move-accelerate-and-scale-benefits-digital-finance).[ SWIFT’s integration with Chainlink for cross-chain interoperability](https://sarsonfunds.com/swift-chainlink-integration-set-for-november-2025-from-pilot-to-live-deployment/), set for deployment in late 2025, positions the traditional messaging giant as the middleware layer connecting proprietary bank chains.[ Chainlink’s cross-chain interoperability protocol (CCIP)](https://blog.chain.link/introducing-the-cross-chain-interoperability-protocol-ccip/) offers banks a standardised way to bridge incompatible blockchains without surrendering control of their individual chains, creating a lucrative toll-booth position for the protocol.

Meanwhile,[ middleware protocols and bridge solutions](https://www.blockchain-council.org/blockchain/how-will-cross-chain-interoperability-shape-blockchain-in-2025/) are attracting significant institutional investment because whoever controls the bridges controls the flow of value across the fragmented landscape. The opportunity mirrors the internet era - just as Cisco profited enormously from selling routers that connected incompatible networks, bridge builders will extract value from every transaction that crosses chain boundaries.[ SWIFT’s move to add blockchain-based ledgers](https://www.tradefinanceglobal.com/posts/sibos-2025-swift-add-blockchain-based-ledger-vaulting-towards-instant-cross-border-payments/) signals that legacy infrastructure providers understand the game; they are not trying to win the blockchain wars, they are positioning themselves as Switzerland - the neutral intermediary everyone needs regardless of which proprietary chain they have chosen. Blockchain technology promised a unified, borderless financial system - it was supposed to unify finance. Instead, banks are splintering it: rather than building on shared, open rails, major institutions are creating **exclusive, permissioned chains** , such as Onyx, Canton, Goldman’s Digital Assets Platform: each compliant but mutually incompatible. The result? A digital replay of correspondent banking, not the replacement of it. Instead of frictionless, borderless money, we are witnessing **dozens of sovereign bank-ledgers that are unable to ‘talk’ to each other, not to mention a lack of global regulatory and legal standards**. Every transfer becomes a bespoke integration problem - precisely the inefficiency blockchains were meant to solve.

Increasingly blockchain-powered platforms have not removed intermediaries, they have simply _created new ones._ The future of digital finance may belong not to those who issue tokenised assets, but to those who make them intelligible to each other.
