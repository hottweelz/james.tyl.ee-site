# The rise of perpetual privacy DEXs

*By Jonny Fry | *

The rise of perpetual DEXs, in particular, signals a potential shift in power. This sentiment has been recently echoed by Binance founder, Changpeng Zhao (CZ), creator of the biggest crypto currency exchange in the world. CZ has [stated that DEX trading volume](https://www.binance.com/en/square/post/28956975916657) could eventually surpass that of CEX and, during a speech, offered a glimpse into his vision for the future, acknowledging that if he could go back twenty years, he would focus on developing an AI trading agent and a privacy-focused perpetual DEX. He further elaborated that this type of decentralised platform would offer a more secure and private trading environment, whereby directly addressing some of the key vulnerabilities associated with traditional CEXs. Indeed, this prediction underscores the rise of perpetual privacy DEXs, with[ Bingx.com reporting](https://bingx.com/en/learn/top-defi-perpetual-protocol-dex-to-know): “ _Hyperliquid dominated the decentralized perpetual market with 70–80% market share, handling over $350 billion in monthly volume and frequently exceeding $30 billion per day. The platform’s revenue soared past $100 million per month.”_

Essentially, perpetual privacy DEXs are platforms that allow you to trade leveraged derivatives without an expiry date, all whilst keeping your activity hidden from public blockchain analysis. This is achieved through groundbreaking privacy technologies such as [zero-knowledge proofs](https://www.circularise.com/blogs/zero-knowledge-proofs-explained-in-3-examples#:~:text=help%20your%20business-,A%20zero%2Dknowledge%20proof%20\(ZKP\)%20is%20a%20method%20of,validity%20of%20the%20statement%20itself.) (zk-proofs) which allow the network to verify your trades without revealing sensitive information, such as your wallet address, position size or profit and loss. By depositing into a shielded pool, trading activity becomes a private affair, visible only to the trader. This enables users to demonstrate that they have sufficient collateral for a trade without exposing their balance or identity. In addition, to sever the on-chain link between deposits and trading activity, these protocols often utilise [mixers](https://www.coinmetro.com/learning-lab/crypto-mixers-privacy-tools-and-regulatory-challenges) or [shielded pools](https://docs.pantherprotocol.io/docs/panther-core/panther-core/shielded-pool) which cryptographically scramble transaction paths. Shielded pools are privacy-preserving smart contract structures in blockchain, such as in Zcash or Panther Protocol and use zk-proofs to encrypt transaction data (such as sender, receiver and amounts) within the pool. This enables confidential transfers whilst still allowing network validation, so helping users maintain anonymity and selective disclosure for compliance purposes. Dark pools, on the other hand, are private trading venues typically used by institutional investors to execute large orders anonymously without exposing trade details or impacting market prices. They aim to reduce market impact and price slippage by hiding order size and timing until after execution. Notably, dark pools have been sued extensively in traditional financial markets for years.

Meanwhile, the rise of these platforms is being driven by a powerful convergence of factors and the proven demand for on-chain perpetuals (as seen with protocols such as [GMX](https://gmx.io/)) and has laid the groundwork. Coupled with the increasing sophistication of MEV attacks and a growing desire for financial privacy, the market is ripe for disruption. Technological maturation, especially in zk-proofs on networks such as Starknet and Solana, has finally made this level of privacy technically feasible.

For the trading mechanics themselves, platforms employ private order books and encrypted matching engines - instead of broadcasting intent to the public blockchain, orders are encrypted and submitted to a network. The matching engine, often powered by secure enclaves such as trusted execution environments (TEEs), then performs the computation to match buy and sell orders without ever decrypting the sensitive details. This combination ensures that the entire trade lifecycle occurs within a veil of privacy, moving beyond the transparent and exploitable nature of traditional decentralised exchanges. [Aster leads](https://www.ainvest.com/news/aster-project-revolutionizing-privacy-decentralized-trading-2509/) with its “hidden orders”, shielding trade direction and size until execution - a design aimed at blunting front-running whilst still promising deep liquidity. Aster also aligns with privacy mandates such as the EU’s [Digital Operational Resilience Act](https://www.eiopa.europa.eu/digital-operational-resilience-act-dora_en) (DORA); [PriveX](https://prvx.io/), launched on [COTI,](https://www.coti.news/news/privex-on-coti-whats-been-achieved-so-far-and-what-comes-next) takes this further with [garbled circuits](https://docs.coti.io/coti-documentation/how-coti-works/advanced-topics/garbled-circuits) and [intent-based execution](https://www.ericsson.com/en/managed-services/ai-intent-based-operations#:~:text=Intent%2Dbased%20operations%20is%20based,network%20to%20achieve%20the%20goal.), building what amounts to a dark pool for perpetuals where trader intent is never publicly broadcast; [Hibachi, built with modular data layers and zk-proofs](https://web3.bitget.com/vi/dapp/hibachi-24742), hides positions and balances altogether, prioritising anonymity over transparency; and [Defx, backed by fresh funding,](https://defx.com/) positions itself as a Layer-1 dark-pool DEX, encrypting order flow and even concealing profit and loss during a trade’s lifecycle. So, whilst these designs give surface to new challenges in liquidity, user trust and regulatory oversight, they also promise discretion and protection against maximal extractable value (MEV) bots: it is estimated that MEV bots have generated over [$1billion of profits](https://www.debutinfotech.com/blog/what-is-a-mev-bot).

In addition, perpetual privacy DEXs’ directly combat front running and MEV by fundamentally obfuscating the transaction data that predators exploit. By leveraging zk-proofs and encrypted order books, these platforms ensure that a trader’s intentions, order size and strategy remain completely hidden from the [public mempool](https://www.ledger.com/unavailable-uk) until after execution. This cryptographic shielding

removes the very economic incentive for MEV as bots can no longer scan for lucrative transactions to exploit, creating a fairer and more secure trading environment. This technological solution caters to a clear and growing user demand for anonymous leverage trading, particularly in a market dominated by surveillance-heavy centralised exchanges (CEXs). Furthermore, whilst CEXs require KYC and constantly monitor user activity for compliance and profit, these privacy-focused platforms offer whales the ability to execute large orders without moving the market and provide all users a refuge from data harvesting and predatory trading strategies. This represents a powerful value proposition: the high-leverage functionality of a CEX combined with the sovereignty and anonymity of pure DeFi. However, this very privacy creates significant new regulatory frictions - the same features that neutralise MEV also inherently obstruct financial surveillance, making it difficult for regulators to enforce anti-money laundering (AML) and counter-financing of terrorism (CFT) regulations. Hence, this positions perpetual privacy DEXs’ on a direct collision course with global financial watchdogs, potentially limiting their accessibility and posing a major hurdle to mainstream adoption despite the clear technological advantages and user demand they address.

The potential of perpetual privacy DEXs to reshape capital markets is profound - should they achieve significant adoption. By enabling large-scale, anonymous leveraged trading without intermediaries, they could unlock unprecedented capital efficiency and strategic freedom, fundamentally fragmenting liquidity away from traditional venues. This democratisation of stealth and leverage, however, exists on a knife’s edge - the very features that constitute their innovation also make them a prime target for global regulators, meaning they could just as plausibly be shut down before ever reaching critical mass. This dichotomy shows the core tension defining the next era of DeFi: the relentless push for technological innovation against the immovable object of regulatory compliance. These platforms are not merely building new products but are actively testing the boundaries of financial sovereignty, challenging the long-held assumption that significant leverage must come with mandated transparency and surveillance. They represent a direct technological rebuttal to the current compliance-heavy paradigm of finance.

Ultimately, the trajectory of perpetual privacy DEXs will serve as a critical case study in the ongoing battle for the soul of decentralised finance. Their fate hinges on fundamental questions: can a workable compromise be found that preserves user privacy and neutrality whilst addressing legitimate regulatory concerns, or are we entering an age of financial privacy crackdowns where such innovations are deemed non-negotiable threats? The answer will determine whether this technology becomes a foundational layer for a new market or a brief, revolutionary footnote.
