# The rise of “commercial bank money tokens”

*By Jonny Fry | *

Payments impact all business because they are required to send and receive money as part of their day-to-day activities. A silent revolution is the rise of [commercial bank money tokens (CBMT)](https://paytechlaw.com/en/e-money-token-vs-commercial-bank-money-token/) and banks are investigating this new digital deposit. These tokens are similar to bank deposits but are stored on private or permissioned blockchains to exploit blockchain technology’s speed without sacrificing commercial bank money’s safety and stability. In recent years, privately developed stablecoins and [central bank digital currencies (CBDCs)](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/central-bank-digital-currency-cbdc.asp) have threatened banks’ dominance in the financial system. Tokenised bank deposits may help banks to compete with fresh money and stay relevant.

[Stablecoins ](https://www.gemini.com/cryptopedia/what-are-stablecoins-how-do-they-work)and CBDCs have long dominated digital money discussions, with crypto trading and international payments increasingly using stablecoins (which are usually backed by cash or short-term securities). Traditional payment systems struggle with speed and programmability, but, in addition, many stablecoins are unregulated and concerns prevail due to a lack of independent

audits on reserves, issuer safety and client risk if something goes wrong. CBDCs are government-backed digital currencies, e.g. [China’s digital yuan](https://www.scmp.com/economy/china-economy/article/3316651/world-going-all-stablecoins-chinas-digital-yuan-any-different); European pilot schemes and a number

of other jurisdictions are also running pilot projects. They are safe, universal and resilient but they present several concerns. Central bankers must address privacy, monitoring, operational costs and the risk of undermining banks because banks need deposits to lend if individuals hold CBDCs directly instead of commercial bank accounts. Commercial bank money tokens offer an alternative - they gain the benefits of tokenisation whilst maintaining commercial bank moneys’ deposit insurance, regulatory supervision and trusted client relationships.

[Deposit tokens](https://www.jpmorgan.com/kinexys/content-hub/deposit-tokens#:~:text=A%20Deposit%20Token%20is%20an,been%20deposited%20by%20a%20customer.) (commercial bank money tokens) use blockchain technology and, typically, each token is a direct claim on a bank’s deposit - hence, they continue to rely on fractional banking infrastructure. In practice, this means that bank customers with one deposit token have one unit of stable currency in their account, all be it that the token is still, in effect, a digital form of an IoU from a bank as its value is only as good as the bank’s ability to pay. Commercial bank tokens are issued by licensed banks, not stablecoin start ups - they are a blockchain-native extension of the present financial system, not a shadow banking test. And, similar to stablecoins, they offer several technological advantages. Tokenised deposits can settle transactions 24/7 offer programmability and can use smart contracts for complex financial activities. They connect the old financial system to the new digital economy by serving as digital rails for wholesale settlement and payments.

JPMorgan Chase was one of the first to test these waters with its [JPM Coin in 2019](https://www.proshare.co/articles/jpmorgan-chase-unveils-the-first-digital-coin-by-a-bank?menu=Technology&classification=Read&category=BlockChain%20%26%20Cryptos). JPM Coin, based on a private blockchain, allows institutional clients to make fast, dollar-for-dollar payments within the bank’s system. JPM Coin is mostly used on JPMorgan’s network but it has shown that deposit tokens can scale - it processes billions of dollars in business transactions daily. Meanwhile, [Citi Token Services](https://www.citigroup.com/global/businesses/services/services-digital-assets), tested in numerous markets, has also advanced this sector. Citi emphasises on cross-border settlement and programmability, envisioning corporate clients and institutional investors simply shifting money between nations. Significantly, these experiments show how huge institutions are preparing for tokens. European momentum is greater - Deutsche, [DZ](https://www.ledgerinsights.com/dz-bank-crypto-cooperative-banks/), [Commerzbank](https://www.commerzbank.com/account-cashmanagement/products/payments/online-banking/), [UniCredit](https://www.unicreditgroup.eu/en.html) and [Helaba ](https://www.helaba.com/int/)have tested commercial bank money tokens (CBMTs). Siemens, Mercedes-Benz and BASF participated in these pilots to test 24/7 corporate payments, token conversion between banks and atomic financial transaction resolution. The results have indicated how deposit tokens may enhance industrial processes such as supply chain management, hourly machinery payment and trade financing. In addition, these projects promoted interoperability so banks could exchange and identify tokens, reducing fragmentation.

**The advantages of tokenised deposits**

> · Some claim they are safer than private stablecoins since they are issued by regulated banks - yet their backing relies on the bank being able to meet its liabilities.
> 
> · Instant settlement and programming are other benefits of tokenised deposits. Traditional payment systems involve intermediaries and batch processing, which can take days for transfers and settlement. However, blockchain-powered transactions using tokenised deposits can settle immediately and follow predefined rules. Smart contracts may automatically pay for provided goods or services - this saves money by eliminating manual verification.
> 
> · Cross border transactions will also benefit. Since international money transfers involve numerous correspondent banks and clearing systems, delays and excessive costs are common. Tokenised deposits could simplify this by allowing banks settle transactions instantly on common ledgers. Corporate treasuries that manage global supply chains and wholesale settlement banks may be affected by this.

Although deposit tokens have many advantages, some issues must be resolved before they can be extensively employed. Important issues include interoperability - if every bank creates its own token on its own platform, the system may fracture, making it less efficient and impact on global liquidity in financial markets. In addition, creating standards that operate with diverse groups and jurisdictions takes effort. Another challenge is integration of central bank systems - bank tokens must ideally connect to central bank reserves to be final. Wholesale CBDCs or real time gross settlement can do this and also some concepts ask for “trigger solutions” to integrate blockchain transactions with central bank infrastructure. Regulations also cause concern - central bankers such as Andrew Bailey of the Bank of England advise against banks creating stablecoin-like products without sufficient regulation. Regulators worry that the spread of tokens could threaten financial system stability and yet many authorities recognise that tokenised deposits may be safer than unregulated stablecoins and a good halfway ground. In addition, rules that encourage new ideas and reduce systematic failure are needed although, recently, [Andrew Baily has stated:](https://www.ft.com/content/eb013c4e-ed53-498b-9d75-2b5d9c7ecc65?accessToken=zwAGQNIVchnIkdPrATxO7VNJi9OddStdnH7MZQ.MEUCIQCmBmj1z5s3p2sEVooTL-vTIn7uU2ZKSFtkZSk1X894jgIgHy8-TH8bjQKq3K6IlEcsBRtZbyxAkoh1Kw9QXG3C5pg&sharetype=gift&token=cee36b8b-9615-492c-95ad-83d6d9f07532) “ _It should also be possible to have innovation in the form of money. It would therefore be wrong to be against stablecoins as a matter of principle. Indeed, I do not hold that view, recognising their potential in driving innovation in payments systems both at home and across borders.”_ Finally, banks should avoid segmented implementations - despite its success within JPMorgan, JPM Coin is not available outside of its network. Deposit tokens must expand beyond tiny experiments to become networks that connect many enterprises, industries and regions to shift global money.

With this in mind, it would be sagacious to note that deposit tokens are not intended to fully replace CBDCs or stablecoins - instead, they may co-exist as complementary forms of digital money. CBDCs offer sovereign backing and may be particularly suited to retail payments or public-sector use cases. Stablecoins, despite their challenges, continue to play an important role in crypto markets, increasingly offering an alternative way for peer top peer payments and decentralised finance. Deposit tokens, meanwhile, are especially well-suited to wholesale settlement, corporate payments and regulated environments where trust in the issuer is paramount. Indeed, six of the UK biggest banks have recognised this and are looking to issue a [Great British Deposit Token](https://www.ukfinance.org.uk/news-and-insight/press-release/uk-finance-announces-live-pilot-phase-deliver-tokenised-sterling#:~:text=Participating%20firms%20currently%20include%20Barclays,from%20Quant%2C%20EY%20and%20Linklaters.). By occupying this middle ground, banks can ensure that they remain central players in the future of money so, rather than ceding ground to FinTechs or central banks, they can leverage their existing trust and infrastructure to deliver digital money that works within the rules of the system whilst still embracing technological innovation. It appears commercial bank money tokens will evolve slowly; as banks test new use cases and establish infrastructure, we may expect additional pilots and proofs of concept.

However, these efforts will need to collaborate to build compatible standards and networks. The Regulated Liability Network, Fnality in the UK and Partior in Asia are developing multi-bank settlement platforms for tokenised finance. And, collaboration with regulators is crucial - the US GENIUS Act and other rules demonstrate banks are more willing to test tokenised money in a regulated environment. Meanwhile, the BIS and Financial Stability Board both want worldwide,

uniform norms: how effectively innovation and monitoring operate will determine deposit token growth. Tokenised deposits may affect the economy beyond finance. They could enable industrial IoT applications where devices automatically pay for services or use. They may simplify trade finance and supply chain management by reducing paperwork and human involvement and may allow real-time programmable money to establish new business models. The gradual rise of commercial bank money tokens requires a new technology and approach - banks were slow to react to digital advances, but now they are ready to compete with stablecoins and CBDCs. If successful, they may maintain their financial system leadership and usher in programmable, tokenised money.

In essence, the transformation unfolding with commercial bank money tokens may not be grabbing headlines like the flashy promises of CBDCs or the hype cycles of stablecoins, but it could prove just as consequential. By marrying the stability and regulatory safeguards of traditional banking with the efficiency and innovation of blockchain technology, these tokens represent a pragmatic path forward. They allow banks to evolve, regulators to maintain oversight and customers to enjoy the benefits of digital money without the risks of the unregulated frontier. As pilots expand and networks mature, the rise of commercial bank money tokens may mark the next great shift in how money moves through our economies. It is a quiet revolution, one happening not on the fringes of finance, but at its very core.
