# When tokenised debt goes bad

*By Jonny Fry | *

[Tokenisation](https://www.investing.com/news/stock-market-news/explainerwhat-is-tokenization-and-is-it-cryptos-next-big-thing-4147569?utm_source=google&utm_medium=cpc&utm_campaign=16886354810&utm_content=592783803824&utm_term=dsa-1463805041937_&GL_Ad_ID=592783803824&GL_Campaign_ID=16886354810&ISP=1&gad_source=1&gad_campaignid=16886354810&gbraid=0AAAAAoiKYHKV1ULvlL_Dwtyo18wDkr0Ay&gclid=CjwKCAiA2svIBhB-EiwARWDPji1NQxZewIhw1-s8SwkK23gvdGsGrdwcpcLnXHG34P6Qjbk0GQRpeBoCDyEQAvD_BwE) is remaking fixed income markets. Governments, supranationals and corporates are experimenting with bonds and loans represented as tokens on distributed ledgers whereby promising faster settlement, fractional ownership and 24/7 secondary trading. However, when a borrower fails to pay, tokenised debt introduces new operational, legal and technical default dynamics.

Arguably, debt is the backbone of modern economies, from government bonds funding national budgets to corporate loans fuelling global commerce and individuals taking out a mortgage to buy their home. Yet, as finance increasingly migrates onto blockchains, a fundamental question looms: what happens when tokenised debt goes bad? The new frontier of on-chain debt instruments, including [tokenised bonds](https://www.bakermckenzie.com/en/insight/publications/2025/06/tokenization-in-financial-services), [decentralised credit markets](https://www.investopedia.com/decentralized-finance-defi-5113835) and [smart contract–based lending](https://questdb.com/glossary/smart-contract-based-lending/), promises radical efficiency. Settlement is instantaneous, ownership is transparent and contracts can self-execute without intermediaries. But with automation comes a paradox: defaults now unfold in code, not only in courtrooms. The next sovereign or corporate default may not start with missed payments and bondholder meetings, but with oracle triggers, frozen smart contracts and decentralised juries deliberating in digital forums. Therefore, as the financial system experiments with tokenised debt, understanding how these new mechanisms will behave under stress is no longer theoretical, it is essential.

**What “tokenised debt” changes - and what it does not change**

At its core, a tokenised bond or loan is still a promise to repay. The token is merely a registry entry that represents rights to interest and principal, i.e. a blockchain-powered way to record in a decentralised manner. Tokenisation creates a [bearer security](https://uk.practicallaw.thomsonreuters.com/Glossary/UKPracticalLaw/I25019b03e8db11e398db8b09b4f043e0?transitionType=Default&contextData=\(sc.Default\)&firstPage=true) \- such instruments we have spent the last fifty years getting rid of. Where tokenisation changes the economics is in automation (payments encoded in smart contracts), fractionalisation (enabling small holders access) and the potential to settle ownership and payments instantly on-chain. These features can reduce frictions and costs, but they also create challenges. When interest/coupons or principal is missed, there is potentially no single operations team to call; instead, encoded logic, off-chain data feeds and adjudication protocols determine outcomes. That shift creates two broad consequences. Firstly, some traditional default paths (court-ordered restructurings, trustee interventions, manual reconciliations) become harder to run in real time on a public ledger. Secondly, new failure modes appear bugs in contract code, corrupted oracle feeds that report false ability-to-pay signals and co-ordination failures among dispersed token holders. Furthermore, on a truly global decentralised blockchain, which has nodes in multiple jurisdictions, it can be problematic for courts in one jurisdiction to make a ruling if the records are held in multiple countries. With global institutions such as Société Générale issuing tokenised bonds and then selling them in the US, it is potentially a mere matter of time before these jurisdictional challenges surface. Essentially, tokenised debt converts conventional debt instruments, bonds, notes and loans into digital tokens on a blockchain. Each token represents a fraction of the underlying asset and can be traded, tracked and settled instantly: the benefits are significant, including:

> · **transparency** \- every transaction is recorded on-chain, reducing reconciliation errors.
> 
> · **liquidity** \- fractional ownership opens up access to smaller investors.
> 
> · **automation** \- smart contracts manage coupon payments, maturity and compliance in real time.

Meanwhile, institutions such as the [European Investment Bank (EIB)](https://www.eib.org/en/index) and the [World Bank](https://www.worldbank.org/ext/en/home) have already issued blockchain-based bonds, and private-sector experiments are accelerating with platforms such as [Centrifuge](https://centrifuge.io/), [Maple Finance](https://maple.finance/) and [Ondo Finance](https://ondo.finance/) introducing tokenised debt instruments to both retail and institutional markets.

**Smart contracts: automating both payment and pain**

Smart contracts are self-executing code governing tokenised debt instruments. They automatically distribute coupon payments, trigger collateral transfers and enforce covenants. In a functioning market, this is revolutionary. But when payments fail, code becomes law and law can be unforgiving and, in traditional debt, default is a legal event. It requires notice, time for remedy and often negotiation. In tokenised debt, default can be instantaneous and automated. Relying on predefined conditions, a smart contract can:

> · instantly transfer collateral to lenders when an oracle reports a missed payment
> 
> · freeze or burn tokens to prevent secondary trading of defaulted instruments
> 
> · lock out issuers from accessing capital pools

This precision eliminates ambiguity but it also removes flexibility - there is no room for negotiation in a system where default is a line of code. In addition, a temporary delay in a sovereign bond payment, which might otherwise be resolved diplomatically, could trigger an irreversible liquidation event on-chain - this has already been seen in DeFi markets. During market volatility, protocols such as [MakerDAO](https://makerdao.com/) and [Aave](https://aave.com/) automatically liquidate positions when collateral ratios are breached - regardless of context. Unsurprisingly, a smart contract is only as good as the data it receives. Enter oracles, trusted data feeds that deliver off-chain information (such as price data, interest rates or payment confirmations) into the blockchain. In tokenised debt, oracles confirm critical events such as coupon payments, currency exchange rates or sovereign credit ratings. But this creates a new point of failure. If an oracle mistakenly reports that a payment has been missed (or worse, if it is hacked), smart contracts could trigger false defaults. Funds could be seized, assets sold and tokens frozen before anyone realises the data was wrong. More worryingly, this is not theoretical - Chainalysis estimated that in 2022, oracle manipulation attacks cost DeFi protocols over [$400 million](https://www.chainalysis.com/blog/oracle-manipulation-attacks-rising/#:~:text=Overall%2C%20we%20estimate%20that%20in,DOJ%20also%20bringing%20an%20indictment.) as malicious actors exploited price feeds to trigger liquidations or drain liquidity pools. Therefore, in order to mitigate this, developers are building decentralised oracle networks (such as [Chainlink](https://chain.link/) and [Pyth](https://www.pyth.network/)) that aggregate data from multiple sources and use consensus to verify accuracy. Nonetheless even with redundancy, oracles cannot perfectly capture real-world nuance.

So, once a default occurs or a dispute arises over whether it even happened, who decides the outcome? In traditional finance, courts, trustees and arbitration panels adjudicate claims, however, in decentralised finance, that role increasingly falls to decentralised courts such as [Kleros](https://kleros.io/), Aragon Court or [LexDAO](https://lexdao.org/). These digital courts rely on juror networks - token holders who review evidence and vote on disputes. Their decisions are recorded on-chain and enforced by smart contracts and, certainly for minor disputes (e.g., contract bugs, small liquidations), this model works surprisingly well. But applying it to sovereign or corporate debt crises raises enormous challenges, including:

> · **jurisdiction** \- which nation recognises the decision of a blockchain jury?
> 
> · **evidence** \- how can off-chain data (like bank statements or government decrees) be securely and verifiably introduced into an on-chain dispute?
> 
> · **legitimacy** \- who selects jurors and how do we prevent token-based bias or manipulation?

A digital jury ruling that “Country X defaulted” might carry weight on-chain but no legal standing in international law. This gap between digital enforcement and real-world authority could lead to dual realities - one on the blockchain, one in the courts. Meanwhile, sovereign debt is inherently political. Defaults involve central banks, the IMF and creditor committees, not algorithms. Yet some governments are experimenting with tokenised bonds - such as [El Salvador’s “Volcano Bonds”](https://ticotimes.net/2025/10/14/el-salvador-rolls-out-bitcoin-bonds-amid-crypto-surge) and [Kazakhstan’s blockchain debt pilots.](https://cryptodnes.bg/en/kazakhstan-pilots-tenge-backed-stablecoin-with-solana-and-mastercard/) The benefits are alluring: transparency, lower issuance costs and global investor access. But the risks are unique. Hence, to prevent digital defaults from spiralling into systemic chaos, several hybrid solutions are emerging, including:

> · **legal wrappers for smart contracts** \- many tokenised bond pilots use a “hybrid” model, smart contracts that automate operations but defer ultimate enforcement to legal contracts under national law. This allows for human intervention when needed.
> 
> · **multi-layer oracle verification** \- instead of relying on a single oracle, protocols now use multi-signature oracle systems, requiring multiple independent confirmations before triggering default clauses.
> 
> · **programmable arbitration** \- smart contracts can include “pause” or “dispute” functions, allowing time for human arbitration before liquidation occurs. This blends automation with flexibility.
> 
> · **regulatory sandboxes** \- governments are experimenting with [DLT bond sandboxes](https://www.youhodler.com/education/dlt-pilot-regime-a-new-framework-for-blockchain-based-financial-markets), such as those in the EU, Hong Kong and the UAE, to understand how tokenised debt interacts with existing bankruptcy and securities laws.

However, the automation of debt enforcement introduces moral hazards. Computer code cannot consider humanitarian crises, sanctions or political realities. There is the real potential for a lack of diplomacy, negotiation and forgiveness - key tools in sovereign finance which become secondary if we resort to automated enforcement by computer code. Aka smart contracts. Furthermore, smart contract- based defaults can propagate instantly across networks, triggering liquidations in other protocols that hold related assets and so causing a ‘domino effect’ cascade of trading. Tokenised debt is not simply a technical innovation - it is a philosophical transformation. It replaces trust in intermediaries with trust in code and legal flexibility with algorithmic precision. But when code governs credit, defaults become deterministic events. The next global debt crisis might not unfold in boardrooms or courtrooms but on-chain, executed by smart contracts, verified by oracles and adjudicated by faceless decentralised juries. The challenge ahead is to ensure that this new digital debt infrastructure remains resilient, fair and human centred. Smart contracts must be designed with fail-safes, oracles must be transparent and decentralised and decentralised arbitration must evolve from code-based justice to context-aware governance.
