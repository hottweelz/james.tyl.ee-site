# Ethereum, stablecoins and big breaks

*By Jonny Fry | *

Significant concerns arise, however, such as:

> · can its scaling roadmap truly handle this immense pressure?
> 
> · is its dominance threatened by rivals such as Solana, Polkadot and Tron, which excel at low-cost payments?
> 
> · how will new, compliance-focused stablecoins such as Tether’s USAT (targeting [100m potential users](https://sg.finance.yahoo.com/news/tether-eyes-fresh-investments-push-170815989.html?guccounter=1&guce_referrer=aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cuZ29vZ2xlLmNvbS8&guce_referrer_sig=AQAAAKiwSUrS40ebp2U0lxpCtl1qmcOdXqbqcXkT-2i8vTaf9iurSuR9SgDBsMjfYPEWgA-TjShodnuzQSMUZu4l-4Hyw8osyG-QOTNibsGAf1vW89XspY5daKZBtB2DVBjcNB0acVCTU8vknifPh4xJR7PwWMZ-XnUXzBauwrqH7az-) in just North America alone) reshape the competitive landscape?
> 
> · has Ethereum’s big break come or should we wait for another?

Moreover, as Bitcoin became the most popular cryptocurrency and still accounts for over [57% of the entire cryptocurrency market](https://www.coingecko.com/en/categories/stablecoins) out of over [19,000 cryptocurrencies](https://www.coingecko.com/en/categories/stablecoins). And whilst BTC is the best known and one of the best performing crypto currencies, stablecoins (with their institutional adoption) could potentially surpass it in terms of value - and many are using Ethereum. Essentially, Ethereum became the central hub for stablecoins thanks to its innovative introduction of[ smart contracts](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/smart-contracts.asp) and the creation of the ERC-20 token standard. This standard made it incredibly easy for developers to create stablecoins, such as USDT and USDC, without building a new blockchain from scratch. However, the platform’s real importance lies in its role as the birthplace of[ decentralised finance (DeFi)](https://www.trmlabs.com/glossary/decentralized-finance). DeFi protocols, including lending, borrowing and trading platforms, rely heavily on stablecoins to function as a stable unit of account whereby creating a massive, interconnected ecosystem that cemented Ethereum’s position as the leading blockchain for these assets. Furthermore, Ethereum continues to [attract more developers](https://www.bitget.com/news/detail/12560605017985) than other blockchains whereby maintaining Ethereum as one of the most import blockchains.

The [“DeFi Summer” of 2020](https://www.google.com/search?q=https://yield.app/blog/a-look-back-at-2020-the-year-of-defi%23:~:text%3DBy%2520most%2520measures%252C%2520DeFi%2520exceeded,%252C%2520yield%2520farming%252C%2520and%2520insurance.), which was a period of explosive growth on the Ethereum blockchain, fundamentally altered the role of stablecoins. This era saw an over [2,000% growth in the total value locked (TVL)](https://medium.com/@lily_trangpham/the-formation-of-defi-summer-2020-conditions-for-a-new-defi-summer-a419d53d0d31) in DeFi protocols - from under $1 billion at the start of the year to over $15 billion by its close. As users flocked to lending and borrowing platforms, they needed a stable medium of exchange, a role perfectly filled by stablecoins such as Tether and USD Coin. Furthermore, this boom cemented these assets as the foundational “cash” of the ecosystem, not only proving their critical utility but also solidifying Ethereum’s position as the premier network for these assets - thanks to its robust smart contract infrastructure and established network effects. The momentum of this period has since been amplified by institutional adoption, with the trend being most prominently demonstrated by the launch of [PayPal’s stablecoin](https://newsroom.paypal-corp.com/2023-08-07-PayPal-Launches-U-S-Dollar-Stablecoin) (built on the Ethereum network). PayPal’s decision to leverage Ethereum for its regulated, dollar-pegged asset is a powerful vote of confidence from a global finance leader. In fact, a recent EY-Parthenon survey [found that 13% of financial institutions and corporations globally](https://www.ey.com/en_us/insights/financial-services/cost-savings-and-speed-drive-stablecoin-adoption) are already using stablecoins, with 54% of non-users expecting to adopt them in the next six to twelve months. Investors are pouring money into stablecoin-related companies at an unprecedented rate, with 14 firms raising a combined $537

million so far this year according to data from [DeFiLlama](https://defillama.com/raises?sector=Stablecoin+Infrastructure&sector=Stablecoin). This growing institutional demand not only validates stablecoins as a crucial financial tool but also reinforces Ethereum’s status as the definitive platform for serious, regulated players entering the digital asset space. [As reported by JP Morgan](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/ethereum-suited-meteoric-stablecoin-growth-163117578.html): “ _We think this dynamic (stablecoins growing faster than the crypto market) perpetuates the theme of stablecoins’ ongoing divergence from the broader crypto ecosystem as stablecoin use cases and adoption mature._ ” Meanwhile, a [prediction by Keyrock and Bitso](https://www.coindesk.com/markets/2025/08/14/stablecoin-payments-projected-to-top-usd1t-annually-by-2030-market-maker-keyrock-says) puts $1 trillion in annual processing volume for stablecoins.

Certainly, Ethereum’s stablecoin supply is swelling at an historic pace, [adding $1 billion a day](https://cointelegraph.com/news/ethereum-added-5b-new-stablecoins-every-day-last-week) mid-September 2025 and [reaching a record $165 billion on-chain](https://coincentral.com/stablecoin-market-hits-record-high-as-ethereum-dominates-with-165-billion-supply/), giving it nearly 70% of the global market. Stablecoins, along with tokenised gold (now approximately [$3.57 billion)](https://www.coingecko.com/en/categories/tokenized-gold#:~:text=The%20Tokenized%20Gold%20market%20cap,No%20results%20found) doubled year-to-date and US treasuries (over 70% market share) are transforming Ethereum into the settlement hub for digital value. To many, this dominance looks inevitable: Ethereum’s rollup-centric roadmap, with [proto-danksharding (EIP-4844) live](https://ethereum.org/roadmap/danksharding/), full danksharding ahead and upgrades such as [verkle trees](https://www.ccn.com/education/crypto/merkle-vs-verkle-trees-key-differences-explained/) on the horizon, offers a clear path to scale. But inevitability is dangerous in crypto. Liquidity is splintered across a variety of blockchains such as Arbitrum, Optimism, Base, zkSync and others, so creating friction. And most rollups still depend on centralised sequencers, raising resilience concerns. Meanwhile, Tron [holds 27% of the stablecoin market](https://cointelegraph.com/news/ethereum-added-5b-new-stablecoins-every-day-last-week) and dominates cheap, everyday payments in emerging economies, showing that user experience often outweighs decentralisation. Regulation adds another layer of risk: stablecoins are the lifeblood of Ethereum’s growth, yet issuers such as Tether and Circle remain politically fragile. The launch of USAT, or USA₮, a new stablecoin issued by Tether, underscores this fragility - unlike USDT, which operates globally, USAT is designed specifically for the US market and built to comply with American financial regulations such as the GENIUS Act. By appointing key personnel with US policy backgrounds and partnering with regulated entities such as Anchorage Digital Bank, Tether is positioning USAT as a “compliance-first” stablecoin that can serve US institutions and compete directly with USDC. This move signals a shift in Tether’s strategy: addressing past regulatory concerns whilst leveraging its global reach to reinforce the US dollar’s dominance in the digital economy.

Hence, Ethereum is suspended between two truths: it is technically poised to be the inevitable settlement layer for money and assets, yet it is also exposed to fragmentation, to centralisation in its scaling stack and to regulatory choke points that could reshape stablecoin competition. JP Morgan has [lowered its forecast for the stablecoin market](https://www.reuters.com/business/finance/jpmorgan-wary-stablecoins-trillion-dollar-growth-bets-cuts-them-by-half-2025-07-03/), predicting it will reach $500 billion by 2028 (half of previous trillion-dollar projections); the bank’s scepticism is based on its finding that only a small portion of stablecoin use is for payments, with the vast majority being for crypto-native activities such as trading. But whilst the market momentum appears unstoppable, not everyone is convinced. JP Morgan believes the infrastructure to support widespread adoption is still underdeveloped whereby making rapid growth unlikely despite recent regulatory clarity from the GENIUS Act. However, with announcements that [Citi,](https://www.coindesk.com/business/2025/10/27/wall-street-bank-citi-coinbase-partner-to-expand-digital-asset-payments) which “ _currently banks 90% of the top e-commerce firms and 15 of the world’s 20 largest FinTechs_ ” and is working with Coinbase to create digital payments solutions, could mean JP Morgan may well revise its stablecoins further. Whether Ethereum’s dominance hardens into permanence or cracks under its own growth will depend not merely on its roadmap, but on how it weathers challenges from rival chains, regulatory shifts and new instruments such as USAT, designed to tighten the dollar’s grip on programable digital payments.
