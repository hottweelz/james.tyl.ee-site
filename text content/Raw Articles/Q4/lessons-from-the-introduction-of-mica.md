# Lessons from the introduction of MiCA

*By Jonny Fry | *

The EU’s Markets in Crypto-Assets (MiCA) regulation has begun to reshape the region’s crypto landscape in profound ways. For stablecoins, MiCA bans algorithmic and yield-bearing models, mandates full fiat-backed reserves and requires EU-based licensing and audit oversight for e-money and asset-referenced tokens. Subsequently, these requirements have led to the [delisting of non-compliant tokens (e.g. Tether’s USDT](https://vaultody.com/blog/296-what-mica-means-for-tether-usdt-delistings-custody-and-the-future-of-stablecoins-in-the-eea)) from platforms such as Coinbase and Kraken whilst boosting compliant alternatives such as Circle’s USDC and Société Générale’s EUR CoinVertible (EURCV). Exchanges and other crypto-asset service providers (CASPs) are now subject to a unified licensing regime that enables [“passporting”](https://www.eba.europa.eu/single-rule-book-qa/qna/view/publicId/2024_7167) across the European Economic Area (EEA), replacing the previously fragmented system of national rules. Within the first half-year of MiCA’s rollout, nearly forty banks, FinTechs and crypto-native firms have secured authorisations including Coinbase, Kraken, Crypto.com, Bitpanda, Bitstamp, BitGo, eToro, OKX and Amdax.

Traditional banks such as BBVA, Baader Bank AG, Commerzbank and Société Générale Forge, as well as FinTech platforms such as N26, Trade Republic, MoonPay and Robinhood, have also joined the list of license holders. Bitpanda, for example, transitioned from managing seventeen different national licenses to a mere three MiCA passport-able licenses, streamlining its operations significantly. Moreover, [France is looking to tax unrealised gain](https://www.globallegalinsights.com/news/france-proposes-taxing-crypto-as-unproductive-wealth/#:~:text=France's%20proposed%20'Imp%C3%B4t%20sur%20la,up%20its%20under%2Dpressure%20finances.) from its citizens holding cryptos as it scrabbles to find ways to reduce French national debt. Yet, not all major players have embraced the framework:

> · [Tether has resisted authorisation](https://www.tradingview.com/news/cointelegraph:ca75a2328094b:0-why-tether-refuses-to-comply-with-mica/), publicly questioning the burden of MiCA’s reserve requirements which mandate that 60% of backing assets be held in low-risk EU institutions and has shifted focus toward emerging markets with fewer restrictions.
> 
> · Binance’s complex and decentralised corporate structure, coupled with its history of legal issues, has hindered its ability to meet MiCA’s governance and risk management standards, resulting in limited service offerings for EU users.
> 
> · Regulators have also warned of a potential [“stablecoin loophole”](https://www.london.edu/news/regulatory-framework-should-be-established-for-tokens-issued-inside-and-outside-the-eu) in which Europe-based issuers could pool reserves with entities in third countries, creating space for regulatory arbitrage and systemic risk in times of stress.

And whilst MiCA aims to harmonise crypto rules across the EU, implementation has been uneven. Germany leads in adoption with [twelve licensed providers](https://ibrahimkazeem.medium.com/eu-licenses-53-crypto-firms-under-mica-but-tether-and-binance-are-nowhere-in-sight-cc8b2adfb3e2), followed closely by the Netherlands with eleven, and Malta has the third highest with five, whereby reflecting efficient supervisory processes in these jurisdictions. Other member states have been slower with some regulators demanding excessive data whereby increasing costs for CASPs and undermining the promise of a frictionless, harmonised market. Seemingly, this “implementation uncertainty” has created challenges for companies seeking to use passporting for cross-border growth; enforcement has been particularly visible in the stablecoin sector where MiCA’s strict governance and reserve rules have reshaped market offerings in a matter of months.

Moreover, the regulation has sparked both adaptation and retreat among market participants. In particular, large exchanges and payment providers have embraced MiCA’s framework, using it to solidify their EU operations. OKX [launched](https://www.cointribune.com/en/okx-officially-arrives-in-france-its-time-to-explore-regulated-crypto/) a regulated platform in France, Bybit [introduced](https://learn.bybit.com/en/regulations/bybit-europe-eu-and-micar) Bybit.eu for twenty-nine European Economic Area (EEA) countries, and Circle [secured](https://www.circle.com/pressroom/circle-is-first-global-stablecoin-issuer-to-comply-with-mica-eus-landmark-crypto-law) French regulatory approval for USDC and EURC issuance. Ripple is [actively seeking licenses](https://cointelegraph.com/news/ripple-confirms-mica-license-intention) for tokenised payments, whilst US and Asian custody, infrastructure and exchange firms have [targeted ](https://cointelegraph.com/news/bitpanda-crypto-unicorn-mica-license-germany)Germany as [an entry point](https://cointelegraph.com/news/bitgo-crypto-custody-mica-license-germany) to the EU’s large consumer market. However, for smaller players, MiCA’s operational and capital requirements are far more challenging. The obligation to hold substantial reserves in EU banks has led some to explore relocation to jurisdictions such as the UAE, UK and the US, where rules are lighter. Analysts expect this dynamic to trigger consolidation with smaller CASPs merging into better-capitalised, MiCA-compliant competitors. Regulatory sandboxes and compliance-focused platforms such as [Swisstronik](https://x.com/swisstronik/status/1886395446135861664) are also helping firms adapt, offering privacy-preserving KYC/AML tools, token wrapping services and blockchain infrastructure designed for MiCA compliance.

Certainly, practical experience from early compliance efforts has underscored the depth of MiCA’s demands. Legal experts note that the regulation imposes “institutional-grade” expectations, requiring robust governance structures, clear reporting lines and comprehensive risk-management frameworks. Many firms have struggled to determine which requirements apply, whether they need authorisation and how to navigate the process, particularly during the “[grandfathering period.](https://www.esma.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2024-12/List_of_MiCA_grandfathering_periods_art._143_3.pdf)” Non-EU companies must establish a legal presence in Europe with at least one director based there, adding operational complexity. Compliance leaders emphasise the need for early engagement with national regulators and a company-wide culture of alignment, and RegTech providers [highlight the role of technology](https://marketguard.io/blog/mica-regulation-the-role-of-reg-tech-in-streamlining-mi-car-compliance) in meeting MiCA’s standards, from transaction monitoring to blocking high-risk wallets. Furthermore, uneven supervisory practices and heavy data demands from some local regulators remain pain points, driving up costs and slowing implementation.

Beyond the EU, MiCA’s influence is being felt globally. Its scope and structure have put pressure on US and Asian regulators, many of whom operate in fragmented environments. In the US, overlapping jurisdictions between the CFTC and SEC create uncertainty whilst Asian countries have a patchwork of national rules. MiCA’s unified framework stands out as a potential global template for comprehensive crypto regulation. At the same time, the European Central Bank has [warned that the EU remains vulnerable](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/RegData/etudes/STUD/2025/764390/ECTI_STU\(2025\)764390_EN.pdf) to spillovers from foreign policy shifts (especially from the US) in stablecoin markets. According to [Exchange rates.com](https://www.exchangerates.org.uk/news/44449/2025-11-04-digital-euro-news-ecb-pushes-ahead-us-focused-on-stablecoins.html): “ _The ECB has framed the digital euro as a safeguard for Europe’s financial independence. Policymakers have expressed concern about the continent’s dependence on US firms such as Visa, Mastercard and PayPal - and the dominance of**dollar-backed stablecoins** in global payments.”_ Meanwhile in Asia, Chinese economist, Hang Ming, has [echoed these concerns](https://www.ainvest.com/news/chinese-economist-warns-dollar-stablecoin-dominance-urges-digital-yuan-expansion-2503/), warning that US dominance in stablecoins could extend its control over global financial systems. Still, major US and Asian firms are pursuing German or French MiCA compliance to access the EU market, often partnering with local license holders for faster entry.

Ultimately, the next six to twelve months will be pivotal; cracks are already beginning to emerge, [with Italy France and Austria](https://www.tradingview.com/news/cointelegraph:66169e1bb094b:0-mica-under-pressure-as-national-regulators-challenge-passporting/#:~:text=As%20the%20European%20Union%20rolls,practice%20known%20as%20regulatory%20arbitrage.) voicing concerns that passporting could lead to companies seeking a jurisdiction that has a lighter regulatory touch within the EU. Rules for stablecoins have been in effect since June 30th, 2024, and the remainder of MiCA (covering other crypto-assets and CASPs) took effect on December 30th, 2024. In 2025, the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) has been active in shaping MiCA’s rollout, issuing supervisory guidelines against market abuse in crypto markets, setting criteria for assessing the knowledge and competence of CASP staff and [clarifying](https://www.regulationtomorrow.com/france/esma-final-report-guidelines-for-the-criteria-on-the-assessment-of-knowledge-and-competence-under-mica/) when crypto-assets qualify as financial instruments under MiFID II. It has also warned firms against misleading marketing, particularly when mixing regulated and unregulated services and has [criticised Malta’s crypto licensing process](https://www.reuters.com/sustainability/boards-policy-regulation/european-securities-regulator-warns-about-crypto-firms-misleading-customers-2025-07-11/) for inconsistent scrutiny. As regulatory clarity improves, Europe is increasingly positioning itself as a “safe harbour” for institutional capital, even as compliance costs and uneven enforcement remain challenges. Meanwhile, the market is already re-aligning: Tether continues to be de-listed from European platforms and is focusing on emerging markets, Binance faces hurdles that limit its EU expansion, and smaller projects either adapt, consolidate or exit. For serious players, MiCA is emerging as a survival ticket for long-term relevance in the EU, a demanding but potentially rewarding entry point into a large, unified and closely regulated market.
