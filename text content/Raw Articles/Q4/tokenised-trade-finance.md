# Tokenised trade finance

*By Jonny Fry | *

Global trade is at a crossroads: the world’s supply chains, once optimised for efficiency and scale, are now being restructured under the pressure of tariffs, sanctions and geopolitical realignment. The US China trade tensions have escalated into a broader contest of technological and economic dominance whilst regional conflicts and protectionist regulations have redrawn the map of global

commerce. The results are unmistakable: higher costs, fractured supply chains and a shortage of affordable trade credit. [According to the International Finance Corporation, the global trade finance gap - representing unmet demand for short-term credit among exporters and importers - has widened to over $2.5 trillion in 2025](https://www.ifc.org/en/interviews/2025/scaling-up-supply-chain-finance-could-unlock-billions-for-sme-s). Notably, small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs), which account for 90% of global businesses, bear the brunt of this shortfall. Traditional banks, constrained by stricter compliance rules and risk aversion, are retreating from cross-border lending (particularly in emerging markets) and the vacuum they leave behind threatens to choke the very arteries of global commerce.

Yet in this climate of contraction, a technological revolution is quietly offering a path forward. Blockchain-powered tokenised trade finance, the digitisation and fractionalisation of trade assets on decentralised ledgers, promises to rebuild liquidity, transparency and trust in a system that sorely needs all three. Trade finance has always been the lifeblood of international commerce, providing the credit, guarantees and documentation that make cross-border transactions possible. Traditionally, these mechanisms relied on complex paperwork, multiple intermediaries and lengthy settlement times - letters of credit, bills of lading and insurance certificates often travelled as physical documents across continents, creating inefficiencies and fraud risks. In addition, over the past decade, efforts to digitise trade processes have made limited progress - centralised platforms such as Bolero, Contour and Marco Polo Network attempted to streamline workflows but faced adoption challenges due to interoperability issues and inconsistent regulation across jurisdictions. The use of blockchain technology, however, changes this paradigm by enabling shared, tamper-proof records accessible to all parties in a transaction (i.e. exporters, importers, banks, insurers and regulators) without requiring a single point of control. Every shipment, invoice and credit note can be tokenised, verified and traded digitally, dramatically reducing the friction of international trade. And, as protectionist barriers rise, this decentralised infrastructure offers something traditional systems cannot: trust without centralisation. Indeed, it was for this reason that the UK passed the Electronic Trade Documents Bill, with the [UK Government](https://www.gov.uk/government/news/paperless-trade-for-uk-businesses-to-boost-growth) estimating it would: “ _Boost the UK’s international trade, already worth more than[£1.4 trillion](https://www.gov.uk/government/statistics/uk-trade-in-numbers/uk-trade-in-numbers-web-version), and will reduce the estimated 28.5 billion paper trade documents printed and flown around the world daily.”_

At its core, tokenised trade finance converts tangible and contractual assets (such as invoices, receivables or letters of credit) into digital tokens recorded on a blockchain. These tokens can represent ownership or claim rights and can be transferred or sold to investors seeking yield - much like securitised instruments. So, for exporters and SMEs, this transformation is game-changing; instead of waiting 60 to 120 days for buyers to settle invoices, businesses can sell their tokenised receivables on-chain, instantly unlocking liquidity. Meanwhile, for investors, these tokens offer exposure to short-term, asset-backed instruments with transparent risk profiles and auditable histories; platforms such as Centrifuge, Tradeteq and Polytrade have already demonstrated the viability of this model. [Centrifuge](https://docs.centrifuge.io/), for instance, allows SMEs to finance invoices using decentralised liquidity from DeFi pools on MakerDAO and Aave, so bridging real-world credit with blockchain-based capital, and [Tradeteq](https://www.tradeteq.com/news/tradeteq-launches-trade-finance-backed-tokens-on-xdc-blockchain), a London-based fintech, has tokenised over $1 billion worth of trade finance assets for institutional investors, bringing an historically illiquid market into the digital age.

This convergence of decentralised technology and traditional trade finance is creating what analysts call TradeFi, a hybrid infrastructure combining the stability of regulated finance with the accessibility of decentralised networks. Nonetheless, the rise of tokenised trade finance must be understood in the context of a broader systemic retreat by traditional lenders. Following successive financial crises, banks have become increasingly risk-averse and compliance-heavy. Stricter Basel III and IV capital requirements, along with anti-money-laundering (AML) regulations, have made it costly to finance small-ticket cross-border transactions. Major institutions such as HSBC, Deutsche Bank and Standard Chartered, once pillars of global trade finance, have scaled back operations in emerging markets, citing regulatory overhead and low margins. Interestingly, the[ Asian Development Bank estimates that 45% of trade finance applications](https://traydstream.com/from-bottlenecks-to-breakthroughs-closing-the-2-5-trillion-trade-finance-gap-through-smart-infrastructure/) from SMEs in developing countries are now rejected by traditional banks. This retreat leaves a widening gap between credit supply and demand, and blockchain-based platforms, unconstrained by legacy infrastructure, are stepping into this void. By leveraging tokenisation, they can fractionalise credit risk, enabling a diverse set of global investors - from hedge funds to decentralised autonomous organisations (DAOs) - to participate in trade financing. The result is a more open, liquid and inclusive financial system where capital flows not through a handful of intermediaries but through transparent networks of tokenised trust. In traditional trade finance, opacity has always been a double-edged sword: necessary for confidentiality, yet dangerous for fraud. [The World Economic Forum](https://www3.weforum.org/docs/GETR/2012/GETR_Chapter1.5.pdf) estimates that, significantly, over $50 billion is lost annually to fraudulent trade documents.

However, blockchain’s immutable ledger provides a powerful countermeasure by ensuring that every document, signature and payment is cryptographically verified and time stamped, and tokenisation enhances liquidity by connecting trade finance assets to global capital markets. Investors can now buy fractional interests in diversified trade receivable pools, reducing concentration risk and improving yield opportunities and, because every transaction is recorded on-chain, rating agencies and regulators can audit risk exposures in real time whereby enhancing systemic transparency. Smart contracts coupled with digital money, arguably one of stablecoins best use cases, adds a further layer of efficiency. They automate payment triggers, escrow releases and compliance checks, minimising human error and reducing transaction times from weeks to hours. This automation is particularly valuable in a world where supply chains are fragmented across jurisdictions with differing legal systems. The effect is potentially transformative: trade finance, historically slow, opaque and relationship-driven, is becoming programmable, auditable and globally accessible.

According to [the World Bank](https://www.worldbank.org/en/topic/smefinance), small and medium-sized enterprises account for over 90% of all business and are the unsung heroes of global trade. But they have long suffered from limited access to affordable financing. In emerging economies, SMEs often lack the credit history or collateral required by major banks and even when financing is available, high fees and slow approval processes can erode profit margins. Tokenised trade finance platforms are flipping this model. By digitising invoices and using blockchain-based reputation scores, SMEs can secure funding from a global pool of lenders. In addition, DeFi protocols can assess risk algorithmically, using real-time transaction data instead of static balance sheets - Kenyan coffee exporter, for example, could tokenise its invoices to a European importer and sell those tokens to investors in Singapore or

Dubai within minutes, receiving immediate liquidity. Each transaction is verified on-chain, creating a transparent record that strengthens the exporter’s future creditworthiness. This democratisation of trade finance not only supports small businesses but also injects resilience into global supply chains. When traditional credit lines dry up, decentralised liquidity can keep trade flowing.

So, as blockchain-based trade finance matures, institutional players are beginning to take notice. Global payment networks, insurers and export credit agencies are exploring partnerships to bridge on-chain and off-chain financing. [Swift](https://www.swift.com/news-events/news/swift-add-blockchain-based-ledger), the global interbank network, is testing blockchain-based document exchange protocols to improve settlement transparency. Meanwhile, the International Chamber of Commerce (ICC) has launched the [digital standards initiative](https://iccwbo.org/news-publications/news/icc-digital-standards-initiative-launches-complete-framework-for-supply-chain-digitalisation/) (DSI) to harmonise legal frameworks for electronic trade documentation, an essential step toward global adoption. Regulators, once sceptical, are now adapting. The European Union’s Markets in Crypto-Assets (MiCA) framework provides legal clarity for token issuance and custody, whilst Singapore’s Monetary Authority (MAS) has issued guidelines for tokenised securities and trade instruments. These regulatory milestones are critical: they ensure that tokenised trade assets can be recognised as legitimate collateral, paving the way for institutional-scale liquidity. The convergence of regulatory clarity and technological maturity is turning blockchain-based trade finance from a niche experiment into an emerging asset class.

However, despite its promise, tokenised trade finance faces significant hurdles, including:

> · interoperability remains a key concern, different blockchain platforms often lack common standards, creating fragmentation rather than cohesion and without universal protocols, cross-border transactions may still require intermediaries to bridge digital silos.
> 
> · legal enforceability is another challenge - whilst digital tokens can represent ownership, their recognition under trade and bankruptcy law varies across jurisdictions. Governments must clarify how tokenised claims relate to traditional legal instruments like promissory notes and invoices.
> 
> · cybersecurity and data privacy risks also loom large. As more financial operations move on-chain, smart contract vulnerabilities or oracle failures could expose participants to new
> 
> forms of risk. Institutional adoption will depend on robust security audits and standardised insurance frameworks.
> 
> · market depth poses a question. Whilst DeFi investors have shown appetite for tokenised assets, large-scale institutional liquidity will require standardised credit ratings, yield benchmarks and secondary markets - elements still in development.

Yet, despite this, the trajectory is clear: tokenised trade finance is moving from experimentation to execution. As traditional banking retrenches and trade tensions persist, blockchain offers a neutral infrastructure for rebuilding trust and liquidity in global commerce. In the near future, trade receivables, letters of credit and even shipping insurance could all exist as digital tokens, instantaneously transferable and verifiable across borders. Artificial intelligence may further integrate with blockchain systems to assess creditworthiness, automate compliance and predict supply chain disruptions. A fragmented world does not mean a disconnected one; decentralised networks are proving that value can move freely even when politics cannot. Protectionism and geopolitical rivalry have fractured the traditional machinery of global trade. Banks are pulling back, paperwork is piling up and exporters are struggling to find liquidity. By digitising trust and democratising access to capital, tokenised trade finance is redefining who can participate in the

global economy. It empowers small businesses, reconnects fragmented supply chains and injects transparency into one of the world’s oldest financial systems. If the 20th century’s trade boom was built on containerisation, the 21st may well be built on tokenisation. In an age of walls and tariffs, the open architecture of blockchain could be the foundation that keeps trade and trust flowing.
