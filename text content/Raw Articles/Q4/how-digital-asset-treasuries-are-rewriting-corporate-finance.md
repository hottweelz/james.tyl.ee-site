# How digital asset treasuries are rewriting corporate finance

*By Jonny Fry | *

The year 2025 has cemented the [digital asset treasury (DAT) as a strategic necessity](https://www.henleyglobal.com/publications/crypto-wealth-report-2025/digital-asset-treasuries-new-capital-flow) rather than a mere technological experiment. This transformation is fundamentally reshaping corporate finance by moving beyond the constraints of traditional, fiat-anchored reserves. DATs are redefining how global companies manage liquidity, generate yield and hedge against macroeconomic volatility -operating within markets that are borderless, global and open 24/7.

The [traditional corporate treasury](https://www.henleyglobal.com/publications/crypto-wealth-report-2025/digital-asset-treasuries-new-capital-flow) is inherently limited by its reliance on fiat cash, short-term deposits and government bonds, operating according to regional bank cut-off times and market hours. In stark contrast, the [DAT model](https://www.halborn.com/blog/post/what-is-a-digital-asset-treasury-dat-company) leverages the inherent characteristics of blockchain technology to combine the liquidity of cash with the yield potential offered by decentralised finance (DeFi) and the upside of strategic exposure to assets such as Bitcoin (BTC), Ethereum (ETH) and Solana (SOL).

A [key characteristic of modern DAT companies](https://www.dlapiper.com/en-us/insights/publications/2025/10/key-capital-market-trends-digital-asset-treasuries) is their explicit business model, which revolves around the long-term accumulation of significant reserves of digital assets. These firms often employ sophisticated, yield-enhancing trading strategies, thereby blurring the established lines between traditional corporate finance and the digital asset ecosystem. This strategic shift is evident in the increasing number of US public companies restructuring their treasury functions to embrace digital assets. Moreover, this [trend has been strongly supported](https://www.dlapiper.com/en-us/insights/publications/2025/10/key-capital-market-trends-digital-asset-treasuries) by established capital markets. DAT companies have successfully raised significant capital, utilising instruments ranging from public and private offerings to Special Purpose Acquisition Company (SPAC) transactions and reverse mergers, specifically to acquire assets as reserve holdings. This capital market activity is driven by a critical demand dynamic: [DATs serve as a vital institutional gateway](https://www.skadden.com/insights/publications/2025/06/insights-june-2025/the-proliferation-of-cryptoasset-treasury-strategies). However, many institutional investors and asset managers face operational and compliance barriers that prevent them from holding digital assets directly. By purchasing the publicly listed equities of DAT companies, they gain indirect, strategic exposure to the asset class through a [regulated and familiar corporate vehicle](https://www.dlapiper.com/en-us/insights/publications/2025/10/key-capital-market-trends-digital-asset-treasuries). The emergence of these entities signals a new model where long-term digital asset accumulation is a core component of broader corporate finance strategy; the philosophical and operational divergence between the old and new models is profound.

The acceleration of DAT adoption is inextricably linked to landmark regulatory and accounting developments [achieved in 2025](https://thomasmurray.com/insights/institutional-adoption-digital-assets-2025-factors-driving-industry-forward) which have significantly reduced perceived risk and provided much-needed legal clarity. A [major historical deterrent](https://dart.deloitte.com/USDART/home/publications/roadmap/digital-assets) for corporations holding crypto assets was the former accounting standard which measured assets at historical cost less impairment. This meant companies were forced to recognise losses instantly whilst being unable to report unrealised gains, whereby misrepresenting the economic reality of the holdings. The Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB) addressed this critical issue through its additional guidance following the [2023 Accounting Standards Update (ASU 2023-08).](https://legal.pwc.de/content/services/global-crypto-regulation-report/pwc-global-crypto-regulation-report-2025.pdf) This update mandates that firms must now measure crypto assets at fair value each reporting period. The shift is transformative; it aligns the accounting practices for digital assets more closely with those used for traditional assets, directly improving transparency and supporting the integration of assets such as Bitcoin onto corporate balance sheets as strategic reserves.

Nonetheless, regulatory certainty around stablecoins has been equally critical. The passage of stablecoin legislation, such as the [Guiding and Establishing National Innovation for US Stablecoins Act (GENIUS Act) in July 2025](https://www.whitehouse.gov/fact-sheets/2025/07/fact-sheet-president-donald-j-trump-signs-genius-act-into-law/), provides a federal framework requiring issuers to maintain high-quality, liquid reserves and undergo regular audits. This legal clarity strengthens institutional confidence, embedding [stablecoins within mainstream financial infrastructure](https://www.dlapiper.com/en-us/insights/publications/2025/10/key-capital-market-trends-digital-asset-treasuries) and establishing them as functional, increasingly compliant transactional rails. For treasurers, [stablecoins offer immediate, scalable benefits](https://www.financialprofessionals.org/training-resources/resources/articles/Details/what-treasury-and-payments-professionals-need-to-know-about-stablecoins-after-the-genius-act) in cross-border payments therefore solving issues of slow, expensive transfers particularly in emerging markets where intermediary layers are numerous. They also enable [flexible liquidity management](https://www.pwc.com/us/en/tech-effect/emerging-tech/stablecoin-for-treasurers.html), allowing treasurers to pool cash across borders in real time without being restricted by conventional bank processing cut-off times. However, treasurers must look ahead - although stablecoins provide immediate utility, the US Treasury anticipates that central bank digital currencies (CBDCs) will likely need to replace privately issued stablecoins as the [primary digital currency](https://www.pwc.com/us/en/tech-effect/emerging-tech/stablecoin-for-treasurers.html) underpinning future tokenised transactions, mirroring historical monetary evolution. This perspective necessitates that DAT infrastructure is designed for maximum adaptability to future shifts [towards tokenised deposits or government-backed digital currencies](https://home.treasury.gov/system/files/221/TBACCharge2Q42024.pdf). The most profound impact of the DAT model is the ability to leverage [programmable finance](https://quant.network/perspectives/programmable-treasury-automation-for-smarter-liquidity-and-seamless-execution/), which fundamentally shifts treasury operations from reactive management to real-time, algorithmic orchestration.

For global businesses, digital innovation provides critical improvements over legacy systems. [application programming interfaces (APIs)](https://www.jpmorgan.com/insights/payments/fx-cross-border/digital-innovation-transforming-cross-border-payments) now integrate seamlessly into existing treasury infrastructure, providing real-time visibility into foreign exchange (FX) rates, thereby accelerating reconciliation and enabling treasurers to mitigate currency exposure more effectively.****[Programmable treasury automation](https://quant.network/perspectives/programmable-treasury-automation-for-smarter-liquidity-and-seamless-execution/) takes this a step further by embedding rules and logic directly into financial workflows. Operations move from static, end-of-day batch processing to event-driven execution. Cash can be automatically moved, allocated or converted the moment a predefined balance threshold or commercial condition is met, such as automatic payment release upon confirmation of goods clearing customs. This instantaneous execution eliminates working capital drag and [accelerates financial cycles](https://www.citigroup.com/global/insights/how-programmable-money-will-redefine-compliance-and-control).

A key feature of programmable money is the [ability to enforce policy-based governance](https://www.citigroup.com/global/insights/how-programmable-money-will-redefine-compliance-and-control) directly through code. Regulatory and policy validation can be built into the transaction before execution, ensuring that compliance is “embedded at the point of transaction”. This transformation allows the treasury function to evolve from a reporting centre to a [dynamic, live control tower for global liquidity](https://quant.network/perspectives/programmable-treasury-automation-for-smarter-liquidity-and-seamless-execution/), combining speed with auditability without adding administrative overhead.**** Furthermore, the [rise of tokenised real world assets (RWAs)](https://thomasmurray.com/insights/institutional-adoption-digital-assets-2025-factors-driving-industry-forward) is improving the fundamental mechanics of collateral and reserve management. [Tokenised US Treasury Funds](https://home.treasury.gov/system/files/221/TBACCharge2Q42024.pdf), such as BlackRock’s BUIDL Fund and Franklin Templeton’s OnChain US Government Money Fund, already represent a market capitalisation of approximately $2billion. This technology is critical for efficiency and stability. For example, platforms such as JPMorgan’s Onyx use tokenised Treasuries to provide real-time, intraday repo solutions. Tokenisation allows high-quality collateral to be posted and returned instantaneously, 24/7, using smart contracts to automate complex financial transactions. This infrastructure [offers a superior, more resilient alternative to traditional](https://discoveryalert.com.au/stablecoin-mechanisms-economic-impact-2025/), fragile short-term funding markets which have historically been vulnerable to sudden liquidity freezes.

The [motivations for incorporating digital assets](https://www.xbto.com/resources/from-custody-to-capital-management-building-a-robust-digital-asset-treasury) extend beyond operational efficiency; they include crucial elements of capital protection, diversification and yield enhancement. Digital assets unlock new opportunities to earn returns on assets that would traditionally remain in [low-yield accounts](https://quant.network/perspectives/programmable-treasury-automation-for-smarter-liquidity-and-seamless-execution/). The integration of decentralised finance (DeFi) platforms, driven by artificial intelligence (AI), is making this viable for treasuries. Automated yield generation systems can algorithmically allocate assets to lending pools, liquidity pairs and staking protocols. These systems continuously weigh crucial factors such as smart contract risk, protocol rewards and gas costs, ensuring that [treasuries maximise returns](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/services/consulting/articles/treasury-digital-asset-adoption.html) whilst strictly observing predetermined risk thresholds. Additionally, corporations view digital assets, particularly Bitcoin, as a [crucial tool for macroeconomic risk mitigation](https://www.halborn.com/blog/post/what-is-a-digital-asset-treasury-dat-company). A strategic allocation provides a pure hedge against currency debasement and inflation whereby contributing to greater financial resiliency and stability than reserves held solely in fiat assets.

Whilst volatility remains a defining feature of the digital asset space, it is increasingly being seen as a manageable risk, a kind of “portfolio hot sauce” that, in minimal allocation, can improve risk/reward profiles when managed appropriately; the [maturation of digital asset](https://www.fidelitydigitalassets.com/research-and-insights/maturation-digital-assets) derivatives markets facilitates this management. [DATs utilise derivatives](https://www.xbto.com/resources/from-custody-to-capital-management-building-a-robust-digital-asset-treasury) to implement powerful hedging strategies, insulating against unfavourable price movements and [options contracts](https://www.morpher.com/blog/hedging-strategies-in-cryptocurrency), such as buying put options or implementing complex strategies such as collars and spreads, act as necessary insurance. Essentially, these tools allow corporate managers to [cap downside losses and preserve gains](https://www.schwab.com/learn/story/how-to-hedge-volatile-market) without needing to liquidate underlying digital asset positions during market downturns. This ability to insure against price fluctuations makes strategic digital asset allocation viable within strict enterprise risk frameworks. Furthermore, the public nature of DAT companies allows institutional investors to capture [arbitrage opportunities](https://www.morpher.com/blog/hedging-strategies-in-cryptocurrency) arising from persistent dislocations between the underlying digital asset value and the market price of the DAT equity.

The adoption of a DAT model [requires a parallel commitment](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/services/consulting/articles/treasury-digital-asset-adoption.html) to robust risk governance across financial, regulatory and technological domains. Treasurers bear the [responsibility of identifying key risks](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/services/consulting/articles/treasury-digital-asset-adoption.html), including liquidity risk, concentration and counterparty risk, regulatory uncertainty and market volatility. Notably, recent high-profile bankruptcies and failures across the digital asset industry have underscored the [acute counterparty risks](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1876431/000162828025035132/exhibit991riskfactors.htm) inherent in the ecosystem, highlighting the importance of stringent due diligence when selecting exchanges or custodians. A more subtle structural risk relates to highly leveraged DAT companies. Analysts note that these firms introduce new channels for systemic risk, potentially transmitting [rapid financial and liquidity effects](https://www.ccn.com/education/crypto/bitcoin-below-100k-collapse-fears-digital-asset-treasuries-dats-blame/) between the crypto and traditional equity markets should a major DAT company experience a rapid share price collapse; this necessitates careful evaluation of exposure and corporate strategy. [Custody](https://www.pwc.com/us/en/tech-effect/emerging-tech/digital-asset-custody-strategies.html) is the foundational layer of any robust digital asset strategy and corporations face a critical choice in their custody model. Hence, whilst traditional custodian banks offer safe-keeping services for securities, digital assets provide institutions the flexibility to become their own banks, opting for non-custodial solutions. A [non-custodial approach](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/services/consulting/articles/treasury-digital-asset-adoption.html) grants absolute sovereignty over assets, leveraging specialised hardware or software wallets for heightened security and risk reduction. However, this demands significant internal expertise to mitigate sophisticated technological vulnerabilities, such as [replay attacks](https://www.taurushq.com/legal/regulatory-risk/risks-digitalassets/). Nonetheless, regardless of the chosen path, compliance with anti-money laundering (AML) and Bank Secrecy Act (BSA) measures, alongside rigorous transaction monitoring, remains paramount.

Digital asset treasuries represent more than a niche financial product; they are an emergent [financial operating system for multinational corporations](https://quant.network/perspectives/programmable-treasury-automation-for-smarter-liquidity-and-seamless-execution/). Digital asset treasuries are not a side-bet on the future of money - they are the opening move in a quiet redrafting of corporate finance itself. As treasuries step beyond fiat-only balance sheets into Bitcoin, Ethereum and tokenised cash instruments, they are turning what used to be a defensive, liquidity-first function into an offensive engine of yield, in an effort to increase a company’s share price. The corporates that master this shift will not simply hold different reserves - they will operate on different temporal and geographical rails with 24/7 liquidity, programmable controls and a more fluid relationship between capital markets and corporate balance sheets. Whilst Bitcoin is predominately favoured by many DAT over time other digital assets will no doubt be used, but companies need to be careful of not being seen as a collective investment scheme. Therefore, in order to accomplish this, then ideally, the company that has created the DAT needs to have a viable income producing business - or, risk the ire of the regulators....
