# Nasdaq tokenisation: what it truly means

*By Jonny Fry | *

Wall Street’s growing push into tokenisation has seen major institutions such as BlackRock, JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs, BNY Mellon, Robinhood and Citi tokenising funds, collateral and money-market instruments. Now Nasdaq is moving to embed this trend into mainstream markets by [formally proposing](https://cointelegraph.com/news/nasdaq-asks-sec-for-rule-change-to-trade-tokenized-stocks) that the SEC modify a number of rules. The primary change would be to allow tokenised versions of equities and exchange-traded products to trade alongside their traditional counterparts. Nasdaq’s initiative would bring tokenised securities onto one of the world’s largest public exchanges, aligning them with existing regulatory, custody and settlement systems such as the Depository Trust & Clearing Corporation (DTCC) which has handled [over $3.8 quadrillion](https://www.dtcc.com/annuals/2024/leadership/) transactions and [98% of US equity transactions](https://www.dtcc.com/clearing-and-settlement-services/equities-trade-capture#:~:text=As%20the%20central%20counterparty%20for,handle%20today's%20enormous%20trading%20volumes.).

**Meanwhile, Chuck Mack, Nasdaq’s senior vice president of North American Markets, has said: “** _Nasdaq’s proposed rule changes would enable member firms and investors to trade tokenized versions of equity securities and exchange traded products (ETPs) on our markets. Our goal is to integrate digital assets into Nasdaq’s current infrastructure and systems, which will advance financial innovation while maintaining stability, fairness and investor protection.”_ If successful, this could mark the first time tokenised assets are fully integrated into the US capital markets’ core infrastructure potentially accelerating adoption across Wall Street, with a further proposed rule change including that all tokenised assets be properly labelled to expedite processing. The request for an SEC rule change is critical as the current regulatory structure simply does not account for the core innovation of tokenisation - a single security being both a traditional security and a digital token traded on a blockchain. Therefore, the legacy system assumes a clear, singular form of record-keeping (e.g., through the DTCC). Nasdaq’s proposal seeks to create a new, hybrid model where it could act as the official bookkeeper for the tokenised version of a stock - which is likely whilst the traditional share continues to exist in parallel.

Moreover, this fundamentally shifts the analysis from whether tokenisation will happen to how it will be governed. It shows Nasdaq is not waiting for a fully decentralised future; it is proactively working to build a regulated, institutionally approved on-ramp for tokenised assets. This is the ultimate form of co-option as, by seeking to write the new rules of the game, Nasdaq aims to ensure it remains the central infrastructure provider and gatekeeper in this new digital era - effectively blending the efficiency of crypto with the trust and regulatory compliance of TradFi. Certainly, this strategic co-option is visible in its structured, step-by-step plan to rebuild market infrastructure for the digital age, starting with the foundational layer: secure custody. Through its Nasdaq Digital Assets division, the exchange had been in the process of establishing institutional-grade custody services before it cancelled in 2022, owing to regulatory concerns. The division, which [officially launched](https://www.nasdaq.com/press-release/nasdaq-establishes-new-digital-assets-business-2022-09-20) in September 2023, is not simply about holding cryptocurrencies for ETFs - it is about building the trusted, regulated vault necessary for handling tokenised versions of traditional securities under the watchful eye of regulators. The core mechanics involve representing a traditional security, e.g. a share of stock, as a digital token on a blockchain. This vision, [as reported by Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/newsletters/2025-07-02/the-stocks-will-be-tokenized), would see a smart contract governing the token’s entire lifecycle: minting a finite supply, automatically distributing dividends and executing corporate actions. The most transformative change lies in settlement - instead of the traditional T+2 cycle, trades would use [atomic settlements](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5211807#:~:text=Abstract,org/10.2139/ssrn.5211807) where smart contracts ensure the instant, simultaneous exchange of the tokenised asset for digital payment and eliminate counterparty risk.

Crucially, Nasdaq is not alone in this pursuit. The London Stock Exchange Group (LSEG) has [announced similar and equally ambitious plans](https://www.ccn.com/news/technology/lseg-nasdaq-tokenization-push/) to develop a digital markets platform for traditional asset tokenisation. The LSGE is launching a [new platform, Digital Markets Infrastructure (DMI)](https://www.ledgerinsights.com/london-stock-exchange-owner-launches-tokenized-private-fund-solution-dmi/), to initially trade tokenised private credit and private equity funds. Significantly, this parallel development by one of the world’s oldest and largest exchanges underscores that this shift is a strategic imperative for the entire legacy financial infrastructure, not merely an experiment by a single player. Ultimately, Nasdaq’s vision is likely to be a hybrid model; the exchange would remain the primary listing venue but its role would evolve into maintaining a golden record on the blockchain. The goal is to eventually digitalise the entire lifecycle of an asset. However, this move by Nasdaq does not validate tokenisation in the way many cryptocurrency enthusiasts might hope. Instead of embracing the decentralised, disintermediated nature of blockchain, Nasdaq is co-opting the technology to improve efficiency within its established market structure. The goal is not to create a new, decentralised financial system but to use the benefits of blockchain. By integrating tokenised securities into its traditional exchange, Nasdaq is implicitly validating the technology’s potential for institutional use, but only on its own terms: keeping control, regulation and the role of intermediaries firmly in place. Moreover, this approach shows how traditional finance is seeking to adapt and harness new technology without fundamentally disrupting its core business model. Arguably, Nasdaq is helping to prepare the infrastructure so as to enable the trading of tokenised securities (as being advocated by companies such as BlackRock and Robinhood) - [Trading view](https://www.tradingview.com/news/financemagnates:d47ab8abd094b:0-tokenization-is-the-biggest-innovation-in-capital-markets-vlad-tenev-pushes-robinhood-beyond-stocks/#:~:text=Robinhood%20is%20investing%20in%20technology,fully%20democratizing%20private%20market%20access.) has reported: “ _Robinhood is investing in technology that could make tokenized assets, including U.S. equities, private companies and real estate, available to retail investors. Tenv, Robinhood’s CEO, called “Tokenization the biggest innovation in capital markets in well over a decade”, highlighting live pilots in Europe and prototypes involving SpaceX and OpenAI. The goal, he explained, is to allow users around the world to access U.S. stocks, trade them around the clock, and ultimately add traditionally hard-to-reach investments, such as art, real estate, or private equity, to their portfolios. Robinhood already offers tokenized stocks to its clients, a move that has proven highly popular but has also drawn the attention of regulators_.”

Meanwhile [Larry Fink, CEO of BlackRock](https://xttrader.com/index.php/new/index/g/e/id/1673.html), argues that the next major shift in global finance will be driven by the tokenisation of traditional assets from equities and bonds to real estate, which will need regulated platforms such as Nasdaq on which to trade. He says the technology offers a way to widen access to mainstream financial markets by delivering fractional, transferable ownership through digital rails. Yet, despite its promise, tokenisation remains at an early stage. Fink points to analysis from Mordor Intelligence showing that the market had already surpassed $2tn in 2025 and could rise above [$13 trillion by 2030. ](https://www.mordorintelligence.com/industry-reports/asset-tokenization-market)For him, the trajectory is clear: tokenisation is moving from experiment to financial infrastructure. Nasdaq’s move also positions it as a gatekeeper in the tokenisation market, directly affecting competition with public blockchains and start-ups by absorbing a key innovation and integrating it into its traditional, centralised framework. Instead of facilitating a genuinely decentralised system, Nasdaq offers institutions a familiar, regulated pathway to tokenisation. This approach is designed to maintain control over trading and settlement, leveraging Nasdaq’s existing market infrastructure and legal standing.

For institutions, this provides a much lower-risk entry point than building on public, permissionless blockchains - which still face significant regulatory uncertainty and a fragmented liquidity landscape. This gatekeeping strategy threatens to contribute to siphoning institutional interest away from the public blockchain ecosystem, potentially limiting the growth of decentralised RWA tokenisation projects; this dynamic could force public blockchains and tokenisation startups to choose between competing and collaborating. The former means focusing on assets and use cases that Nasdaq will not touch (for now), such as offering fractional ownership of illiquid assets or leveraging DeFi’s unique features such as composability and global accessibility. The latter, on the other hand, involves startups becoming infrastructure providers, offering specialised services such as custody, analytics or private blockchain solutions that can integrate with Nasdaq’s system. Ultimately, Nasdaq’s entry signals a future where RWA tokenisation is not a single, revolutionary concept but a bifurcated market: a highly regulated, centralised model for institutional-grade assets and a decentralised, riskier model for more niche, crypto-native use cases.

Certainly, Nasdaq’s strategic integration of blockchain and tokenisation into its existing structure signifies a pivotal moment in the evolution of finance. It validates the technology as a powerful tool for efficiency and innovation but on terms that reinforce the power of centralised, regulated institutions. By acting as a bridge, Nasdaq provides a secure on-ramp for traditional capital whilst, as a gatekeeper, it ensures the new technology conforms to the old rules. The ultimate impact of this approach is not to decentralise finance but to institutionalise blockchain whereby making it a new, powerful component of the existing financial system rather than a replacement for it. This move sets the stage for a hybrid future where the transparency and efficiency of distributed ledger technology are harnessed to serve the established order, defining the next chapter for both Wall Street and the digital asset space.
