# From opacity to integrity: rethinking fraud in the digital asset era

*By Jonny Fry | *

In crypto markets, fraud remains a persistent paradox. Blockchains are inherently immutable and transparent, yet scams, wash trades, money-laundering and market manipulation proliferate. The reason is simple: pseudonymity, cross-chain complexity, mixers and regulatory gaps. Meanwhile, central banks around the world are designing central bank digital currencies (CBDCs) with intrinsic

traceability and programmable features. Therefore, if designed correctly, the tools of blockchain forensics and CBDC transparency could together usher-in a new paradigm of financial integrity.

Blockchain forensics firms such as [Chainalysis](https://go.chainalysis.com/2025-Crypto-Crime-Report.html?utm) and [TRM Labs](https://www.trmlabs.com/) have become indispensable in de-coding illicit flows. Their platforms analyse public ledgers, cluster wallets, trace money through mixing services and attribute illicit activity to known entities. The 2025 Chainalysis Crypto Crime Report underscores how transparency itself is a tool: as crypto crimes scale, blockchain analysis becomes an essential part of investigations. Global legal intelligence platform, [Lexology](https://www.lexology.com/library/detail.aspx?g=c75b03c7-e7e4-4eb8-8aad-770a4c655187&utm), recently described how methodical tracing _“peels back layers of anonymity”_ to attribute responsibility in hacks and fraud cases. A case study from Brazil demonstrates how, by using [Chainalysis](https://www.chainalysis.com/customer-stories/santa-catarina/), the police were able to track down and recover stolen Bitcoins. However, forensics has limits. Privacy coins and cross-chain bridges remain opaque; off-chain mixers and layering strategies persist. Forensics is reactive, it can trace flows after a transaction has happened, but struggles to prevent illicit movement in real time. Stablecoins and CBDCs potentially offer a complementary dimension - that is, built-in auditability and optional programmability. And unlike cryptocurrency systems with public ledgers, a CBDC issued on a permissioned ledger can grant central banks or regulators-controlled visibility into transaction flows - this transforms money into smart, monitored tokens. [Nomad Capitalist](https://nomadcapitalist.com/finance/cbdcs-fight-against-financial-crimes/?utm) argues that CBDCs could enforce automated transaction monitoring, fraud and money laundering detection capabilities, making illicit use of digital money far harder Yet, this is not a surveillance monolith. The [IMF cautions on “](https://www.elibrary.imf.org/view/journals/063/2024/004/article-A001-en.xml?utm) _[leakage”](https://www.elibrary.imf.org/view/journals/063/2024/004/article-A001-en.xml?utm)_[ risks](https://www.elibrary.imf.org/view/journals/063/2024/004/article-A001-en.xml?utm) \- identity abuse, privacy erosion and loss of trust if data controls are weak. The key is creating auditability without pervasive oversight: zero-knowledge proofs, selective disclosure and tiered permission models may help bridge that tension. The real frontier lies in combining forensic analytics with CBDC architecture. Imagine a CBDC system that flags suspicious activity in real time using embedded rules, whilst forensic systems further

dissect suspicious flows across private and public chains. One model - a regulator node within a permissioned CBDC network, receiving anonymised alerts when transactions deviate from norm.

Smart contracts could enforce compliance rules, such as locking funds until identity is proven. Forensic tools could then trace funds once flagged, triangulating with off-chain data to identify

malicious actors. Such infrastructure supports use cases such as detecting front-running in tokenised securities, spotting wash trading in algorithmic DeFi markets or policing sanction evasion. In effect, money and markets become partners in policing integrity. If Vlad Tenev, co-founder and CEO of Robinhood, is correct in his prediction that _[“it is inevitable that the entire financial market will be tokenised”](https://www.finextra.com/newsarticle/46702/robinhood-ceo-calls-tokenisation-unstoppable-freight-train?mc_cid=dbbcab79ff&mc_eid=562a6d0508) ,_**** there will be a growing need for more intelligent payment solutions. As we see equities, bonds, real estate and even money being made available in a tokenised format, the coupons, rent, dividends, interest, etc, could be paid monthly or weekly as opposed to every six months. Therefore, the need to automate tracking and monitoring and, hence, utilise AI powered tools, will grow and grow. Machine learning can certainly help spot anomalies in transaction graphs, unusual clustering, abnormal timing or circular flows, and forensic platforms increasingly use AI to infer ‘risk scores’ of wallets and flows. The synergy is evident: forensic tools classify anomalous addresses, CBDC systems enforce upstream rules and AI learns evolving fraud patterns. However, algorithmic bias and false positives pose risks - misclassified wallet could be unfairly frozen. Essentially, therefore, transparency over the logic of analytics will be critical for public trust.

The combination of forensics and CBDC transparency also raises the spectre of surveillance - citizens may fear ‘Big Brother’ tracking every cent, every penny. The challenge is institutional trust: governance must bind technical power with accountability. Legal guardrails, audit logs and separation of duties must ensure oversight rather than intrusion. This is where privacy-preserving techniques, zero-knowledge proofs, multi-party computations and threshold encryption could allow regulators to verify compliance without full data access. The design choices would define whether the system protects or controls - fraud seldom respects borders. Hence, for such a system to work globally, nations must harmonise AML/CFT frameworks, share intelligence and enable cross-CBDC cooperation. The BIS’s tokenisation vision envisions integrated capital markets built on regulated rails. [Bank for International Settlements projects](https://www.bis.org/publ/arpdf/ar2025e3.htm?utm), such as [mBridge](https://www.bis.org/about/bisih/topics/cbdc/mcbdc_bridge.htm#:~:text=Project%20mBridge%20was%20the%20result,Central%20Bank%20joined%20in%202024.), already connect multiple CBDC systems for real-time cross-border settlement, but co-operation hinges on trust. Nations will need “fraud intelligence networks” that balance sovereignty with shared security. The [RAND Corporation ](https://www.rand.org/content/dam/rand/pubs/research_reports/RRA2900/RRA2952-1/RAND_RRA2952-1.pdf?utm)study on CBDC design emphasises that law enforcement capabilities depend on two critical parameters: privacy settings and intermediation models. Intermediate models, tiered identity, permissioned visibility, offer more nuance. Moreover, innovations such as [threshold signature schemes (TSS)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.23294?utm) help safeguard against key compromise, distributing signing authority across nodes.

In essence, powerful transparency comes with liability. Bad actors will evolve shifting to cash, using crypto mixers or migrating to darknet rails. Overly aggressive enforcement could push activity off chain or to privacy networks. Therefore, the system must calibrate and evolve. There is also the political risk: trust can unravel if citizens suspect misuse. Hence, a credible audit and redress mechanism is essential. Blockchain forensics and digital money transparency, when combined, offer a powerful new risk infrastructure for digital assets - they do not guarantee elimination of fraud, but they can dramatically tilt the odds against it. The financial system is entering a new phase,

where money itself can defend its integrity. The real question is not whether this architecture is possible, but whether institutions are willing to build it and whether society is ready to trust it.
