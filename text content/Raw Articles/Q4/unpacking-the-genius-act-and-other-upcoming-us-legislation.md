# Unpacking the GENIUS Act and other upcoming US legislation

*By Jonny Fry | *

The Guiding and Establishing National Innovation for US Stablecoins Act, or [GENIUS Act, was signed into law by President Donald J. Trump on July 18th, 2025](https://www.whitehouse.gov/briefings-statements/2025/07/the-president-signed-into-law-s-1582/) so marking the first federal statute to create a comprehensive regulatory framework for payment stablecoins in the United States. Introduced in the Senate as S. 1582 by Bill Hagerty (R-TN) on May 21st, 2025, it passed the Senate on June 17th by 68:30 and the House on July 17th by 308:122 during “Crypto Week”. The Act aims to bring transparency, safety and regulatory clarity to the US digital dollar market whereby positioning America as a leader in digital assets as the country looks to foster innovation - attracting investment, protecting consumers and reinforcing the US dollar’s reserve currency status. At its core, the Act requires all stablecoins to be 1:1 backed by US dollars or 3-month US Treasury bills, explicitly prohibiting backing by cryptocurrencies or leverage. In addition, it mandates monthly public disclosures of reserve composition, with annual audits for issuers exceeding $50 billion in circulation. The framework establishes a tiered regulatory system: larger issuers with market capitalisations above $10 billion will fall under federal oversight via the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC) or the Federal Reserve.

Smaller issuers may operate under state oversight if the state regime is deemed “ _substantially similar_ ” to the federal framework. The law also clarifies that payment stablecoins, issued by approved entities, are neither securities nor commodities, placing them outside SEC and CFTC jurisdiction for these specific assets. Issuers are subject to the Bank Secrecy Act for anti-money laundering and sanctions compliance, must prioritise holders’ claims in insolvency, cannot offer interest or yield and are prohibited from making misleading claims of government backing or insurance. The Act further cements the US dollar’s dominance by defining eligible reserve assets narrowly. These restrictions are designed to channel demand toward US debt, bolstering the dollar’s position globally; it also is hoped that the demand for short term treasuries will help reduce interest rates in the US.

A notable structural feature of the GENIUS Act is its dual licensing system for bank and non-bank issuers. Subsidiaries of insured depository institutions and credit unions will remain under their primary regulator, whilst federally licensed non-bank issuers will be supervised directly by the OCC. For smaller issuers (under $10 billion in market capitalisation), state-level regulation is allowed if certified annually by the [Stablecoin Certification Review Committee](https://www.forbes.com/sites/vipinbharathan/2025/07/31/stablecoin-certification-review-committee-mandated-by-the-genius-act/) as equivalent to Federal standards. If a state-regulated issuer surpasses the $10 billion threshold, it must transition to Federal oversight within 360 days, obtain a waiver or cease issuing new stablecoins. Across all tiers, issuers must maintain 1:1 reserve backing, provide monthly reserve reports, meet capital and liquidity requirements and adhere to AML and sanctions compliance - with non-compliance subject to regulatory, civil and criminal penalties. The Act also extends US jurisdiction overseas. Only “ _permitted payment stablecoin issuers_ ” may issue stablecoins to US persons, and non-permitted stablecoins cannot be offered by service providers in the US. An exception exists for issuers in “ _qualifying non-US jurisdictions_ ” with regulations comparable to the GENIUS Act, as determined by the Treasury on the recommendation of the Certification Review Committee. Such issuers must still register with the OCC, maintain US-held reserves to meet domestic liquidity needs, submit to OCC supervision and accept USA legal jurisdiction.

However, critics [warn that the so-called](https://cointelegraph.com/news/us-bankers-want-stablecoin-yield-loophole-closed) “ _Tether loophole”_ could still enable offshore stablecoins to be traded on US decentralised exchanges without compliance whereby potentially allowing illicit flows. Supporters, including [Coinbase](https://www.tradingview.com/news/u_today:c7c7c8be2094b:0-coinbase-ceo-stresses-most-important-moment-for-crypto-now/#:~:text=Brian%20Armstrong%2C%20the%20CEO%20of,that%20is%20expected%20to%20follow.&text=This%20act%20acknowledges%20stablecoins%20as,than%20the%20old%20system%20could.&text=I%20hope%20people%20realize%20how,with%20more%20on%20the%20way.) and the [heads of the SEC](https://www.sec.gov/newsroom/speeches-statements/atkins-statement-genius-act-071725), OCC and CFTC, have praised the GENIUS Act for providing clarity and stability. They argue that public reserve disclosures and annual audits for large issuers will build trust in stablecoins, encouraging their use in cross-border payments, on-chain settlement and liquidity management. However, progressive lawmakers and consumer advocates point to gaps: [no prohibition](https://cointelegraph.com/news/warren-democrats-press-occ-how-deal-with-trump-crypto-ties) on President Trump or his family holding stablecoin business interests, [scope for Big Tech](https://www.banking.senate.gov/imo/media/doc/updatedgeniusanalysis.pdf) firms such as Elon Musk’s X to issue private stablecoins, insufficient safeguards against systemic risk and [ambiguity about whether existing consumer protection laws](https://advocacy.consumerreports.org/press_release/house-passes-genius-act-that-fails-to-protect-consumers-in-stablecoin-market/) \- particularly through the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) - apply to stablecoin transactions. Of note is that implementation will be gradual. Most provisions take effect either 18 months after enactment or 120 days after final implementing regulations are issued. Federal and state regulators are directed to issue these rules within one year. The law’s passage signals a foundational shift in how digital assets will be governed in the US, paving the way for stablecoins’ integration into mainstream finance whilst leaving unresolved debates over consumer protection, financial stability and market competition. The [World Economic Forum](https://www.weforum.org/stories/2025/09/us-genius-act-eu-mica-convergence-crypto-rules/) believe that the GENIUS Act in the US and the EU’s MiCA regulations point to a convergence of crypto regulation, although the potential outcomes could be very different. The US has outlawed CBDCs and looks to embrace market forces and private innovation, whilst the EU still looks to issue the digital € and potentially limit its citizens to the amount they can hold in their digital wallets.

The GENIUS Act, which was passed as part of a broader package of crypto legislation during “Crypto Week”, also included the Digital Asset Market Clarity Act of 2025 [(CLARITY Act)](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IN12583) and the [CBDC Anti-Surveillance State Act.](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/SAP-HR1919.pdf) The CLARITY Act, which passed the House with bipartisan support, seeks to clearly delineate the jurisdiction of the CFTC and SEC over digital assets, so drawing a line between digital commodities and securities. This aims to reduce regulatory uncertainty that has long plagued the crypto sector. The CBDC Anti-Surveillance State Act, also passed by the House, would prohibit the Federal Reserve from researching, designing, building, testing or issuing a central bank digital currency without express congressional approval. Whilst the GENIUS Act addresses the stablecoin market (currently about 7% of the crypto sector), the combined legislative package reflects an ongoing effort to craft a comprehensive regulatory framework for digital assets. Senate leaders, including Senator Tim Scott, have stated that the [CLARITY Act](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IN12583) will serve as a _“strong template”_ for Senate consideration of broader market structure reforms, suggesting that more legislation will follow in the coming months. These parallel efforts point toward a rapidly evolving US regulatory landscape, where stablecoins serve as both a test case and a building block for broader oversight of the digital economy.

In theory, the GENIUS Act, will, at least, enable potential hundreds of different US stablecoins and hence we could potentially have a return to the 1860’s when there where over 8,000 issuers of US dollars. Reeve Collins, [co-founder of Tether](https://cointelegraph.com/news/second-generation-stablecoins) and chairman of STBL, believes the first iteration of stablecoins addressed the challenge of how to put a dollar on a blockchain: “ _Stablecoin 2.0 takes this further with a dual token structure. Instead of embedding yield into the stablecoin, the system separates it, tokenizing both the dollar and the yield. One token functions as the spendable digital dollar, while the other represents the income stream from the underlying collateral._ ” Without a doubt, GENIUS offer a clear regulatory framework to create programmable digital dollars which will enable the payment of coupons, dividends, rent and interest to be paid on tokenised assets. The Clarity Act will enable such tokenised assets to be created with legal and regulatory environment and thus encourage regulated and no regulated companies to engage. As to how quickly and pervasive tokenised digital assets become, we will have to wait and see - but at least innovation is being encouraged and investors will be given a choice as to have they allocate their capital.
