# Centralised v decentralised exchanges (Part 2)

*By Jonny Fry | *

In Part 1 of _“Centralised v decentralised exchanges”,_ we looked at how**** centralised exchanges offer high liquidity, fiat access, advanced features and a user-friendly experience, but require user trust, create custodial risks and face tight regulatory scrutiny. By contrast, decentralised exchanges (DEXs) are trust less, transparent and non-custodial, enabling permissionless peer-to-peer trading and wide token access but they present a steeper learning curve, variable liquidity and higher user responsibility for key management and error risks.**** Centralised exchanges (CEXs) generally boast higher liquidity and greater trading volumes due to their large user base which allows for quicker transaction execution and better price stability; these platforms use a predictable [fee structure](https://www.innreg.com/blog/eu-crypto-regulation-guide) based on maker and taker fees, with rates typically ranging from 0.01% to 0.75%.

In contrast, DEXs often have lower liquidity especially for less popular tokens. This can lead to a phenomenon known as [slippage](https://www.ledger.com/th/academy/dex-or-cex-your-exchange-guide) which is the difference between the expected price of a trade and the price at which the trade is actually executed on-chain. A large trade on a low-liquidity pool can significantly impact the asset’s price and result in a less favourable outcome for the user - slippage is therefore a critical hidden cost of trading on a DEX. Whilst some sources claim DEXs have lower fees, this is a nuanced point. The transactional costs on a DEX include variable network gas fees which can be exceptionally high during periods of network congestion. A user must weigh the certainty of a CEX’s fee structure against the potential for unpredictable costs and execution on a DEX

**The landscape of risk and regulation**

The diverging architectural models of CEXs and DEXs also expose them to fundamentally different types of risk and regulatory pressures. Whilst CEXs are vulnerable to operational and systemic failures, a different set of challenges confronts the decentralised ecosystem. Since a centralised exchange holds vast amounts of user funds in its custody, it becomes a primary target for [hacks](https://www.bitpay.com/blog/cex-vs-dex). However, the most significant threat to a CEX is not always a technical hack but rather a failure of human trust and operational transparency - the [bankruptcy of FTX](https://www.investopedia.com/what-went-wrong-with-ftx-6828447) serves as a critical case study in this regard. The downfall was precipitated by a massive spike in customer withdrawals that exposed an $8 billion hole in FTX’s accounts, which was a direct result of the company lending [more than half](https://www.marketwatch.com/story/ftx-lent-over-half-of-its-customer-funds-to-affiliated-trading-firm-wsj-11668098525) of its customer funds to its sister company, Alameda Research. The FTX scandal was a failure of the custodial model and not a failure of the underlying technology; because the exchange’s operations were opaque, users had no way to verify its solvency or ensure that their funds were being managed responsibly. The incident created a systemic risk for the entire CEX sector and directly contributed to the current shift in user behaviour toward decentralised alternatives where users retain full control over their assets.

Moreover, whilst DEXs eliminate the risk of centralised custody they are not without their own vulnerabilities. They are susceptible to a different class of risk, centred around the security of their underlying [smart contracts](https://www.kucoin.com/research/insights/decentralized-exchanges-dexs-in-2025-market-evolution-technological-innovation-and-the-future-of-defi-trading). Common exploits include [flash loan attacks](https://cointelegraph.com/news/dex-volumes-hit-record-q2-2025-pancakeswap-hyperliquid-lead), oracle manipulation and [re-entrancy attacks](https://hacken.io/discover/reentrancy-attacks/). In a typical flash loan attack, a malicious actor borrows a large amount of funds, manipulates the price of a thinly traded asset within an AMM pool, and then extracts a profit all within a single transaction. [Cross-chain bridges](https://www.rapidinnovation.io/post/comprehensive-guide-to-decentralized-exchanges-dex), which are used to move assets between different blockchains, are another key vulnerability. Significant exploits such as the smart contracts, which resulted in the loss of hundreds of millions of dollars, are often caused by security lapses and social engineering attacks that compromise the bridge’s code. Beyond these specific exploits, DEXs also face a unique and systemic risk known as [maximal extractable value](https://chain.link/education-hub/maximal-extractable-value-mev), or MEV. MEV refers to the value a blockchain validator or miner can extract by re-ordering, including or excluding transactions within a block. A classic example is the [“sandwich attack”](https://cointelegraph.com/news/dex-volumes-hit-record-q2-2025-pancakeswap-hyperliquid-lead) \- here a bot detects a large incoming transaction and executes a buy order before it, and a sell order immediately after it. This front-running action causes slippage for the user’s transaction which benefits the attacker and harms the user. This kind of malicious activity is a direct consequence of the transparency of the [public mempool](https://omniatech.io/pages/how-private-and-public-mempools-work/#:~:text=Public%20Mempools%3A%20The%20Open%20Marketplace&text=Public%20mempools%20offer%20a%20transparent,their%20statuses%20and%20network%20congestion.) and is a risk that is not present on centralised exchanges with their internal off-chain order books.

Centralised exchanges operate under an increasingly restrictive and maturing regulatory framework. In the US, the SEC has intensified its efforts to classify many crypto assets as securities and has launched high-profile enforcement actions against major players such as Coinbase. In Europe, the [Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation](https://www.innreg.com/blog/mica-regulation-guide) (MiCA) has established a comprehensive framework for crypto asset service providers with strict requirements. The regulatory landscape is a one-way street; the constant legal battles and tightening regulations combined with the loss of user trust after incidents such as FTX are creating a powerful headwind for the centralised sector. Whilst CEXs face an increasingly stringent regulatory environment, DEXs have thus far enjoyed a lighter touch. The lack of a central entity or specific geographic location complicates jurisdictional issues and makes them difficult to regulate in a traditional sense. However, regulators are acutely aware of the [money laundering risks](https://www.tastycrypto.com/blog/cex-vs-dex-comparison/) associated with anonymous DEX transactions - this suggests that the current regulatory leniency is likely to be temporary. The next phase of regulation will probably shift focus from the decentralised protocol itself to the more centralised components that provide access to it. This could include the [front-end user interfaces](https://www.tastycrypto.com/blog/cex-vs-dex-comparison/) or the smart contract developers who write the code; the legal status of [immutable smart contracts](https://katten.com/smart-contracts-ruling-forces-a-blockchain-development-rethink) and the potential for regulatory oversight of those who build and provide access to them is an emerging area of law. This suggests that the next battleground for regulatory oversight will be in the DeFi space targeting the human components of a decentralised system.

An analysis of CEXs and DEXs reveals that they are not simply competing for the same user base but are fundamentally distinct parts of a maturing financial ecosystem. Centralised exchanges serve as an essential bridge between traditional and digital finance providing a user-friendly liquid and regulated environment that is comfortable for beginners and institutional investors alike. Whilst their custodial model carries significant risk, their compliance with regulatory frameworks and their provision of fiat on-ramps remain vital for mass adoption. Decentralised exchanges by contrast are the true embodiment of crypto’s core philosophy, offering a non-custodial and permissionless environment that appeals to users prioritising autonomy and privacy. The recent surge in their trading volume is a powerful indicator that a segment of the market is actively seeking alternatives to centralised control. Ultimately, the choice for users is not a simple one and depends entirely on their priorities - users must decide whether they are more comfortable trusting a known company with their funds and personal data in exchange for convenience and support, or if they would rather assume full control over their assets and navigate a more complex technical environment with the inherent risk of smart contract vulnerabilities. The future of cryptocurrency will not be a victory for one over the other, but a symbiotic existence where users intelligently leverage the strengths of both platforms using CEXs for regulated access to fiat and high liquidity trading and DEXs for peer-to-peer swaps and full control over their digital assets.
