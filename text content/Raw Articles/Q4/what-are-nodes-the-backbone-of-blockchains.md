# What are nodes? The backbone of blockchains

*By Jonny Fry | *

The centralised nodes in a[ decentralised structure](https://www.ccn.com/education/glossary/decentralization/) substitute the conventional presence of centralised servers. They collaborate in order to ensure trust and reliability without relying on intermediaries. This shared validation procedure creates the integrity of blockchain technology and it is difficult to manipulate, as well as not being susceptible to single point of failure. Each [node has a role to play](https://www.coinbase.com/learn/crypto-glossary/what-is-a-node-in-cryptocurrency) in validating new transactions, implementing regulations and safeguarding the network against malicious activity. In addition, this system has co-ordination which makes blockchain operations transparent, secure and totally decentralised because the movement of data in the [blockchain depends on the functionality of nodes](https://www.moonpay.com/learn/blockchain); their main tasks, including transaction validation, block verification, participation in the consensus and storing information, are vital to the reliability and security of the network.

In essence, a blockchain transaction is sent to various nodes when users initiate a blockchain transaction. A node will ensure that the transaction is correctly signed by the senders with their private key and the senders have a sufficient balance to accomplish it. Furthermore, the nodes are able to enforce the rules of the network and exclude the possibility of the doubling of the money involved because they can understand that they use the same assets. These checks also have the effect of ensuring that only valid transactions are advanced to the next level - transactions are verified and, when they are verified, they can be added to a block (this is to ensure that this accuracy is carried throughout the blockchain network). Block verification, propagation checking of a block and sending the block simultaneously are key processes in [blockchain technology](https://www.moonpay.com/learn/blockchain). Once transactions are proven, they are bundled by nodes in blocks and every block is again verified to ensure that it has nothing more than legitimate data and that it properly references the preceding block. After approval, the block is then distributed to other nodes in the network and, in so doing, each node is able to update its local ledger to indicate the new block.

The synchronised update makes it consistent and avoids multiple versions of the blockchain and this is the basis of immutability and transparency of blockchain. Moreover, nodes are involved in the process of consensus that defines which block will be the next formal addition to the chain. Hence, various blockchains apply various consensus algorithms - for example, in [proof of work (PoW) systems](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/p/proof-work.asp#:~:text=Proof%20of%20work%20%28PoW%29%20is%20an%20essential%20blockchain%20consensus%20mechanism,peer%2Dto%2Dpeer%20transactions.), including those of Bitcoin, miners are nodes that process computational puzzles to check transactions and to add new blocks; the amount of cryptocurrency staked by a validator determines the number of nodes to include in the block in [proof of stake (PoS) systems](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/p/proof-work.asp); and other models include delegated proof of stake (DPoS) and practical byzantine fault tolerance (PBFT) that change the consensus mechanism in order to improve efficiency and security. Essentially, [consensus mechanisms](https://www.rapidinnovation.io/post/consensus-mechanisms-in-blockchain-proof-of-work-vs-proof-of-stake-and-beyond) are used to make sure that every node concurs on one record of transactions that cannot be tampered with. Hence, with this agreement, blockchains are able to operate without being under centralised supervision. Meanwhile, blockchain storage is also undertaken in nodes - i.e. full nodes archive all the transactions and all the blocks right back to the origin of the network. The ‘pruned’ nodes preserve the new data only and the essential information is maintained, with older records being deleted so that the space may be freed. This decentralised storage technique guarantees that, in the event of failure of certain nodes or their offline, the

blockchain is still available - the firing of thousands of nodes ensures that the network keeps on running well.

[Blockchain networks are made up of various types of nodes](https://www.ccn.com/education/crypto/what-is-a-crypto-node-and-how-does-it-work/) with each node having a particular function to play to ensure balance and efficiency of the system. [Full nodes/complete nodes](https://www.bitstamp.net/en-gb/learn/crypto-101/what-are-bitcoin-blockchain-nodes/) ensure that all verifications of transactions are performed, and the rules of consensus are followed. They keep a full copy of the blockchain registry and this means that no one will be able to manipulate the information. Running a complete node facilitates decentralisation but needs high computing power and storage space. [Pruned/archival nodes](https://nownodes.io/blog/what-is-a-pruned-node-complete-guide/) are full nodes but better space managers since they delete older information once they have been initially synchronised. Conversely, archival nodes store all the blocks and transactions since the network was formed - these nodes will be critical and analyse data, carry out audits and historical checks. Meanwhile, for permissioned or hybrid blockchains, authority nodes of these types are run by the select participants who control the validation and governance functions. [Light nodes](https://www.ccn.com/education/crypto/bitcoin-full-nodes-light-nodes-explained/) are also known as SPV nodes and store very little blockchain data, relying on full nodes to verify a transaction in detail. They allow low-resource devices such as mobile wallets to connect to blockchains effectively.

PoW systems are based on the validator/[mining nodes that add blocks to the chain](https://www.moonpay.com/learn/bitcoin/bitcoin-mining) after solving a cryptographic puzzle and receive rewards as a result of their effort, and the PoS networks have [validator nodes](https://help.coinbase.com/en/coinbase/getting-started/crypto-education/glossary/validator-) which validate the transactions and construct blocks depending on their staked assets. The two roles are core in ensuring that trust and security are upheld over the decentralised systems. Other nodes include:

> · **master nodes** \- carry out superior tasks such as facilitating quick transactions or governance proposals
> 
> · **staking nodes** \- use cryptocurrency as security by posting a stake and receive rewards in being involved with the validation. Both these and master nodes play roles in the performance of networks and economic stability.
> 
> · **super nodes** \- are used in specialised tasks when the network is being upgraded or when the demand is high.
> 
> · **[lightning nodes](https://www.lightspark.com/news/insights/explaining-lightning-nodes)** \- are an off-chain scaling method and allow transactions to be conducted more quickly and cheaply and settled subsequently. The nodes also improve the speed of the network and minimise congestion.

The role of security and hosting in node operations is critical towards securing the individual members as well as the blockchain but, due to the fact that nodes keep important elements of the ledger, they are often the victims of cyberattacks. The threats that are most widespread are [distributed denial-of-service](https://www.cloudflare.com/en-gb/learning/ddos/what-is-a-ddos-attack/) (DDoS) attacks, malware infections and [Sybil attacks](https://www.imperva.com/learn/application-security/sybil-attack/), in which the attackers establish several synthetic nodes to affect network actions. Nodes are also prone to attack of chain re-organisation when they are programmed to an old version of software. Hence, [node security ](https://node.security/)requires a number of protective measures - firewalls, antivirus tools and current software should absolutely be implemented by node operators in order to avoid intrusions. The use of virtual private networks (VPNs) provides an added layer of privacy, whereas the use of multiple nodes brings about redundancy which assists in providing uptime during attack or failure. In addition, [trustworthy node hosting](https://contabo.com/en/crypto/) also is a significant factor; hosting providers should have good connection, good uptime and a geographical spread to avoid localised failures. However, whether to use cloud-based or self-hosted nodes is determined by the availability of resources, budget and the choice of whether to take control of the operations or not.

Essentially, decentralised systems cannot operate without blockchain nodes and also cannot be governed without them. Nodes remove dependencies on central authorities by decentralising data and decision-making, through the participation of many participants and this decentralisation increases transparency and eliminates censorship or one-man rule. Transparency is ensured by using nodes to verify blocks and transactions separately because all participants are able to see and certify the ledger, which provides an environment of self-audits and makes users trust each other. Nodes offer redundancy that enhances reliability of networks - some nodes have copies of the blockchain, in case some of them go offline, and this maintains the operation and availability of data even in case of disruption. In most networks, nodes play a role in governance as well; they are involved in voting to upgrade protocols and policy changes. Notably, this model of participation encourages equity and serves the interests of the community with the development of the network, e.g. [Dash](https://www.dash.org/), in which masternodes vote on development proposals and decide in which hybrid consensus is used as a governance mechanism.

Finally, crypto nodes represent the ideals of decentralisation and safety that characterise [blockchain technology.](https://www.moonpay.com/learn/blockchain) This characteristic of being distributed enables networks to be free of the middlemen and provides a transparent, resilient and self-sustaining digital infrastructure that forms the foundation of the global blockchain ecosystem. The crucial elements that render the blockchain networks to be decentralised, secure and transparent are crypto nodes. They confirm transactions, enforce regulations and store data over a distributed infrastructure. Blockchain systems are made to be decentralised and fair through consensus processes and governance, which do not involve centralised control. So, by having the knowledge of the functioning of these nodes, it is easy to understand why they are referred to as the backbone of blockchain networks. Ultimately, the future and power of blockchain technology are ascertained by their contribution to transparency, trust and decentralisation.
