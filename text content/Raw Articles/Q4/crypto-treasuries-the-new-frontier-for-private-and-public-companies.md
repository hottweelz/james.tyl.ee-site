# Crypto treasuries: the new frontier for private and public companies

*By Jonny Fry | *

The landscape of corporate finance is experiencing a seismic shift as private and public companies increasingly consider holding digital assets on their balance sheets. Creating crypto treasuries - which is essentially a strategic allocation of corporate funds into cryptocurrencies such as Bitcoin, Solana or Ethereum - represents more than a speculative bet. It is becoming a sophisticated financial strategy that signals innovation, hedges against traditional market risks and potentially unlocks significant shareholder value.

Pioneering firms such as [MicroStrategy](https://www.microstrategy.com/en/bitcoin-purchase-prices) have paved the way, accumulating billions in Bitcoin since 2020. Its bold strategy has inspired a growing cohort of public companies to explore similar paths, although each journey presents unique opportunities and challenges. The Smarter Web PLC case demonstrates both the tremendous upside potential, having floated at 4p and seen its share price climb to over [£5.00 - only to fall back to less than 50p](https://www.aquis.eu/companies/SWC). It will be interesting to see how [FalconEdge](https://www.lse.co.uk/news/falconedge-starts-aquis-trading-after-gbp14-million-float-raise-p4ucq2jix4h3mt3.html), another company which floated on Aquis in November, will see its share price experience huge volatility or, in time, simply track Bitcoin. Strategic opportunities for companies include:

> · **hedging against inflation and currency risk** \- in an era of unprecedented monetary expansion and persistent inflation concerns, some cryptocurrencies potentially offer an
> 
> alternative store of value. Unlike fiat currencies, which are subject to central bank policies, Bitcoin’s fixed supply cap of 21 million coins presents a compelling hedge against currency debasement. For UK companies watching sterling fluctuate against major currencies, crypto holdings can provide portfolio diversification beyond traditional forex hedging strategies.
> 
> · **unlocking shareholder value through indirect exposure** \- many institutional and retail investors seek crypto exposure but face regulatory, custodial or mandate restrictions preventing direct ownership. Public companies holding crypto treasuries offer these investors a regulated way to obtain exposure to this asset class asset through traditional equity markets. This dynamic often creates premium valuations for crypto treasury stocks, as seen in [MicroStrategy’s frequent trading above its net asset value.](https://news.bitcoin.com/michael-saylor-explains-4-reasons-mstr-trades-at-a-premium-to-bitcoin-nav/)
> 
> · **marketing and innovation signalling** \- companies adopting crypto treasury strategies immediately position themselves as forward-thinking innovators. This appeals particularly to younger, tech-savvy investors and can enhance brand perception amongst digital-native customer segments. The announcement effect, alone, often drives significant share price movements as market participants reassess the company’s growth trajectory and management vision.
> 
> · **capital markets advantages** \- listed companies enjoy unique advantages in building crypto positions. They can access capital markets through equity raises, convertible notes or debt financing at institutional rates often unavailable to retail crypto buyers. This leverage capability, whilst introducing additional risk, enables accelerated accumulation strategies that can magnify returns during bull markets. However, as Smarter Web has demonstrated, private companies can also enjoy significant share price appreciation by establishing a crypto treasury strategy.

There are, however, a number of challenges and risks associated with establishing a crypto treasury strategy - not least regulatory uncertainty. The regulatory landscape for corporate crypto holdings remains fragmented and evolving. In the UK, the [Financial Conduct Authority (FCA)](https://www.fca.org.uk/firms/cryptoassets) continues developing frameworks whilst companies must navigate complex questions regarding securities law, investment company status and cross-border compliance. The EU’s [Markets in Crypto-Assets (MiCA)](https://www.esma.europa.eu/esmas-activities/digital-finance-and-innovation/markets-crypto-assets-regulation-mica) regulation adds another layer of consideration for companies operating across European markets. Meanwhile, in the US, the [CLARITY Act](https://www.jonesday.com/en/insights/2025/07/us-house-passes-genius-and-clarity-acts-signaling-bipartisan-support-for-digital-assets) (which is in the process of being introduced) may well offer greater regulatory clarity and thus encourage companies to migrate their listings to the US. Certainly, securing digital assets presents unprecedented challenges for traditional corporate treasury functions, whereby providing custody and potential security risks at an operational level. Companies must evaluate institutional custody providers (such as [Coinbase Custody](https://www.coinbase.com/custody) or [Bakkt](https://www.bakkt.com/)), implement robust internal controls and address risks ranging from private key management to potential hacking attempts. The spectre of exchange failures, highlighted by FTX’s collapse, underscores counterparty risk considerations and, whilst private off-line custody is undoubtedly appealing, who, then, has control of such wallets - and can adequate insurance be obtained? Furthermore, cryptocurrencies have historically been very volatile - which can dramatically impact balance sheets. A 50% drawdown, not uncommon in crypto markets, could trigger loan covenants, require impairment charges or necessitate emergency capital raises - not to mention the impact on

a company’s share price. Hence, companies must prepare investors for potential complete loss scenarios whilst maintaining confidence in their long-term strategy _._

Moreover, whilst major cryptocurrencies offer substantial liquidity, converting large positions quickly without adverse price impact requires sophisticated execution. Cold storage security measures (essential for protecting assets) can introduce delays in accessing funds during critical

moments; companies must balance security with operational flexibility. Then there are the inevitable accounting and audit challenges; the absence of universally accepted accounting standards for crypto assets creates reporting complexities. Under current [IFRS guidelines](https://www.ifrs.org/content/dam/ifrs/supporting-implementation/agenda-decisions/2019/holdings-of-cryptocurrencies-june-2019.pdf), cryptocurrencies are typically treated as intangible assets, requiring impairment testing but preventing upward revaluations. Finding auditors with sufficient crypto expertise remains challenging, particularly for smaller firms: after all, what is the agreed price for an asset that trades 24/7 globally? Essentially, implementing a crypto treasury strategy demands comprehensive board oversight and clear governance frameworks. Directors must understand digital asset fundamentals, establish risk parameters and ensure transparent communication with shareholders. Key governance considerations include:

> · **investment policy formation** \- involves defining allocation limits, identifying approved assets, and setting rebalancing triggers to maintain portfolio discipline. This is supported by;
> 
> · **robust risk management framework** \- this establishes volatility tolerance, outlines hedging strategies and specifies exit criteria to safeguard capital.
> 
> · **clear stakeholder communication** \- is also essential, with regular updates on holdings, performance and strategic adjustments ensuring transparency.
> 
> · **ongoing compliance oversight** \- this monitor regulatory developments and enforces adherence to all relevant requirements. The board’s fiduciary duty extends to understanding how crypto holdings impact corporate strategy, capital allocation and long-term value creation. This often necessitates bringing crypto-literate directors onto boards or engaging specialist advisers.

As noted above with the example of MicroStrategy, companies with crypto treasuries often trade at premiums and they could, indeed, trade at a discount to their net asset value whereby introducing basis risk. Management decisions, operational performance and market sentiment toward the

company can cause significant divergence from underlying crypto performance. Undoubtedly, [MicroStrategy](https://www.microstrategy.com/) has been pioneering, having transformed from a business intelligence firm into the world’s largest corporate Bitcoin holder (accumulating over 150,000 BTC). Its aggressive strategy, funded through convertible debt and equity raises, has created extraordinary volatility but delivered substantial returns for long-term shareholders. Key lessons include the importance of CEO conviction, clear communication strategy and maintaining operational business health alongside crypto investments. [Tesla has been arguably more tactical](https://www.tesla.com/), having acquired $1.5 billion Bitcoin purchase in 2021, followed by partial sales, therefore demonstrating a more tactical approach. Its experience highlights accounting complexities and the challenge of balancing environmental concerns with crypto holdings, ultimately leading to a reduced position. On the other hand, [Block Inc.](https://block.xyz/) has integrated Bitcoin into both its treasury and product ecosystem, enabling Bitcoin trading through Cash App whilst holding corporate reserves. Block’s approach is allocation 10% of its profits and so regularly buys Bitcoin, a form of dollar cost averaging; this dual approach leverages operational synergies whilst building long-term value. Meanwhile, the regulatory environment continues evolving rapidly - the UK’s [Bank of England framework](https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/prudential-regulation/publication/2023/december/bank-of-england-approach-to-regulating-crypto-assets) for crypto assets and the FCA’s ongoing consultations signal increasing regulatory clarity and, in the US, potential [SEC approval of spot Bitcoin ETFs](https://www.sec.gov/) could reshape institutional adoption dynamics.

In essence, successful crypto treasury implementation depends on several critical success factors. Organisations need a clear strategic rationale to show how crypto holdings support broader corporate objectives, alongside robust risk management with comprehensive controls and monitoring systems. Maintaining transparent communication with investors about strategy and performance is equally important, as is ensuring regulatory compliance to stay ahead of evolving requirements across jurisdictions. Finally, building technical competence either internally or through qualified advisers enables companies to execute and manage their crypto strategies effectively. The crypto treasury phenomenon represents both extraordinary opportunity and substantial risk for public companies. Smarter Web PLC’s dramatic price journey, from 4p to £5.00 and back to 48p, encapsulates the volatility inherent in this strategy. Yet for companies with appropriate risk tolerance, governance structures and strategic vision, then crypto treasuries offer a pathway to potentially transformative returns.

Success requires careful navigation of regulatory complexity, robust risk management and clear stakeholder communication. Companies must balance the allure of digital asset appreciation against operational risks, accounting challenges and potential reputational impacts. Those who master this balance may find themselves at the forefront of a financial revolution, whilst those who stumble could face severe consequences. As the market matures, we expect continued evolution in regulatory frameworks, institutional infrastructure and best practices. Public companies considering crypto treasuries should move deliberately but decisively, recognising that in rapidly evolving markets, excessive caution can be as costly as reckless speculation. The key lies in thoughtful strategy, robust execution and maintaining flexibility to adapt as the digital asset landscape continues its remarkable transformation.
