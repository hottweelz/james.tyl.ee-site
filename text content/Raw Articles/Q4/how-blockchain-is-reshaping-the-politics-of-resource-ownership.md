# How blockchain is reshaping the politics of resource ownership

*By Jonny Fry | *

For centuries, control of natural resources, oil, gold, water, forests and commodities has defined the balance of power among nations. Wars have been fought over them, borders redrawn and economies built or broken. Yet in the 21st century a new medium of ownership has emerged, one powered by blockchain technology where rights to physical assets are being transformed into digital tokens. Turning something as tangible as lithium ore or a cubic meter of water into code is where tokenisation is already reshaping global politics. Governments, investors and communities are discovering that ownership, once rooted in land and law, can now be fractionalised, globalised and automated. The implications are profound: who owns the resource beneath the soil, in a river or a lake when its value is represented in a token traded halfway around the world? Can a blockchain ledger become a new frontier for geopolitical competition? And will tokenised resource markets empower local communities or dispossess them in new, digital ways? Indeed, back in 2005, a [World Bank](https://blogs.worldbank.org/en/psd/is-water-the-next-oil#:~:text=Garry%20Emmons%20hypothesizes%20that%20skyrocketing,leadership%20from%20the%20public%20sector.%22) blog posed the question - will water be the next oil? Fast forward 20 years and we now have a company selling tokenised spring water.

Tokenisation represents real assets as digital tokens on a blockchain, serving as proof of ownership. These tokens can represent physical items such as oil, farmland or groundwater rights. In traditional finance, ownership is documented through titles and contracts under national law. Blockchain changes this by enabling programmable ownership with smart contracts for automatic transfer and fractional ownership across borders. This allows for liquidity in previously illiquid assets and alters the concept of ownership from geographic to digital. Ownership can now be coded, divided and sold globally without needing physical proximity to the land - [carbon credits](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/carbon_credit.asp) are the earliest large-scale example of natural resource tokenisation. Projects such as [Toucan](https://toucan.earth/about-toucan/), [KlimaDAO](https://www.klimadao.finance/) and [Flowcarbon](https://www.flowcarbon.com/) have put verified carbon offsets on-chain, letting users buy, trade or retire credits transparently. Tokenisation has made carbon markets more efficient, but also controversial. Critics argue that digitising offsets can amplify greenwashing since digital liquidity allows rapid trading detached from on-the-ground verification. Others fear that decentralised carbon markets undermine the role of national regulators or international agreements, such as the [Paris Accord.](https://www.consilium.europa.eu/en/policies/paris-agreement-climate/) Still, the carbon case shows what is possible: a global, transparent resource ledger where environmental assets can be tracked, price and managed in real time. The challenge is ensuring that transparency does not become an excuse for deregulation. Water rights are increasingly being considered for tokenisation, especially in arid regions where allocation disputes are intensifying. In [Australia](https://www.accc.gov.au/system/files/Water%20inquiry%20-%20Submission%20-%20Civic%20Ledger%20-%2027%20January%202020.pdf#:~:text=As%20a%20result%20of%20the%20Feasibility%20Study%2C,and%20providing%20additional%20water%20for%20the%20environment.) and [California](https://www.forbes.com/sites/rachelwolfson/2019/02/08/ibm-pilots-blockchain-and-iot-sensor-solution-to-track-sustainable-groundwater-usage-in-california/), pilot programs have explored blockchain-based water trading platforms. The logic is simple: smart contracts could automate water allocations, enforce usage caps and facilitate peer-to-peer trading among farmers. Yet the moral dimension is complex - turning access to water, a basic human right, into a tradable token raises ethical questions. Could token markets exacerbate inequality between those with access to digital platforms and marginalised communities without them? Tokenisation risks transforming a social good into a speculative asset, echoing criticisms of financialisation in other sectors.

**Sovereignty vs. decentralisation**

Resource ownership has historically been tied to sovereignty; states control what lies beneath their soil. Blockchain introduces an element of de-territorialisation where resource claims exist on networks beyond national jurisdiction, and this decentralisation challenges the authority of states and multilateral institutions. For resource-rich but politically fragile nations, tokenisation can invite new forms of dependency as foreign investors or [DAOs (decentralised autonomous organisations)](https://www.britannica.com/money/what-is-a-decentralized-autonomous-organization) gain influence over extractive sectors via digital tokens. In effect, the control shifts from physical extraction to protocol governance. The question becomes not simply, _“who owns the mine?”_ but _“who controls the smart contract that represents the mine?”_ On the positive side, blockchain’s immutable ledgers can combat corruption and opacity in resource management. By recording every transaction, from extraction to export, on a public chain, governments can build trust with citizens and investors alike. [Nigeria’s pilot blockchain registry for oil royalties](https://www.marketscreener.com/news/nigeria-sets-2-5mn-bpd-oil-target-for-2026-as-reforms-advance-ce7d5fddd088f724#:~:text=The%20licensing%20process%20will%20be,compared%20with%20prior%20discretionary%20rounds.) and [Mongolia’s experiments with mineral-rights transparency](https://eiti.org/blog-post/championing-transparency-mongolias-journey-towards-mining-transparency#:~:text=Milestones%20in%20transparency,implementation%20of%20the%20EITI%20Standard.) reflect this potential. If implemented equitably, tokenisation could become a tool for public accountability, reducing revenue leakages and ensuring fair community benefit sharing. Yet, transparency without governance is a mirage - without strong institutions to interpret and enforce the data, a blockchain record merely makes corruption visible, not necessarily preventable.

**Democratising investment - or digital colonialism?**

Proponents argue that tokenised natural assets can democratise access to wealth creation - a farmer could own a share of a solar farm through tokens; a village co-operative could crowd-fund its water infrastructure; and, indigenous communities could directly monetise conservation efforts. However, critics warn of “digital colonialism” where global investors capture local value through code. Essentially, if blockchain infrastructure and smart contracts are designed in Silicon Valley or Singapore, do local communities truly have agency over their resources? Without inclusive governance, tokenisation could replicate colonial-era patterns of extraction, this time executed through algorithms and digital wallets rather than armies and treaties. For many indigenous and rural communities, land and resources hold spiritual and ancestral meaning beyond economic value. Tokenising these assets risks reducing them to tradeable commodities. A forest tokenised as a “carbon sink” may gain market liquidity but lose its cultural context. The challenge, therefore, is not merely technical but philosophical: can blockchain systems encode local values, rights and traditions without flattening them into global market logic?

Emerging initiatives, such as [Māori-led projects](https://environment.govt.nz/news/funding-to-strengthen-maori-climate-resilience-and-create-economic-benefits/) in New Zealand or [First Nations’ blockchain ventures](https://www.firstnationsblockchain.com/home/) in Canada, show that co-designing token systems with local communities can align technology with tradition. The politics of code must become as inclusive as the politics of land;

as nations recognise the strategic implications of tokenised resources, resource nationalism is adapting to the digital age. Governments may demand that token issuance, trading and governance occur under domestic oversight. For instance, a tokenised copper mine might be required to operate on a national blockchain to prevent capital flight or data leaks. Conversely, global investors may prefer open networks with international liquidity. The clash between sovereign control and decentralised finance could define the next decade of resource geopolitics. One of blockchain’s touted benefits is its potential to support sustainability, tracking deforestation, carbon emissions or water use transparently. But the reality is mixed - whilst green tokenisation can incentivise responsible resource use, it also opens avenues for speculative trading detached from ecological reality. When conservation is monetised through tokens, the motive can shift from stewardship to speculation. Furthermore, the infrastructure of blockchain itself, depending on the network, may consume significant energy, ironically undermining its sustainability claims. The industry is moving toward greener [proof-of-stake systems](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/p/proof-stake-pos.asp) but the tension between environmental ideals and digital profit remains.

To ensure tokenised resources serve humanity rather than deepen inequality, a new governance architecture is required whereby bridging technology, law, and ethics. Legal integration will be required**** as**** states must clarify how tokenised ownership interacts with property law, resource rights and environmental regulations - tokens without legal enforceability risk becoming speculative fictions. Furthermore, to ensure local acceptance and adoption community inclusion is vital in order that local and indigenous communities are able to participate in designing tokenisation frameworks affecting their lands. This includes equitable revenue-sharing and consent mechanisms. Nations must assert control over how resource data, geological, hydrological or environmental, is tokenised and shared internationally. Tokenisation’s promise lies not just in efficiency but in redefining accountability, making ownership transparent, equitable and sustainable. Yet, without careful governance, it risks repeating old extractive logics in digital disguise.

Blockchains are achieving more than simply digitising assets, they are reprogramming power. The shift from paper deeds to programmable tokens transforms who can own, trade and profit from the Earth’s resources. This transformation could democratise access to wealth and enhance transparency, or it could produce a new form of digital feudalism where code replaces law and algorithmic elites govern the commons. In addition, the politics of resource ownership are moving beyond the ledger into a domain where sovereignty, identity and technology intersect. The question for policymakers and citizens alike is whether this new era of tokenised resources will empower communities or merely tokenise their futures.
