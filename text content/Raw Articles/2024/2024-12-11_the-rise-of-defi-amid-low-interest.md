# The rise of DeFi amid low interest rates

The modern DeFi ecosystem has thrived in an environment shaped by low and in, some cases, even negative interest rates. When central banks reduce interest rates, the returns on savings accounts, bonds and other traditional financial products diminish whereby pushing investors to seek alternative sources of yield. This trend has been particularly pronounced since the 2008 financial crisis, as successive rounds of [quantitative easing (QE)](https://www.forbes.com/advisor/investing/quantitative-easing-qe/#:~:text=Quantitative%20easing%E2%80%94QE%20for%20short,lending%20to%20consumers%20and%20businesses.) drove down yields on government bonds and made conservative investments far less attractive. In this context, DeFi protocols began to look akin to an exciting alternative for those looking for levels of income higher than traditional savings accounts. DeFi platforms enable users to “yield farm” by locking up their crypto assets to earn rewards. These rewards come from interest paid on loans, transaction fees within decentralised exchanges (DEXs) and governance tokens issued by various blockchains. With [annual percentage yields (APYs)](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/wealth-management/annual-percentage-yield/) sometimes reaching double or even triple digits, [yield farming](https://www.investopedia.com/what-is-yield-farming-7098519) has attracted retail and institutional investors alike, many of whom viewed it as a way to profit from their digital assets.

In essence, yield farming involves using digital assets to provide liquidity to DeFi protocols which, in turn, reward these liquidity providers with interest payments and token incentives. For example, an individual might deposit stablecoins into a [liquidity pool on a DEX](https://bitpay.com/blog/liquidity-pools-explained/#:~:text=A%20liquidity%20pool%20is%20a,different%20assets%20within%20the%20pool.) (which others can then borrow or trade against) generating fees that are distributed back to the pool’s participants. This concept of incentivised liquidity is the backbone of many DeFi platforms, including those such as Uniswap, Aave and Compound. (NB - do remember that if you use liquidity pools, then tax is likely to be due on any profits; for more on this topic [click here](https://cryptotaxcalculator.io/us/guides/liquidity-pools/)). Low interest rates have amplified the appeal of yield farming and in traditional finance, low rates usually indicate a cheaper borrowing environment but also yield lower returns on deposits. In contrast, DeFi protocols offer considerably higher returns, with incentives structured to attract liquidity; this has drawn users from traditional finance who are seeking better yields than they can get from banks. Moreover, low rates in the traditional financial world have made DeFi’s high returns look even more attractive by comparison, accelerating adoption of crypto-based financial solutions and shifting liquidity from conventional assets to DeFi protocols. However, yield farming attractiveness comes under pressure as central banks raise interest rates to combat inflation; higher interest rates may make traditional investments more competitive than DeFi yields, decreasing capital flow into decentralised protocols. Although this macro-economic upheaval may cause investors to reconsider yield farming and liquidity provision risk-reward, some believe yield farming's attractiveness may survive rising rates since it offers financial innovation, ownership and autonomy in addition to large profits. Moreover, DeFi's permissionless, transparent and borderless characteristics may endure for many despite rising interest rates. And, as liquidity pools receive less capital inflow, protocols might adjust their incentives, leading to lower yields for yield farmers. This adjustment could be particularly impactful on smaller DeFi projects which often depend heavily on high yields to attract liquidity although larger, more established DeFi protocols might weather this shift better since they have diverse revenue streams and a more stable user base. Additionally, some DeFi protocols already operate with mechanisms designed to counterbalance yield compression - for instance, [Compound and Aave](https://www.okx.com/learn/aave-compound-defi-lending#:~:text=Aave%20has%20a%20fixed%20interest,assets%20in%20the%20lending%20pools.), dynamically adjust interest rates based on supply and demand which means that if liquidity drops, the yield paid out to liquidity providers could increase to attract more capital. This flexibility, whilst beneficial, still depends on the broader appetite for DeFi which may be tempered by an attractive rate environment in traditional finance.

Stablecoins also play a critical role in DeFi yield farming as they allow participants to lock in returns without being exposed to the volatility of cryptocurrencies such as Bitcoin or Ethereum. For instance, yield farmers often provide liquidity using stablecoins pegged to the US$, earning yields that, although high, are shielded from crypto price swings. In a rising rate environment, however, the advantages of holding and farming with stablecoins could diminish if more stable, inflation-resistant assets in traditional finance start offering competitive yields. Furthermore, the risks associated with DeFi, which were previously masked by the high yields, may become more prominent as competition from traditional finance increases. Smart contract vulnerabilities, impermanent loss and governance risks are issues that investors in DeFi willingly accept due to the appeal of high returns. If traditional finance offers a safer alternative with similar yields, these risks might deter potential DeFi users and divert capital back to conventional investments. The degree to which rising rates will impact DeFi participation remains uncertain and is likely to vary based on investor types. Institutional investors may be more prone to shifting their funds to traditional financial assets if these offer similar returns with lower risks. On the other hand, DeFi has attracted a significant number of retail investors who may prioritise the decentralised and self-custodial nature of DeFi over yield alone. For these users, the appeal of financial autonomy, asset control and the potential for capital gains from holding governance tokens could outweigh the draw of modestly higher yields in conventional finance. Moreover, the DeFi sector has demonstrated resilience and adaptability - as regulations evolve and more DeFi protocols incorporate risk-mitigation features such as insurance and transparency in governance, DeFi’s value proposition may shift from purely yield-focused to one of long-term viability, resilience and financial sovereignty.

Yet DeFi is not merely about yield farming - it has introduced innovative financial instruments and decentralised governance models that go beyond mere returns. Many proponents argue that DeFi’s potential lies in transforming finance, rather than just in competing on yields with traditional banks. Decentralised autonomous organisations (DAOs), tokenised assets and programmable financial contracts settling in real time with less reliant on intermediaries, are just a few examples of DeFi’s unique offerings. This innovation-driven appeal suggests that, whilst the yield-farming sector might face pressure from rising interest rates, DeFi as a broader movement might still retain significant relevance. Projects that offer unique utility, such as real-world asset tokenisation or cross-border payment solutions, may continue to attract users and capital. This evolution could help DeFi retain its competitive edge, even as traditional finance regains some allure; the relationship between traditional interest rates and DeFi yields is complex and fluid. And, whilst the low-interest-rate environment has undeniably contributed to the explosive growth of yield farming and liquidity provision in DeFi, an impending rate hike cycle could prompt a recalibration. DeFi protocols may respond with dynamic rate adjustments, innovative products or enhanced security and governance features to retain capital and continue to appeal to yield-seeking investors. Ultimately, DeFi’s future amid rising interest rates may hinge on its ability to evolve beyond high returns and to offer sustainable, innovative financial solutions that differentiate it from traditional finance. For those who see DeFi, not as more than a short-term yield opportunity but rather as a pioneering shift in financial autonomy and inclusivity, the sector’s long-term potential might continue to shine, regardless of rate fluctuations in the traditional financial world. Whether DeFi’s allure can endure in a world of rising rates remains an open question - one that will test the resilience, adaptability and intrinsic value of decentralised finance as a transformative force in the global economy.
