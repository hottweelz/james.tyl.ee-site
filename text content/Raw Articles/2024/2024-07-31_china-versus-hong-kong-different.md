# China versus Hong Kong different approaches to cryptocurrencies

**I. Regulatory Differences on cryptocurrencies**

> **A. Mainland China’s stance on cryptocurrencies and blockchain**
> 
> **(1) Ban on initial coin offerings (“ICOs”) and cryptocurrency exchanges**

Mainland China has implemented a stringent regulatory framework for ICOs and cryptocurrencies. The evolution of this framework can be traced through several key regulatory actions:

> (1) In 2013, Chinese authorities prohibited financial institutions from engaging in Bitcoin-related business.
> 
> (2) In 2017, China banned ICOs and cryptocurrency exchanges, prohibiting all forms of ICO activities and exchange services for token financing and trading platforms.
> 
> (3) In 2018, the government further tightened controls by prohibiting payment institutions from facilitating cryptocurrency transactions.
> 
> (4) A significant crackdown occurred in 2021 with the release of the "_Circular on Further Preventing and Resolving the Risks of Speculation in Cryptocurrency Trading_ (“**Circular 237** ”). This circular:
> 
> · Declared cryptocurrency-related business activities illegal
> 
> · Targeted offshore exchanges providing services to domestic citizens
> 
> · Enhanced supervision and enforcement measures.
> 
> (5) Additionally, the government has banned cryptocurrency mining and clarified potential criminal liabilities for those involved in crypto-related fraud or money laundering.

Despite these strict regulations, merely holding cryptocurrencies is not illegal in mainland China. The Beijing No. 1 Intermediate People’s Court stated in a 2018 ruling that “ _virtual commodities such as Bitcoin can be legally held by anyone, but current policies prohibit their use for exchanges with legal tender_.” However, this legal holding status does not necessarily translate into legal protection for cryptocurrency owners in disputes or transactions, especially if enforcement within mainland China is required and involves conversion between legal tender and cryptocurrencies. This principle was reinforced in a 2022 guiding case (No. 199) issued by the Supreme People’s Court of China (“**SPC** ”). In this case, the court annulled an arbitration award having ordered compensation based on Bitcoin’s value. The court held that such compensation indirectly supported transactions between cryptocurrency and legal tender, violating Chinese financial regulations and public interest. By designating this as a guiding case, the SPC sets a benchmark for all levels of courts on handling similar cases involving cryptocurrencies. I.e., attempts to validate or enforce cryptocurrency transactions through legal or arbitration processes may be rejected by Chinese courts if they involve conversion to legal tender. Consequently, both foreign cryptocurrency operators and domestic cryptocurrency investors should be aware of the compliance risks they face and the potential difficulties in enforcing related claims within mainland China. While holding cryptocurrencies itself is not illegal, parties may face significant limitations in obtaining legal protection.

> **(2) Encouragement of blockchain technology**

Despite the ban on ICOs and cryptocurrencies, mainland China actively encourages the development of blockchain technology through various national and local policies. In 2021, the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology [(“](https://policycn.com/public/commentaries/beijing-plans-to-have-world-leading-blockchain-firms-by-2025)**[MIIT](https://policycn.com/public/commentaries/beijing-plans-to-have-world-leading-blockchain-firms-by-2025)**[”) and Cyberspace Administration of China (“](https://policycn.com/public/commentaries/beijing-plans-to-have-world-leading-blockchain-firms-by-2025)**[CAC](https://policycn.com/public/commentaries/beijing-plans-to-have-world-leading-blockchain-firms-by-2025)**[”)](https://policycn.com/public/commentaries/beijing-plans-to-have-world-leading-blockchain-firms-by-2025) issued a guiding opinion aiming for world-class blockchain development by 2025. The plan seeks widespread blockchain applications across the economy, including product traceability, data circulation, and supply chain management. It aims to foster 3-5 internationally competitive enterprises, a few innovative leaders, and create 3-5 blockchain industry clusters. Local governments including Beijing, Shanghai, and Chongqing support blockchain development. Beijing's 2023-2025 Work Programme promotes blockchain research.

Furthermore, mainland China is advancing blockchain technology by formulating various standards. In 2023, MIIT, CAC, and the National Standardisation Administration released a guideline providing direction for creating [blockchain standards.](https://www.globaltimes.cn/page/202306/1291823.shtml) According to China Academy of Information and Communications Technology’s 2023 Blockchain White Paper, by the end of 2023, there are 3 national standards, 8 industrial standards, 167 group standards, and 31 regional standards. These standards cover sectors such as information security, IoT, finance, publishing, smart contracts, domain name resolution, government affairs, e-certificates, and public services. Blockchain service in mainland China is primarily regulated by the 2019 _[Administrative Provisions on Blockchain Information Services](https://www.loc.gov/item/global-legal-monitor/2019-02-12/china-rules-on-blockchain-based-information-services-issued-requiring-authentication-of-users-real-identities/#:~:text=\(Feb.,governing%20blockchain%2Dbased%20information%20services.)_[.](https://www.loc.gov/item/global-legal-monitor/2019-02-12/china-rules-on-blockchain-based-information-services-issued-requiring-authentication-of-users-real-identities/#:~:text=\(Feb.,governing%20blockchain%2Dbased%20information%20services.) These provisions define blockchain information services as those offered to the public via internet websites or apps using blockchain technology. Service providers shall file with the CAC within 10 working days of starting operations and may need additional permits for certain activities, such as telecom services. The regulations also require providers to implement security measures, including user registration, information review, emergency response protocols, and security protection measures.

> **B. Hong Kong’s approach**

Hong Kong has taken significant steps to position itself as a leading cryptocurrency hub in Asia. Hong Kong regulators had been cautious in their road to legislating cryptocurrency and virtual asset activities, and now appear to have a clear vision of regulations needed to be in place which will help solidify its position as the region’s crypto hub. The pressure to regulate came after a relatively challenging end to 2023, which included the cryptocurrency scandal surrounding the over-the-counter exchange, JPEX, involving the reported loss of assets of over [HK$1.65 billion](https://www.financemagnates.com/cryptocurrency/lawsuit-filed-as-investors-seek-to-reclaim-lost-assets-in-jpex-scandal/) for retail investors. More recently, the Personal Data Privacy Commission (“**PCPD** ”) of Hong Kong conducted [six raids on Worldcoin](https://www.pcpd.org.hk/english/news_events/media_statements/press_20240131.html), a cryptocurrency initiative that uses iris scans for identification and token earning purposes. There have been concerns that the JPEX and Worldcoin events could sour investors’ views on cryptocurrency in Hong Kong, as demonstrated by the Hong Kong Securities and Futures Commission (“**SFC** ”) setting up a cryptocurrency-focused working group to address public attitudes following the incidents. It has therefore become a priority for the Government to sharpen its tools on virtual asset (“**VA** ”) regulation, for example by establishing a licensing system and regulatory requirements for VA service providers and OTC regimes. 2024 has, however, shown positive signs for the Hong Kong VA market. With cryptocurrency prices rising and the first spot Bitcoin and Ether exchange-traded funds (“**ETFs** ”) applications being conditionally accepted by the SFC, investors remain bullish in the sector. On the latter point, Hong Kong regulators are the first in the region to debut crypto ETFs, showing their intent to lead the way and their confidence in the development of the industry. In April this year, the first spot [Bitcoin and Ether ETFs launched on the Hong Kong exchange](https://www.cnbc.com/2024/04/30/asias-first-spot-bitcoin-and-ether-etfs-debut-in-hong-kong.html#:~:text=Hong%20Kong%20on%20Tuesday%20launched,the%20cryptocurrencies%20at%20spot%20prices.), with the potential for these to become available to mainland Chinese investors if the products are proved to be risk controllable.

**II. Update on Digital Currency Developments**

**A. Mainland China’s Central Bank Digital Currency (CBDC) project**

E-CNY, a type of CBDC developed by the PBoC, has been in development and pilot testing since 2020 in major Chinese cities. Despite active testing and limited circulation, there is no definitive legislation establishing its legal status. Two drafts mentioning e-CNY have been published so far.

> i) The draft amendment to the _Law on People’s Bank of China_ in 2020 provided legal basis for the existence and use of the digital form of the Renminbi currency.
> 
> ii) The draft _Administrative Measures for Online Games_ issued in 2023 mandated the use of real-name e-CNY wallets for trading cryptocurrencies in online games, aiming to combat money laundering and telecom fraud.

The e-CNY pilot scheme now covers 17 provinces, enabling citizens to use it for various payments, including fees and shopping. The PBOC has also introduced a solution for foreign visitors to use e-CNY, first implemented during the 2022 Beijing Winter Olympics. Local governments are promoting [e-CNY usage. Jiangsu Province's 2023](https://www.scmp.com/economy/china-economy/article/3237317/whats-state-chinas-digital-yuan-2023) plan aims to expand its use in retail payments. Changshu has required public servants and state-owned enterprise salaries to be paid in e-CNY, and Guangzhou allows housing fund payments in e-CNY. Moreover, the government is promoting e-CNY for international trade and cross-border payments. Initiatives include a pilot for tourism payments between China and Singapore, a multilateral digital currency bridge project with Hong Kong, Thailand, and UAE, and participation in the BIS Innovation Hub’s Multiple CBDC Bridge to explore interoperability with Hong Kong and Singapore. These efforts aim to boost international trade and enhance global digital currency capabilities.

**B. Hong Kong’s stance and collaboration**

The Hong Kong Monetary Authority (“**HKMA** ”) has been actively exploring a CBDC since 2017 to understand their benefits and how they may be applied in both a wholesale and retail context. As part of the “Fintech 2025” strategy announced in 2021, the HKMA has been experimenting with CBDCs through various projects. In the wholesale context, the HKMA commenced its journey in 2017 with [Project LionRock](https://www.hkma.gov.hk/eng/key-functions/international-financial-centre/fintech/central-bank-digital-currency/#:~:text=The%20HKMA's%20initial%20exploration%20of,delivery%2Dversus%2Dpayment%20settlements.). This project studied potential applications of CBDCs in handling large-value payments and delivery-versus-payment settlements. In 2019, the HKMA and the Bank of Thailand commenced [Project Inthanon-LionRock](https://www.hkma.gov.hk/media/eng/doc/key-functions/financial-infrastructure/Report_on_Project_Inthanon-LionRock.pdf) to study the application of CBDCs for cross-border payments. This has since developed into [Project mBridge](https://www.bis.org/publ/othp40.htm) and now includes mainland China and the United Arab Emirates and has produced a trial platform which can facilitate real-time cross-border exchange transactions in a multi-jurisdictional context and on a 24/7 basis. In 2022, the project entered the pilot phase and during a six-week period conducted over 160 payments totalling over HK$171 million. The next phase of the project will be to continue to work on the platform, with a view to welcoming new participants and evolving it to become a minimum viable product which is used in both the public and private sectors.

In March 2024, the [HKMA launched Project Ensemble](https://fintech.global/2024/05/08/hkma-spearheads-project-ensemble-to-revolutionise-tokenisation-standards/), a new wholesale central bank digital currency (“**wCBDC** ”) project, to support the development of the tokenisation market in Hong Kong. The details of the sandbox which is to be used as part of the trial are to be released later on this year, which are expected to include the settlement of tokenised real world assets. In a retail context, the HKMA began researching retail CBDC in 2021, and has embarked on Project e-HKD to examine the prospect of issuing a retail CBDC in Hong Kong, i.e. an e-HKD. The HKMA is currently in Phase 2 of the project, which is expected to last until mid-2025 to evaluate the use cases of an e-HKD if this was to be released.

The divergent paths of China and Hong Kong in digital currency development reflect broader strategic and ideological differences. China’s centralized, government-led approach contrasts with Hong Kong’s market-driven, regulatory-focused strategy. How will these differing approaches influence the global financial landscape, and what implications will they have for the future of digital currencies and blockchain technology? Can Hong Kong’s progressive stance mitigate risks and harness the benefits of cryptocurrencies, while China’s cautious but ambitious CBDC efforts reshape global economic interactions?
