# Digital assets and AI: what happens when machines own crypto?

The [intersection of artificial intelligence (AI) and digital assets](https://www.galaxy.com/insights/research/understanding-intersection-crypto-ai/) represents a transformative shift in technology and finance. The way in which blockchains and the digital assets they can create and begin to interact with AI is reshaping how we interact with these digital assets. But, what if machines themselves owned and traded crypto? This emerging trend holds profound implications for the future of finance and technology that could revolutionise digital asset trading, enhance user experiences and address challenges such as identity verification and deep fakes. Hence, understanding this intersection is essential for navigating the future landscape of digital assets and their governance. Moreover, the financial services sector is navigating a [pivotal transformation](https://www.deloitte.com/ng/en/services/risk-advisory/services/how-artificial-intelligence-is-transforming-the-financial-services-industry.html) driven by AI, fundamentally changing traditional practices and enhancing operational efficiency. AI technologies, including [algorithmic trading and predictive analytics](https://www.fool.com/investing/stock-market/market-sectors/information-technology/ai-stocks/ai-in-investing/), are redefining financial markets by automating decision-making and increasing the speed and accuracy of trades. For instance, algorithmic trading allows for swift execution of intricate transactions, empowering traders to capitalise on subtle market fluctuations and quickly respond to ongoing events. [Wealthfront and Betterment](https://www.nerdwallet.com/article/investing/betterment-vs-wealthfront) are investment platforms that leverage artificial intelligence to improve portfolio management, delivering investment strategies tailored to adapt to market shifts. The capability of AI to analyse large volumes of data enables traders to make well-informed decisions with exceptional speed and accuracy. And, by [examining historical patterns and assessing market sentiment](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1319157824001046#:~:text=For%20example%2C%20AI's%20ability%20to,with%20incredibly%20powerful%20computers%20and), AI improves risk management and uncovers profitable opportunities. Additionally, the ongoing monitoring of markets and the flexibility of AI systems equip traders to handle the complexities of digital assets, fostering innovation and providing a competitive edge in the fast-changing financial environment.

[Machines are already being paid in almost real time](https://www.ledgerinsights.com/jp-morgan-says-jpm-coin-transactions-have-exploded-because-of-programmability/) for work they do using the JP Coin as a form of payment, but the concept of machines owning crypto wallets and assets marks a transformative shift in the digital asset landscape. Instead of highlighting ‘machines owning cryptos’, it would be clearer to say ‘machines assisting with managing crypto and assets’. Besides, since when did AI own crypto when it is doing the work for us, the employer? Hence, this paradigm anticipates autonomous systems (driven by AI), [managing and trading cryptocurrencies with less human intervention](https://www.brightspot.com/cms-resources/digital-asset-management-guide/the-role-of-ai-in-digital-asset-management). Currently, AI operates primarily as an assistant, facilitating trading decisions through [crypto bots that analyse market data and execute predefined strategies](https://koinly.io/blog/best-crypto-trading-bots/). However, the notion of machines autonomously owning digital assets is not far-fetched; as technology continues to advance, the potential for AI to evolve into systems capable of independent decision-making becomes increasingly plausible. After all, [Coinbase reported that two AI bots have now traded a digital asset between themselves](https://www.unlock-bc.com/127991/new-milestone-blockchain-technology-coinbase-witnesses-first-ai-to-ai-crypto-transaction/#:~:text=On%20August%2030%2C%20Coinbase%20CEO,tokens%20from%20another%20AI%20bot.). Meanwhile, one of the significant benefits of AI-driven ownership lies in its ability to enhance efficiency. By removing human emotions from trading decisions, [AI systems can execute trades based purely on analytical insights](https://fluidai.com/blog/ai-powered-crypto-wallets-the-future-of-digital-asset-management), which significantly lowers the risk of impulsive actions that typically lead to financial losses. This objective approach to trading may result in higher returns in the long term, since machines can process vast amounts of market data and adjust strategies in real time. Autonomous trading carries substantial implications, particularly regarding blockchain technology. And, by utilising smart contracts and decentralised platforms, machines can engage directly with digital assets, facilitating transactions without the involvement of intermediaries. This independence could lead to more efficient markets, allowing for instantaneous asset trading and minimised costs. However, the prospect of machines managing digital assets raises ethical and regulatory questions, although Larry Fink, CEO of BlackRock, envisages a future of [mass customisation](https://uk.finance.yahoo.com/news/blackrock-tokenisation-real-world-assets-zodia-executive-050037893.html) where AI bots are managing individuals’ portfolios of digital assets. Establishing accountability and transparency in autonomous systems will be crucial as AI capabilities continue to evolve. The future of digital asset management may hinge on balancing innovation with responsible governance, ensuring that the rise of machine ownership benefits all participants in the financial ecosystem.

In addition, AI is becoming increasingly significant in financial services, but it does bring with it [crucial ethical dilemmas related to trust, transparency and accountability](https://www.hso.com/blog/understanding-the-ethical-risks-of-ai-in-financial-services). AI systems can analyse extensive data to make complex financial decisions, often without substantial human supervision. This autonomy raises challenges regarding how AI determines specific outcomes, which can lead to concerns about the clarity of these algorithms and the challenges of [inbuilt bias](https://www.ibm.com/think/topics/shedding-light-on-ai-bias-with-real-world-examples) that can find its way into AI. In particular, proprietary algorithms tend to operate as “[black boxes](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/human-dynamics/articles/10.3389/fhumd.2024.1421273/full),” hindering regulators, clients and companies from fully understanding the reasons behind certain financial decisions. Correspondingly, AI applications in finance have the capability to create and escalate risks, prompting serious concerns regarding consumer and investor protection. For example, AI-driven trading systems can enhance market volatility, which may lead to substantial financial losses or prices spiralling ever higher, and increasingly more AI bots driving the price of an asset. To address these risks effectively, policymakers must take a forward-looking approach that weighs the rapid benefits of AI against the necessity of safeguarding consumers and investors. The [EU’s Artificial Intelligence Act (AI Act)](https://ioaglobal.org/blog/role-of-aI-in-finance/) provides a fundamental framework in this area, laying out a plan that emphasises transparency, accountability and ethical standards for AI systems, particularly those posing high or critical risks.

Accountability is yet another crucial issue. When mistakes happen, it becomes challenging to assign responsibility due to the complexities involved in AI's decision-making processes. If an AI system results in financial losses or makes biased decisions, establishing liability can be difficult, creating legal and ethical challenges for financial institutions. Likewise, biases in AI algorithms are a significant issue. As these systems learn from historical data, they may adopt the biases inherent in that data, resulting in unjust outcomes such as biased loan approval processes that disproportionately affect particular demographic groups. These ethical dilemmas give rise to regulatory challenges, as oversight mechanisms struggle to keep up with the swift evolution of AI technologies. Undoubtedly, AI has vast potential for transforming financial services, driving efficiencies and offering sharper insights. Nevertheless, the integration of AI into the financial sector requires careful attention to prevent unintended consequences. In decentralised financial ecosystems, [AI-powered machines can engage in increasingly complex interactions](https://www.rapidinnovation.io/post/decentralized-finance-ai-transforming-financial-ecosystems-blockchain-innovations-2024#7-future-of-ai-and-defi-in-financial-ecosystems), fundamentally altering how transactions are carried out and decisions are made. These machines, equipped with cutting-edge algorithms and real-time data processing capabilities, can facilitate machine-to-machine trading, executing trades autonomously and optimizing investment strategies without the need for human oversight. This evolution may lead to streamlined processes, greater efficiency and lower costs by enabling swift, data-driven decision-making. Yet, an intriguing question arises: might these machines surpass human investors in their intelligence? While AI systems are adept at analysing extensive amounts of data and spotting patterns that may go unnoticed by human traders, they still function within established parameters and depend on historical data. Human oversight is vital, as AI does not possess the contextual comprehension and emotional intelligence that frequently influence market trends - yet! Moreover, ethical considerations and accountability in AI decision-making underscore the need for human involvement to guarantee responsible trading practices.

The development of interactions between AI-driven machines presents vast opportunities, but it is important to balance this with human expertise and oversight to protect against biases and unforeseen repercussions. As these technologies evolve, encouraging collaboration between humans and machines will be key to fostering a more effective and just financial landscape. The future of AI in the digital asset market heralds’ transformative innovations that could reshape investment strategies. As AI technologies advance, we can anticipate enhanced predictive analytics, enabling investors to more effectively foresee market trends and fine-tune their portfolios. Developments such as advanced machine learning algorithms and real-time data processing will equip traders to make quicker, more informed decisions with greater precision. Moreover, integrating AI with blockchain technology has the potential to foster decentralised autonomous organisations (DAOs) that operate without human supervision, enabling them to make real-time adjustments to investment strategies in line with market trends. This shift could cultivate a more automated and data-driven trading environment, helping to mitigate human biases. As this dynamic between humans and AI progresses, teamwork will be essential. Whilst AI can improve decision-making processes, human insight and ethical judgment will remain key in addressing the complexities of the digital asset market, ensuring that technology complements rather than supplants the human touch in finance.

Certainly, the future of AI in the digital asset market is poised for groundbreaking change, reshaping investment strategies through enhanced predictive analytics and real-time data processing. By merging AI with blockchain technology, we could see the rise of DAOs that adapt investment strategies autonomously whereby fostering a more data-driven trading landscape. Nevertheless, we must confront a staggering question: will our reliance on AI reshape not only our financial futures but also redefine what it means to be human in an age where algorithms hold sway over our economic destinies?
