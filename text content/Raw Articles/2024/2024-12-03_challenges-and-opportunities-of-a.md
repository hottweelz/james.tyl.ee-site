# Challenges and opportunities of a crypto IPO in a volatile market

For a while now, crypto businesses have been gravitating toward IPOs so as to access a larger pool of capital, gain legitimacy in the market and provide liquidity for investors and early stakeholders. USDC-issuer, Circle, has attempted to [go public multiple times,](https://www.ccn.com/news/circle-ipo-ftx-restart-crypto-2024/) especially as stablecoin adoption grows. But whilst it has not executed an IPO as yet, Circle’s CEO has confirmed to [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-10-25/circle-ceo-says-the-company-s-ipo-ambitions-remain-despite-delay?embedded-checkout=true) that he is still looking to IPO the company. Regardless, it is still pushing for its [IPO plans in Hong Kong](https://www.crypto-news-flash.com/usdc-issuer-circle-prepares-for-ipo-with-hong-kong-expansion-and-regulatory-hopes/). Many other companies such as [Blockchain.com](http://blockchain.com), [Bitpanda](https://www.cryptopolitan.com/bitpanda-considers-ipo-or-sale/) and [Ripple](https://www.cnbc.com/2024/01/17/crypto-firm-ripple-explored-ipo-outside-us-but-wont-go-public-soon.html) have also considered going public; for a crypto business to go public, it typically follows the same path as any company by preparing a data room, finding an underwriter, completing regulatory document filings, etc. And, after regulatory and exchange approvals, the company then promotes the IPO via a roadshow to attract investors. Finally, it launches, issuing tokens or shares on the exchange with some reserved for underwriters.

In Frankfurt, Bitpanda is strategically [exploring an IPO](https://cryptopotato.com/bitpanda-exploring-potential-ipo-in-germany-report/) \- the company being valued at around $4 billion. Founded in 2014, Bitpanda has partnered with major financial institutions such as Deutsche Bank and Landesbank Baden-Württemberg to expand its crypto and payment services across Europe. The firm has also enlisted Citigroup and JPMorgan to advise on the process, though a final decision has yet to be made; it has [recently collaborated with Coinbase](https://blog.bitpanda.com/en/bitpanda-partners-coinbase-build-future-digital-investing) to enhance its European market presence. Meanwhile, another crypto company that has rarely been out of the press is Ripple which has [acquired Switzerland-based crypto custody firm, Metaco](https://www.reuters.com/markets/deals/ripple-buys-crypto-custody-firm-metaco-250-million-2023-05-18/) \- a strategic move to boost its infrastructure and improve the company’s appeal to institutional investors. However, Ripple is [not actively](https://u.today/ripple-ceo-says-ipo-is-not-high-priority) pursuing an IPO, [because of its battles with the SEC](https://insidebitcoins.com/news/ripples-anticipated-ipo-speculation-regulatory-hurdles-and-the-potential-for-20x-valuation-surge-1). For Ripple, the capital raised could be used to enhance the XRP Ledger and explore new blockchain applications, potentially setting a trend for other blockchain companies to follow suit. However, some [critics argue that ‘going public’ could reduce Ripple's control over its operations](https://coinmarketcap.com/community/articles/67169b4568f87c1a72fc24b6/), fearing the company may become overly-focused on shareholder interests (hindering the blockchain's development and growth) as it would have to answer to external investors. Certainly, [Bitpanda’s IPO](https://cryptopotato.com/bitpanda-exploring-potential-ipo-in-germany-report/) and Ripple’s exploration could potentially serve as a case study in understanding how public markets can impact crypto’s mainstream acceptance. Bitpanda’s move to Germany and Ripple's exploration of an IPO outside the US further reflects a trend among those companies seeking a crypto IPO in a volatile market. Ripple's CEO [reports the company had looked further](https://cnbc.com/2024/01/17/crypto-firm-ripple-explored-ipo-outside-us-but-wont-go-public-soon.html) afield because of the ‘hostile’ SEC, referring to the ongoing legal battle between Ripple and the US Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) over the classification of its XRP token as a security - notably though, other crypto-centric companies continue to remain committed to the US. Circle [moved its headquarters to New York](https://www.reuters.com/technology/crypto-firm-circle-move-headquarters-new-york-city-ahead-planned-ipo-2024-09-13/) ahead of its IPO and Europe’s most prominent Bitcoin miner, Northern Data, is also [looking to launch its IPO in the US](https://cryptoslate.com/europes-largest-bitcoin-miner-northern-data-to-launch-ipo-in-the-us/). However, a closer look at Northern Data actually [reveals that its IPO](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-07-02/northern-data-may-be-testing-limits-of-ai-frenzy-with-unit-ipo) is for its combined AI cloud computing and data centre businesses. Previously, the company had explored separate IPOs (one for its cloud unit and another for its Bitcoin mining operations) so the decision to merge the AI and data centre units for a joint offering raises questions about the company’s strategy. However, it could signal an effort to present a more unified and attractive proposition to investors amid growing interest in AI technologies.

Meanwhile, although it is easier to operate in the EU owing to the uniformity of law binding member countries together, European companies have increasingly [turned to US exchanges](https://www.euronews.com/business/2024/01/19/the-big-question-whats-driving-european-companies-to-us-exchanges-vc-of-nyse-explains-all) for listings due to advantages such as greater access to liquidity and larger investor bases. Furthermore, US exchanges (especially the NYSE and NASDAQ) offer more funding opportunities and exposure to tech-focused investors, which can be less accessible in European markets. This shift is driven by the need of these companies for capital and growth opportunities in competitive, innovation-driven markets - however, fewer and fewer IPOs have actually been approved. [Ark Invest](https://www.benzinga.com/24/11/41928272/cathie-woods-ark-invest-foresees-crypto-industry-boost-under-incoming-trump-administration) and [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2024-10-22/why-the-crypto-industry-s-ipo-drought-may-end-after-the-election) state that the crypto industry IPO drought should end post the election US. Recent reports indicate Kraken is moving closer to an IPO, possibly as early as 2025, with the exchange [reportedly discussing](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-06-06/crypto-exchange-kraken-said-to-be-in-talks-for-pre-ipo-fund-raising-round) a pre-IPO funding round to raise over $100 million. And, for those traditional investors wary of crypto volatility, IPOs offer a sense of stability and trust through regulated financial disclosures - in essence, traditional IPOs are one of many ways to trade in a volatile market publicly. Furthermore, special purpose acquisition vehicles (SPACs) provide a less risky alternative for crypto companies to go public, bypassing the complexities of a traditional IPO. For instance, Coincheck, a Japanese crypto exchange, is [merging with Thunder Bridge Capital Partners IV](https://www.pymnts.com/spac/2022/crypto-spacs-are-setting-the-standard/) in a deal valued at $1.3 billion​. Similarly, Core Scientific, a major Bitcoin mining firm, [successfully went public](https://carpenterwellington.com/post/bitcoin-mining-company-cipher-mining-looks-go-publiccipher-mining-bitcoin-mining-company-looks-go-public/) via a SPAC,​ and Bakkt Holdings (a digital asset platform) and Cipher Mining, (another Bitcoin miner) [chose the SPAC route​.](https://www.pymnts.com/spac/2022/crypto-spacs-are-setting-the-standard/)

However, Deloitte [reports that going public through a SPAC in the digital asset sector](https://www2.deloitte.com/us/en/pages/audit/articles/spac-cryptocurrency.html) presents several challenges - accounting for digital assets is complex (as they are classified as non-financial, intangible assets), meaning they must be written down when their value decreases but cannot be revalued upward if the market improves. This leads to discrepancies in financial statements, where the lowest market value during the holding period may be reported. Furthermore, the lack of comprehensive guidelines from the US Inland Revenue Services creates uncertainty and companies may face risks in developing tax positions, requiring careful disclosure in public filings. Lastly, internal controls, particularly around the custody of digital assets, are critical - companies must implement strong security measures for self-custody or ensure third-party custodians meet rigorous standards. This ensures auditors can confidently assess asset management, avoiding reputational risks such as being labelled “unauditable”. [eToro](https://beincrypto.com/9t2b_AFF_ENG_LEARN_etoro_signup) also initially pursued a SPAC but [mutually backed out](https://www.reuters.com/world/middle-east/israels-etoro-betsy-cohen-backed-spac-end-merger-deal-2022-07-05/) with its partner bank; eToro is now seeking an IPO in either New York or London. Hence, whilst SPACs offer speed and reduced regulatory friction, they may provide a different level of legitimacy than a traditional IPO, potentially impacting long-term investor confidence. And, regardless of the challenges and opportunities traditional IPOs or SPAC IPOs present, regulators are hot on their trail. The increased activity surrounding SPACs has led US regulators, particularly the SEC, to examine these transactions closely. In a [release](http://www.sec.gov/newsroom/press-releases/2024-8), the SEC has introduced [new rules](https://www.alston.com/en/insights/publications/2024/02/sec-adopts-amendments-to-the-spac-and-de-spac) to enhance investor protection, particularly in SPAC IPOs and de-SPAC transactions (business combinations between SPACs and target companies). These new regulations aim to address issues such as transparency in projections, conflicts of interest and the adequacy of disclosures, ensuring that SPACs are more aligned with the protections typically afforded in traditional IPOs​.

Certainly, the volatile journey of crypto IPOs underscores both the promise and peril of merging blockchain innovation with traditional markets. The sector stands at a crossroads - will IPOs bridge the gap between decentralised ambitions and centralised scrutiny, or will they succumb to the very volatility they aim to legitimise? The race to go public is not simply about raising funds - it is a litmus test for how the world integrates the next wave of financial technology.
