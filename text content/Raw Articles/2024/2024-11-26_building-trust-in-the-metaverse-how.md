# Building trust in the metaverse: how soulbound tokens could transform social interactions and online communities

The concept of [Soulbound Tokens (SBTs)](https://cointelegraph.com/news/what-are-soulbound-tokens-sbts-and-how-do-they-work) originates from the world of blockchain and decentralised finance (DeFi), inspired by the “soulbound” concept in video games, where certain items cannot be transferred between players. Proposed by Ethereum co-founder, Vitalik Buterin, SBTs are designed to be non-transferable digital assets that are permanently linked to a particular individual's digital identity or “soul”. However, unlike traditional fungible tokens or even non-fungible tokens (NFTs) that can be bought, sold or traded, SBTs are immovable; they are tied directly to a user and cannot be transferred to another person. This non-transferability introduces a new level of permanence and accountability, making SBTs ideal for representing an individual's credentials, achievements or reputation. In essence, [SBTs serve as a form of digital identity](https://www.ccn.com/education/crypto/soulbound-tokens/) verification, creating a foundation for establishing trust and accountability in the digital realm. And, as virtual worlds grow increasingly immersive and decentralised, they also become more susceptible to problems such as identity manipulation, fraud and trolling. Traditional online environments have long grappled with these issues, but the stakes are even higher in the metaverse. With more immersive interactions and valuable virtual assets at play, the potential for malicious behaviour can threaten the integrity of the community and dissuade genuine users from engaging. Furthermore, a significant aspect of this challenge is the anonymous nature of many digital interactions, where avatars and pseudonyms allow individuals to mask their true identities. Yet, whilst anonymity can provide freedom and privacy, it can also lead to a lack of accountability. In virtual spaces where it is difficult to verify the authenticity of a user's identity or reputation, malicious actors can easily disrupt communities, scam others, or impersonate reputable figures.

And, as virtual worlds grow increasingly immersive and decentralised, they also become more susceptible to problems such as identity manipulation, fraud and trolling; traditional online environments have long grappled with these issues, but the stakes are even higher in the metaverse. With more immersive interactions and valuable virtual assets at play, the potential for malicious behaviour can threaten the integrity of the community and dissuade genuine users from engaging. A significant aspect of this challenge is the anonymous nature of many digital interactions, where avatars and pseudonyms allow individuals to mask their true identities. And, whilst anonymity can provide freedom and privacy, it can also lead to a lack of accountability. In virtual spaces where it is difficult to verify the authenticity of a user's identity or reputation, malicious actors can easily disrupt communities, scam others or impersonate reputable figures.

This is where SBTs come in - by acting as a form of digital identity verification, SBTs offer a way to establish and verify trustworthiness within the metaverse, creating an environment that promotes authentic and accountable interactions. These tokens can transform social interactions, and companies such as [Mint Kudos](https://mintkudos.xyz/) and [Masa finance](https://www.fintechnexus.com/masa-and-celo-launch-soulbound-token-digital-identity/) are able to help create soul bound tokens. Masa signed up [over 36,000 people](https://www.solulab.com/soulbound-tokens-guide/) within a few weeks of launching so demonstrating its significance in the blockchain space. One of the primary applications of SBTs in the metaverse is in verifying membership and managing access control. Certainly, in many online communities and social networks, membership verification is crucial for ensuring that only trusted and vetted individuals can participate; traditional methods rely on usernames and passwords, which can be hacked, stolen or shared. By linking membership to a non-transferable SBT, organisations and communities can ensure that only legitimate members have access to specific areas or privileges within a virtual environment. For example, a professional network in the metaverse could issue an SBT to verify that a user is a certified member of a particular industry or holds specific qualifications. This token could serve as a digital badge, visible to others in the community, whereby allowing them to verify the user's credentials at a glance. However, unlike other forms of digital certification, an SBT cannot be transferred or sold, thus ensuring that it represents the genuine identity and credentials of the user.

Furthermore, in the metaverse, trust is critical for forming meaningful connections and fostering collaboration. SBTs could enable the development of digital trust scores where a user's reputation is assessed based on their interactions and behaviour within a virtual community. Trust scores are already prevalent in various digital platforms, such as rating systems for rideshare drivers or review scores for online sellers; SBTs could bring this concept to a new level by making trust scores an intrinsic, non-transferable part of a user's digital identity. Essentially, a digital trust score (represented by an SBT) could be based on a range of factors, such as the user’s adherence to community guidelines, the number of positive interactions they have had or feedback from other community members. This score would be a transparent, immutable indicator of a user’s trustworthiness within the metaverse and, hence, such a system could reduce instances of trolling, harassment and other negative behaviours since individuals with low trust scores might face restrictions or be excluded from certain spaces. Conversely, high trust scores could unlock access to more exclusive communities, rewarding users who contribute positively. Fraud and impersonation are pervasive issues in online environments and, in the metaverse, where virtual assets and transactions carry real value, these problems can be even more damaging; without clear mechanisms for verifying the identity and reputation of users, malicious actors can easily scam or impersonate others. SBTs provide a solution by linking an individual’s digital identity to verifiable credentials that cannot be transferred or falsified. For instance, an SBT could certify that a user is the legitimate owner of a particular digital asset or holds a specific position within a community. And, unlike traditional NFTs (which can be transferred) an SBT remains permanently associated with the original owner. This immutability could make it significantly harder for fraudsters to create fake profiles or impersonate others, thereby reducing the risk of scams and helping to build a safer virtual environment.

Meanwhile, the rise of decentralised social networks, where control is distributed rather than held by a central authority, has brought about new opportunities for user autonomy and privacy. However, it has also introduced challenges related to governance and accountability. In centralised platforms, administrators can enforce rules and monitor behaviour but, in decentralised systems, users often rely on community-driven moderation which can be inconsistent and less effective. SBTs could help solve these challenges by embedding a level of accountability directly into users’ digital identities. In decentralised networks, individuals could receive SBTs based on their contributions to the community, participation in governance or adherence to community standards. These tokens would serve as a public, non-transferable record of a user’s reputation and contributions, allowing other users to gauge their trustworthiness. By linking reputation to an immutable digital token, decentralised networks could create a form of community-driven governance that is fair and transparent. Users with positive reputations and high trust scores would be more likely to be trusted in decision-making roles, whilst those with poor reputations would face limitations on their influence within the network. This form of self-regulation could help maintain a healthy and respectful environment without relying on centralised authority.

However, whilst SBTs offer significant benefits in terms of trust and accountability, they also raise important privacy considerations. Since SBTs are permanently linked to an individual's digital identity, there is a risk that they could be used to track users across different virtual environments, potentially infringing on their privacy. So, in order to address these concerns, it is essential to implement safeguards that allow users to control which information is publicly accessible and which remains private. One potential solution is to allow users to possess multiple “souls”, or digital identities, each associated with different SBTs. This way, a user could maintain separate identities for different purposes - for instance, one for professional networking and another for personal interactions, all the while keeping them compartmentalised. Additionally, advanced cryptographic techniques, such as [zero-knowledge proofs](https://chain.link/education/zero-knowledge-proof-zkp#:~:text=Zero%2DKnowledge%20Proof%20Example,-A%20conceptual%20example&text=Alice%20wants%20to%20prove%20to,knowing%20which%20path%20was%20taken\).), could be employed to enable users to verify their credentials without revealing specific details about their identity. Yet, whilst the potential of SBTs in the metaverse is vast, there are still challenges and limitations that need to be addressed before widespread adoption can occur, including:

> · **revocation and updating**

Since SBTs are designed to be permanent, it can be difficult to revoke or update them in case of an error or a change in a user’s status. Solutions such as “expiration dates” for certain types of SBTs, or systems for authorised revocation by trusted entities, might be necessary to prevent issues related to outdated or inaccurate tokens.

> · **centralisation risks**

If a centralised authority controls the issuance of SBTs, it could lead to a concentration of power which goes against the decentralised ethos of the metaverse. Designing a decentralised framework for SBT issuance and management will be crucial for maintaining the autonomy of users.

> · **interoperability**

For SBTs to be effective, they need to be compatible across various metaverse platforms and ecosystems. Establishing common standards and protocols will be essential for creating a unified and interoperable system that allows users to carry their reputation and credentials across different virtual spaces.

But, despite the challenges, the potential of soulbound tokens to transform social interactions in the metaverse is undeniable. By creating a system where trust, reputation and accountability are embedded directly into users' digital identities, SBTs could lay the groundwork for a more secure, authentic and enriching virtual experience. Imagine a metaverse where individuals can confidently interact and collaborate and form communities, knowing that their counterparts are trustworthy and verified. SBTs could help make this vision a reality by offering a transparent, decentralised and non-transferable system for verifying credentials and establishing digital trust. As a result, could this mean that soulbound tokens (SBTs) redefine trust in the metaverse? However, although these non-transferable digital credentials (tied permanently to a user’s identity) promise to revolutionise online interactions by combating fraud, verifying membership and creating immutable trust scores, do they also risk turning digital identities into a double-edged sword? Whilst SBTs could enable safer, more transparent metaverse ecosystems, questions linger. How do we address their permanence if mistakes or changes arise? Could their implementation compromise privacy, allowing identities to be traced across platforms? And, if centralised authorities control SBT issuance, do they undermine the decentralised ethos they aim to support? In essence, as digital spaces grow more immersive, SBTs present an exciting yet complex frontier. So, will they build the foundation for a more secure and accountable metaverse, or will they introduce new challenges we are yet to fully grasp?
