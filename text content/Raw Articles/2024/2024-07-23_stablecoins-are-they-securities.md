# Stablecoins are they securities?

Although at a glance, quite a dry “black letter” topic the question of whether earning interest from stablecoins might mean they are a “security” has a practical importance for anyone who owns them. A stablecoin per se is not a security. Is there any risk it becomes one if you earn interest on it? In recent years the SEC has brought proceedings against the issuers or sponsors of various cryptocurrencies on the basis that the cryptocurrencies are securities that have not been properly registered or distributed in accordance with US federal law on securities. This has included; Bitcoin, Binance, XRT (SEC v Ripple), Luna (SEC v Terraform).

In order to determine if a digital asset is a security US courts and the SEC have relied upon the US Supreme Court’s Howey Test to analyse whether a cryptocurrency is an “ _investment contract” and therefore a security. The test has 4 limbs; an investment contract will exist if: “A person [1] invests his money [2] in a common enterprise and [3] is led to expect profits [4] solely from the efforts of the promoter or a third party._ ”

Applying this test Bitcoin has not been found to be a security because it only satisfied the first limb of the Howey test, namely; that it was an investment of money. There was no joint enterprise or pooling of investor funds and the value did not depend on a third party. In the proceedings relating to XRP there have been conflicting judgments relating to whether the cryptocurrency is a security and an appeal court verdict remains outstanding on the issue.

The same Howey test will also be applied to determine whether a stablecoin is a security. The result of its application is very fact dependent and various considerations are at play that relate to how the digital asset is set up and operates.

The 3rd limb of the Howey test is that the investor should have a reasonable expectation of profit. This could be a share of income, profits or a gain from capital appreciation. The obvious starting point is that a stablecoin cannot be a security because its value is pegged, for example to the US dollar, there can be no expectation of profits. It also matters who is receiving the payment of interest and whether this is a reference to the holder of the stablecoin or the sponsor/issuer. [The UK FCA’s discussion paper](https://gbr01.safelinks.protection.outlook.com/?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.fca.org.uk%2Fpublication%2Fdiscussion%2Fdp23-4.pdf&data=05%7C02%7CJames.RamsdenKC%40Astraea-Group.com%7C0c6feccc98bd401d1c6408dca8190f21%7C0b41d8b3015d450f8fd47be5861b7416%7C1%7C0%7C638570072964237219%7CUnknown%7CTWFpbGZsb3d8eyJWIjoiMC4wLjAwMDAiLCJQIjoiV2luMzIiLCJBTiI6Ik1haWwiLCJXVCI6Mn0%3D%7C0%7C%7C%7C&sdata=BK3Ob%2Bgw8q7HECmnrlfACubgkVoic52I46dcAuVyHV0%3D&reserved=0) at 3.12 suggests that stablecoin _issuers_ derive their profits from interest and returns on the assets that back the stablecoin.

In contrast the Howey test approaches the analysis from the perspective of the purchaser and the expectation of profit must be theirs. The problem with this approach is that the analysis cuts both ways. It could equally be said that the issuer invests its money in a common enterprise expecting profits from the third parties in the form of purchasers.

Most regulators will only be concerned with whether a stablecoin is a security when the interest is paid to the holder of the stablecoin. Interest payments would not usually be part of the arrangements between issuer and purchaser. Instead, if crypto traders want to earn interest from their stablecoins they can do this by using other specialist loan platforms to lend their stablecoins to others in return for interest. Alternatively, they can deposit their stablecoins in a “savings account” that pays interest. Some crypto exchanges also pay interest to traders who deposit their stablecoins with them. Examples of the different methods are listed[ here](https://blog.ledn.io/en/stablecoin-interest).

In each of these scenarios the interest arises from external arrangements with 3rd parties. This might suggest the interest payments are not part of the “investment contract” that would make stablecoin a security. However a [2001 report by The President’s Working Group on Financial Markets](https://gbr01.safelinks.protection.outlook.com/?url=https%3A%2F%2Fhome.treasury.gov%2Fsystem%2Ffiles%2F136%2FStableCoinReport_Nov1_508.pdf&data=05%7C02%7CJames.RamsdenKC%40Astraea-Group.com%7C0c6feccc98bd401d1c6408dca8190f21%7C0b41d8b3015d450f8fd47be5861b7416%7C1%7C0%7C638570072964261268%7CUnknown%7CTWFpbGZsb3d8eyJWIjoiMC4wLjAwMDAiLCJQIjoiV2luMzIiLCJBTiI6Ik1haWwiLCJXVCI6Mn0%3D%7C0%7C%7C%7C&sdata=6G3zjTW70ulCA%2BlysnzN%2F1QB%2BPstF1IYPj7UadlBxwE%3D&reserved=0) (p 11) states that “ _much of the trading, lending, and borrowing activity currently fueled by stablecoins on digital asset trading platforms and within DeFi similarly may constitute securities and/or derivatives transactions that must be conducted in compliance with federal securities laws…”_ This suggests that even if the interest arrangements are completely separate to the investment contract the stablecoin could be a security.

An expectation that the asset could be sold for gain would mean the investor had a reasonable expectation of profits. It is feasible that traders might trade stablecoins pegged to fiat currencies just as conventional market currencies are traded for profit. Some stablecoins can be pegged to assets that are not fiat, such as gold. Investors in gold can expect to profit from a rise in gold prices and there is no reason why this would not be the same for investors in a gold stablecoin.

In my view the better argument is that where investors in gold seek to take advantage of fluctuations in the gold market there is no “investment contract” without any effort by the sellers to increase the value of the gold. The key, in my view, is the third and fourth limbs of the Howey Test. They seem to be the relevant tests and if they are applied it seems unlikely that an interest bearing stablecoin becomes a security.

So why is that?

Helpful factors that point away from an asset being a security include:

> · “ _Price appreciation resulting solely from external market forces (such as general inflationary trends or the economy) impacting the supply and demand for an underlying asset generally is not considered “profit_ ” under the Howey test.”
> 
> · where the digital asset “ _acts as a substitute for real (or fiat) currency_ ”
> 
> · where “ _The digital asset is marketed in a manner that emphasizes the functionality of the digital asset, and not the potential for the increase in market value of the digital asset_.”

Contrary pointers to a “reasonable expectation of profits” include:

> · “ _the digital asset is transferable or traded on or through a secondary market or platform, or is expected to be in the future_.” [As noted above, stablecoins can be loaned and traded on exchanges].
> 
> · where “ _the [promoter, sponsor or third party] continues to expend funds from proceeds or operations to enhance the functionality or value of the network or digital asset.”_

The most articulate and compelling argument I have found is from the issuer of USDC, a stablecoin pegged to the US dollar who[ filed this amicus brief](https://gbr01.safelinks.protection.outlook.com/?url=https%3A%2F%2F6778953.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net%2Fhubfs%2F6778953%2FFILE%2520STAMPED%2520Motion%2520for%2520Leave%2520to%2520File.pdf&data=05%7C02%7CJames.RamsdenKC%40Astraea-Group.com%7C0c6feccc98bd401d1c6408dca8190f21%7C0b41d8b3015d450f8fd47be5861b7416%7C1%7C0%7C638570072964272010%7CUnknown%7CTWFpbGZsb3d8eyJWIjoiMC4wLjAwMDAiLCJQIjoiV2luMzIiLCJBTiI6Ik1haWwiLCJXVCI6Mn0%3D%7C0%7C%7C%7C&sdata=ceiMRNW1cWsszYSmak5soxr3imW%2B1G20MQHJWlcHVE4%3D&reserved=0) in SEC v Binance Holdings Limited with submissions on why stablecoins are _not_ securities. In that case the SEC argued that Binance’s stablecoin BUSD _was_ a security. As part of its case the SEC said that Binance offered interest earnings through a reward programme.

Circle’s amicus counter arguments included:

> · that stablecoins “ _do not independently give buyers any potential for profit, and certainly not based on the efforts of the stablecoin iss_ uer.”
> 
> · Payment stablecoins that are redeemable at a constant value are different from instruments normally regulated by the SEC, for example “ _People do not use a stock certificate to buy pizza_ ”
> 
> · “ _purchasers of payment stablecoins have no reasonable expectation of profits, because they know from how stablecoins are marketed and operate that the coins have a fixed value and that buyers will be able to redeem their tokens only at the same price at which they purchased them_.”
> 
> · “ _purchasers of payment stablecoins are not part of any common enterprise_ ” [this goes to limb 2 of Howey]
> 
> · “ _any “profits” associated with payment stablecoins cannot be attributed “predominantly” (or at all) to the efforts of the stablecoin issuer_ ” …”Any increased purchasing power that a stablecoin accrues reflects merely fluctuation in the underlying value of the dollar, something entirely outside issuers’ control.”
> 
> · “ _stablecoins most closely fall within the statutory category of “currency_ ”, which the securities laws exclude from the definition of a security” … “ _Courts have interpreted this category to include not only literal fiat currency, but also “cash substitute[s]” that can be later “redeemed” for dollars_ ”.

In my view those arguments are pretty bomb proof. Interest bearing stablecoins should not be regarded as securities.

A Quick Update On [The Economic Crime & Corporate Transparency Act 2023](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2023/56)

When I last wrote for [Digital Bytes on 8th May 2024](https://digitalbytes.substack.com/p/the-state-and-digital-asset-enforcement) I vented about this legislation because of the extraordinarily draconian powers it introduced and the relative stealth with which that was done, by quiet amendment to the well-known Proceeds of Crime Act 2002.

Perhaps unsurprisingly these powers seem not to have been deployed in the way the legislators intended because this new legislation not only impacts crypto assets but any digital assets that is stored/transferred using Distributed ledger technology or a blockchain. There have been very few instances of cryptoassets being seized only at the start of an investigation and before any arrest; a power introduced by S47C(1).

So far as my team at Astraea Group is aware, there have been no instances of UK law enforcement exercising their new power to “destroy” cryptoassets as provided for by S.67AA(2)(c), something I observed in my previous article seemed practically impossible.

Similarly, there has apparently been no exercise of the equally mystifying new power in S.67AA(4) to forcibly repatriate cryptoassets, something the [Tulip Trading case](https://www.4pumpcourt.com/tulip-trading-limited-v-wladimir-van-der-laan-and-ors-2023-ewca-civ-83/) should have warned against.

We now have a new UK Government. It includes a number of individuals, both in the House of Commons and House of Lords, who genuinely understand this sector and the law. That is a promising change from what has gone before. We shall see how long these troublesome new provisions therefore survive. They could go the same way as the UK’s Rwanda policy – “dead on arrival”?
