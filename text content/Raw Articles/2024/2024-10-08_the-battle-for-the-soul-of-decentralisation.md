# The battle for the soul of decentralisation

Whilst we cannot be certain when the Web3 version of the internet went mainstream, we can be sure it emerged as a vision for a new iteration of the internet that would be more decentralised, open and user-centric than the current Web2 model. However, decentralisation has been corrupted by the same concept it seeks to solve, and this has led to a fundamental misunderstanding of its original meaning. The Web3 term for this phenomenon is [decentralisation theatre](https://www.coindesk.com/consensus-magazine/2023/06/08/are-daos-doomed-to-decentralization-theater/), which is a situation wherein a project pretends to be more decentralised than it truly is. Essentially, before the Web3 became the poster child for decentralisation, its spirit was embodied in the torrent sites that proliferated across the internet. Powered by peer-to-peer networks, these platforms offered a glimpse of a future where content distribution is decentralised - torrent sites (such as [Pirate Bay](https://news.bitcoin.com/a-deeper-look-into-the-pirate-bays-mysterious-piratetoken-soft-launch/) and [YIFY](https://cointelegraph.com/top-people-in-crypto-and-blockchain-2021/andre-cronje#:~:text=Andre%20Cronje%20%3A%20Founder%20of%20Yearn.)) epitomised decentralisation. Users could share files directly with each other, bypassing centralised servers. Once a symbol of decentralisation, BitTorrent was eventually [acquired by Justin Sun's Web3 company, TRON,](https://www.ccn.com/tron-founder-justin-sun-acquires-bittorrent-after-legal-battle/) marking a significant shift in decentralised technologies. Whilst Web3 has evolved decentralisation, its essence is now challenged in the ongoing battle about its interpretation.

The frontier of this battle has seen the US Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) actively pursue classifying digital tokens as securities, aiming to regulate them under traditional securities laws - although the [SEC has recently stated](https://beincrypto.com/sec-crypto-asset-securities/) that “ _crypto asset securities as contracts tied to sales, not the crypto assets themselves.”_ Whilst many tokens have been scrutinised, Bitcoin has been deemed by the [SEC not to be a security](https://www.bitsofblocks.io/post/sec-chair-confirms-btc-is-a-commodity-but-infers-eth-is-a-security#:~:text=In%20a%20recent%20interview%20with,of%20remaining%20cryptocurrencies%20are%20securities.) and, largely due to its decentralised nature, [Ethereum has also avoided](https://www.coindesk.com/business/2024/04/04/ether-could-avoid-designation-as-a-security-with-centralization-risk-easing-jpmorgan-says/) being categorised as a security largely due to its decentralised nature._****_ Ethereum, akin to other Web3 bodies (and despite its decentralised nature), has had this exact nature put to the test - they have all faced regulatory requirements that challenge the core principles of decentralisation. In several countries, companies operating in the Web3 space must submit user lists to comply with regulations, mainly when digital assets are classified as securities. This classification mandates the disclosure of sensitive user information, which runs counter to the decentralised ethos of privacy and control that Web3 promises. Digital exchanges are typically required to conduct know your customer (KYC) procedures, resulting in centralised databases that store personal user data. These centralised repositories become [prime targets for cyber-attacks](https://www.ccn.com/news/crypto/crypto-exchange-kyc-hack/), further reneging on the promised security and this tension between regulatory compliance and maintaining the protection and privacy of decentralised ecosystems continues to be a significant challenge for Web3 businesses. The constant bending of Web3 to fit Web2 moulds can compromise [Web3’s core.](https://www.coindesk.com/learn/what-is-web-3-and-why-is-everyone-talking-about-it/)

The theatrics of the decentralisation theatre have spread to most cryptocurrencies, including Bitcoin. The [centralisation of control within the Bitcoin network](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/bitcoins-centralization-problem-david-b-hoppe) has become a growing concern as a small number of companies now hold a significant share of the network's mining power. Indeed, [over 5% of Bitcoins](https://www.bitsofblocks.io/post/sec-chair-confirms-btc-is-a-commodity-but-infers-eth-is-a-security#:~:text=In%20a%20recent%20interview%20with,of%20remaining%20cryptocurrencies%20are%20securities.) are owned by exchange traded funds (ETFs) and these Bitcoins are managed by traditional highly regulated asset managers. This concentration of influence undermines Bitcoin's foundational principle of decentralisation, raising questions about the long-term sustainability of its distributed network. With a few key players dominating mining, the risk of reduced security, potential collusion and manipulation, increases. This trend challenges the broader decentralisation movement, proving the difficulty of maintaining a truly decentralised system in the face of economic incentives and market dynamics. And it does not even stop here. The main goal of Web3 has always been to empower users and [create](https://web3.foundation/about/) _“a decentralised and fair internet where users control their data, identity, and destiny.”_ In trying to go mainstream or to avoid regulations, many Web3 companies have sacrificed the decentralisation aspect of their offering. Recent events have accentuated the limitations of so-called “decentralised” Web 3 companies - a recent Amazon Web Services [outage](https://www.vice.com/en/article/wxdnxy/amazons-server-outage-took-down-a-decentralized-crypto-exchange) caused a “decentralised” exchange to crash while other centralised exchanges, such as [Coinbase](https://coinmarketcap.com/alexandria/article/coinbase-down-as-amazon-web-services-suffers-outage) and [Binance.US](http://binance.us), were also hit.

Meanwhile, since NFTs are stored on blockchains, many NFT applications and services rely on centralised platforms such as OpenSea for data access and infrastructure. Moxie Marlinspike discovered this dependency when he discovered that even popular crypto wallets (e.g. Metamask) use centralised services such as Etherscan to retrieve user transaction data. Furthermore, this revelation challenges the notion of [true decentralisation](https://www.vice.com/en/article/n7nzbx/researchers-the-nft-economy-is-just-as-unequal-as-the-real-one) in the NFT space and, by extension, Web3. Furthermore, the battle for the soul of decentralisation often involves countries, the most recent being Telegram founder, Pavel Durov, who [broke his silence ](https://cointelegraph.com/news/pavel-durov-breaks-silence-for-first-time-since-arrest)after being arrested in France in August. He expressed surprise at the arrest - the French authorities had targeted him instead of the company. Durov had fled Russia in the past because the state wanted access to “encryption keys” for surveillance. And when French authorities wanted to crack down on content on Telgram Durov is being charged. [Brazil's recent ban](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/crkmpe53l6jo) on X (formerly Twitter) sparked increased interest in privacy-focused platforms such as Mullvad VPN, which has remained steadfast in resisting government pressures by not storing user data. Increased interest in Mullvad came after it was [endorsed](https://longportapp.com/en/news/213207738) by Jack Dorsey shortly after the Twitter ban - Dorsey has also [invested in decentralised Bitcoin mining](https://www.forbes.com/sites/digital-assets/2023/12/01/jack-dorsey-backs-ocean-in-shifting-toward-decentralized-bitcoin-mining/) in the past. His interest in Web3 is akin to Mullvad’s resilience, which shows the importance of decentralised, privacy-first approaches. Furthermore, Web3 can draw lessons from Mullvad’s commitment to privacy by building infrastructure prioritising user control and anonymity. Mullvad VPN has [resisted Swedish government pressure](https://mullvad.net/en/blog/mullvad-vpn-was-subject-to-a-search-warrant-customer-data-not-compromised) by not storing user data, whereby demonstrating resilience even under legal scrutiny. By refusing to compromise data security, platforms such as Mullvad and Telegram exemplify the principles Web3 aims to achieve in creating a decentralised, user-focused internet.

Essentially, the struggle to uphold decentralised principles is not merely a semantic dispute but a reflection of deeper societal and cultural tensions. People yearn for freedom, autonomy and a fairer world transcending centralised systems' limitations. The battle for decentralisation's soul might not be a sprint to a finish line but a journey marked by countless stops and unexpected turns. And on this path, we are constantly reminded of its true essence: its ability to empower individuals, foster innovation and resist the concentration of power. As Web3 navigates this journey, we encounter challenges that demand careful consideration. The need for measures to combat harmful content is exemplified by the [World Economic Forum,](https://www.weforum.org/agenda/2024/09/how-are-social-media-services-moderated-to-usually-avoid-legal-trouble-a3d47a326a/) which questioned: “ _Is patience waning with efforts to moderate content on social media_. The integration of AI, birthing decentralised AI, can play a crucial role in enhancing the efficiency of such measures and providing rapid responses to emerging threats.
