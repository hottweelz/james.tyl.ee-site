# How AI and blockchain can transform supply chains 

Modern supply chains have hit a level of complexity that has never been seen before as global trade becomes ever more diversified. The growing digitization of the world economy, the development of [IoT technology](https://www.zdnet.com/article/what-is-the-internet-of-things-everything-you-need-to-know-about-the-iot-right-now/) and advanced personality-based targeted marketing techniques have created an environment where customer markets are instantly available, adaptable and trackable. Businesses are constantly striving for ways to improve their profit margins but as, [COVID-19 dramatically demonstrated](https://www.ey.com/en_gl/insights/supply-chain/how-covid-19-impacted-supply-chains-and-what-comes-next), it can be hard to maintain supply chains from raw materials to the final product. Customers, companies and suppliers increasingly span industries, demographics and a wide selection of jurisdictions. Our supply chains have indeed proved to be vulnerable as has been seen by a lack of [computer chips and other electronic components](https://steatite-embedded.co.uk/insights/tech-explained/electronic-component-shortages-and-the-impact/). Investigative consumer interaction and critical analysis have revealed the significance of ethically sourced raw materials and supply chain transparency for product value and long-term growth. Businesses also struggle to comprehend product development due to the functional complexity of the supply chain. This makes firms more susceptible to bogus supplies and harder to engage stakeholders.

Blockchain technology is increasingly being used across [many different industries](https://www.techtarget.com/searchcio/feature/Todays-blockchain-use-cases-and-industry-applications#:~:text=Top%20blockchain%20use%20cases&text=%22For%20example%2C%20financial%20services%20can,The%20possibilities%20are%20endless.%22), ushering-in greater transparency and independent ledgers that store information about transactions. This information can be seen, checked and, most importantly, be almost impossible to alter, thus fostering greater trust. Furthermore, as research from [Barclays Corporate Banking](https://www.themanufacturer.com/articles/conscious-consumer-many-care-provenance-products/) found almost six years ago: “.._consumers now fervently care about the provenance of their products, this is perhaps most evident in the fashion and food industry_.” Therefore, the ability to prove the provenance for a product could be significant for the end consumer and the existing highly complex supply chains can make this a real challenge. AI, coupled with blockchain technology, will transform business and supply chain management ([SCM](https://www.oracle.com/ng/scm/what-is-supply-chain-management/)). According to IDC,[ half of the supply chains](https://www.oracle.com/a/ocom/docs/applications/scm/idc-supply-chain-planning-paper.pdf) use AI for long-term demand forecasting and planning. Top SCM spenders average $17 million. Before 2024, almost [50% of G2000](https://www.inboundlogistics.com/cms/article/manufacturing-it-investing-for-the-future/) manufacturing enterprises may use AI for SCM. Essentially, AI and the blockchain-powered platforms can track a product from raw materials to the final product on a shelf. Digitising physical assets and generating a distributed transactional record increases transparency that can be shared with various stakeholders that are engaged in a supply chain even if these parties are spread globally. Thus, supply chain management using AI and blockchain can help address the challenges of shipping delays, fraud, and errors and improve administration costs. Furthermore, AI and [blockchain-powered apps](https://www.intuz.com/blockchain) offer flexibility, making it possible to reach extensive databases worldwide. This is especially helpful when dealing with high-value items such as [pharmaceutical drugs](https://www.pwc.co.uk/healthcare/pdf/health-blockchain-supplychain-report%20v4.pdf) and gems, which consistently lead to scams or fakes. When less time is spent verifying data, more time can be spent ensuring that products are delivered safely and securely. And, by making it easier to add more users, the technologies also improve the product management system, lower delivery costs and build consumer trust faster.

AI and blockchain reduce paperwork and bureaucracy, particularly since up to [10% of freight invoices](https://www.mckinsey.com/~/media/McKinsey/Industries/Retail/Our%252520Insights/Future%252520of%252520retail%252520operations%252520Winning%252520in%252520a%252520digital%252520era/McK_Retail-Ops-2020_FullIssue-RGB-hyperlinks-011620.pdf) require information, causing conflicts and supply line issues. The use of AI and blockchain can help automate storing information digitally and track the time between pickup and delivery, eliminating the need for a paper trail; this connects the wholesaler, logistics service provider and customer. [Supply chain automation](https://www.intuz.com/blog/iot-in-supply-chain-management) reduces human errors by automating much of the reporting and recording of movements of goods. Indeed, [McKinsey](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/travel-logistics-and-infrastructure/our-insights/the-multi-billion-dollar-paper-jam-unlocking-trade-by-digitalizing-documentation) claims that trade documentation is a paper-intensive and resource-consuming process: “ _An electronic bill of lading could save $6.5 billion in direct costs and enable $40 billion in global trade_ ”. Now that the electronic bill of lading (sometimes referred to as the “[Blockchain Bill](https://www.forbes.com/sites/lawrencewintermeyer/2023/09/21/the-blockchain-bill-that-doesnt-mention-blockchain-uk-leads-the-digital-space-race-in-trade/)”) has been passed, it will be interesting to see if these savings can be achieved. Supply chains can be a logistical juggle, involving physical tasks and copious amounts of paperwork. The efficient use transportation is not only good for a company’s sustainability credentials but can offer a competitive advantage over other suppliers. AI and blockchain technology can make it easier for different people to view the information ensuring supply chains are better connected - information, therefore, moves smoothly and so reduces queries and questions as to the timing of deliveries, etc. **** Companies that use AI and blockchains to manage their supply chains may gather and analyse data on how items are created, where they originate from, what raw materials they utilise, how they are handled, etc. However, for the various parties engaged on a supply chain it is prudent to master AI and blockchain tools in order to improve supply chain networks going forward**.** The word "chain" in SCM evokes thoughts of connections, working together and partnerships; a strong relationship between the people involved in a process makes it work. And, for practical value to be made, this cooperation is needed. When a company uses AI and blockchain in its supply chain management (SCM), it takes on a significant project with many parts. The technologies can make it easier for multiple parties inside and outside the company and this makes supply lines more efficient and clearer. Five ways AI and blockchain can be used are:

> · **supply-chain planning using machine learning**

Planning the acquisition, production and distribution of products and commodities is known as “supply planning”. SCM relies heavily on machine learning-powered systems for improved inventory, customer demand and supply forecasting; they help supply chain managers make better choices.

> · **automatic translation and language understanding**

[Natural language processing (NLP)](https://www.techtarget.com/searchenterpriseai/definition/natural-language-processing-NLP) blends AI and ML to let people interact naturally with computers through text or sound. Applications of natural language processing include intelligent assistants, digital ‘phone calls and email filters. The supply chain can benefit from NLP by reducing administrative costs and making better use of foreign language data. Due to unique language hurdles between suppliers and customers, compiling enormous data sets and expediting compliance and auditing procedures is now feasible.

> · **utilising ML for warehouse and stock management**

Supply chains depend on robust inventory and warehouse management systems. Service and consumer-focused businesses must avoid overstocking and understocking. Stores can predict product demand with a forecasting tool and machine learning. This operation was never automated until ML, it was mainly done by hand where inventory errors were inevitable. Here are [eight ways ML](https://www.scjunction.com/blog/ai-and-machine-learning-transform-warehouse) can help warehouse management.

> · **blockchain-based settlements for the[automotive supply chain](https://www.autostoday.com/blog/blockchain-impact-on-the-automotive-industry)**

The technology enables instant, global money transfers between any two parties, with no need for intermediary banks or wire transfers. It takes minutes, as opposed to days and is rapid and risk-free. This facilitates productive blockchain creation.

> · **shipment quality assurance with smart contracts**

In logistics, “smart contract” refers to an electronic contract with built-in triggers for its execution. For instance, after the manufacturer verifies on the blockchain that the logistics provider has dispatched the items by the manufacturer's specifications, payment to the logistics provider is released. This assures a high standard of delivery and particularly relevant use case in the [supply of pharmaceuticals](https://fastercapital.com/content/Smart-contract-development--Smart-Contracts-in-Supply-Chain-Management--Boosting-Efficiency-and-Trust.html).

In conclusion, according to the shipping firm [Maersk:](https://www.maersk.com/logistics-explained/transportation-and-freight/2023/09/27/sea-freight-guide#:~:text=Approximately%2080%25%20of%20the%20world's,were%20transported%20via%20ocean%20freight.) “ _Approximately 80% of the world’s goods are transported by sea_ ”, meaning that $billion of raw materials and finished products are literally floating about the world’s oceans at any one time. Hence, it is not difficult to understand why businesses need to use whatever tools they can to improve their supply chains. So, as companies integrate AI and blockchain, will supply chains become more interconnected and resilient? But, are the benefits worth the challenges, and what new questions will arise as we embrace these technologies? Surely the future of global trade hinges on these answers in order to shape a more efficient, transparent and trustworthy supply chain landscape.
