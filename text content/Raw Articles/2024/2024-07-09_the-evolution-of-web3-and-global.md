# The evolution of Web3 and global politics 

Initially, although cryptocurrencies were dismissed as fantasies designed and used by tech savvy geeks, they are increasingly being taken more seriously as use cases continue to evolve and the number of users expands on a global basis. As more people use cryptocurrencies they sway votes, politicians are increasingly paying attention to them. Look at how [in the US, Biden](https://www.nasdaq.com/articles/biden-campaign-reportedly-exploring-bitcoin-and-crypto-donations) is now looking at accepting Bitcoin to help fund his re-election campaign. Cryptocurrency is also a large and growing industry involving its miners, investors and consumer infrastructure. It is attracting more businesses that accept cryptocurrencies as payment, and overall confidence in crypto is rising, which is a different story from back in 2021 when a [Civic Science poll revealed](https://civicscience.com/paypal-now-sells-cryptocurrency-but-are-people-buying/) that less than 20% of Americans had a positive opinion of cryptocurrency. Only 5% were currently invested in and enjoying crypto, and only 7% planned to invest in the future. And 66% of Americans did not entertain owning crypto. [In 2016](https://www.statista.com/statistics/1202503/global-cryptocurrency-user-base/), there were five million users of crypto, however, now there are almost 500 million users worldwide. Crypto’s future for next ten years is uncertain but, nonetheless, it is likely to be a much more significant part of the financial world. And in politics, too. In 2023, prior to Nigeria’s [fiasco](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-06-14/nigeria-drops-some-charges-against-binance-s-tigran-gambaryan) with Binance executives before it’s last general election, unbanning cryptocurrency was a campaign talking point, swaying an already apathetic younger population. A Bitcoin.com [survey this year on US voters’ views](https://news.bitcoin.com/survey-77-believe-presidential-candidates-should-be-informed-on-ai-and-crypto/) on cryptocurrency shows their interest in it and their desire for presidential candidates to be informed about it. The survey further finds that many voters prioritise financial stability and believe the current economic system lacks transparency. 74% of voters believe the current economic system lacks transparency and 48% would be more inclined to invest in cryptocurrency if regulations were more transparent. Yet, despite these concerns, trust in traditional financial institutions remains robust at 81%. Furthermore, voters are evenly split on which political party better supports cryptocurrency, with 30% each favouring Democrats and Republicans. Additionally, 77% of likely voters desire presidential candidates to stay informed about innovative technologies such as AI and crypto.

Interestingly, former President Trump now supports cryptocurrencies, shifting from his past critical stance. Historically, he has called them [scams](https://www.bbc.com/news/business-57392734) and a _["disaster waiting to happen"](https://www.benzinga.com/markets/cryptocurrency/21/09/22753280/former-us-president-donald-trump-crypto-is-a-disaster-waiting-to-happen?utm_campaign=partner_feed&utm_source=yahooFinance&utm_medium=partner_feed&utm_content=site)_. Now, Trump pledges to stop a US central bank digital currency (CBDC); he wants Bitcoin mining in the US and has recently [sat down with the crypto community](https://www.msnbc.com/rachel-maddow-show/maddowblog/circumstances-surrounding-trumps-crypto-flip-seem-familiar-rcna158284). Notably, Trump’s campaign accepts crypto donations, framing it as a national security issue to attract young voters. David Bailey of BTC Inc., who organised the meeting, has reported that the [has reported that the mining meeting](https://www.cnbc.com/2024/06/13/donald-trump-says-he-wants-all-future-bitcoin-to-be-mined-in-the-us.html) with Trump is to his 2024 campaign; Trump aims to raise at least $100 million and 5 million votes. Meanwhile, billionaire Mark Cuban had [warned President Biden](https://www.newsweek.com/mark-cuban-crypto-biden-sec-regulation-election-1913224) to choose between SEC Chair, Gensler and crypto voters, suggesting it could affect his re-election chances. Interestingly, Biden's campaign is considering is accepting crypto donations through Coinbase Commerce, whereby following Trump's lead. Crypto donation acceptance clearly aims to attract crypto-focused voters and wealthy pro-crypto donors. Ryan Selkis, a crypto fundraising leader, notes the potential impact of crypto supporters on tight races:[ “….](https://www.coindesk.com/consensus-magazine/2023/12/04/ryan-selkis-messari-crypto-politics/)_[when you think about the folks who really have made crypto either their livelihood or just philosophically feel very strongly[in favour of it"](https://www.coindesk.com/consensus-magazine/2023/12/04/ryan-selkis-messari-crypto-politics/)_ , there are enough to tip the balance in crucial elections. Despite criticism for [opposing](https://zerocap.com/insights/snippets/biden-vetoes-sab-121/) a bipartisan crypto initiative, targeting crypto exchanges and accusing wealthy crypto investors of exploiting tax loopholes, SEC Chair [Gary Gensler, targeting crypto exchanges with regulation-by-enforcement](https://www.nasdaq.com/articles/gary-genslers-approach-to-crypto-regulation-and-its-implications-for-investors), has been facing congressional criticism, and [Senator Elizabeth Warren leads an anti-crypto campaign](https://www.politico.com/news/2023/02/14/elizabeth-warren-anti-crypto-ftx-00082624). All these have been analysed and shown to potentially hurt Biden’s chance at a second term. Now, Biden's campaign seeks to show support for the industry: "_Certain states are only won or lost by tens of thousands of votes_ ," so, “ _you don't need to have 50 million people become single-issue voters. You only need a couple hundred thousand in the right areas_." And, with increasing pressure from his advisors to engage with the crypto community, the campaign's efforts have intensified, though discussions about accepting crypto donations remain exploratory. Pro-crypto donors have also mobilised significant resources, with [crypto-backed super PACs amassing a $100 million war chest](https://www.citizen.org/article/cryptobros-united-fairshake-super-pac-2024-elections/), highlighting the influence of wealthy donors in the election.

The aforementioned Selkis [recently handed](https://www.coindesk.com/consensus-magazine/2023/12/04/ryan-selkis-messari-crypto-politics/) off his work to Fairshake, a political action committee (PAC) with about $15 million in funds. Brian Armstrong, CEO of Coinbase, donated $1 million to Fairshake, aiming for the PAC to raise $50 million or more; Fairshake has already spent $1.2 million on television ads for House candidates. The PAC seeks to elect pro-crypto candidates and re-elect supportive incumbents, a goal Selkis believes justifies his combative public style. Likewise, Lobby3 is a Web3 community founded by Yang and his former campaign manager, Zach Graumann, focusing on using Web3 technology to [empower blockchain communities for better representation in Washington](https://nftnow.com/features/lobby3-andrew-yang-getting-political-web3/). Meanwhile, when Indonesia approached its 2024 elections, young voters were set to be pivotal, with over half of eligible voters under 40 years old and one-third under 30. Candidates, including frontrunner, Prabowo Subianto, and his 36-year-old running mate, Gibran Rakabuming Raka, [vigorously courted this demographic](https://www.nasdaq.com/articles/indonesian-presidential-frontrunners-are-wooing-genz-voters-with-web3). The Prabowo-Gibran campaign, aiming to appeal to younger voters, introduced "Konco Kulo Gibran: THE DUO," a gamified education platform created in collaboration with Saakuru Labs. This platform offers a gas-fee-free blockchain experience where users can play games, earn NFTs and stay connected to the campaign. But, Web3 did not become a US topical/political issue overnight. Despite regulatory hurdles, Web3 innovation in the US has been fuelled by a thriving domestic market and innovations. And Japan is following suit with its government actively supporting Web3 development, embracing Web3 as a critical driver of economic growth. Prime Minister Kishida [sees it as a "new capitalism"](https://jingdailyculture.com/japan-embraces-web3-as-new-form-of-capitalism/) and actively promotes its development and regulation. The government is even leading G7 discussions on Web3 and this embrace could turn it into a political talking point, similar to that of [El Salvador's](https://insights.som.yale.edu/insights/el-salvador-adopted-bitcoin-as-an-official-currency-salvadorans-mostly-shrugged#:~:text=In%20an%20effort%20to%20boost,cryptocurrency%20to%20fall%20off%20quickly.) which made Bitcoin a legal currency for goods and services in the country. After all, if Web3 proves transformative for Japan's economy, its political future will likely be tied to its success - if it survives long enough to cement itself. Currently, [Japan faces political shifts with scandals and upcoming party leadership elections](https://www.coindesk.com/policy/2024/04/28/japans-embrace-of-web3-uncertain-as-ruling-party-under-threat/); the future of Web3 in Japan may see new developments depending on the outcomes.

In essence, Web3 cuts across political lines and, whilst initially embraced by right-wing libertarians for its anti-government aspects, some on the left see it as a weapon against authoritarian control and a path to financial equality. Furthermore, the debate becomes more complex with regulation, with the left favouring consumer protection and environmental concerns, whilst the right leans towards less oversight. Even different cryptocurrencies attract different political views, and the intersection of finance and technology in Web3 affects national power and competitiveness. Digital land ownership and partnerships with Web3 experts certainly shape the US position. Environmentalism and other political issues also influence this evolving landscape, with innovative Web3 solutions offering potential answers. Ultimately, crypto is a tool, akin to a gun, whose impact depends entirely on the user's intent. It can empower individuals, bypass centralised control and be used for illegal activities. And the same can be said for fiat money, referred to as general-purpose technology. But, Web3, like most technologies, is neutral - and its impact depends on the people who wield it….
