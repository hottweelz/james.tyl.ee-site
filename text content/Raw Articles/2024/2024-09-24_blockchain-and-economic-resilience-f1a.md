# Blockchain and economic resilience: enhancing stability in times of crisis (Part 2)

Blockchain technology can enhance the ability of governments, organisations and communities to respond to and recover from crises. By providing a secure and transparent platform for coordination and information sharing, blockchains can improve the efficiency and effectiveness of crisis response efforts. The technology can be used to improve the distribution of disaster relief and [humanitarian aid](https://humanitarianadvisorygroup.org/blockchain-for-social-impact-in-aid-and-development/#:~:text=Blockchain%20in%20humanitarian%20response,programming%20and%20boosting%20humanitarian%20financing.) by ensuring that funds and resources are allocated transparently and efficiently. For example, blockchain can be used to create digital vouchers or tokens that can be distributed to affected individuals and redeemed for essential goods and services; this ensures that aid reaches those who need it most and reduces the risk of fraud and mismanagement. Notably, the [United Nations World Food Programme (WFP)](https://www.weforum.org/organizations/united-nations-world-food-programme-wfp-1a3b3b9f-8724-4464-9cc7-a970a6bc7d37/) has been using blockchain technology to improve the efficiency and transparency of humanitarian aid distribution, with its “Building Blocks” project leveraging blockchain to distribute food assistance to refugees in Jordan. In times of crisis, effective coordination and collaboration among various stakeholders are crucial for a successful response and blockchain can facilitate this by providing a decentralised platform for information sharing and decision-making. For example, blockchain can be used to track and coordinate the [distribution of medical supplies during a pandemic](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8817762/#:~:text=In%20fact%2C%20Khurshid%20\(2020\),the%20medical%20supply%20chain%20shortages.), ensuring that resources are allocated where they are needed most. Indeed, blockchains can be used to create a resilient infrastructure that can withstand and recover from shocks and that can help communities and economies recover more quickly from crises - i.e. blockchain can be used to create [decentralised energy grids](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/378884796_Decentralized_Energy_Trading_for_Grids_Using_Blockchain_for_Sustainable_Smart_Cities#:~:text=Blockchain%20technology%20possesses%20the%20potential,rely%20on%20renewable%20energy%20sources.) that are less vulnerable to disruptions or to manage and protect critical data in the event of a cyber-attack.

Meanwhile, China has been actively exploring the use of blockchain technology to enhance its financial infrastructure. The People's Bank of China (PBOC) has launched several blockchain initiatives, including the development of a [Digital Currency Electronic Payment (DCEP) system](https://www.institutmontaigne.org/en/expressions/chinas-digital-currency-i-sailing-ship), which leverages blockchain technology to create a digital version of the Chinese yuan. In addition to the DCEP system, China has also been using blockchain to streamline cross-border trade and finance. For example, the [Blockchain Service Network (BSN)](https://www.cnbc.com/2022/05/16/china-blockchain-explainer-what-is-bsn-.html) is a government-backed initiative that provides a standardised platform for the development and deployment of blockchain applications, including those related to trade and finance. And by promoting the adoption of blockchain technology, China aims to enhance the resilience of its financial system and strengthen its position in the global economy. However, whilst blockchain technology offers significant potential for enhancing economic resilience, it also presents several challenges and risks that must be addressed:

> · **scalability** \- one of the primary challenges facing blockchain technology is scalability. Many blockchain networks, particularly those using proof of work (PoW) consensus mechanisms, face limitations in terms of transaction processing speed and capacity. In times of crisis, when rapid response and high transaction volumes are critical, these scalability issues could hinder the effectiveness of blockchain-based solutions.
> 
> · **regulatory uncertainty** \- the regulatory environment for blockchain technology remains uncertain in many jurisdictions. This uncertainty can create challenges for the adoption and implementation of blockchain-based solutions, particularly in highly regulated sectors such as finance and healthcare. Clear and consistent regulatory frameworks are needed to provide certainty and foster innovation while ensuring that blockchain applications are secure and compliant.
> 
> · **interoperability** \- the lack of interoperability between different blockchain networks and traditional systems can create barriers to the widespread adoption of blockchain technology. For blockchain to enhance economic resilience effectively, it must be able to integrate seamlessly with existing infrastructure and systems. Developing standards and protocols for interoperability is crucial to overcoming this challenge.
> 
> · **security risks** \- whilst blockchain is generally considered secure, it is not immune to security risks. For example, blockchain networks can be vulnerable to attacks such as ‘51% attacks’, where a single entity gains control of the majority of the network's computing power. Additionally, the use of smart contracts introduces the potential for coding errors or vulnerabilities that could be exploited. Ensuring the security and robustness of blockchain applications is essential to their effectiveness in enhancing economic resilience.
> 
> · **privacy concerns** \- the transparency of blockchain, while beneficial for trust and accountability, can also raise privacy concerns. In some cases, the information recorded on a blockchain may be sensitive or confidential, and the inability to alter or delete data can create challenges for protecting privacy. Developing privacy-enhancing technologies and frameworks for blockchain is necessary to address these concerns.

Blockchain technology stands at the precipice of revolutionising our approach to economic resilience, particularly in the face of crises that threaten the stability of global systems. Yet, the true challenge lies not merely in adoption but in navigating the intricate landscape of scalability, regulatory frameworks and ethical considerations. How do we balance the unprecedented power of decentralisation with the need for oversight and security? The early successes of blockchain applications in places such as Estonia and within initiatives such as the UN World Food Programme suggest that we are on the cusp of something transformative. But as we move forward, the question remains: can we harness this technology in a way that not only withstands future crises but also creates a more just and resilient global economy? The answer to this question will determine whether blockchain becomes the bedrock of a new economic order or simply another tool in the arsenal of those already in power.
