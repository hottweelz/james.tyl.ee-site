# Blockchain's role in global agriculture

According to [Worldometer](https://www.worldometers.info/world-population/), the world population is estimated to be 8.2 billion. This ever-increasing figure ([predicted](https://appinventiv.com/blog/blockchain-in-agriculture-and-food-sector/) to reach 11.2 billion by 2100) exerts tremendous pressure on agriculture, with a need for [98% rise in food production](https://agricultureguruji.com/blockchain-in-agriculture/#Use_of_Blockchain_In_Agriculture). However, the industry is currently faced with challenges that hinder its efficiency - lack of transparency in the supply chain has made room for food fraud, safety issues and a disconnect between producers and consumers. Approximately 33% of global food production goes to waste as a result of inadequate transportation, inefficient storage and handling practices therefore trust, quality, safety and transparency are essential in the world's influential sector, the backbone of the global economy - agriculture. As a transformative force with potential applications, blockchain technology has [emerged a game-changer,](https://www.agdaily.com/smartnews/blockchain-technology-transforming-agricultural-industry/) addressing the challenges facing agriculture and unlocking new opportunities for all stakeholders in the agricultural ecosystem. Offering a decentralised and immutable transaction record, the technology can improve traceability and transparency in agricultural supply chains whilst streamlining data management, ensuring secure data sharing among stakeholders and enhancing efficient, transparent transactions. In its study, [MarketDigits](https://www.globenewswire.com/news-release/2024/01/15/2809288/0/en/Blockchain-in-Agriculture-and-Food-Supply-Chain-Market-projected-to-reach-USD-5996-5-Million-by-2030-growing-at-a-CAGR-of-52-3-during-the-forecast-period-of-2023-2030-says-MarketDi.html) predicted the market size of blockchain in agriculture and food supply chain to be at $315.5 million in 2023 but estimated to reach about $6 billion by 2030 - observing a CAGR of 52.3%. Also, a research report by [Spherical Insights & Consulting](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/global-blockchain-agriculture-market-size-180000834.html) projected the global blockchain in agriculture market size to grow from $500 million in 2023 to $21.46 billion by 2033. Many other studies have also predicted the expected boom with convincing evidence.

Meanwhile, blockchain technology [facilitates transparent peer-to-peer (P2P) transactions](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/378402728_Sustainable_Agriculture_The_Role_of_Blockchain_Technology_in_Ensuring_Food_Security), eliminating the need for a many of the current intermediaries in the agricultural sector. The technology's decentralised nature establishes trust and confidence between producers and consumers, abolishing the need for a central authority, thus reducing transaction costs within the agricultural industry. Blockchain technology provides a reliable mechanism for tracking transactions conducted among unidentified participants, thereby achieving prompt detection of fraud and flaws. To strengthen transparency, trust and accountability, blockchain appears as an integral element of modern-day agriculture, increasing the volume of reliable information available concerning inventories, contracts in agriculture and the general state of the farms. For example, [Eden Green](https://www.edengreen.com/blog-collection/blockchain-technology-in-agriculture#:~:text=When%20they%20employ%20blockchain%20in,a%20complete%20picture%20over%20time.) offers farmers sensors to measure temperature, humidity and soil health, so monitoring conditions for growing crops; all this data is recorded on a blockchain. The blockchain technology offers a new tool to face the challenges of food quality and safety whilst enabling the capture of data that is verifiable. And, as a reliable method of storing data, blockchains can document and store information related to all stages of a product's value chain, from field to fork. Here are a selection of ways blockchains are impacting the agriculture sector:

> · **supply chain transparency**

To satisfy the growing consumer demand for food safety and quality products, there is a need to reduce information asymmetry among stakeholders and strengthen a relationship of trust between the producers and consumers. In today's modern agricultural supply chain, transparency is imperative. Blockchains provide end-to-end visibility in the supply chain, helping track food from farm to table. Real-time tracking reduces fraud, improves traceability and enhances food safety by enabling instant access to product history, identification and resolution of quality issues. [Walmart](https://www.walmart.com/), in a bid to apply blockchain technology across the food supply chain, partnered with IBM in 2016, [using the latter's Food Trust Network to trace produce like leafy greens](https://corporate.walmart.com/content/dam/corporate/documents/press-center/in-wake-of-romaine-e-coli-scare-walmart-deploys-blockchain-to-track-leafy-greens/blockchain-supplier-letter-sept-2018.pdf), thus reducing the time needed to track food origins from 7 days to 2.2 seconds. This swift traceability prevents infected food from reaching consumers.

> · **smart contracts for agricultural transactions**

Blockchain's smart contracts offer automation of agreements between stakeholders, ensuring transactions are executed once predefined conditions are met; the elimination of middlemen/intermediaries is time-saving and cost-effective. [AgriDigital](https://www.agridigital.io/), an Australian start-up founded in 2015, uses blockchain smart contracts to [automate grain transactions and payments](https://www.forbes.com.au/news/innovation/grain-brain/). The company which conducted the world’s first live settlement of a physical commodity through blockchain in 2016, transacted about 21 million tonnes of grain at a value of over $6 billion in 2021.

> · **food security and sustainability**

[IBM's Food Trust Blockchain](https://www.ibm.com/products/supply-chain-intelligence-suite/food-trust#:~:text=Built%20on%20IBM%20Blockchain%C2%AE,provenance%2C%20transactions%20and%20processing%20details.) achieves this by tracking food origins, ensuring sustainably sourced products and better demand forecasting.

> · **financial inclusion for farmers**

Blockchain empowers smallholder farmers and facilitates their inclusion in the global agricultural supply chain by providing transparent record of transactions and eliminating intermediaries. The technology offers decentralised financial services, giving farmers access to credit, crop insurance and fair pricing mechanisms through tokenisation and decentralised finance. [Twiga Foods](https://kenyanwallstreet.com/twiga-foods-to-offer-blockchain-based-microloans-to-food-kiosk-owners-in-kenya/), a Kenya-based B2B logistics platform, has partnered with IBM to help small-scale farmers and food vendors whereby offering them credits using blockchain.

> · **crop traceability and certification**

Both the consumers and the manufacturers in modern agriculture wish for the utmost quality in their products. The ability to track every aspect of every stage of a product's development enables the consumers to know the origin of a product, the sustainability of the production method and the fair share to the producers. Blockchain permits certification of crops by recording every stage of the growing process on a public ledger, thereby increasing trust among consumers. [Rainforest Alliance](https://www.rainforest-alliance.org/insights/rainforest-alliance-certified-coffee/), an international agricultural certification organisation, uses blockchain to verify coffee production standards so ensuring ethical sourcing.

> · **tokenisation of agricultural assets**

Tokenising agricultural assets such as land, crops or livestock improves consumer relationships and financial results of agribusiness firms, providing an innovative way to raise capital or crowdfund for agricultural projects. [TE-FOOD](https://te-food.com/solution/blockchain/), a public-permissioned blockchain, tokenises agricultural assets to promote investment in farming ventures.

> · **data sharing and intellectual property protection**

Using blockchain technology and internet of things (IoT) in the agricultural ecosystem results in secure data collection, storage and sharing. Whilst IoT devices record data related to crucial factors such as weather, soil quality, irrigation or crop yield, blockchain stores them. This approach ensures data integrity and secure data sharing among stakeholders whilst protecting intellectual property rights. [AgriLedger](https://agriledger.com/#:~:text=Our%20SmartChain%20Solutions%20harness%20blockchain%20technology%20to%20ensure,journey%20of%20produce%20across%20the%20global%20food%20system.), a fintech company founded by Genevieve Leveille in 2016, leverages blockchain technology and data analytics to protect farmers' intellectual property and manage data sharing for better decision-making.

However, although blockchain has great potential in agriculture, there are still [challenges](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/348812149_Applying_Blockchain_in_Agriculture_A_Study_on_Blockchain_Technology_Benefits_and_Challenges) that must be addressed. The lack of international regulations and standards for blockchain adoption in agriculture hinders extensive implementation and interoperability among different blockchain solutions. Moreover, capturing the rural farming communities where connectivity and digital literacy might be deficient is challenging, as partaking in blockchain networks requires some level of digital awareness and reliable internet access. Blockchain feeds on data, so its effectiveness and accuracy depend on the legitimacy and authenticity of the information fed to it (which also depend on the participants) as the knowledge produced and used in decentralised agricultural process is controlled and distributed by individual farmers. Lastly, these innovations must be deployed [equitably](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/378402728_Sustainable_Agriculture_The_Role_of_Blockchain_Technology_in_Ensuring_Food_Security), capturing the smallholders and the large farms whereby ensuring that all stakeholders benefit from their value. Blockchain is poised to revolutionise global agriculture, promising unprecedented transparency, efficiency and trust in an industry struggling with waste, fraud and inequality. From Walmart’s blockchain-powered food traceability cutting tracking time from seven days to seconds, to AgriDigital’s automation of grain trade through smart contracts, the potential is enormous. Yet, critical questions remain: can rural farmers with limited digital access fully benefit? Will the lack of global standards hinder its reach? And, as blockchain depends on the integrity of input data, how do we ensure trust in the very system meant to secure it? Hence, for agriculture to truly reap blockchain’s rewards, it must bridge the digital divide and create systems that include the smallest players, not simply the largest. Ultimately, will this technology be the great equaliser - or another tool of exclusion?
