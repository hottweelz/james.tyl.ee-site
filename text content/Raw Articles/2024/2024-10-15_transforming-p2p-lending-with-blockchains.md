# Transforming P2P lending with blockchains 

Peer 2Peer (P2P) lending in 2024 is expected to be worth over [$510billion](https://www.futuremarketinsights.com/reports/peer-to-peer-lending-market) and estimated to grow to be worth over $1.7trillion by 2034. [P2P lending platforms](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/p/peer-to-peer-lending.asp) serve as online marketplaces where individuals or businesses can obtain loans from other individuals without going through a financial institution. These platforms typically allow lenders to earn interest on their funds whilst borrowers benefit from lower interest rates than those offered by traditional banks. P2P lending also provides access to credit for those underserved populations who may not qualify for traditional loans. By eliminating intermediaries, P2P platforms typically can offer lower fees and better interest rates for both borrowers and lenders. [According to iwoca:](https://www.iwoca.co.uk/finance-explained/peer-to-peer-lending) “P _eer-to-peer loan sizes tend to be the same as small business loan sizes - between £5,000 and £500,000_ ”, whereby demonstrating that lenders have the ability to diversify their portfolios by funding multiple small loans instead of one large loan.

However, despite its advantages, P2P lending faces several challenges. Borrowers and lenders often need more information about each other (leading to concerns over creditworthiness and potential defaults) because they are not able to rely on a trusted third party who has carried out due diligence. Indeed, the regulatory landscape for P2P lending is still evolving thus creating uncertainty for platforms and users, and, unfortunately, the digital nature of P2P lending does make it susceptible to fraud and identity theft. Hence, the role blockchain technology affords is to improve transparency so allowing all participants to verify transactions and borrower histories, in turn increasing trust and reducing the risk of fraud. Furthermore, the deployment of smart contracts with the terms directly written into code can automate the loan agreement process, enforcing conditions and executing transactions without the need for intermediaries. For example, a smart contract could automatically disburse funds to a borrower once specific conditions are met. Thus, by removing intermediaries, blockchain technology can lower costs and make the lending process more efficient. This decentralisation also empowers users, giving them more control over their financial transactions. The cryptographic nature of blockchains enhances security whereby reducing the risk of data breaches and fraud - each transaction is recorded and verified by multiple participants in the network, making unauthorised alterations virtually impossible. Blockchain technology also allows for the tokenisation and thus fractionalisation of real-world assets, enabling borrowers to leverage their assets as collateral in a more accessible manner and this can broaden the range of collateralised loans available on P2P platforms.

**How digital assets can transform P2P lending**

> · **cryptocurrency as collateral**

Borrowers can use cryptocurrencies as collateral for loans, providing a way to access liquidity without selling their assets. This can be particularly appealing in volatile markets where asset values fluctuate rapidly.

> · **lower transaction costs**

Using digital assets for transactions can significantly reduce fees associated with currency conversion and payment processing, making P2P lending more cost-effective.

> · **global reach**

Digital assets operate on a global scale, allowing P2P lending platforms to connect borrowers and lenders across borders. This can expand the market and create new funding opportunities.

> · **programmable money**

Digital assets can be programmed to execute transactions automatically based on predefined criteria, enhancing the efficiency of loan disbursements and repayments.

> · **reduced default risk**

The use of digital assets can mitigate default risk. For instance, if a borrower fails to repay a loan, the platform can automatically seize the digital assets used as collateral.

**Case studies: blockchain and digital assets in P2P lending**

> · [Aave](https://aave.com/) is a decentralised finance (DeFi) platform that allows users to lend and borrow cryptocurrencies. By utilising blockchain technology, Aave provides a transparent and secure environment for P2P lending. Borrowers can choose from various cryptocurrencies to collateralise their loans, whilst lenders can earn interest on their digital assets. Aave's use of smart contracts automates the lending process, reducing the need for intermediaries.
> 
> · [SALT Lending](https://saltlending.com/) allows users to secure loans using their cryptocurrency holdings as collateral. By leveraging blockchain technology, SALT creates a streamlined borrowing experience whilst minimising risks for lenders. The platform uses smart contracts to execute loans automatically, ensuring transparency and efficiency.
> 
> · [ETHLend,](https://defiprime.com/ethlend) now part of Aave, was one of the first P2P lending platforms built on Ethereum. It allowed users to lend and borrow Ethereum and other [ERC-20 tokens](https://www.investopedia.com/news/what-erc20-and-what-does-it-mean-ethereum/). The platform’s use of smart contracts facilitated the lending process while ensuring that loans were secured with collateral.

Nonetheless, there is the need to be mindful of various regulatory challenges when integrating blockchain technology and digital assets into P2P lending; many jurisdictions have yet to establish clear guidelines for cryptocurrencies and blockchain-based lending platforms. As the adoption of blockchain technology and digital assets grows, regulators are likely to focus on a variety of topics such as consumer protection through to ensuring that borrowers and lenders have adequate protection against fraud and defaults. Anti-money laundering (AML) and know your customer (KYC) regulations ensure P2P platforms implement strict verification processes to prevent illicit activities, however, the need to create clear guidelines on how digital assets and cryptocurrency transactions will be taxed remains an important issue to be considered (although many jurisdictions have already addressed the way digital assets transactions are taxed). Significantly, the value of digital assets can fluctuate, posing risks for both borrowers and lenders which can deter investors using digital assets - essentially, if a borrower uses volatile assets as collateral, the lender may face an increased risk of default if the asset's value drops. Blockchain technology itself is still evolving, and so platforms must address potential vulnerabilities and security risks - the lack of regulation can also create uncertainty for users. Hence, for blockchain and digital assets to transform P2P lending, widespread adoption is necessary and educating users about the benefits and risks of these technologies will be crucial. However, by leveraging smart contracts and digital assets, the P2P lending space is certainly moving towards a more decentralised, accessible model that benefits borrowers and lenders alike.

So, how about imagining a world where loans are executed without intermediaries, where transaction costs are minimised and where the risks of fraud are drastically reduced through cryptographic security? Yet, as we witness the growth of blockchain-driven P2P lending, several questions arise. How will regulatory bodies respond to this decentralised financial revolution? Can digital assets truly mitigate default risk, or will their volatility introduce new challenges? And, as borrowers and lenders span international borders, how do we ensure financial inclusivity without compromising on security and regulation? These questions prompt us to consider the role of traditional financial systems in a rapidly digitising world. Essentially, are we prepared to embrace a decentralised approach to credit, and what does that mean for the future of financial institutions as intermediaries? The potential is vast, but navigating the balance between innovation, security and regulation will certainly be critical in shaping the future of P2P lending.
