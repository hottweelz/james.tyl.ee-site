# Visa tokenised asset platform: disrupting stablecoins and cross-border payments?

Visa's tokenised asset platform (VTAP) was launched in [October 2024 and is Visa’s](https://usa.visa.com/about-visa/newsroom/press-releases.releaseId.20881.html) effort to streamline the issuance and management of tokenised assets, encompassing fiat-backed tokens, stablecoins and potentially central bank digital currencies (CBDCs). VTAP significantly advances digital payments and potentially the use of blockchain technology, in time, on a global level. This move is certainly not unexpected, as 2024 is shaping to be the “projected” year of Web3 and tokenisation, a [trend](https://coinspaidmedia.com/forecasts/top-2024-trends-web3/) many have agreed will dominate Web3. And so far, we have seen many different types of assets being tokenised. Furthermore, the firms that are actually tokenising assets include some of the biggest asset managers and banks globally. Indeed, the Boston Consulting Group (BCG) [estimates](https://cointelegraph.com/news/tokenization-of-illiquid-assets-to-reach-16t-by-2030-report) that the tokenisation of illiquid assets will reach $16trillion by 2030. With such a massive market potential, it is of no surprise that Visa wanted to carve out a significant share through the VTAP. However, given the intense competition from other organisations and bodies vying for the same prize, the questions remain: is there enough room for everyone and, furthermore, what already established Web3 provision could be eroded in their bid for disruption and innovations? Meanwhile, [Marketsable](https://marketsable.com/real-world-asset-tokenization-report-q3-2024/) has issued a report on real-world asset (RWA) tokenisation and reveals how institutions such as Goldman Sachs and BlackRock are driving blockchain adoption for assets such as, cash (stablecoins/tokenised deposits), commercial debt instruments, funds, real estate and government securities.

And, not to be left behind, are native Web3 organisations such as Ripple, Tether, Circle, Archax, Tokeny, Swarm and MANTRA in driving adoption of tokenisation. One firm certainly active in this space is Ripple, having released plans for a RLUSD stablecoin together with [plans to bring RWAs](https://archax.com/insights/ripple-and-archax-expand-xrp-ledger-partnership) such as stocks onto the XRP Ledger. Similarly, Tether's $100 million [investment](https://www.coindesk.com/business/2024/09/09/tethers-100m-investment-in-latam-agriculture-firm-may-be-a-tokenization-play/) in Adecoagro aims to diversify its holdings through agricultural asset tokenisation. Through Agrotoken, [Adecoagro](https://www.adecoagro.com/en) tokenises commodities such as grains, which aligns with its broader strategy beyond stablecoins - having historically invested in Bitcoin, gold and commercial loans. Various other firms have eyed [Tethers $5.2billion profit](https://blockworks.co/news/tether-q2-2024-earnings-report) in the first half of 2024 and are trying to compete for Tether's dominance in the stablecoin market. Many have ventured into the bigger tokenisation sub-industry, aiming to capitalise on the growing demand for tokenised assets. However, the question remains: could outsiders actually be outmanoeuvring these established native players? Whilst stablecoins can be considered early versions of RWAs, they represent liquid assets - and RWA tokenisation primarily focuses on illiquid assets. This distinction highlights the unique opportunities and challenges associated with the tokenisation market. Moreover, VTAP aims to capitalise on this momentum, potentially disrupting established players such as Tether, Ripple and Circle. Other TradFi bodies currently exploring RWA tokenisation and a variety of other examples can be found[ here](https://fortunafi.beehiiv.com/p/2024-10-predictions-for-rwa-stablecoins), with other notably examples including:

> · **JPMorgan Chase** \- JPMorgan has been at the forefront through its [Onyx platform](https://www.jpmorgan.com/onyx/index) which focuses on tokenising US Treasury bonds and offers new liquidity opportunities.
> 
> · **Goldman Sachs** \- Goldman Sachs is exploring tokenised real estate and private equity. Its digital asset platform has conducted pilot projects, including tokenised bonds.
> 
> · **HSBC** \- [HSBC’s digital vault initiativ](https://r3.com/usecases/hsbc-digital-vault-and-tokenized-gold-2/)e aims to tokenise bonds and private securities, streamlining asset management.
> 
> · **Société Générale** \- the French bank has issued bonds using blockchain, further integrating tokenised assets into European markets.
> 
> · **Swiss banks (SIX Digital Exchange) -** Swiss banks are enabling secure trading of tokenised equities and bonds through the [SIX Digital Exchange.](https://ch.linkedin.com/company/six-digital-exchange)
> 
> · **[State Street](https://www.reuters.com/business/finance/custody-giant-state-street-expands-crypto-services-new-partnership-2024-08-20/) **– now going to offer custody for digital assets and tokenising some of the mutual funds they manage.

These traditional finance companies have been innovating in RWA tokenisation and, comparing their recent offerings, have been highlighting the competitive landscape and global adoption in a multitude of jurisdictions. VTAP’s [aim to help financial institutions](https://developer.visa.com/capabilities/visa-tokenized-asset-platform/) tokenise RWA, help fiat currencies to handle central bank digital currencies, and might directly compete with the stablecoins and remittance services. Particularly if Visa's platform gains broad adoption through its 130 million merchants in over 200 countries. Furthermore, by leveraging its global payment infrastructure, trusted brand and regulatory compliance, Visa’s established network and institutional partnerships would enable faster, cheaper and more reliable cross-border payments, making it attractive to mainstream users and businesses. VTAP could offer seamless integration with merchants and financial institutions, providing lower entry barriers than decentralised platforms, which could require technical knowledge. Additionally, Visa’s platform would likely prioritise regulatory adherence, using KYC and AML measures, which could appeal to users and businesses seeking legal certainty. And, with features such as enhanced consumer protection, greater liquidity and multi-asset support, VTAP could attract a broad range of users whilst undercutting Web3 platforms whereby emphasising decentralisation and privacy. Visa’s scale and experience in processing high transaction volumes ([3.94 trillion transaction in Q3 2024)](https://ycharts.com/indicators/visa_inc_v_total_transaction_volume_quarterly) and offering fraud prevention could establish VTAP as a competitive force, so drawing institutional investors and mainstream users away from decentralised alternatives - BBVA is already set to [pioneer](https://www.bbva.com/en/innovation/bbva-to-explore-the-potential-of-visas-new-tokenized-asset-platform/) VTAP’s platform.

However, whilst VTAP benefits stablecoins, it introduces significant downsides, particularly centralisation. Essentially, Visa’s control over the platform could undermine the decentralised nature of stablecoins, making them reliant on traditional financial infrastructure. And this reliance could reduce their appeal for low-cost remittances and microtransactions. Additionally, Visa’s platform could actually compete directly with existing stablecoins and remittance services, potentially creating regulatory challenges and centralising space control. These trade-offs could stifle innovation and privacy whilst introducing greater dependence on a centralised entity, exposing them to system failures, cyberattacks, operational disruptions, etc. Yet, if there is any hope that both worlds will grow together, perhaps we could learn from Visa’s fellow Fintech company, Paypal which is partnering with Paxos, an Ethereum-based regulated blockchain infrastructure; Paxos has also [collaborated with the payments platform, Stripe](https://www.ledgerinsights.com/stripe-accepts-stablecoin-payments-using-paxos-infrastructure/). Earlier in the year, Paxos [received approval to launch](https://paxos.com/blog/paxos-secures-full-approval-to-offer-digital-payment-token-services-from-monetary-authority-of-singapore-dbs-to-be-primary-banking-partner/) its USDP stablecoin on the Solana blockchain starting January 2024. This transition from Ethereum to Solana highlights the latter's advantages, such as low transaction costs and high performance. Paxos' regulatory oversight remains critical in delivering secure blockchain solutions across different platforms. _“Regulatory oversight ensures that Paxos only builds solutions on blockchain protocols with the greatest security and integrity”,_ Paxos [reported in a statement.](https://paxos.com/2023/12/22/paxos-expands-to-the-solana-blockchain/#:~:text=Regulatory%20oversight%20ensures%20that%20Paxos,to%20improve%20the%20financial%20system.) This collaboration helps to demonstrate how traditional financial institutions can integrate with decentralised technologies, paving the way for a more inclusive and innovative global economy. However, maybe Visa has launched its tokenised platform in response to [Mastercard’s multi token network](https://www.ledgerinsights.com/mastercard-executes-first-tokenized-deposit-transactions-with-stanchart-subsidiaries/) (launched in 2023) and which, in June 2024, executed its first tokenised deposit?

Nonetheless, the onus is on Web3 players to collaborate so as to find ways to make their infrastructures work together without Web3 losing its soul. And, as tokenisation continues to gain momentum, cross-chain interoperability will be essential for widespread adoption. VTAP's commitment to seamless interoperability between banks should catalyse the entire Web3 ecosystem. Furthermore, it is a clear call to action for native Web3 players to prioritise interoperability solutions and foster a more connected and interconnected digital landscape. As highlighted on a [recent LinkedIn post:](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/visa-outperforming-sp-500-2029-bohdan-vorona-bts0e/) “ _Visa has a dominant position in the global payments ecosystem. The strength of Visa's economic moat is reflected in its impressive financial metrics, with operating margins (using net revenue) reaching 67% in fiscal 2023. Returns on invested capital are quite healthy, averaging 34% over the past five years and 58% if goodwill is excluded. It's a clear indication of the company's competitive advantages and its ability to generate substantial value from its assets and investments_.” Presumably, therefore, VTAP is a pre-emptive move because, surely, as interest and use of tokenisation expands, then Visa (and the same is true for Mastercard) will not wish to miss out on these new revenue streams?
