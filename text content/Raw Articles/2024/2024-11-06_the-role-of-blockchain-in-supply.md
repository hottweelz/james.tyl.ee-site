# The role of blockchain in supply chain transparency: going beyond finance

Initially recognised for revolutionising the financial sector, blockchain technology is now reshaping industries far beyond finance. One area where blockchain is profoundly impacting is supply chain management, where transparency and trust are critical. With the increasing complexity of global supply chains, issues such as fraud, counterfeiting and unethical sourcing have become major concerns. Blockchain offers a solution by providing an immutable, decentralised ledger that enhances transparency, accountability and efficiency. 

**Challenges in traditional supply chains**

Traditional supply chains involve numerous stakeholders, including manufacturers, suppliers, logistics providers and retailers - all operating in siloed systems. This lack of interconnectedness often leads to inefficiencies and reduced visibility, making it difficult to track a product’s journey from raw material to finished goods. Some of the key challenges include:

> · **fraud and counterfeiting** -**** in industries such as pharmaceuticals, counterfeit products are a serious problem, posing risks to consumer safety and company reputation. It is estimated up to [10% of medicines in developed countries and 30% in developing jurisdictions](https://www.openpr.com/news/3708272/anti-counterfeit-pharmaceutical-packaging-market-size) are counterfeit.
> 
> · **unethical sourcing** \- in sectors such as fashion and food, ensuring ethical sourcing and labour practices is increasingly important, yet difficult to verify. According to [PwC](https://www.pwc.com/gx/en/news-room/press-releases/2024/pwc-2024-voice-of-consumer-survey.html), consumers are willing to pay “ _9.7% more, on average, for sustainably produced or sourced goods_.”
> 
> · **inefficiencies** \- paper-based records and manual processes slow down supply chains and increase the likelihood of human error.
> 
> · **consumer trust** \- today’s consumers demand transparency and accountability. Indeed, [83% of consumers globally](https://www.sk.agency/transparency-in-marketing-building-trust-with-data-driven-insights/#:~:text=Transparency%2C%20defined%20as%20openness%2C%20honesty,deciding%20which%20brands%20to%20support.) believe transparency is crucial when deciding which brands to support. They want to know where products come from, how they were made and whether they align with ethical and environmental standards.

Blockchain technology has the potential to address all of these challenges by creating a single, secure source of truth that can be accessed and verified by all parties involved in the supply chain. Using blockchain-powered platforms, every participant in the supply chain can access the same, immutable record of transactions. This decentralised approach eliminates the need for intermediaries and ensures that no single party can alter the data. Blockchains allows products to be tracked in real time from their point of origin to their final destination - as [Oracle highlighted](https://www.oracle.com/uk/blockchain/what-is-blockchain/blockchain-for-supply-chain/) in a recent article: “ _AI, IoT, and other technologies offer capabilities that complement blockchain. By combining them, supply chains will benefit from better and more automated workflow documentation._ ” Every step of a product’s journey is recorded on the blockchain, providing a clear audit trail that can be accessed instantly by stakeholders. Certainly, blockchain's potential in supply chain management is already being realised across several industries. For example, [IBM’s Food Trust blockchain platform](https://www.ibm.com/products/supply-chain-intelligence-suite/food-trust) is a leading example of blockchain being used to ensure food safety and traceability. Major retailers, including [Walmart,](https://www.lfdecentralizedtrust.org/case-studies/walmart-case-study#:~:text=Walmart%20thought%20that%20blockchain%20technology,projects%20to%20test%20the%20system.) have adopted the platform to track the journey of fresh produce from farm to store; this enables faster identification of contaminated products during food recalls and provides consumers with confidence in the safety and origin of their food. The fashion industry is using blockchain, as can be seen by [H&M](https://www.iotworldtoday.com/iiot/h-amp-m-launches-blockchain-based-clothes-rental-service) with its recently launched clothes rental service with scannable IoT labels on the clothing. Meanwhile, luxury brands such as [Gucci, Louis Vuitton, Givenchy and Prada](file:///Users/susanneharris/Library/Containers/net.whatsapp.WhatsApp/Data/tmp/documents/7A3919BC-0EE8-40A2-B394-73EB28464441/xpedia.co.uk/%3flocale=en_GB&siteid=3&semcid=UK.B.GOOGLE.BT-c-EN.GT&semdtl=a118255096848.b1145235712401.g1kwd-29861120.e1c.m1CjwKCAjw5_GmBhBIEiwA5QSMxPNQtcvvZH3OA1kWF0z54XIBos74QgsjG2HS127fHjtMT-s_CZ4oixoCN84QAvD_BwE.r116293446496fe3a2a2215116bd40a5c2b54333bf4cc4fe8e588af27164b29ab0.c1zY8Hv5DYiHWCDqwz6wqZNA.j11006801.k1.d1624813133122.h1e.i1.l1.n1.o1.p1.q1.s1.t1.x1.f1.u1.v1.w1&gclid=CjwKCAjw5_GmBhBIEiwA5QSMxPNQtcvvZH3OA1kWF0z54XIBos74QgsjG2HS127fHjtMT-s_CZ4oixoCN84QAvD_BwE) are employing blockchain to ensure that their supply chains adhere to fair labour practices and that raw materials are sourced sustainably; consumers can scan QR codes on products to learn about the garment’s journey from raw material to final product. The technology is also being deployed to help tackle [$431 billion global fraud that the World Health Organisation,](https://www.cnbc.com/2023/12/11/fraud-in-a-bottle-big-pharma-takes-on-counterfeit-drugs.html) estimates for pharmaceutical drugs. By tracking the origin and handling of pharmaceutical products, blockchain can ensure that only legitimate drugs reach consumers, enhancing safety and trust. Furthermore,**** blockchain offers numerous benefits for supply chain management, including:

> · **ncreased transparency** , blockchains ensures that every transaction and movement of goods within the supply chain is recorded and available for verification. This level of transparency is crucial in industries where ethical sourcing, authenticity, and safety are paramount.
> 
> · **enhanced efficiency** , with blockchain, the need for intermediaries is reduced, and many manual processes are automated. Smart contracts (self-executing agreements coded into the blockchain) can automatically trigger actions, such as payments or shipments, when certain conditions are met.
> 
> · **accountability** , blockchain technology holds all parties accountable by ensuring that data cannot be altered or tampered with. Any irregularities in the supply chain, such as delays, fraud, or non-compliance with regulations, can be quickly identified and addressed.
> 
> · **consumer trust** , with greater transparency, companies can build trust with consumers by providing verifiable information about the origin, quality, and ethical standards of their products. Consumers can make more informed choices, and companies that prioritize transparency are likely to gain a competitive edge.

Undoubtedly, as more industries recognise the benefits of blockchain, adoption is expected to grow, further transforming supply chain management. In addition to improving transparency and efficiency, blockchain is likely to play a key role in promoting sustainability. By providing a clear record of a product’s environmental footprint, companies can ensure that their supply chains align with sustainability goals. Blockchain technology is also being used and integrated with other emerging technologies such as the internet of things (IoT) and artificial intelligence (AI). For instance, IoT sensors embedded in products could automatically feed data into the blockchain, providing real-time updates on the condition and location of goods; AI could be used to analyse this data to predict supply chain disruptions and optimise logistics in ways that were previously impossible. Yet, despite its potential, blockchain faces several challenges in widespread adoption since implementing blockchain technology often requires a significant initial investment in infrastructure and technology. Many companies, especially smaller businesses, could find it difficult to justify these costs without a clear return on investment. The technology ideally requires cross-border cooperation, but the regulatory landscape surrounding blockchain remains uncertain. Differences in national regulations and the lack of standardised blockchain protocols pose challenges for global supply chain integration. Therefore, for blockchain technology to deliver its full potential in supply chains, widespread collaboration among stakeholders (including manufacturers, logistics providers, retailers and governments) is necessary, and this level of cooperation can be difficult to achieve.

However, whilst challenges remain, the future of blockchain in supply chains looks promising with the potential to reshape industries, promote sustainability and create a new standard of transparency in global trade. As blockchain transforms supply chain management by enhancing transparency, reducing fraud and enabling ethical sourcing, questions arise about the future of global commerce. Can blockchain achieve widespread adoption despite high costs, regulatory challenges, and the need for global cooperation? How will integrating blockchain with technologies like IoT and AI reshape the efficiency and resilience of supply chains? And, with consumers demanding more transparency, will companies that prioritise blockchain-driven accountability gain a competitive advantage? This emerging technology challenges industries to rethink trust, traceability and sustainability in supply chains - will they rise to the occasion?
