# Fully homomorphic encryption (FHE): a solution for blockchain interoperability?

The advent of blockchain technology has transformed data management and transmission, yet it introduces substantial challenges. One major hurdle is blockchain interoperability - the capacity for various blockchain networks to exchange and secure data effectively. [Fully homomorphic encryption (FHE)](https://www.ibm.com/topics/homomorphic-encryption#:~:text=Fully%20homomorphic%20encryption%20\(FHE\)%20is,various%20security%20and%20privacy%20risks) presents a revolutionary approach to these challenges - it ensures data remains encrypted during computation, allowing for secure and private transactions across diverse platforms without needing decryption. Potentially, this is extremely useful as businesses expand into hybrid multi-cloud settings where data privacy and security are crucial. Implementing FHE can help blockchain networks achieve genuine interoperability without sacrificing confidentiality since it allows computations on encrypted data without needing to decrypt the data first, preserving data privacy and security throughout the process. Imagine a scenario where data is securely locked in a “black box” and computations can be performed on it without ever unlocking the box. As illustrated by [Craig Gentry](https://chain.link/education-hub/homomorphic-encryption), this concept is brilliantly akin to wearing special gloves that allow you to manipulate objects inside the box without ever seeing them.

Meanwhile, FHE could have profound effects across multiple industries; currently a company’s data is spread across a number of cloud providers, but FHE facilitates secure data processing without revealing sensitive information to external parties. Hence, this allows organisations to conduct high-value analytics, collaborate safely in both public and private clouds and apply AI and machine learning to encrypted data - all whilst upholding rigorous privacy standards. [In financial services, healthcare and retail](https://www.ibm.com/topics/homomorphic-encryption#:~:text=Fully%20homomorphic%20encryption%20\(FHE\)%20is,various%20security%20and%20privacy%20risks) also, where privacy regulations often limit the sharing and processing of sensitive information, FHE’s importance is particularly clear - it allows for the secure sharing and analysis of data, supporting predictive analytics in finance, confidential research in healthcare and encrypted search capabilities in retail, all whilst maintaining strict data confidentiality. Furthermore, in a multi-blockchain environment, [blockchain interoperability](https://www.omfif.org/2024/02/finance-needs-a-blockchain-interoperability-solution/#:~:text=However%2C%20ensuring%20the%20safety%20of,is%20shared%20across%20different%20blockchains) (with varied protocols, consensus mechanisms and data formats), is essential. Without this integration, the potential and utility of blockchain networks are significantly curtailed, rendering them as isolated functional units. However, achieving interoperability presents several challenges such as ensuring that data and transactions retain their security, privacy and integrity as they traverse different networks. Different blockchains may have varying security standards, and a flaw in one network could jeopardise the integrity of interconnected systems. Additionally, the public nature of many blockchains can raise privacy issues, particularly when sensitive information is transferred or accessed across networks.

Essentially, interoperability solutions usually work by creating standardised protocols that enable interaction among various blockchains whilst maintaining each network’s unique characteristics and security frameworks. Meanwhile, [cross-chain bridges](https://chain.link/education-hub/cross-chain-bridge), which support the transfer of assets and data across blockchains, often face trade-offs concerning speed, cost and security. Privacy-preserving technologies such as FHE can play a supportive role; FHE allows computations on encrypted data, enabling secure processing across different blockchain networks without revealing the underlying information. [Imagine performing calculations directly on encrypted data without needing to decrypt it](https://tokenminds.co/blog/blockchain-development/homomorphic-enryption-on-blockchain) \- the results remain accurate, and the data stays protected. This capability ensures that, as data moves between interoperable blockchains, its privacy and security are preserved, enabling [multiple critical applications](https://www.halborn.com/blog/post/fully-homomorphic-encryption-fhe-and-the-blockchain). FHE allows data to remain private where sensitive information is securely stored and accessed on a blockchain, restricting access only to those authorised entities. Additionally, FHE supports private transactions by validating them without revealing the transaction amount, thereby upholding user confidentiality. Creating private smart contracts is another area where FHE also excels; these contracts stay encrypted on a blockchain, safeguarding the code and intellectual property from unauthorised access or reverse engineering. In addition, FHE strengthens secure multi-party computation (MPC)**** by allowing encrypted data to be processed among different parties while maintaining the confidentiality of sensitive information. Another feature is FHE supports confidential voting within decentralised autonomous organisations (DAOs), ensuring that individual votes remain private. It also offers protection against [frontrunning](https://www.business-standard.com/markets/news/explainer-what-is-front-running-124062400786_1.html) by encrypting transaction data, making it difficult for bots to exploit pending transactions. FHE can also be beneficial in a regulated environment __ providing encryption solutions that align with data privacy laws, all while allowing secure, distributed computations on the blockchain.

[Fhenix is the first Layer 2 solution](https://blockonomi.com/fhenix/) to bring FHE to the Ethereum blockchain, enabling computation over encrypted data addressing privacy and security challenges in Web3 applications. Fhenix allows developers to create secure, private applications without deep cryptographic knowledge. This innovation enhances blockchain's utility particularly in scenarios requiring high confidentiality - such as private financial transactions and decentralised gaming - setting it apart from other privacy solutions such as [ZK-SNARKs](https://z.cash/learn/what-are-zk-snarks/). Another provider of FHE solutions is [Zama](https://tech.eu/2024/03/07/zama-raises-73m-for-its-privacy-preserving-homomorphic-encryption/#:~:text=Zama%20builds%20fully%20homomorphic%20encryption,organisations%2C%20governments%20and%20consumers%20alike), making FHE accessible to over 3,000 developers and enabling the creation of privacy-first applications across blockchain, AI, healthcare and financial services.

The [European Central Bank (ECB)](https://cointelegraph.com/news/ecb-explores-blockchain-mpc-technology) is also engaged in exploring how FHE can be incorporated into its central bank digital currency (CBDC) endeavours. In conjunction with Zama, the ECB is testing blockchain-based approaches to enhance liquidity management whilst preserving encrypted data confidentiality; this project

addresses the challenges of secure data handling within financial systems. However, although FHE shows great potential, it encounters [several issues that limit its practical application](https://www.arakt.com/blog/issues-with-homomorphic-encryption). One of the foremost problems is its computational cost because the overhead required to perform operations on encrypted data makes FHE less practical for managing large datasets or real-time processing. This substantial resource demand can pose a significant barrier, particularly in scenarios where swift data processing is crucial. Managing encryption keys poses a significant challenge since ensuring their secrecy and security is crucial but difficult, especially with multiple parties involved. Moreover, FHE is not ideal for all computations; tasks such as sorting and searching become notably complex when dealing with encrypted data which limits its range of applications. Furthermore, the sophisticated nature of implementing FHE demands expertise that smaller organisations might lack. Striking the right balance between security and performance is a major trade-off, with improvements in one area often leading to reductions in the other.

Hence, whilst FHE presents a promising method for enhancing blockchain interoperability, it is not the sole solution. [Several significant projects](https://www.finextra.com/blogposting/25112/blockchain-interoperability-solutions-and-challenges-where-are-we-now) have emerged to tackle the challenge of enabling smooth interaction between various blockchain networks. For example, [Chainlink’s Cross-Chain Interoperability Protocol (CCIP)](https://www.ccn.com/education/chainlinks-cross-chain-interoperability-protocol-ccip-explained/) enables secure blockchain communication through cross-chain messages rather than conventional bridges. Similarly, [Cosmos](https://cosmos.network/) (sometimes referred to as the ‘internet of blockchains’) uses a hub-and-spoke model, linking multiple blockchains through its Cosmos Hub and facilitating value transfer with inter-blockchain communication. [Polkadot,](https://polkadot.com/) another significant player, connects various blockchains using parachains and native bridges, allowing them to exchange both data and value[. Wanchain](https://www.wanchain.org/) introduces decentralised bridges to link diverse blockchain networks, whilst the [Canton Network](https://www.canton.network/) focuses on providing privacy-enabled interoperability for institutional assets. Each of these solutions presents unique advantages and challenges, contributing to a diverse landscape of blockchain interoperability methods, each with its own approach to achieving secure and efficient data transfer across different networks.

In essence, FHE offers a groundbreaking solution for blockchain interoperability by enabling secure, encrypted data processing across different networks. As businesses integrate data across hybrid multicloud environments, FHE ensures privacy and confidentiality without compromising functionality. However, challenges such as computational cost and key management persist. So, as we advance, the pivotal question is: will FHE truly overcome the barriers of blockchain interoperability, or will it reveal new obstacles we need to address?
