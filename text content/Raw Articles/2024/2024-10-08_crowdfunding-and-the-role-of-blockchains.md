# Crowdfunding and the role of blockchains

[Crowdfunding](https://www.gofundme.com/c/crowdfunding) is a method of raising funds from a large number of individuals, often via online platforms. It allows entrepreneurs to present their projects to a global audience and collect small amounts of money from many backers, which can cumulatively reach substantial amounts. The success of crowdfunding has been admirably demonstrated by Revolut which raised capital via crowdfunding, so valuing the company at £40million (it is now valued at $45billion). There are also various different types of crowdfunding; reward-based crowdfunding is where backers contribute to a project in exchange for rewards, typically in the form of products or experiences; equity-based crowdfunding is typically when investors receive shares in the company or project in exchange for their investment, allowing them to profit if the venture succeeds; and, finally, there is donation-based crowdfunding which is when usually individuals donate money to support causes or projects without expecting anything in return.

In addition, there are a range of crowdfunding platforms (such as [Kickstarter](https://www.kickstarter.com/) and [Indiegogo](https://www.indiegogo.com/)) which have facilitated countless projects, enabling creators to bypass traditional funding routes such as banks and venture capitalists. However, these platforms also face limitations including high fees, limited access to international markets and concerns about transparency and trust. It is, in part, why we have seen the increasing use of blockchain technology being used because it securely records transactions across multiple computers which enables all participants access to a single version of the truth, so enhancing transparency and security. The use of blockchain technology has introduced various types of tokens; utility tokens, which are tokens that provide users with access to a product or service and are often linked to a specific project; security tokens represent ownership in an underlying asset, such as equity in a company, and are subject to regulatory oversight; and digital currencies such as Bitcoin and Ethereum that can be used for transactions on crowdfunding platforms. This has led to blockchain and digital assets reshaping crowdfunding in a variety of ways. Blockchain’s transparency means that backers can verify how funds are being used, ensuring that their contributions are going toward the intended purposes. For example, a startup raising funds for a new product can regularly update its backers via the blockchain, showing how their investments are being allocated, thereby fostering trust and accountability.

Traditional crowdfunding platforms often charge significant fees (which can eat into the funds raised) but blockchain technology can reduce the involvement of intermediaries, enabling peer-to-peer transactions that can significantly reduce costs. This reduction in fees is particularly beneficial for small projects that may struggle to meet the minimum funding requirements on conventional platforms. By utilising digital assets for transactions, startups can retain a larger portion of the funds they raise and because blockchain technology operates on a global scale, it allows projects to access a broader pool of potential backers in a variety of jurisdictions. This international reach can lead to increased funding opportunities for entrepreneurs, especially those in emerging markets, although one needs to be mindful of relevant regulations. Additionally, the use of cryptocurrencies facilitates cross-border transactions without the need for currency conversion, making it easier for backers to contribute regardless of their location. But security is a critical concern in crowdfunding, as projects often deal with sensitive financial information. Blockchain technology enhances security through cryptographic protocols that protect transactions and user data and by utilising smart contracts (self-executing contracts with the terms of the agreement directly written into code) crowdfunding platforms can automate processes and enforce conditions without the need for third-party oversight. This reduces the risk of fraud and ensures that funds are released only when predetermined criteria are met and blockchain also enables the tokenisation of assets, allowing for fractional ownership. This means that investors can purchase smaller stakes in a project, making it more accessible for individuals who may not have the means to invest large sums of money. For instance, a real estate project could be tokenised, allowing multiple investors to own a portion of the property. This democratisation of investment opportunities can significantly increase participation in crowdfunding initiatives. Furthermore,**** blockchains and the digital assets they create can foster a sense of community amongst backers which can lead to a large number of investors - for example, Dogecoin is held in over [7million wallets.](https://www.coincarp.com/currencies/dogecoin/richlist/) Try finding out how many shareholders in a public company such as BP or Apple there are is far from easy….Many crowdfunding projects have dedicated communities that actively engage with creators, provide feedback and contribute ideas, and by using blockchain, project creators can offer tokens as rewards for community involvement, incentivising supporters to contribute beyond financial backing. This creates a more invested and loyal community around the project. So, whilst blockchain and digital assets offer significant advantages, they also come with challenges and risks that must be addressed:

> · **regulatory uncertainty**

The regulatory landscape for blockchain and digital assets is still evolving. Governments around the world are grappling with how to regulate cryptocurrencies and token sales, leading to uncertainty for crowdfunding platforms and their users. Regulatory compliance is crucial, especially for equity-based crowdfunding, where investors need legal protections. Startups must navigate complex legal frameworks to avoid penalties or shutdowns.

> · **market volatility**

The value of digital assets can be highly volatile, which poses risks for both project creators and backers. If a project accepts cryptocurrency as a form of payment, the value may fluctuate significantly between the time of contribution and the project's completion. This volatility can lead to unpredictability in funding and financial planning for both parties, potentially jeopardising the success of crowdfunding campaigns.

> · **technical complexity**

The integration of blockchain and digital assets may be daunting for traditional crowdfunding platforms and their users. Many individuals may lack the technical knowledge required to navigate blockchain systems, limiting their participation in these new models.

> · **security concerns**

Despite blockchain’s security features, the technology is not immune to attacks. Smart contracts can contain vulnerabilities that hackers may exploit, leading to potential losses for backers and project creators.

**Case studies of blockchain in crowdfunding**

**Case study 1: Indiegogo**

[Indiegogo](https://www.indiegogo.com/), one of the leading crowdfunding platforms, has embraced blockchain technology by launching its platform for token-based crowdfunding. This initiative allows creators to raise funds through digital tokens, expanding their reach and funding options. By integrating blockchain, Indiegogo aims to provide enhanced transparency and lower fees, enabling project creators to connect with a global audience of potential backers.

**Case study 2: DAOs (decentralised autonomous organisations)**

[DAOs](https://www.investopedia.com/tech/what-dao/) represent a novel approach to crowdfunding, allowing communities to collectively fund projects using blockchain technology. Participants contribute funds in cryptocurrency and decisions regarding the allocation of those funds are made through democratic voting mechanisms. DAOs exemplify how blockchain can reshape the traditional funding model by empowering communities and enabling decentralised decision-making.

**Case study 3: StartEngine**

[StartEngine](https://www.startengine.com/) is an equity crowdfunding platform that has incorporated blockchain technology to facilitate tokenised equity offerings. By allowing investors to purchase shares in startups through digital tokens, StartEngine has made it easier for companies to raise capital whilst providing investors with greater liquidity. This model aligns with the growing trend of tokenisation and demonstrates how blockchain can enhance the equity crowdfunding landscape.

Furthermore, when looking to the future for crowdfunding we are beginning to see the rise of hybrid models**.** As blockchain technology and digital assets become more mainstream, we can expect the emergence of hybrid crowdfunding models that combine traditional methods with blockchain innovations. These models may offer multiple funding options such as equity, rewards and token-based contributions, so allowing project creators to diversify their funding strategies. And, as governments develop clearer regulatory frameworks for blockchain and digital assets, crowdfunding platforms will gain greater legitimacy. This regulatory clarity will encourage more investors to participate in crowdfunding initiatives, fostering innovation and growth in the sector. Undoubtedly, the growth of decentralised finance (DeFi) will further reshape the crowdfunding landscape; DeFi platforms offer innovative lending and investment opportunities allowing users to earn interest on their contributions and participate in liquidity pools. By integrating DeFi with crowdfunding, project creators can access new funding sources whilst offering backers more attractive investment options. Examples of forms active in this space include ENS DAO, Gitcoin grants, Sushi Swap and CrowdInvest.

Crowdfunding has essentially evolved from a niche concept to a global funding mechanism - now even more accessible and transparent with blockchain integration. Whilst platforms such as Kickstarter and Indiegogo laid the foundation, the inclusion of digital assets and blockchain technology offers enhanced security, global reach and fractional ownership. These innovations democratise funding, making it available to more entrepreneurs whilst fostering community involvement and transparency. Yet, challenges such as regulatory uncertainty and market volatility remain. So, will the rise of blockchain-powered crowdfunding pave the way for a more equitable financial ecosystem, or will regulatory hurdles and technical complexities limit its potential? Ultimately, as crowdfunding transforms, it urges us to reconsider the value of community-driven investment and the role of decentralised finance in shaping the future of funding.
