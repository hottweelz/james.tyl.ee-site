# How blockchain technology is impacting private equity, private credit markets and SMEs

[Private equity (PE)](https://www.titan.com/articles/private-equity-returns) involves investment funds that acquire and manage private companies or delist public companies, with the goal of improving their operations and profitability before eventually selling them for a profit. According to [McKinsey & Co.](https://www.mckinsey.com/~/media/mckinsey/industries/private%20equity%20and%20principal%20investors/our%20insights/mckinseys%20private%20markets%20annual%20review/2023/mckinsey-global-private-markets-review-2023.pdf), since 2008, PE funds have generally outperformed other private market asset classes and comparable public market investments. However, this performance can vary and the lack of public financial disclosures for privately held assets makes assessing their performance challenging. McKinsey estimates that the total value of [PE funds exceeds $13trillion](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/private-capital/our-insights/mckinseys-private-markets-annual-review), with over $3.7trillion in cash and waiting to be invested. Private equity became recognised as a separate investment category from the mid-20th century onwards, garnering notable attention during the 1980s and 1990s. Over time, the sector has diversified to encompass buyouts, venture capital, growth capital and distressed asset investments.

The significance of private equity in the global economy cannot be overstated. PE firms play a crucial role in driving innovation, fostering growth and creating value within their portfolio companies. They provide capital to businesses that may lack access to traditional financing, assist in operational restructuring and often introduce new management expertise. For instance, the [Cambridge Associates’ US Private Equity Index](https://www.cambridgeassociates.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/WEB-2021-Q4-USPE-Benchmark-Book.pdf) reported an average annual return of 14.65% over the two decades ending December 31st, 2021 - outperforming the S&P 500's 5.91% return over the same period. However, during the decade up to June 2020, the S&P 500 achieved an average annual return of 13.99%, slightly surpassing private equity's 13.77%. Meanwhile, private equity evaluates its success through metrics such as internal rate of return (IRR) and multiple of invested capital (MOIC). IRR gauges projected growth by considering cash flow timing whilst MOIC quantifies overall investment return; these metrics are crucial for assessing the lasting impact of PE investments. The [comparison between private equity (PE) and public equity (e.g., S&P 500) performance](https://www.insead.edu/sites/default/files/assets/dept/centres/gpei/docs/re-thinking-pe-risk-and-reward.pdf) reveals nuanced insights into investor preferences and risk management strategies. Historically, US private equity funds have demonstrated superior returns compared to the broader stock market, notably outperforming the S&P 500 index over extended periods. For instance, data spanning decades shows that US PE buyout funds have averaged net returns of 13.1%, significantly outpacing the S&P 500's average return of around 8.1%. Comparing these asset classes involves considering volatility as a critical factor; private equity investments are often perceived as less volatile than public equities. This perception may be misleading due to reporting practices that could systematically understate true volatility. Public equities, on the other hand, experience daily market fluctuations, offering more transparency but potentially higher volatility as investment vehicles. However, we are beginning to see blockchain-powered tokenised PE funds from asset managers such as [KKR](https://media.kkr.com/rss-feed/news-release/?news_id=5aa593b4-0063-4e7b-a0cc-b9d2d1668d21). Furthermore, [Citi Group](https://www.marketsmedia.com/tokenization-advances-in-private-markets/) has been working with PE managers, Wellington Management and WisdomTree, which will enable investors to trade more frequently and hopefully improve the transparency of these PE funds as the assets will need to be valued more frequently.

Meanwhile, long-term returns analysis suggests that whilst PE has historically delivered higher returns than public equities, recent trends indicate a convergence in performance, particularly in leveraged public market strategies that mimic PE characteristics. This convergence underscores the evolving landscape where investors seek to balance risk and return across asset classes. The private credit market, originating in the 1990s to cater to companies overlooked by conventional banks and public markets, has developed into a substantial [$2.1 trillion sector worldwide](https://www.imf.org/en/Blogs/Articles/2024/04/08/fast-growing-USD2-trillion-private-credit-market-warrants-closer-watch) primarily led by prominent financial institutions and affluent investors, as reported by the IMF. This market's expansion has been driven by its flexibility and customised financial offerings, appealing to institutional investors pursuing higher yields with reduced volatility compared to conventional investments. Yet, despite its benefits, the migration of lending from regulated banking sectors to the opaque world of private credit introduces complexities. Valuation practices are infrequent and assessing credit quality can be challenging, raising concerns about systemic risks within interconnected financial ecosystems involving private equity, commercial banks and investors. According to data provider and analytics dashboard, [RWA.xyz](https://app.rwa.xyz/), over $8billion of private credit has already been tokenised so making it the largest asset class to embrace tokenisation by some considerable margin. And whilst private credit presents manageable risks to financial stability at present, its ongoing expansion and evolving dynamics warrant continued vigilance. Hence, a growing consensus has spurred – blockchain technology offers potential solutions to enhance market access and transparency, which could shape the future landscape of private credit.

Furthermore, regulatory oversight and risk management will be crucial in ensuring the sustainable growth and resilience of this significant segment of the financial market. With [small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs)](https://www.oecd.org/cfe/leed/1918307.pdf) numbering around 400 million globally, they play a pivotal role in economic advancement and workforce expansion on a global scale. They are the primary drivers of job growth, comprising over 95% of businesses and contributing to 60%-70% of total employment. Yet, despite their significance, SMEs face multiple challenges, especially in accessing conventional financing, adopting new technologies and enhancing managerial skills. And these difficulties are exacerbated in a globalised, technology-driven environment where economies of scale are less crucial. Governments play a pivotal role in improving SME access to funding, upgrading information infrastructures and facilitating international market participation. [Blockchain technology](https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/sites/18ac5acb-en/index.html?itemId=/content/component/18ac5acb-en) offers potential solutions to these challenges by lowering information disparities and transaction expenses and, by improving transparency, accountability and trust in business dealings, blockchain can help SMEs surmount obstacles linked to size and operational transparency. Furthermore, blockchain-driven advancements in supply chain management and business models can boost SME efficiency and the quality of their products/services. In the [private credit sector](https://www.coindesk.com/business/2024/02/29/crypto-for-advisors-private-credit-meets-the-blockchain/), blockchain-driven platforms are revolutionising how debt instruments are issued and managed, especially benefiting small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs). Unlike traditional finance, which restricts private credit to large institutions and the wealthy, blockchain platforms are democratising access to the $1.6 trillion private debt market for smaller investors. This technology enables the [tokenisation of debt instruments](https://digitalfrontier.com/articles/private-credit-markets-embracing-blockchain), representing them as digital assets on the blockchain.

An example of this has been ABN Amro which issued a tokenised debt instrument and raised €5milliom for a firm called [Versteda to help refinance its Green Assets](https://www.abnamro.com/en/news/abn-amro-registers-first-digital-green-bond-on-the-public-blockchain). This approach lowers transaction costs and enhances liquidity by enabling secondary market trading of these tokens - tokenising assets such as accounts receivable, inventory or future cash flows into digital tokens on a blockchain. For instance, [Sygnum](https://www.sygnum.com/) and [Franklin Templeton](https://www.franklintempleton.com/) are pioneering tokenised investment products that cater to SMEs, leveraging blockchain's potential to streamline processes and increase market participation. These innovations aim to democratise access to private credit - historically limited to institutional investors and high-net-worth individuals. Several case studies illustrate the practical application of blockchain in SME financing. [Centrifuge](https://centrifuge.io/) enables SMEs to leverage real-world assets for financing through decentralised finance (DeFi), circumventing traditional banking intermediaries and enhancing capital accessibility; [Maple](https://maple.finance/) focuses on serving institutional and accredited investors with diverse lending opportunities, including collateralised loans backed by U.S. Treasury bills and high-grade debt; [Goldfinch](https://goldfinch.finance/) facilitates decentralised borrowing without requiring cryptocurrency collateral, expanding access to financing for SMEs entering the digital asset market. Private credit tokenisation represents a pivotal advancement in financial markets, leveraging blockchain technology to revolutionise access and efficiency. [Figure Technology Solutions](https://www.pymnts.com/blockchain/2024/figure-debuts-blockchain-based-private-credit-marketplace/) recently launched a blockchain-based marketplace for private credit loans, facilitating a multi-seller, multi-buyer ecosystem that enhances liquidity and transparency across transactions. This initiative streamlines loan origination and funding processes, reducing settlement times from months to days through the Provenance blockchain. Similarly, [Fasanara Capital](https://www.reedsmith.com/en/news/2024/05/reed-smith-fasanara-capital-private-credit-onchain-securitisation), in collaboration with Untangled Finance, pioneered a private credit tokenisation pool on the Celo blockchain. This innovative structure, compliant with Luxembourg's securitisation laws, collateralises diverse French working capital assets sourced from FinTech lender, Karmen; it enables accredited investors in the UK and the US to participate in tokenised debt assets, offering enhanced market access and investment flexibility. There has been a [133% surge in active private loans via digital ledgers since early 2023, totalling approximately $581 million](https://onchain.org/magazine/what-is-private-credit-tokenization-and-how-does-it-work/) and this growth underscores blockchain's role in democratising access to private credit, empowering smaller investors and enhancing market liquidity.

In the coming years, the future of private equity, SMEs and blockchain technology promises transformative growth and innovation. But, as private equity evolves, leveraging technological advancements and adapting to regulatory shifts will be crucial. Blockchain technology stands to revolutionise traditional financial markets by enhancing transparency, lowering transaction costs and democratising access to capital for SMEs. The incorporation of blockchain in private credit markets presents opportunities for improved market efficiency and greater investor involvement. Moving forward, establishing clear regulatory frameworks and robust risk management protocols will be essential to effectively harnessing blockchain's potential across these dynamic sectors. Digital money powered by blockchain technology would enable programmable digital payments to be made and thus automate the buying and selling of these assets as well as the payment of coupons - ideally, in real time.
