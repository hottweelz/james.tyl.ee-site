# Programmable universal basic income (PUBI) using CBDCs

*By Jonny Fry | *

Central bank digital currencies (CBDCs) are digital forms of a nation's fiat money, issued and regulated by a central bank. But unlike decentralised cryptocurrencies such as Bitcoin or Ethereum, CBDCs are sovereign-backed and integrated into the monetary system. Their potential lies in their programmability - i.e. the ability to embed rules, conditions and automation directly into the currency itself through smart contracts. These contracts are self-executing agreements with the terms of the contract directly written into code. When deployed on CBDC infrastructures, they allow for real-time, conditional transfers of money whereby eliminating intermediaries and bureaucratic processes. Combining CBDCs with programmable logic opens up new dimensions in economic policy design. Therefore, instead of blunt monetary tools, governments gain access to fine-grained instruments capable of targeted fiscal interventions, tailored precisely to economic needs and citizen profiles.

[Programmable universal basic income (PUBI)](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/b/basic-income.asp) transforms the philosophical ideal of universal basic income into an automated, policy-embedded digital reality. In a PUBI system, every citizen receives a regular stipend directly into their digital wallets but, unlike traditional UBI schemes, PUBI introduces programmability conditions that align with specific economic or social objectives. For instance, a government could program PUBI funds to be spendable, only on essential goods such as food, healthcare, education or public transportation. Alternatively, they could set expiry dates on the funds to encourage immediate consumption and stimulate economic activity, thereby functioning as a dynamic fiscal stimulus tool. This digital infrastructure enables instantaneous distribution, reducing administrative delays, fraud risks and the overheads associated with welfare disbursement. More importantly, it enables policymakers to embed behavioural nudges directly into the currency, providing a proactive tool to guide economic activity toward desired societal outcomes. Meantime, one of the persistent challenges in welfare distribution is ensuring that funds reach the intended recipients without leakage, duplication or fraud. Traditional [know your customer (KYC)](https://www.idnow.io/regulation/what-is-kyc/) processes are often cumbersome, fragmented and susceptible to exploitation but PUBI proposes a more robust and scalable identity solution through [non-fungible tokens (NFTs)](https://www.kraken.com/learn/what-are-non-fungible-tokens-nft). In this context, “verifiable identity” NFTs function as unique, tamper-proof digital identifiers assigned to each citizen. However, unlike conventional identity documents, these NFTs are cryptographically secure, universally verifiable on the blockchain and can be updated in real-time with changes in a person’s eligibility status or demographic data. By linking PUBI disbursements to these identity NFTs, governments can ensure that UBI payments are uniquely associated with legitimate recipients. This identity layer also opens the door for multi-tiered policy frameworks where benefits can be customised based on demographic, geographic or socio-economic criteria without compromising privacy.

Traditional UBI schemes equally face criticism for their “one-size-fits-all” approach but, with PUBI, programmability enables a high degree of policy flexibility meaning that governments could design conditionality frameworks that align with macroeconomic objectives, societal needs or crisis-response strategies. For example, during a public health emergency, PUBI smart contracts could prioritise funds to be spent only on healthcare-related expenses or home essentials. During economic downturns, expiration mechanisms could be used to encourage immediate spending whereby functioning as an automatic stabiliser to boost demand. Programmable conditions could also introduce “geofencing” capabilities and funds could be tagged for use within specific local economies thereby supporting small businesses or regions facing economic hardship. Of significance is that this fine-tuned targeting is virtually impossible in traditional monetary disbursement systems but becomes a programmable feature in a PUBI framework. Furthermore, PUBI systems could incorporate dynamic eligibility criteria, for example, smart contracts could automatically adjust the amount or frequency of disbursements based on real-time economic indicators such as unemployment rates or inflation data, thus creating an adaptive fiscal mechanism.

One of PUBI's most transformative impacts lies in its potential to drastically reduce the bureaucratic apparatus traditionally needed for welfare disbursement. Current welfare systems are plagued with inefficiencies stemming from verification processes, means-testing, fraud detection and multi-layered administrative workflows. These inefficiencies not only result in high operational costs but also lead to exclusion errors where rightful beneficiaries are left out - with PUBI using a CBDC, these inefficiencies are mitigated through automated execution. Smart contracts eliminate the need for manual validation processes whilst verifiable identity NFTs ensure airtight recipient authentication. Payments are direct, real-time and fully auditable on the blockchain, fostering transparency and reducing opportunities for corruption or misuse. The reduction in administrative complexity can also foster greater public trust in welfare systems as transparency becomes a built-in feature rather than an external audit requirement. Essentially, citizens would have direct, real-time visibility into their PUBI transactions, enhancing their sense of financial security and inclusion. However, a frequently raised concern in CBDC and PUBI discussions is the issue of digital access and literacy. In a world where not every citizen has equal access to smartphones, internet connectivity or digital literacy, ensuring the inclusiveness of PUBI becomes a policy imperative. Therefore, governments would need to design parallel initiatives that promote digital financial inclusion. This might include issuing offline-capable CBDC smart cards for populations in remote or underserved regions, establishing community digital kiosks or collaborating with telecom providers to offer subsidised digital wallets. Another approach could be integrating PUBI systems into existing mobile money networks prevalent in many developing countries. Ensuring that the design of PUBI interfaces is user-friendly, multilingual and accessible to persons with disabilities would be essential in achieving true universality.

However, whilst the programmability of PUBI offers numerous policy benefits, it also raises important debates around privacy, autonomy and surveillance. The idea of a currency that can be programmed to restrict spending choices introduces a potential slippery slope towards overreach and paternalism. Citizens may resist a system that dictates how and where they can spend their own UBI funds. Furthermore, the ability to track every transaction raises concerns of financial surveillance, with implications for civil liberties and individual autonomy. Therefore to address these concerns, governments must establish robust data governance frameworks. Privacy-preserving technologies such as zero-knowledge proofs or differential privacy techniques can be integrated to ensure that while the system remains auditable for compliance, individual transaction data is not exposed unnecessarily. Additionally, there needs to be a clear legal and ethical architecture defining the scope and limits of programmability. Public discourse, stakeholder consultations and transparent legislative processes will be crucial in defining acceptable boundaries whereby ensuring that programmability serves the public good without encroaching on personal freedoms.

Without doubt, the implementation of PUBI could have wide-ranging macroeconomic implications - from a monetary policy perspective, programmable disbursements could act as a real-time fiscal policy lever, allowing governments to manage consumption patterns, stimulate local economies or respond dynamically to economic shocks. Additionally, PUBI could serve as a partial replacement for some traditional welfare programs, streamlining government expenditures and potentially reducing fiscal deficits caused by welfare inefficiencies. However, there is a balancing act to maintain. Injecting programmable money into the economy at scale could risk inflationary pressures if not carefully calibrated with broader economic conditions. Policymakers would need to integrate PUBI into a holistic economic strategy, potentially using automatic stabilisers in smart contracts to modulate disbursements based on inflation, employment rates and other macroeconomic indicators. Despite these considerable challenges, the vision of programmable universal basic income in conjunction with CBDCs offers a compelling glimpse into a potential future. It holds the promise of a more efficient, targeted and resilient social safety net, capable of adapting to the rapid economic shifts of the 21st century. It could foster greater financial inclusion by providing direct access to central bank money for the unbanked and underbanked and could enhance the efficacy of fiscal policy, allowing governments to inject stimulus directly into the economy with precise control over its application. Moreover, the integration of verifiable identity NFTs could build a foundation for a more secure and privacy-respecting digital identity ecosystem, extending beyond simply UBI.

So, as nations around the world explore and pilot CBDCs, the debate around programmability and its application (particularly for welfare programs such as UBI) will intensify; it necessitates a multidisciplinary approach, drawing expertise from economics, technology, law, ethics and social policy. The journey towards a PUBI on CBDC rails is not merely a technological one - it is a societal choice that will profoundly impact the relationship between citizens, their governments and the very nature of money itself. Navigating this intricate landscape requires foresight, democratic deliberation and a commitment to designing systems that prioritise human well-being and fundamental rights amidst the undeniable allure of technological efficiency. The programmable future of money beckons and with it, the potential for a fundamentally reshaped social contract.
