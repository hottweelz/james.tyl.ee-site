# Tokenised deposits

*By Jonny Fry | *

The increasing expansion and development of blockchain into a world of its own highlights the need for digital forms of payment. The core concept is to create a way to transfer value that will function as a liquid means of payment and storage of value in blockchain-native environments aside from stablecoins. [Deposits tokens](https://forkast.news/are-deposit-tokens-the-new-stablecoins/#:~:text=Deposit%20tokens%20are%20issued%20on%20a%20blockchain%20by%20a%20licensed,are%20issued%20by%20central%20banks.) were first proposed by DBS Bank, JPMorgan and SGX, May 2022 when Project Guardian was launched as a form of digital money. They are the equivalent of the already existing deposits, held by licensed depository institutions but recorded on a blockchain. Tokenised deposits, such as bank deposits, can support several use cases such as trading and settlement, domestic and cross-border payments and provision of cash collateral. The Swiss Bank Association (SBA) published a [white paper](https://www.swissbanking.ch/_Resources/Persistent/9/4/1/1/941178de59b98030206fc15ac8c99012f65df30b/SBA_The_Deposit_Token_EN_2023.pdf) about four months ago with the aim of introducing a tokenised Swiss Franc; the bank association is now coordinating a tokenised deposit project with a selection of other banks.

Source: [Oliver Wyman and Onyx by J.P. Morgan Analysis](https://www.oliverwyman.com/our-expertise/insights/2023/feb/deposit-token-a-foundation-for-stable-digital-money.html)

**E-money, stablecoins, tokenised deposits: different, or not**?

Over time, stablecoins have served crypto traders by offering a welcome shelter during times of crypto volatility. Unlike traditional cryptocurrencies such as Bitcoin and Ethereum, stablecoins’ value is pegged to a particular asset e.g., a fiat currency, gold or government bond, making them less volatile than traditional cryptocurrencies. Since there exists a strong bond between stablecoins and the traditional banking sector (many stablecoins hold bank deposits), crises in banking can exact an impact on stablecoins - as was seen in March with [stablecoin provider, Circle](https://forkast.news/headlines/circle-crypto-com-coinbase-silvergate/). However, this raises questions about the stability of stablecoins; currently valued at [$128 billion](https://www.theblock.co/data/decentralized-finance/stablecoins), the stablecoin market has generated institutional interest in digital assets. Despite this, the [USDC ‘depeg’ event](https://forkast.news/usdc-stablecoin-silicon-valley-bank-silvergate/) and, later, the collapse of [Silicon Valley Bank (SVB)](https://forkast.news/circles-usdc-stablecoin-regains-parity-as-regulators-act-to-stop-bank-run-risk/) and [Silvergate](https://forkast.news/circles-usdc-stablecoin-regains-parity-as-regulators-act-to-stop-bank-run-risk/) (which pushed Circle to the edge of losing its liquidity) caused panic within the community. Whilst these events further strengthen the question of how unstable stablecoins are, it was made obvious that stablecoins are designed to be more stable than traditional cryptocurrencies. And, depending on how stablecoins are structured, they are not completely immune to the instabilities in the market and thus can experience price volatility. 

**Are tokenised deposits better than stablecoins?**

Last year, researchers with the Federal Bank of New York [questioned the stability of stablecoins](https://www.pymnts.com/cryptocurrency/2022/fed-paper-finds-stablecoin-risks-are-manageable-and-come-with-rewards/) and ended up stating that they are _un_ likely to be the future of payments. A research group consisting of economics professor, Rod Garratt (University of California), Michael Lee and Antoine Martin (New York Federal Reserve’s research and statistics group) and Joseph Torregrossa (assistant vice president at the Federal Reserve Bank of New York) maintained that stablecoins are _not_ efficient for transferring money if distributed ledger technology is to become incorporated into traditional finance. Their report has suggested that tokenised deposits be the way ahead, [stating that](https://www.pymnts.com/cryptocurrency/2022/ny-fed-tokenized-deposits-better-than-stablecoins) “ _Tokenized deposits will represent a claim on the depositor’s commercial bank, just as regular deposit does_.” Meanwhile,**** according to research released by JPMorgan, deposit tokens are a more stable and reliable digital asset and could overtake stablecoins in popularity. The firm, being cautious of the lack of regulation of stablecoins, has argued in favour of regulated deposit tokens and, whilst some institutions have asserted that stablecoins are a solution to many problems at hand, JPMorgan has promoted deposit tokens over stablecoins. The researchers have pointed out that deposit tokens, being backed by commercial bank money, are subject to the same protections and regulations as deposit insurance and are therefore a better choice for traditional bankers.**** Furthermore, back in April this year, the Bank for International Settlements (BIS) [took a stance in a published paper](https://www.bis.org/publ/bisbull73.pdf), comparing stablecoins to the previously unstable era of privately-issued bank notes before the US Federal Reserve was created. In favour of the stability of tokenised deposits, the BIS argues that the currency supports the ‘singleness’ of money, making it interchangeable in all forms. As a [report](https://www.finextra.com/newsarticle/41776/jp-morgan-touts-bank-issued-deposit-tokens) from global management consulting firm, Oliver Wyman, observes: “ _Unlike stablecoins, deposit tokens also benefit from connectivity to traditional banking infrastructures and regulatory safeguards that already support bank deposits.”_

**Implementing deposit tokens: the pros, cons and challenges**

Deposit tokens are issued on a blockchain by a licensed depository token to represent a deposit claim, differentiating them from stablecoins (which are typically issued by non-bank entities) and [central bank digital currencies (CBCDs)](https://forkast.news/topic/cryptocurrencies/cbdc/). However, as with any new technology, deposit tokens face challenges as regards implementation and adoption. Being discussed side by side, [regulatory clarification](https://www.bis.org/innovation_hub/dynamo_study.pdf) for deposit tokens, stablecoins and CBCDs is necessary for organisations to explore wholesale use cases. Despite the recent efforts by regulators to actively endorse real-world use cases and ensure investors’ protection, variability in legal taxonomies and licensing requirements are impeding adoption efforts. Concerning these digital assets, regulators’ efforts (publishing consultation papers delineating their approach to digital assets) have continually been met by the persisting discrepancies as regards legal taxonomies, definitions and responsibilities across different jurisdictions. This necessitates stronger cooperation and coordination efforts channelled to supporting cross-jurisdiction interoperability, which will enable market participants to make more responsible and sustainable progress. The recent announcement in the [Deutsche Bundesbank](https://www.bundesbank.de/de/publikationen/berichte/monatsberichte/monatsbericht-juli-2023-781624) report that “ _deposit tokens may or may not be considered a deposit so it remains unclear whether tokenized deposits would be covered by deposit insurance_ ” serves to highlight the inconsistencies of how tokenised deposits may be treated. Having observed the growing interest in the adoption of central bank digital currencies, stablecoins and deposit tokens by major banking and non-banking institutions across various jurisdictions, [a paper recently published](https://www.bis.org/innovation_hub/dynamo_study.pdf) by the Bank for International Settlements and supported by Quinlan & Associates noted that technology and regulations are in their early stages of development and may result in siloed initiatives within individual “walled gardens”, so leading to fundamental differences in operating procedures and corporate strategies - an anomaly that should be avoided. The [publishers stated](https://www.bis.org/innovation_hub/dynamo_study.pdf), “ _Despite industry convergence challenges, we encourage institutions to keep a close eye on potential interoperability solutions that could unlock the full potential of this new asset class in the years to come_.”

Given that deposit tokens typically will need to be issued by a bank but stablecoins do not need to have a bank, at least in the short term we are likely to see both forms of digital payments continue to be promoted and their use case explored. As financial institutions and regulators continue to prioritise transparency and consumer protection, digital currencies offer them an alternative way to deliver a more inclusive and efficient means compared to the traditional methods of payment. However, financial markets and regulators need to balance the demand for stability and keep the current status quo versus embrace innovation which offers the allure of lower costs, less risk and faster payments. The dilemma is set to continue but it seems that digital currencies in some shape or form will become increasingly available and so offer an alternative way to make payments.
