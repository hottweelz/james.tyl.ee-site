# The unintended consequences of ubiquitous digital assets

*By Jonny Fry | *

When digital assets first entered mainstream conversation, much of the attention focused on volatile cryptocurrencies such as Bitcoin. But the real transformation now unfolding is less about speculative coins and more about the quiet, steady rise of programmable hyper-liquid digital money [stablecoins](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/stablecoin.asp), [tokenised deposits](https://www.settlemint.com/deposit-tokenization#:~:text=Tokenized%20deposits%20digitally%20represent%20traditional,the%20efficiency%20of%20blockchain%20technology.) and the [tokenisation of real-world assets (RWA)](https://www.venly.io/blog/demystifying-rwa-the-benefits-and-trends-in-real-world-asset-tokenization) such as property, government bonds and even fine art. These innovations all promise faster settlement, broader access and new ways to unlock liquidity - a stablecoin can move across borders in seconds without banking delays and tokenised real estate share can be traded similar to stock, allowing investors gain fractional exposure to prime properties. Furthermore, banks exploring tokenised deposits see them as a bridge between today’s regulated systems and tomorrow’s programmable finance. Yet, as with all technological shifts, the adoption of ubiquitous digital assets comes with unintended consequences - some environmental, some social and others deeply economic. Nevertheless, unlike the early crypto era, these challenges are not to do with raw computing power or unregulated anonymity alone, they are about the complex ways digitised finance interacts with infrastructure, policy and human behaviour.

Although stablecoins and tokenised deposits do not require energy-intensive [proof-of-work (PoW) mining](https://www.geeksforgeeks.org/ethical-hacking/blockchain-proof-of-work-pow/), that does not mean they are carbon-neutral. The systems enabling instant settlement data centres, blockchain networks and high-speed payment rails still consume significant power. Large-scale stablecoin operations often run on public blockchains such as Ethereum or on permissioned distributed ledgers maintained by consortiums of banks. These platforms rely on server clusters that must be constantly online - replicated across jurisdictions for redundancy and compliance.

Even [proof-of-stake chains](https://www.gemini.com/cryptopedia/what-is-staking-crypto-proof-of-stake-pos-blockchains), whilst far more efficient than the Bitcoin blockchain, still draw energy from growing global data infrastructure. In addition to this, there is the off-chain infrastructure. [The tokenisation of real-world assets](https://chain.link/education-hub/real-world-assets-rwas-explained) depends on accurate, secure and often real-time data feeds, supply chain tracking and legal title updates; this ‘oracle’ infrastructure adds another layer of energy demand and hardware requirements. Meanwhile, data centres are increasingly located in regions with cheap electricity, so echoing the concentration patterns seen in crypto mining. And whilst the footprint is smaller than PoW mining, the cumulative energy cost of a global, always-on tokenised financial system, is far from trivial - especially when combined with the insatiable demand for energy from AI-driven compliance and analytics.

The 2022 Ethereum [PoW-to-PoS](https://hedera.com/learning/consensus-algorithms/proof-of-stake-vs-proof-of-work) change [reduced energy utilisation by 99%](https://www.ey.com/en_ch/insights/technology/how-does-the-ethereum-merge-help-the-real-and-virtual-world-save-energy), indicating more sustainable options. Without widespread low-energy consensus models, digital global asset markets are able to release massive amounts of carbon and digitising traditionally slow-moving assets can unintentionally increase transaction velocity. A tokenised bond might trade hands dozens of times a day instead of quarterly whereby amplifying the processing and settlement load on networks and their supporting infrastructure. Notably, [digital assets](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/d/digital-asset-framework.asp) were touted as financial inclusion instruments because they were open, geographically neutral and gatekeeping resistant; and this vision has come true for some. Those in countries with poor banking infrastructure utilise cryptocurrency for remittances, savings and cross-border trading but others live in a less inclusive world. Wealth is concentrated in digital assets and studies demonstrate that a few addresses hold most Bitcoin. Early adopters with foresight, technical skills or disposable funds to participate at cheap costs made exponential gains with the well-positioned benefitting disproportionately. Large institutions testing tokenised deposits can obtain liquidity and settlement advantages first and tokenised real estate/private credit offers frequently attract high-net-worth investors who must be accredited under numerous securities rules.

A major claim for stablecoins and tokenisation is that they democratise finance - a Kenyan farmer might preserve his assets with a dollar-backed stablecoin whilst a Brazilian young investor could acquire $10 worth of Manhattan skyscraper shares on a tokenised property market. Unsurprisingly, however, geography and socioeconomic background also affect stablecoin adoption. Although dollar stablecoins have helped remittance corridors, expensive smartphone expenses, poor internet and the requirement for self-custody tools keep many out. Stablecoins also allow wealthier people to shift cash overseas whilst others stay locked in local currency risks, worsening inequality in capital-controlled regions. Furthermore, regulation promotes inequality - large companies and wealthy individuals can afford legal knowledge and costly compliance processes to participate in uncertain or changing jurisdictions, whilst smaller firms and casual customers cannot. The digital asset boom may create a new financial aristocracy for those who can navigate its technological, legal and economic complexities. And RWA tokenisation creates stratifications - early tokenised market investors, sometimes with insider networks or sophisticated platforms, may build appreciating holdings before products reach mainstream markets. Similar to early Bitcoin users, the advantage accumulates over time, concentrating wealth in the “token economy.”

However, stablecoins and tokenised assets carry new types of risks alongside familiar ones - price stability in a well-collateralised stablecoin may reduce volatility but systemic risk can arise if a major issuer fails or if collateral quality deteriorates. We saw glimpses of this in algorithmic stablecoin collapses, though regulated, asset-backed models ought to be more far more resilient. The programmability of tokenised assets widely hailed as a feature also creates avenues for exploitation. Smart contracts governing tokenised bonds or deposits can have vulnerabilities and, if exploited, these could freeze or misdirect large volumes of value instantly. Moreover, unlike a traditional financial institution, there may be no human “circuit breaker” able to pause trades. Market manipulation takes on new forms in tokenised ecosystems - a tokenised commodity could be subject to orchestrated valuation manipulations if the underlying data feed (oracle) is compromised. And in thinly traded [tokenised RWA markets](https://www.coindesk.com/business/2025/06/26/real-world-asset-tokenization-market-has-grown-almost-fivefold-in-3-years), even modest trades by large players could swing prices significantly. The pseudonymity of certain blockchain-based stablecoins also introduces compliance challenges. Whilst regulated banks issuing tokenised deposits operate within [KYC/AML frameworks](https://www.idnow.io/regulation/aml-kyc-overview/), cross-chain transfers between permissioned and public systems can create monitoring blind spots potentially facilitating illicit flows without robust oversight. For communities already underserved by traditional finance, these risks are not abstract. A stablecoin wallet drained by a smart contract exploit or a fraudulent tokenised investment platform can mean life-altering losses, especially in countries without strong consumer protection laws for digital assets.

Digital asset ubiquity unintentionally favours speculative adoption over utility-driven adoption. Blockchain technology offers supply chain monitoring, decentralised identities and programmable finance, yet most economic activity is short-term trade and price speculation. Speculative bias can drown out valuable efforts and [VC investment](https://www.deutschewealth.com/en/insights/investing-insights/asset-class-insights/venture-capital-investing-closer-look/what-is-venture-capital-investing.html) generally prioritises coin releases above infrastructure development. Protocols with good marketing but weak foundations may outsell technically better solutions therefore misallocating resources. Furthermore, frequent speculation raises market volatility and this puts individual investors at risk and lowers ecosystem confidence, limiting mainstream adoption of transformational products. The capacity to tokenise nearly any asset from art and music to real estate and personal data has been a breakthrough in liquidity and ownership democratisation. Tokenisation also has subtle social and psychological effects; being able to trade any object, experience or connection can change cultural values. The 2021 boom of [non-fungible tokens (NFTs)](https://www.kaspersky.com/resource-center/definitions/what-is-an-nft) gave artists new funding sources but also raised concerns about commodifying creativity. Critics felt that market pricing replaced creative value, promoting speculation rather than expression. Social capital also risks being traded in a tokenised society. Influencer currencies, reputation tokens and social graph marketplaces might blend community and commerce, changing how individuals engage and self-identify.

Meanwhile, disruptions in one ecosystem can swiftly spread due to ubiquitous digital assets. A big exchange hack, stablecoin de-pegging or protocol attack might shake linked marketplaces. [The 2022 TerraUSD collapse](https://www.chainalysis.com/blog/how-terrausd-collapsed/) caused billions in losses, damaged faith in algorithmic stablecoins and spread to other projects. As more traditional and digital financial infrastructure uses tokenised systems, systemic risk grows. Smart contracts may execute flawlessly but, if the logic is flawed, the fault propagates fast and at scale. This “code as law” approach lacks circuit breakers unless intentionally constructed, leaving systems prone to catastrophic failures. Hence, multifaceted solutions are needed to address omnipresent digital assets' unintended effects so, by using energy-efficient consensus techniques, wallet security and privacy-preserving transaction layers, the technology can reduce any downsides. Regulations may level the playing field, protect consumers and control systemic risk without inhibiting innovation and educating people on digital asset dangers, duties and possibilities may prevent exploitation and create more equitable systems. Open-source technologies, awareness initiatives and truthful disclosures can also close the knowledge gap - hence, from art markets to cross-border transfers, digital assets have transformed finance and technology. So, their widespread use has benefits speed, accessibility and programmability but also unforeseen repercussions for environmental sustainability, social justice, privacy and geopolitical stability. Decisions now will shape the future and, if left uncontrolled, environmental damage, financial inequity and economic spying might increase. However, purposeful design, legislative foresight and cultural awareness may lead to a more fair, sustainable and privacy-respecting digital economy. The challenge is to reconcile innovation with accountability since technological advancement is never neutral. With our code, systems and incentives then markets and societies will be shaped. After all, digital assets are social, not merely financial - but how we handle their unexpected implications today will decide whether they empower or divide.
