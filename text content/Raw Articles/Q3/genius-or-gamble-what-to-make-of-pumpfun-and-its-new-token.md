# Genius or gamble? what to make of Pump.fun and its new token

*By Jonny Fry | *

**Memecoins** are a type of crypto inspired by internet memes, jokes or cultural references rather than solving a specific technical or financial problem. Usually, they gain popularity through social media hype and online communities as opposed to fundamental utility or innovation. Memecoins typically have little intrinsic value or underlying use case - they experience huge volatility but have still proved be very popular as a way to engage with global audiences digitally on-line. Interestingly, the number of memecoins being created has been growing.

Nonetheless, memecoins are not particularly new. As soon as Bitcoin debuted in 2009, people were already spinning up new coins to replicate BTC’s journey and make some quick money. From the earliest Bitcoin forks to Dogecoin and Ethereum’s 2017 ICO frenzy, the line between satire and speculation has always been thin. But in early 2024, a platform emerged that erased the blurry line.[ Pump.fun,](https://www.wired.com/story/pumpfun-founder-memecoin-rugpulls-teen/) launched on Solana by Noah Tweedale, Alon Cohen and Dylan Kerler, reduced token creation to performance art. For the price of a sandwich ($2), anyone could launch a token using a bonding-curve model - no roadmap, no code, no promise of future value. It democratised crypto in the most chaotic way imaginable: by handing the printer to everyone. One trader was found to have [launched over 17,000 tokens](https://99bitcoins.com/news/pump-fun-meme-coin-factory-trader-made-840k-by-launching-17000-tokens-in-3-months/) in three months, making over $840,000. The result was staggering - by mid-2025, over 11 million tokens had been launched whereby generating $700 million in revenue and turning Pump.fun into the undisputed winner of the memecoin market - so rendering memecoins more of a feature than a bug.

Pump.fun took off at the peak of Solana's 2024 revival and quickly cultivated a user base that treated token creation similar to content creation. In late 2024, the platform introduced a livestream feature that saw token creators perform outlandish stunts on camera in order to promote their projects. These ranged from pyrotechnics to Russian roulette, as [creators tried to pump their coins](https://www.dlnews.com/articles/defi/fake-suicide-on-pump-fun-prompted-outrage-and-a-shutdown/) with views, memes and virality. A 13-year-old who created a memecoin called _Gen Z Quant_ cashed out $50,000 and watched as the community ironically revived it, pumping its market cap to $85 million. The episode was hailed by some as performance art but condemned by others as reckless gambling - the lines between investment, play and performance had never been blurrier. However, following a disturbing series of events, including a [livestreamed suicide](https://www.livemint.com/news/trends/crypto-trader-last-post-before-kill-self-suicide-livestream-viral-crash-out-throw-your-life-away-ask-if-really-matters-11740384635684.html) in early 2025, Pump.fun removed the feature, but not before it had cemented its reputation as the ultimate meme-coin circus - even inspiring an episode in the latest season of dystopian TV series ‘Black Mirror’. With the launch of Pump.fun’s official mobile app on Apple and Android, it brought back [livestreaming to it users.](https://www.dlnews.com/articles/defi/pump-fun-revives-livestreams-after-chaos-cratering-volume/) Today, Pump.fun strives to mint a token of its own - $PUMP. The token is being positioned for a $1 billion raise, so valuing the platform at $4 billion and, at first glance, it might appear to be the obvious next step - just another Web3 platform doing what every successful protocol eventually does. But here, it hits differently. This is not [Blur launching a token](https://nftnow.com/guides/blurs-token-just-dropped-heres-what-you-need-to-know/) to reward liquidity providers and it is not [Friend.tech gamifying engagement](https://flagship.fyi/outposts/dapps/the-rise-of-friendtech-how-it-became-the-biggest-social-app-on-base/). This is a platform that, by design, resists permanence whereby attempting to carve out a symbol of permanence. The contradiction is glaring and the question is whether it is genius or a gamble.

Hence, to make a case for genius, the argument lies in the architecture. Pump.fun did not rush into a token launch; it built toward it with intent. Pump began with frictionless token creation, lowering the barriers to entry and transforming memecoin launches into a form of content. Liquidity followed as users embraced the thrill of rapid speculation. Then came the tools: PumpSwap, a native decentralised exchange designed to capture volume that previously leaked to a competitor, and [Raydium](https://blockworks.co/news/raydium-launching-pumpfun-version), a mobile app to widen access and a livestreaming feature that amplified social virality. Each piece tightened the feedback loop between creation, performance and trading. PumpSwap, in particular, marked a strategic leap forward. By removing the [6 SOL migration fee](https://www.theblock.co/post/347360/pump-fun-launches-dex-called-pumpswap-to-instantly-migrate-graduated-tokens) and allowing free token transfers, it kept liquidity in-house whilst introducing locked liquidity to protect traders from rug pulls. This not only increased trust but also repositioned memecoin creation as something more sustainable. For creators, it meant fewer barriers, revenue sharing and legitimacy. For the broader market, it hinted at a maturing space; one where hype could be supported by infrastructure and speculation can give way to creative but accountable financial play. But the brilliance of the idea does not cancel out the gamble - Pump.fun’s entire value proposition is that nothing matters for more than 24 hours. [According to Solidus Labs](https://www.soliduslabs.com/reports/solana-rug-pulls-pump-dumps-crypto-compliance), a staggering 98.6% of tokens created on Pump.fun are either scams or soft rug pulls, a figure that undoubtedly reflects growing concerns across the broader crypto industry. But Pump.fun [sees things differently](https://www.coindesk.com/business/2025/05/07/98-of-tokens-on-pump-fun-have-been-rug-pulls-or-an-act-of-fraud-new-report-says). The platform argues that its true value lies in enabling a functioning marketplace that connects motivated buyers and sellers - and, in capturing the cultural expressions that users choose to assign value to.

By contrast, a token is a slow thing - it asks people to hold, govern and care. Questions arising are: who is the token really for, and will its intended audience choose to assign value to $PUMP? Launching a token significantly increases Pump.fun’s legal exposure, especially as it is already facing [class-action lawsuits](https://www.dlapiper.com/en/insights/publications/2025/02/meme-coins-alleged-to-be-securities-in-two-new-class-actions) over [unregistered securities](https://www.ccn.com/education/crypto/pump-fun-lawsuit-explained-crypto-impact/) and intellectual property issues in the US, and being required to [leave the UK](https://cointelegraph.com/news/fca-issues-warning-bans-pump-fun-uk) following a warning from the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA). The [emergence of LaunchLab](https://en.cryptonomist.ch/2025/03/19/raydium-presents-launchlab-a-new-alternative-to-pump-fun-for-the-launch-of-memecoin-on-solana/), from Raydium, comes at a pertinent time - about a month after the news that Pump.fun was developing its own internal Automatic Market Maker (AMM) and so ending what had been an unofficial collaboration with Raydium. However, what could prevent users from going back to Raydium if they do not find this new ecosystem palatable? And more so, despite the separation, Raydium has been getting along fine post-launch. The bigger gamble though, is spiritual: can a platform built on chaos survive structure? Does a token kill the fun? Will users shift from wild experiments to calculated farming? And will VCs, governance forums and “roadmaps” turn the punk energy into polished bloat? Even Solana is not immune. If $PUMP succeeds, it could cement Solana’s place as crypto’s cultural chain, the home of participatory finance, memes and speculative art. If it fails, it could reinforce the “casino chain” stigma Solana has been trying to shake. Either way, the shockwaves will not be local.

Pump.fun’s genius was turning nothing into something, but the gamble lies in thinking it can now turn something into everything. Whether $PUMP becomes the keystone of a new financial subculture or merely another memecoin punchline, will depend not on how well it is launched, but on how many decide to believe in it, own it and trade it.
