# Blockchain’s identity crisis: self-sovereign identity vs. privacy coins vs. surveillance

*By Jonny Fry | *

One of the defining characteristics of blockchain technology is transparency. In public blockchains, such as Bitcoin and Ethereum, every transaction is visible to anyone with access to the ledger. This design, whilst ideal for validating trust in a decentralised environment, creates challenges when it comes to privacy. Even though pseudonymous addresses represent users, advances in blockchain analytics have made it increasingly easy to link these addresses to real-world identities through behaviour patterns, IP tracking and off-chain data. Unsurprisinlgy, this transparency is seen as a virtue in combating fraud, enabling auditability and supporting accountability, however, when financial transactions, medical data and identity credentials are exposed (even pseudonymously) privacy becomes an illusion. The immutability of blockchain only heightens this risk since, once data is added, it cannot be altered or erased. Hence, the fundamental question becomes: how can individuals retain control over their personal information in a system that is inherently public?

Self-sovereign[ identification](https://www.techtarget.com/searchsecurity/definition/self-sovereign-identity) (SSI) is a promising solution to blockchain's identification dilemma. SSI is a paradigm change in digital identity management away from centralised systems where major organisations or governments control personal data. Instead, SSI gives people full control over their digital credentials and personal data. [Decentralised identities (DIDs)](https://www.dock.io/post/decentralized-identifiers), [verifiable credentials (VCs)](https://www.oneidentity.com/learn/what-are-verifiable-credentials-in-cybersecurity.aspx) and a blockchain or DLT for anchoring and validating these elements form the foundation of SSI. User-created DIDs are globally unique and independent of central authorities where users manage these digital addresses, akin to a wallet-sized identity card. VCs are digital - i.e. cryptographically secure attestations of an individual's traits or credentials issued by trustworthy institutions. In addition, user devices store these VCs in digital wallets. This “selective disclosure” capacity makes SSI powerful - SSI holders can limit their information to what is needed for a given encounter with a service provider, unlike with traditional identification verification. And, to establish they are over 18, they might show a genuine document that only proves their age, not their birthdate, residence or name; this greatly decreases data exposure for identity theft and misuse.

Moreover, SSI relies on the blockchain's immutable, tamper-resistant ledger for DID registration and VC cryptographic proof verification. The cryptographic hashes and proofs on the public blockchain verify the credentials' integrity and validity but sensitive personal data is not kept there (for privacy and GDPR compliance). Therefore, decentralised verification eliminates a central mediator, increasing trust and security. Sovrin and the [European Self-Sovereign Identity Framework (ESSIF)](https://essif-lab.eu/) are pioneering SSI solutions using [Hyperledger Indy](https://www.geeksforgeeks.org/computer-networks/hyperledger-indy-in-blockchain/) for decentralised identity management. There are many uses, from easing financial institution KYC to safe patient data management in healthcare and verifiable academic qualifications. SSI offers a strong alternative to centralised identification systems by empowering individuals to control their digital selves.

**Privacy coins: shielding transactions from prying eyes**

SSI emphasises identification and selective disclosure whereas privacy coins address financial anonymity. Advanced encryption algorithms were used to hide the sender, receiver and amount in Monero, Zcash and Dash - [ring signatures](https://kyushu-u.elsevierpure.com/en/publications/a-survey-on-group-signatures-and-ring-signatures-traceability-vs-#:~:text=Both%20group%20and%20ring%20signatures,to%20form%20ad%2Dhoc%20groups.) and [stealth addresses](https://chainstack.com/stealth-addresses-blockchain-transaction-privacy/#:~:text=What%20are%20Stealth%20Addresses?,a%20special%20%E2%80%9Cspending%20key.%E2%80%9D) obscure the origin and destination of money in Monero whilst [zk-SNARKs](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/z/zksnark.asp) (zero-knowledge succinct non-interactive arguments of knowledge) enable shielded transactions on Zcash's network. Notably, these privacy currencies are the opposite of Bitcoin's extreme openness and are popular among ideologically or surveillance-averse financial privacy advocates.

Blockchain technologies, especially privacy-enhancing ones, have caught the attention of governments and established organisations. Privacy coins are closely monitored by regulators - their ability to promote money laundering, terrorism funding and other illegal acts has led to de-platforming and delisting. Major exchanges have abandoned support for these currencies and South Korea and Japan have banned them. The IRS has announced rewards for Monero transaction tracking software with this antagonistic reaction showing the widening gap between cryptocurrency's privacy-maximalist ideal and nation-state monitoring. Unsurprisingly, due to worries about financial crime, terrorism financing and tax avoidance, governments distrust the anonymity of privacy coins. Global regulators are struggling to integrate this technology into AML and KYC frameworks and to comply with regulations, some exchanges have delisted privacy coins whilst others are considering “traceability” solutions for private transactions. Many argue that total anonymity inhibits law enforcement's capacity to resist illegal activity whereby threatening national security and financial stability.

However, privacy coins and SSI advocates maintain that privacy is a human right that protects people from unlawful monitoring, data exploitation and discrimination. They argue that limiting privacy tools promotes surveillance nations and degrades democracy; they also note that privacy-enhancing technology can protect whistleblowers, secure payments in politically sensitive places and provide competitive business intelligence. Financial institutions and huge enterprises are also negotiating this difficult terrain - they have regard for blockchain's efficiency and security but are concerned about regulatory compliance and reputational damage. Even if it compromises privacy, they pursue regulatory transparency and auditability. Many corporate blockchain solutions use permissioned or private blockchains where members are identified and transactions can be traced

or SSI systems for selective disclosure and compliance. The response from governments and institutions is multifaceted, and includes:

> · **regulatory scrutiny and bans** \- some nations have outright banned or severely restricted the use of privacy coins, citing concerns about illicit activities. Others are developing stricter reporting requirements for cryptocurrency exchanges dealing with privacy-enhanced assets.
> 
> · **embracing controlled transparency** \- many governments and institutions are exploring blockchain for specific applications (e.g., supply chain tracking, digital fiat currencies) where a degree of transparency is desired, but they are wary of the ‘Wild West’ nature of fully public and anonymous blockchains.
> 
> · **investment in SSI and verifiable credentials -** there is a growing recognition of SSI's potential to create a more secure and user-centric digital identity infrastructure. Governments are investing in research and pilot programs for SSI, understanding that empowering individuals with control over their data can also lead to more efficient and trustworthy digital interactions. The EU's ESSIF is a prime example of a governmental body actively promoting SSI.
> 
> · **developing traceability tools** \- law enforcement agencies and blockchain analytics firms are continuously developing sophisticated tools to de-anonymise transactions on public blockchains, including those involving some privacy coins. This is an ongoing ‘cat-and-mouse’ game between privacy-enhancing technologies and surveillance capabilities.
> 
> · **international collaboration** \- given the global nature of blockchain, there is an increasing push for international collaboration to develop harmonised regulatory frameworks that address both privacy concerns and the need to combat financial crime.

Unsurprisingly, transparency and privacy are philosophically at odds; libertarians created blockchain to avoid centralisation, yet the technology must satisfy power structures to become widespread. This divides the crypto community into controlled, monitored and decentralised, privacy-focused areas, but self-sovereign identification strikes a balance between privacy and compliance. Selective disclosure allows users to establish their service eligibility without providing personal data. Regulation allows institutions to accept cryptographic proofs without retaining sensitive client data, whereby lowering liability and protecting privacy. However, trust structures must change for this ambition to materialise. Institutions must trust credential verifiers and interoperability standards must arise across jurisdictions. Also, technology must be more user-friendly since mainstream people find private key and digital wallet management too complicated. Privacy coins may stay niche or marginalised owing to regulatory resistance but their future may be in combination with other privacy-enhancing programs, or Tor or I2P networks. So, unless global financial privacy views change, they will not be widely used and the state will presumably continue to monitor and govern. Governments will want more money flow visibility as ransomware, tax evasion and geopolitical instability rise. If convenient, fast and integrated with current systems, CBDCs may become normalised, however, privacy as a basic right in digital banking may suffer.

Furthermore, this identity dilemma creates a divided blockchain future - compliance, auditability and control will be prioritised in business and government blockchain systems and KYC-compliant platforms, CBDCs and business blockchains will rule the formal financial ecosystem. However, privacy-focused solutions such as Monero and decentralised identity wallets will remain countercultural, appealing to people who value privacy and autonomy above all else. Uncomfortably, though, these universes may collide. SSI, privacy-preserving smart contracts (using homomorphic encryption or ZKPs) and regulated privacy currencies might bridge the gap because they provide compliance without disclosure and confidence without supervision - certainly, user demand, regulatory openness and technology maturity determine success. Blockchain's identity dilemma is about building a digital society, not merely proving our identities online. Therefore, are we moving towards a future of complete traceability where every money and identity-based action is tracked and audited? And can we design systems that protect privacy, trust and autonomy against state and corporate power? Answers will not come from technology alone - legal frameworks, societal agreements and knowledgeable citizens advocating for digital rights are needed. Blockchain allows us to rethink identity and privacy, but whether we use it to liberate or control is unclear.
