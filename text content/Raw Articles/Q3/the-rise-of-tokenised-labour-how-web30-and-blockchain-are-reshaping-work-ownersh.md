# The rise of tokenised labour: how Web3.0 and blockchain are reshaping work ownership and compensation

*By Jonny Fry | *

Traditionally, labour has been defined by centralised structures corporations, institutions and platforms. Employees trade time and skills for a fixed salary or hourly wage. Freelancers may get more autonomy but still rely on centralised platforms that set the rules and extract value through fees; ownership rarely enters the equation. A software developer might build a billion-dollar product, but if stock or equity in the organisation that monetises it is not held, the developer’s upside is capped. Likewise, gig workers face limited bargaining power and opaque payment systems. Tokenised labour flips this script and refers to the process of representing work, whether time, effort, creativity or results, as a blockchain-based token. These tokens, fungible (i.e. cryptocurrencies) or non-fungible (NFTs), can be bought, sold or exchanged within decentralised ecosystems essentially enabling labour itself to become a tradable digital asset. They can represent completed tasks, contribution rights, proof of work or even future value streams tied to the outcome of a project. But [unlike conventional wage models](https://www.brickken.com/en/post/tokenization-the-gig-economy-exploring-the-futures-potential) that emphasise fixed salaries and central control, tokenised labour introduces a programmable and participatory framework where the value of work is dynamically shared and owned. Compensation can be automated, real-time and transparent. More importantly, workers may retain fractional ownership of the platforms or products they help build.

At the heart of tokenised labour lies Web3, an internet vision that centres on user ownership and decentralisation. Smart contracts and decentralised applications (dApps) allow people to engage in peer-to-peer work agreements without middlemen or centralised employers. Decentralised autonomous organisations (DAOs), for instance, enable workers to contribute to global projects, get compensated in tokens and even vote on how the organisation evolves, effectively making labourers stakeholders. The use of blockchain technology enables the creation of an immutable ledger, recording contributions, timestamps and ownership history. Smart contracts can automatically release payments when conditions are met (e.g. completing a task or submitting deliverables). The deployment of DAOs, which are effectively community-governed groups (i.e. co-operatives) mean contributors can propose work, get paid in governance tokens and vote on collective decisions. Finally, decentralised finance (DeFi) platforms enable liquidity, yield farming and even collateralisation of labour tokens, unlocking new financial use cases.

Once earned, labour tokens can plug directly into the DeFi ecosystem. Workers can lend, stake or invest their tokens, turning their labour into an income-generating asset. This blurs the traditional lines between salary, equity and investment, creating hybrid earning pathways where contributors build wealth whilst maintaining autonomy over their income streams. Projects such as [Braintrust](https://www.usebraintrust.com/) (a decentralised talent network), [Gitcoin](https://www.gitcoin.co/) (supporting open-source development) and [Mirror](https://mirror.xyz/) (a Web3 publishing platform) are already pioneering tokenised labour models. Contributors earn tokens for their work, vote on governance proposals and benefit from the collective upside of the network’s growth. Meanwhile, DAOs such as [Friends with Benefits](https://wiki.fwb.help/) and [Bankless DAO](https://blackflagdao.notion.site/) offer micro-grants and bounty systems where tasks from content creation to development are broken into small, token-compensated units. Experts now tokenise their time as NFTs for example, thirty-minute consulting sessions or mentorship hours which can be bought, traded or resold. Developers contributing to open-source projects can receive tokens through bounty systems that recognise both code and community engagement.

Tokenised labour allows skills to be globally accessible as people are able to carry their reputation, skills and work history across various platforms. Digital credentials, NFT-based portfolios and verifiable reputation scores make establishing trust in anonymous or pseudonymous environments easier. A freelance developer’s [GitHub](https://github.com/) contributions or a designer’s work in the metaverse can now be tokenised and leveraged as proof of capability accessible and transparent for all to see. This is enabling new business and renumeration models to be created with tokenised labour, compensation no longer bound to one-time payments. Instead, smart contracts enable ongoing revenue-sharing, micro-payments and royalties that reflect long-term contribution and value creation. Artists may receive royalties every time their NFTs are resold; developers might earn a share of a project’s token value that grows over time; this fractionalised model can align incentives and encourage sustained, high-quality work. Furthermore, all transactions and contributions are publicly auditable on-chain and anyone with an internet connection and a crypto wallet can participate, so bypassing traditional gatekeepers. The promise is tantalising offering greater equity, borderless collaboration and more responsive compensation. But challenges loom. Regulatory ambiguity, income volatility, lack of worker protections and the steep learning curve of Web3 tools can alienate many potential contributors. However, questions have been asked: Are labour tokens securities? How should governments tax or regulate work done on-chain? What happens when someone's time is “traded” similar to stock?

So, without thoughtful governance and inclusive design, there is a risk of simply recreating power imbalances in a digital disguise and tokenised labour hints at a deeper shift in the social contract. If labour becomes an investable asset, it challenges long-standing notions about employment, pensions and even social safety nets. It may also enable new forms of wealth distribution through tokens and DAOs, workers in emerging markets can gain access to income and capital previously out of reach. In the long run, tokenised labour may also enable universal basic capital: an evolution of universal basic income where everyone owns a share of productive networks. As tokenised labour gains momentum, we may witness a reshaping of the very idea of employment. Traditional companies could adopt hybrid models where staff are paid partly in equity-like tokens. Global labour markets may open up to anyone with internet access, transforming skills into financial leverage. And perhaps we will see digital unions blockchain-based guilds that advocate for fair algorithms and sustainable work conditions in this brave new labour economy.

The **global tokenisation market** was valued at around [$5.02 billion in 2024](https://www.marketdataforecast.com/market-reports/tokenization-market) and is projected to reach [$28.97 billion](https://www.marketdataforecast.com/market-reports/tokenization-market)**by 2033,** growing at a [CAGR of 21.5%](https://www.marketdataforecast.com/market-reports/tokenization-market). So, whilst most of this growth is driven by sectors such as finance, healthcare and cybersecurity, labour tokenisation is emerging as a niche with strong potential. Certainly, tokenised labour is more than a tech trend - it is a paradigm shift. As blockchain and Web3 redefine trust, ownership and coordination, we are witnessing the birth of a labour system that is more transparent, equitable and aligned with human contribution. Whether this leads to empowerment or exploitation will depend on how we design these systems and who gets a seat at the table.
