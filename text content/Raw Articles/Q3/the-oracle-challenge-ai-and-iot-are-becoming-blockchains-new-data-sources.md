# The oracle challenge: AI and IoT are becoming blockchain's new data sources

*By Jonny Fry | *

Blockchain technology's immutable ledgers and trustless smart contracts promise to revolutionise transactions and agreements. Self-executing smart contracts, put straight into code, automate procedures and remove intermediaries. Their greatest strengths, deterministic and isolated, are also their biggest weaknesses: i.e. the “oracle problem”. Blockchains are insular and cannot access real-world data outside their network, and oracle problems arise when smart contracts cannot natively communicate with external data, events or systems. Oracles have been historically centralised entities or simple data feeds, creating a single point of failure that undermines blockchain's decentralisation. Moreover, an oracle is needed for smart contracts to interact with the physical or digital world beyond on-chain processes. However, no matter how good the coding is, a smart contract that depends on a hacked, biased or inaccurate oracle will fail. Reintroducing trust in a trust less system negates blockchain's benefit - however, as the blockchain ecosystem matures, two transformative technologies, AI and the [IoT](https://www.techtarget.com/iotagenda/definition/Internet-of-Things-IoT), are emerging as powerful new data sources, transforming the oracle landscape and adding unprecedented real-world context to smart contracts.

Hence, understanding AI and IoT's underlying implications is essential to understanding their role in solving the oracle dilemma. For example, imagine a smart contract that pays crop insurance dependent on rainfall - the contract cannot determine regional rainfall without external data. A supply chain smart contract may need to validate product temperature throughout transportation, whilst a financial smart contract may need an exchange's real-time asset pricing; yet a smart contract is blind to external reality in all these cases. Previously, oracle systems had used centralised data sources which were functional but vulnerable. Data might have been corrupted, the provider could fall offline, or one organisation could filter information whereby controlling decentralised apps. The aim was to input external data into blockchains in a decentralised, verifiable, and tamper-proof method to enable smart contract execution. This centralisation barrier potentially hinders smart contract adoption for complicated, real asset use cases. Pattern recognition, anomaly detection and decision-making using massive amounts of organised or unstructured data are AI strengths and so this is where AI may provide contextual insights rather than mere points when connected with blockchain technology. Before sending stock prices to a smart contract, an AI-based oracle are able to analyse market sentiment, detect bot-driven activity or compensate for anomalies. Key applications include decentralised insurance: “Did it rain more than 5 inches today?” might activate traditional blockchain insurance smart contracts, however, AI can analyse satellite pictures, climate data and historical trends to improve its estimate. It can also detect fraudulent patterns such as modified weather sensors or claim behaviour abnormalities before payments. Prediction markets are another potential use case - [Augur ](https://www.gemini.com/cryptopedia/augur-prediction-market-rep-coin-augur-betting)and [Polymarket ](https://docs.polymarket.com/polymarket-learn/get-started/what-is-polymarket)require realistic, real-world event resolution so, to detect events, AI can monitor massive datasets such as news and social media. The smart contract receives confidence scores and weighted conclusions from [NLP algorithms](https://www.datarobot.com/blog/what-is-natural-language-processing-introduction-to-nlp/) that evaluate unclear headlines or contradicting information. Financial derivatives and DAOs use AI for real-time risk assessment, treasury optimisation and predictive analytics-based decision-making. AI-enhanced oracles allow smart contracts to adapt to changing situations without set logic.

However, new challenges do exist - AI models are opaque and hard to audit and probabilistic predictions inject uncertainty into a predictable system. The data AI trains on is also important because biased or manipulated data might provide erroneous outcomes. Integrating such systems into blockchain demands new validation, verification and accountability approaches - IoT gives edge data whilst AI interprets. Meanwhile, smart gadgets, including sensors, cameras, GPS modules and environmental monitors can feed blockchain systems in real time; these gadgets extend smart contracts into the real world as physical oracles. [IoT RFID tags](https://www.techtarget.com/searcherp/tip/RFID-vs-IoT-What-are-the-differences) and GPS trackers can check products in transit position, temperature and condition in supply chain management. Smart contracts might disburse money upon delivery confirmation or implement penalties for temperature violations. Hence, these contracts now respond to sensor input instead of human reporting. Soil sensors can monitor moisture and nutrients for crop insurance and resource allocation and have been used in a [variety of countries](https://sentektechnologies.com/case-studies/). Smart meters can measure energy usage and output in decentralised grids, enabling blockchain-based peer-to-peer energy trading. A solar panel that generates more electricity than can be immediately consumed by one home can negotiate and sell to a neighbouring residence using IoT measurements.

One disruptive example is decentralised transportation - smart contracts may govern access, payments and maintenance for sensor-equipped autonomous cars, drones and robots connected via 5G. Imagine a drone delivery service that geolocates and captures images to validate delivery and trigger a blockchain payment - human middleman becomes unnecessary. But IoT devices do bring weaknesses. Hacking, tampering and data spoofing are possible, physical sensors may be controlled (unlike cryptography) and a malevolent actor might fake a GPS signal to confirm delivery. These settings require powerful HSMs, tamper-resistant devices and perhaps edge AI-based anomaly detection solutions to maintain data integrity.

[Decentralised oracle networks (DONs)](https://cointelegraph.com/learn/articles/what-is-a-blockchain-oracle-and-how-does-it-work) allow AI and IoT to work together to solve the oracle challenge. IoT devices collect real-time data from the physical world, which AI analyses, translates and verifies into smart contract intelligence. Imagine a smart contract managing carbon credits using real-time environmental data - forest-wide IoT sensors assess carbon sequestration, air quality and tree growth and an AI algorithm analyses raw data, finds anomalies, filters noise and produces verified carbon offset measures. This AI-processed, certified data is sent to the [blockchain via DON](https://chain.link/education/blockchain-oracles). DONs such as Chainlink aggregate data from many oracles (including AI models and IoT devices), cross-reference it and apply cryptographic proofs to assure data integrity before it reaches the smart contract. Decentralisation at the oracle layer greatly lowers single points of failure and data tampering. Several IoT sensors might submit environmental data and several AI models could analyse it - the DON would then aggregate and validate the consensus, creating a robust and tamper-resistant data stream. In doing so, this combined approach addresses many of the individual challenges:

> · **enhanced data quality** \- AI can detect and correct errors, biases, or malicious inputs from IoT devices, improving the overall accuracy and reliability of the data.
> 
> · **contextual intelligence** \- IoT provides the raw facts, while AI provides the context and interpretation, enabling smart contracts to make more informed decisions.
> 
> · **scalability** \- edge computing, often used with IoT, can preprocess data locally, reducing the load on the blockchain and AI models.
> 
> · **security** \- cryptographic techniques, secure hardware, and decentralized aggregation within DONs bolster the security and trustworthiness of the entire data pipeline from the physical world to the blockchain.

Truly autonomous economic systems require AI-processed real-world data from a huge network of IoT devices. No longer limited to on-chain logic, smart contracts may now interact with and react to physical and digital complexity without human involvement. This therefore enables tremendous automation, efficiency and trust across sectors. [Decentralised autonomous organisations (DAOs)](https://www.techtarget.com/searchcio/definition/decentralized-autonomous-organization-DAO) might manage large treasuries and execute complicated investment plans using AI-driven market research and oracle-fed real-time economic statistics. Through IoT sensors and AI models, parametric insurance might pay out instantly for natural disasters or supply chain disruptions whereby avoiding long claims processes. Autonomous systems might follow products from manufacture to delivery, assess conditions, and disburse payments after specified milestones in logistics and supply chain management. AI might improve routes and forecast delays, while IoT sensors guarantee shipping conditions. This might create transparent, self-managing supply networks that reduce fraud and boost efficiency. Smart contracts might automate medical record access based on verified patient permission (supplied by SSI oracles) or manage pharmaceutical supply chains using IoT sensors, assuring medication integrity and AI-validating authenticity. Autonomous systems might control electricity grids, garbage collection and public transit in smart cities using real-time data from IoT sensors and AI-driven prediction models whereby making cities more efficient and sustainable. These autonomous economic systems would therefore cut expenses, eliminate human mistakes and prevent corruption and manipulation. They show the way to a more efficient, transparent and trustless global economy where code enforces agreements and real-world data informs them.

The “oracle problem” with AI and IoT integration is promising, but it also confronts several obstacles. Scalability is an issue since handling and validating data from billions of IoT devices and processing it with complex AI models needs advanced infrastructure and consensus processes. The data pipeline from the sensor to the smart contract must be secure against physical and cyberattacks and IoT devices and AI models need standard protocols and data formats to interface with blockchain networks. Furthermore, regulatory clarity is still developing - legal frameworks must adapt to accommodate liability, data ownership and AI-driven smart contract judgments as autonomous economic systems become more common. Meanwhile, AI's participation in autonomous systems has ethical issues (notably with bias and responsibility) that demand careful thought and effective governance mechanisms. AI, IoT and blockchain, aided by enhanced decentralised oracle networks, will expand smart contract capabilities. Smart contracts will soon be sentient, context-aware entities that can interact independently with the actual world. This evolution of the oracle problem is a foundational shift that will unlock blockchain technology's full potential, enabling truly decentralised and autonomous economic systems that can adapt, learn and execute in our interconnected world. However, although difficult, the trip offers a more efficient, transparent and trustworthy global infrastructure.
