# The stablecoin landscape: key players and emerging trends

*By Jonny Fry | *

Stablecoins have evolved from niche crypto tools into global payment infrastructure. With over [US$250 billion](https://mmc.vc/research/stablecoins-entering-their-untethered-growth-era/) in circulating supply, they now account for the vast majority of transaction volume in digital asset markets. More importantly though, is that stablecoins are fast becoming a pillar of real-world finance, particularly in regions where local currencies are unstable or financial infrastructure is lacking. As they enter a new phase of regulatory scrutiny, interoperability innovation and cross-border relevance, understanding the evolving landscape is therefore crucial for investors, developers and regulators alike.

The original appeal of stablecoins was simple: combine the stability of fiat currencies with the programmability and speed of blockchain. Early versions such as [Tether (USDT)](https://tether.to/en/) provided a USD-pegged digital asset that could move across exchanges 24/7. Today, stablecoins such as Tether’s (USDT) [Coin (USDC)](https://www.circle.com/en/usdc), [DAI](https://makerdao.com/en/), [PayPal USD (PYUSD)](https://www.paypal.com/us/digital-wallet/manage-money/crypto/pyusd) and others serve diverse use cases, ranging from decentralised finance (DeFi) to global payroll, remittances and trade settlement. In emerging markets such as Latin America and Sub-Saharan Africa, stablecoins are witnessing over 40% year-on-year growth in small transaction volumes, largely due to inflation-driven demand for [digital dollars](https://www.elibrary.imf.org/view/journals/087/2025/004/article-A001-en.pdf). In more mature markets, institutional use is rising steadily, especially for cross-border liquidity, tokenised assets and treasury operations. The stablecoin ecosystem today includes centralised issuers, decentralised protocols and hybrid models, such as:

> · **USDT (Tether)** \- this remains the largest by [market capitalisation](https://coinmarketcap.com/view/stablecoin/) and volume. It dominates offshore trading but has often been criticised for a [lack of transparency](https://www.weforum.org/stories/2025/03/stablecoins-cryptocurrency-on-rise-financial-systems/). Despite this, it remains the default option in many high-volume markets, particularly in Asia.
> 
> · **USDC (Circle)** \- is gaining traction with regulated institutions. Circle’s recent [IPO](https://techcrunch.com/2025/06/13/circle-goes-public-crypto-stablecoin/) and consistent push for compliance and audits have made it a preferred option for enterprises and FinTechs. USDC is now available on multiple chains, with Circle offering a [Cross-Chain Transfer Protocol](https://www.circle.com/blog/ccpt-mainnet-launch) and developer gateway to simplify issuance.
> 
> · **DAI (MakerDAO)** \- this is the leading decentralised stablecoin, collateralised by crypto assets such as ETH and staked ETH. Governed by a [MakerDAO](https://coins.ph/academy/what-is-dai-everything-you-need-to-know/), it is censorship-resistant and widely used in DeFi, although it faces challenges around peg stability and collateral quality.
> 
> · **FRAX** \- offers a [hybrid model](https://www.spglobal.com/_assets/documents/ratings/research/101590842.pdf), partly algorithmic and partly collateral-backed, blending decentralised mechanisms with real-world reserve exposure.
> 
> · **PYUSD (PayPal) and other corporate-backed stablecoins** \- these represent the next wave of institutional entries into the space. Backed by regulated entities such as [Paxos](https://www.paxos.com/newsroom/paypal-launches-us-dollar-backed-stablecoin), they bring with them a consumer-facing approach to stablecoin adoption.
> 
> · **[FDUSD, EURC](https://www.bis.org/publ/arpdf/ar2025e3.htm)**[, and ](https://www.bis.org/publ/arpdf/ar2025e3.htm)**[GHO](https://www.bis.org/publ/arpdf/ar2025e3.htm)** \- these are among a growing list of region-specific and DeFi-native stablecoins designed to diversify the asset class further.

We are increasingly seeing tokens backed by [tokenised treasuries or commodities such as Pax Gold](https://documents.keyrock.com/hubfs/The-Great-Tokenization-Shift.pdf) emerge as alternatives or complements to fiat-pegged models. Moreover, central bank digital currencies (CBDCs) are often seen as rivals to private stablecoins, however, their respective roles may be more complementary than competitive. CBDCs, such as the [e-CNY](https://www.bis.org/publ/bppdf/bispap123_e.pdf) and the proposed [digital euro](https://www.ecb.europa.eu/euro/digital_euro/html/index.en.html), are state-issued currencies aimed at modernising domestic payments under full government control. In contrast, stablecoins are programmable, interoperable and borderless, whereby appealing to users seeking flexibility especially in jurisdictions with dollarisation or weak banking systems. Notably, the [US is leaning away from a retail CBDC](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/econographics/central-bank-digital-currencies-versus-stablecoins-divergent-eu-and-us-perspectives/). Policymakers have instead indicated a preference for regulating private dollar-backed stablecoins to promote the dollar's role in the global financial system. In contrast, the EU is investing heavily in its digital euro framework, with [MiCA](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/RegData/etudes/IDAN/2025/764387/ECTI_IDA\(2025\)764387_EN.pdf) serving as a containment strategy for unregulated stablecoins. Meanwhile, the [Bank for International Settlements](https://www.bis.org/publ/othp65.htm) has argued for an ecosystem where CBDCs and stablecoins co-exist, with interoperability protocols enabling regulated interactions between public and private systems. This hybrid vision is already being tested in pilots such as [Chainlink's CCIP](https://cointelegraph.com/news/hong-kong-government-chainlink-ccip-cbdc-pilot-project) and [Project mBridge](https://www.bis.org/publ/othp59.htm), which aim to connect different national digital currencies with compliant stablecoin infrastructures.

Unsurprisingly, regulation is quickly becoming the most significant catalyst for stablecoin adoption; until recently, the lack of clear rules led to jurisdictional arbitrage and compliance gaps but that is now changing. In 2025, the proposed [STABLE Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/118th-congress/house-bill/4766) and recently passed [GENIUS Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/394/text) in the US aim to create a licensing framework for stablecoin issuers, requiring 1:1 fiat backing, independent audits and restrictions on misleading claims regarding government backing. Issuers would also need to comply with OFAC, AML and KYC obligations. Indeed, as Reuters reported the _[“SEC has hinted at treating certain stablecoins like cash.”](https://tax.thomsonreuters.com/news/digital-dollars-on-the-horizon-fasb-launches-study-amid-white-house-push-for-clarity/)_ Together with other leading issuers, [Circle has welcomed this clarity](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1876042/000119312525135795/d737521d424b4.htm), arguing that it will allow institutional capital to enter the market with more confidence. The legislation stops short of classifying stablecoins as securities, instead treating them as a new category of payment instrument. Meanwhile, the EU’s [Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA)](https://www.esma.europa.eu/esmas-activities/digital-finance-and-innovation/markets-crypto-assets-regulation-mica), which came into effect in 2024, distinguishes between E-Money Tokens and Asset-Referenced Tokens. Issuers must obtain a licence, maintain full reserves and comply with redemption rights and governance standards. Significant issuers face additional scrutiny by the European Banking Authority. MiCA is widely seen as setting a global precedent, with countries in Asia and Latin America referencing it as a model for their own laws. In Asia-Pacific, Singapore has introduced a comprehensive framework for [single-currency stablecoins](https://www.mas.gov.sg/news/media-releases/2023/mas-finalises-stablecoin-regulatory-framework) pegged to G10 currencies. Issuers must meet capital and redemption requirements and provide transparent disclosures. Hong Kong’s new licensing regime went live in 2025 and is focused initially on [HKD-pegged stablecoins](https://www.morganlewis.com/pubs/2025/06/hong-kongs-stablecoins-ordinance-to-take-effect-august-1-an-overview-of-the-regulatory-framework), with plans to expand to foreign currencies. China is exploring [RMB-backed stablecoin](https://www.reuters.com/world/china/chinas-tech-giants-lobby-offshore-yuan-stablecoin-sources-say-2025-07-03/)s issued abroad, signalling a strategic interest in internationalising its currency through compliant private channels. Meanwhile, with emerging markets in countries such as Brazil, Nigeria and the Philippines, the stablecoin use has grown organically, often beyond formal financial oversight. In Latin America and Africa, stablecoins fill critical gaps in access to stable value so enabling small businesses and freelancers to hold digital US dollars and receive payments without relying on legacy banking systems. Regional FinTechs are launching tailored solutions to support payroll, savings and trade finance. However, regulators are beginning to draft laws to integrate stablecoins into financial policy. The [IMF has published frameworks](https://www.elibrary.imf.org/view/journals/063/2022/008/article-A001-en.xml) to help developing economies assess their stablecoin exposure and manage risks to monetary policy.

However, the usability of stablecoins depends on more than regulation. Seamless interoperability across chains and between stablecoins and CBDCs is essential for scaling real-world use. [Chainlink’s Cross-Chain Interoperability Protocol (CCIP)](https://blog.chain.link/automated-compliance-engine-technical-overview) allows tokens to move securely across chains whilst maintaining compliance rules - this is critical for institutions that need to meet jurisdiction-specific requirements. CCIP has already been used in pilots involving [CBDC-to-stablecoin swaps](https://www.bitget.com/news/detail/12560604804575), such as between Australia’s A$DC and Hong Kong’s e-HKD. In addition, [Visa](https://corporate.visa.com/en/sites/visa-perspectives/innovation/visas-role-in-stablecoins.html) has tested stablecoin settlement across its network using USDC on Solana, demonstrating real-time cross-border payments with reduced friction and [Mastercard](https://www.mastercard.com/us/en/news-and-trends/stories/2025/mastercard-stablecoin-utility-and-scale.html) is also supporting multi-token settlement networks that can handle stablecoins from licensed issuers. Meanwhile, custody and tokenisation platforms such as [Fireblocks](https://www.fireblocks.com/platforms/tokenization/) and [Anchorage](https://www.anchorage.com/) are building the back-end infrastructure that large financial institutions need to integrate stablecoins with existing systems. Looking ahead, the future of stablecoins hinges on their integration with both financial policy and technical infrastructure. Regulatory frameworks are maturing and infrastructure providers are building the rails for real-time, compliant transactions. For institutional investors, stablecoins now offer more than simply yield or liquidity. They represent a new layer of programmable finance with applications across capital markets, commerce and cross-border trade. For regulators, the key challenge will be to balance innovation with safeguards, ensuring that stablecoins enhance financial inclusion without undermining monetary sovereignty. So, as CBDCs, stablecoins and tokenised assets converge, those institutions that move first to build capabilities in this space may find themselves at the forefront of the next generation of global finance.
