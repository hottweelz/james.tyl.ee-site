# Centralised v decentralised cryptocurrency (Part 1)

*By Jonny Fry | *

The cryptocurrency landscape is defined by a fundamental philosophical and architectural schism. On one side stands the [centralised exchange](https://www.rapidinnovation.io/post/what-is-a-centralized-exchange-cex) or CEX, an entity that mirrors traditional finance with its emphasis on intermediary control and a single point of authority. On the other side is the [decentralised exchange](https://withpersona.com/blog/decentralized-exchanges-and-kyc) or DEX, a new model that seeks to eliminate the middleman entirely embodying the core ethos of self-custody and peer-to-peer transactions. The choice between these two exchange models is not simply a matter of preference but a strategic decision involving a complex set of trade-offs. Each has very different architectural foundations, offers different user experiences and market dynamics and presents unique risks as well as regulatory challenges, but offers alternative ways for users in our fast-evolving digital financial landscape.

**Core principles and architectural foundations**

To understand the divergent nature of CEXs and DEXs, one must first grasp the core principles that govern their architecture and operational models. These foundational differences dictate every subsequent aspect of their functionality from user-facing features to inherent vulnerabilities.

A centralised cryptocurrency exchange is a digital platform controlled by a central entity that facilitates the buying and selling of cryptocurrencies. This entity operates as a third-party intermediary, managing all aspects of the trading process including user accounts transactions and data storage. Well-known examples of these exchanges include platforms such as Coinbase, Archax, Binance, Crypto.com, Upbit and Kraken. The defining operational mechanism of a CEX is the [order book system](https://www.cointracker.io/blog/order-book#:~:text=Centralized%20crypto%20exchanges%20\(CEXs\)%20use,based%20on%20supply%20and%20demand.) \- this digital ledger acts as a real-time record of all buy (bid) and sell (ask) orders for a specific digital asset. When a buyer’s bid aligns with a seller’s ask, a matching engine within the exchange's internal infrastructure automatically executes the trade. This process is conducted off-chain without needing to settle every transaction on a public blockchain, thereby ensuring streamlined and rapid operations. Furthermore, this architectural model is built entirely on the principle of trust. Users must place their faith in the CEX to manage their assets and facilitate trades responsibly. This centralisation allows the CEX to provide a highly efficient and user-friendly experience including features such as customer support and streamlined processes that a decentralised model cannot easily replicate. However, this concentration of power creates a significant security vulnerability: the single point of failure. All user funds are held in [custodial wallets](https://www.ledger.com/th/academy/dex-or-cex-your-exchange-guide) managed by the exchange, meaning users do not possess or control their [private keys](https://www.ledger.com/th/academy/dex-or-cex-your-exchange-guide). This lack of self-sovereignty over their assets means users must rely on the exchange to act in their best interests which, in turn, introduces an inherent counterparty risk.

In stark contrast, a decentralised exchange is a peer-to-peer marketplace that connects cryptocurrency buyers and sellers without any central authority or intermediary. Instead of relying on traditional order books, the dominant model for modern DEXs is the [automated market maker](https://www.gemini.com/cryptopedia/amm-what-are-automated-market-makers) or AMM. The AMM model was a direct architectural innovation developed to solve a critical issue that plagued early decentralised platforms: a pervasive lack of liquidity. AMMs function by using [liquidity pools](https://www.gemini.com/cryptopedia/amm-what-are-automated-market-makers) where users deposit a pair of assets into a [smart contract](https://katten.com/smart-contracts-ruling-forces-a-blockchain-development-rethink). The price of these tokens is determined by a [constant mathematical formula](https://kaironlabs.com/blog/uniswap-and-automated-market-makers-a-basic-explanation) \- such as the popular “x * y = k”. When a trade is initiated, a user swaps one token for another directly with the liquidity pool and the algorithm automatically adjusts the price based on the new ratio of assets within the pool. This model removes the need to find a direct counterparty for every trade. Anyone can become a [liquidity provider](https://support.bitcoin.com/en/articles/6870335-slippage-on-the-verse-dex) by contributing assets to a pool and, in return, a share of the trading fees is earnt, thereby incentivising community participation. This system, pioneered by platforms such as [Uniswap](https://docs.uniswap.org/concepts/uniswap-protocol), embodies the core ethos of blockchain technology: it functions without a central entity and is open for public participation. Furthermore, since all trades are executed on-chain via smart contracts, every transaction is publicly verifiable ensuring a high degree of operational transparency.

**The user journey and market realities**

The architectural differences between CEXs and DEXs translate into two fundamentally different user experiences and market realities. Whilst CEXs prioritise accessibility and liquidity, DEXs offer a different set of advantages and challenges that appeal to a distinct segment of the market. Centralised exchanges are undeniably more accessible and user-friendly, offering an experience that closely resembles traditional financial services. A significant aspect of this accessibility is the provision of crucial “[fiat on-ramps](https://www.mastercard.com/us/en/news-and-trends/stories/2025/what-are-crypto-on-ramps-crypto-off-ramps.html)”, which are services that allow users to convert government-issued money into crypto using familiar methods such as bank transfers and credit cards. This seamless integration with the traditional financial system combined with features such as customer support and a simple user interface makes CEXs an ideal gateway for beginners entering the crypto market. Conversely, DEXs offer a less appealing customer interface requiring a steeper learning curve and a certain level of technical knowledge. To use a DEX, a trader must first acquire a compatible [digital wallet](https://www.tastycrypto.com/blog/cex-vs-dex-comparison/) and is then solely responsible for the secure management of his/her [private keys](https://www.bitpay.com/blog/cex-vs-dex) which act as the access credentials. In addition, the process of understanding and paying for network [gas fees](https://www.tastycrypto.com/blog/cex-vs-dex-comparison/) can be complex and unpredictable. Crucially, should a user make an error such as sending funds to the wrong address, there is no central entity or customer support to assist in recovering those assets. A key consequence of this decentralised design is that DEXs cannot natively offer fiat on-ramps. Since fiat currency is managed by a centralised banking system, the conversion process requires a trusted intermediary to handle the transaction. This creates a fundamental barrier for DEXs and establishes a symbiotic relationship where many users must first interact with a CEX to convert their fiat into crypto before migrating to a DEX for more specialised trading.

For years, centralised exchanges have maintained a dominant lead in trading volume. In 2024, for example, CEXs processed a total [spot trading volume of $14.3 trillion.](https://oakresearch.io/en/reports/sectors/cex-vs-dex-report-2024-forecasts-2025) However, recent market data suggests a profound shift in momentum. The landscape is evolving with a notable resurgence in DEX activity. In early 2024, the DEX-to-CEX spot trading volume ratio was approximately [9.3% but it surged to over 20% by January 2025](https://cointelegraph.com/news/dex-volumes-hit-record-q2-2025-pancakeswap-hyperliquid-lead), so marking a historic first. This upward trend continued into the second quarter of 2025, with the ratio reaching a new record high of 0.23. The trading volume for decentralised perpetual protocols also [doubled from 2023 to 2024](https://www.coingecko.com/research/publications/state-of-crypto-perpetuals-2024), showing that this trend is not limited to spot markets. This marked resurgence is a direct consequence of a shift in market sentiment driven by two key factors; first, the intensification of regulatory efforts by bodies such as the [US or SEC has eroded user trust and prompted many to seek decentralised alternatives](https://www.dfinsolutions.com/knowledge-hub/thought-leadership/knowledge-resources/sec-cryptocurrency-regulations); second, the rise of narrative-driven innovations such as the success of memecoins on networks (e.g. [Solana](https://oakresearch.io/en/reports/sectors/cex-vs-dex-report-2024-forecasts-2025)) and the revival of [decentralised finance](https://www.investopedia.com/decentralized-finance-defi-5113835) (DeFi) on Ethereum, funnelled massive trading volumes directly into the decentralised ecosystem. This suggests that DEXs are becoming the primary platforms for speculative and on-chain native trading, whilst CEXs remain the preferred gateway for the broader more traditional crypto market.

As we have seen,**** the digital asset exchange landscape is defined by a deep philosophical and design divide. CEXs offer high liquidity, fiat access, advanced features and a user-friendly experience, but require user trust, create custodial risks and face tight regulatory scrutiny. By contrast, DEXs are trust less, transparent and non-custodial, whereby enabling permissionless peer-to-peer trading and wide token access, but presenting a steeper learning curve, variable liquidity and higher user responsibility for key management and error risks. This is not merely a technology choice, it is a choice about risk, sovereignty and how we want the future of finance to be governed.
