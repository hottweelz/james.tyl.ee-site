# How blockchain-enabled digital assets are transforming supply chains and trade finance

*By Jonny Fry | *

For centuries, trade has been shaped by two immutable realities: goods move slowly and money moves even slower. Cargo ships still spend weeks crossing oceans, invoices still sit in processing queues and the paperwork that accompanies an international shipment can fill an entire binder. Meanwhile, payment systems, especially for cross-border transactions, can lag behind the speed of modern commerce by days or even weeks therefore creating costly delays and liquidity constraints for businesses. However, a technological shift is now underway that promises to synchronise the movement of goods with the movement of value. It is called the [internet of value](https://ripple.com/insights/the-internet-of-value-what-it-means-and-how-it-benefits-everyone/) and is emerging at the intersection of blockchain technology, digital money (including stablecoins and [tokenised deposits](https://blogs.infosys.com/emerging-technology-solutions/blockchain-01/tokenized-deposit-a-game-changer-in-the-financial-industry.html)) and the tokenisation of real-world assets. Very similar to how the early internet connected information instantly across the globe, the internet of value is enabling the seamless transfer of assets - whether physical goods, digital titles or currency and without the friction, opacity and latency that have long been hallmarks of global trade.

One of the most significant breakthroughs lies in supply chain transparency. A product’s journey, from raw materials to the consumer’s hands, has traditionally been obscured by fragmented records, siloed systems and trust gaps between parties. But blockchain’s immutable ledger changes that dynamic. Imagine a coffee bean harvested in Colombia - from the moment it is weighed and bagged, a blockchain record is created whereby linking its unique identifier to key data points: farm of origin, harvest date, sustainability certifications, etc. As it moves through processing facilities, exporters, shippers, customs inspections and retail distribution, each handoff is logged on the same shared ledger. The result is a single source of truth accessible to all authorised participants farmers, exporters, logistics providers, wholesalers, retailers, regulators, and even the end consumer scanning a QR code on the packaging. This transparency reduces disputes, deters fraud and allows rapid pinpointing of problems such as identifying the origin of contamination in a food recall. More importantly, when combined with digital money, these records become actionable. A logistics company can receive stablecoin payment automatically the moment a shipment’s arrival is verified on the blockchain, without waiting weeks for bank processing. Furthermore, tokenised deposits issued by regulated banks can be embedded into these systems whereby allowing instant settlement in fiat-backed digital form within trusted, compliant frameworks.

It is well-known that the pharmaceutical and luxury goods industries face counterfeiting - i.e. holograms and RFID tags can be copied or circumvented. But blockchain-powered platforms make provenance tracking much better; an unchangeable ledger records each stage of a product's existence, making counterfeits with a bad chain of custody readily traceable. For instance, a high-end watch tokenised at production has a blockchain-linked digital “birth certificate”. A buyer may instantly check the watch's digital record and ownership transfer at resale with these authenticity checks enabling safe, validated payments using stablecoins or tokenised deposits. In addition, smart contracts can hold funds until the blockchain verifies provenance, completing the trust loop between products and payment. Moreover, blockchain integration in supply chains improves efficiency (less glamorous, but equally important) beyond transparency. Documents, including bills of lading, letters of credit and customs declarations, are still manually processed for international shipping. These processes can be automated via smart contracts which automatically disburse stablecoin or tokenised deposit payment once a package clears customs and [IoT sensors verify its condition.](https://www.coursera.org/articles/internet-of-things) Significant as well is that temperature differences for perishable items can activate pre-set dispute resolution stages without human participation, and automating warehouses for payment clearing decreases idle time, administrative costs and corporate working capital. Therefore, cutting days off the payment cycle undoubtedly gives consumer electronics and fashion companies, for example, a competitive edge.

Meanwhile, trade finance follows money as supply chains follow products. In supporting $trillions in worldwide trade, this industry has remained paper-dependent, complicated and sluggish to modernise. Letters of credit, documentary collections and invoice factoring need considerable verification, multi-party coordination and financial intermediaries. Fund release might take weeks or months, in turn, locking up exporters' and importers' cash. However, digital assets enabled by blockchains are changing conventional regulations. A shared ledger may track a shipment's departure, customs clearance and delivery in real time. Triggers can automatically release digital payments so that, instead of multi-day correspondent banking, stablecoins provide rapid cross-border transactions. In addition, since they are tied to stable currencies such as the US dollar or euro, they avoid cryptocurrency volatility whilst maintaining blockchain's programmability and speed. Furthermore, directly issued tokenised deposits by commercial banks incorporate digital settlement capacity into regulated banking relationships. Traditional trade finance intermediaries minimise risk by ensuring items are as stated and payments are safe and blockchain's shared ledger and digital asset integration can provide such assurances faster. Smart contracts can release payments only when independent verifiers post inspection reports to the blockchain and meet parameters. Hence, [tokenised funds are safeguarded](https://tokeny.com/tokenized-funds/) and cannot be diverted before settlement whereby reducing counterparty risk. Essentially, these systems may incorporate compliance -wallet or account-level KYC and AML checks ensure that all parties in the digital transaction chain fulfil regulatory standards.

Meanwhile, the same technology that powers digital money may change trade finance by tokenising real-world assets. Before delivery, blockchain tokens can represent commodities such as copper, wheat and crude oil for fractional ownership, collateralisation and secondary market trading. A coffee exporter may tokenise the shipment's worth to acquire short-term finance whilst the commodities are at sea and stablecoins or tokenised deposits can be issued against this collateral for quick liquidity. Tokenisation allows illiquid assets to be traded - a warehouse's industrial pieces might be fractionalised into tokens and sold to various investors, each receiving a cut of revenues when sold or used in production. Capital flows more freely for global trade actors; blockchain-enabled digital assets are most powerful when supply chain transparency and trade finance automation are combined. Consider a German pharmaceutical business sending temperature-sensitive vaccinations to Kenya. IoT sensors track package temperature, location and handling and the manufacturer, logistics suppliers, customs officials and Kenyan health ministry may access a blockchain with all data points. Furthermore, the Kenyan government's tokenised deposit payment is escrowed by a licensed bank on the same ledger under a smart contract for trade financing. The contract updates upon cargo departure, arrival in Nairobi and customs clearance and the contract pays immediately when the last IoT sensor verifies the vaccinations have arrived inside the necessary temperature range. From shipment to settlement, there is no manual paperwork, no payment delays and full transparency. Funding and equipment travel together across borders in near real time.

So, whilst blockchain systems themselves require energy, the efficiency gains in global trade can offset much of this impact. According to the International Chamber of Commerce and [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2023-10-03/-25-trillion-global-cargo-trade-relies-on-4-billion-paper-bills): _“Global trade still relies heavily on paper documents, with an estimated 4 billion paper-based documents circulating daily within the international cargo trade, which is worth $25 trillion.”_ Without doubt, reducing paperwork, eliminating redundant transport of documents and minimising idle time for goods all contribute to lowering the carbon footprint of trade. Moreover, tokenisation can encourage more sustainable practices - for example, carbon credits tied to supply chain activities can be tokenised, enabling companies to offset emissions automatically as goods move through the chain. Payments for sustainable sourcing such as premiums for verified fair-trade materials can be embedded into smart contracts, ensuring that ethical suppliers are rewarded immediately. However, the transition to a blockchain-enabled internet of value is not without obstacles. Legacy systems and regulatory fragmentation can slow adoption and integration between permissioned and public blockchains must be seamless to allow secure movement of both goods records and payments. Questions of governance also loom large: who controls the shared ledgers, how are disputes resolved and what happens if smart contracts fail? The answers will necessitate collaboration between technology providers, financial institutions, regulators and the industries they serve.

In essence, the combination of blockchain-based supply chain records, digital money in the form of stablecoins and tokenised deposits and the tokenisation of real-world assets is reshaping the very mechanics of global trade. It is reducing friction, increasing trust and creating a tighter synchronisation between the movement of goods and the movement of money. The vision of the internet of value is not simply about speed - it is about reliability, transparency and inclusivity. By digitising and automating the flows of commerce, blockchain-enabled systems can extend trade finance to smaller businesses, increase __ accountability in supply chains and unlock liquidity from assets once trapped in complex paper processes. The transformation will be gradual and uneven, but its trajectory is clear. Just as the internet redefined communication, the internet of value will redefine trade, not by replacing goods with data, but by ensuring that goods and value move together, securely and at the speed of trust.
