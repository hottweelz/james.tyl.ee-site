# From retail to real-world: is the UK ready for the next wave of crypto adoption and tokenisation?

*By Jonny Fry | *

A recent[ Cointelegraph article](https://cointelegraph.com/news/uk-crypto-regulation-lags-behind-mica-genius-act) noted that the UK is _“lagging behind”_ the EU and US in crypto rules. The EU’s Markets in Crypto-Assets regulation (MiCA) is in force, whilst the US has passed the GENIUS Act in the Senate house and is aiming to have it approved in the house of Representatives by August. In contrast, the UK is still finalising its regulatory framework and running consultative papers. Yet beyond this delay lies a more strategic question: is the UK market-ready to lead the next wave of digital asset innovation particularly the institutional tokenisation of [real-world assets (RWAs)](https://chain.link/education-hub/real-world-assets-rwas-explained)? Certainly, the UK’s crypto base is expanding as, according to[ FCA research](https://www.fca.org.uk/publication/research-notes/cryptoasset-consumer-research-2024-wave-5.pdf), 93% of UK adults are aware of crypto and around 12% own these new types of assets. This figure is equivalent to 7 million people, an increase from a mere 4% in 2021. Furthermore, adoption is being fuelled by both long-term savers and first-time investors: in 2024, 26% of holders used long-term savings to invest whilst 14% used credit facilities. Yet risks remain - many investors rely on informal advice with only 30% conducting their own research and half depending on friends. Moreover, the FCA warns that most cryptoassets remain unregulated and lack consumer protections; with this in mind, [26% of non-crypto owners](https://www.fca.org.uk/publication/research-notes/cryptoasset-consumer-research-2024-wave-5.pdf) say they would consider investing if stronger regulation were introduced.

Meanwhile, institutional tokenisation is gathering global pace. [CoinDesk](https://www.coindesk.com/business/2025/06/26/real-world-asset-tokenization-market-has-grown-almost-fivefold-in-3-years) reports that, as of mid-2025, the tokenised RWA market reached $24 billion - nearly a fivefold increase in three years.[ Standard Chartered](https://www.sc.com/en/press-release/trade-finance-to-play-substantial-role-in-usd-30-1-trillion-tokenised-real-world-assets-market-by-2034/) projects tokenised assets could reach $30 trillion by 2034 and this growth is not speculative;[ BlackRock’s $2.9 billion tokenised Treasury fund](https://www.coindesk.com/markets/2025/06/18/blackrocks-29b-tokenized-treasury-fund-now-accepted-as-collateral-on-cryptocom-deribit) is now accepted as collateral on platforms such as Crypto.com and Deribit; [Apollo’s tokenised private credit fund](https://www.crowdfundinsider.com/2025/06/243388-tokenized-private-credit-hits-100m-milestone-with-apollo-and-securitize/) has reached $100 million in on-chain assets; and [Tether (USDT)](https://www.gate.com/learn/articles/top-10-stablecoins-by-market-capitalization-a-comprehensive-overview/131043), the largest stablecoin, has a circulating supply of over $146 billion - representing a 46% year-on-year increase. And, according to[ Deloitte](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/about/press-room/deloitte-releases-2025-financial-services-industry-predictions-report.html), tokenised platforms could reduce cross-border payment costs by as much as $50 billion by 2030. Of note - the UK has taken structural steps toward tokenisation. In January 2024, the FCA and Bank of England launched the[ Digital Securities Sandbox (DSS)](https://www.fca.org.uk/firms/innovation/digital-securities-sandbox) which aims to position the UK as a digital finance hub under the [Financial Services and Markets Act 2023](https://www.hoganlovells.com/en/publications/uk-digital-securities-sandbox-for-financial-market-infrastructures) (Digital Securities Sandbox Regulations 2023). The sandbox provides a five-year regulatory regime, allowing live issuance, trading and settlement of tokenised assets on distributed ledgers within tailored regulatory guardrails. The initiative endeavours to place the UK as a digital finance hub by fostering innovation, efficiency and market integrity. However, as of mid-2025, no public data on sandbox participants or outcomes has been released and this lack of visibility contrasts with strong frameworks. In April 2025, HM Treasury announced a[ US-UK digital asset working group](https://www.gov.uk/government/news/new-cryptoasset-rules-to-drive-growth-and-protect-consumers), including plans for a transatlantic digital securities sandbox. This complements the government’s “Plan for Change” - a forthcoming competitiveness strategy promising support for crypto innovation. But despite these ambitions, practical outcomes lag behind peers. In Hong Kong, the [HKMA’s Project Ensemble Sandbox](https://www.ledgerinsights.com/hsbc-stanchart-bank-of-china-hk-share-plans-for-hk-tokenized-deposit-sandbox/), backed by banks such as HSBC and Standard Chartered, is actively trialling tokenised bonds and deposits. Meanwhile, [Singapore’s Project Guardian](https://www.mas.gov.sg/schemes-and-initiatives/project-guardian) has launched pilots for tokenised funds, FX swaps and securities via collaborations with UBS, Citi, Fidelity, SBI Digital Markets and others. In the UAE, the [VARA (Virtual Assets Regulatory Authority)](https://www.pwc.com/m1/en/publications/uae-virtual-assets-market.html) which was established in early 2022, serves as a dedicated virtual asset regulator, granting licenses, promoting commercial rollouts and even launching metaverse initiatives

Certainly, encouraging signs exist. In the UK, the[ Regulated Liability Network (RLN)](https://www.ukfinance.org.uk/regulated-liability-network) brings together eleven major banks, including Barclays, NatWest and HSBC - all testing tokenised deposits and DLT-based settlements. In addition, technology providers,[ Quant and R3](https://quant.network/press-releases/r3-and-quant-support-the-publication-of-uk-finances-uk-regulated-liability-network-experimentation-phase-report/), are supporting the initiative with interoperability and orchestration infrastructure - the project explores multi-asset settlement, digital bonds and payment-versus-payment capabilities on a shared ledger. Crypto-native firms are also making significant strides in the UK institutional space - the [FCA has licensed Bitpanda](https://blog.bitpanda.com/en/bitpanda-secures-fca-approval-uk-expansion) to offer institutional trading and custody services through its fully FCA-registered Bitpanda Custody arm. Meanwhile, [Zodia Custody](https://www.sc.com/en/press-release/standard-chartered-and-northern-trust-announce-zodia-custody-receives-fca-registration/), backed by Standard Chartered and Northern Trust, has received FCA registration and provides institutional-grade crypto custody services for clients. Even fund managers are testing the waters. The [Investment Association published a roadmap on fund tokenisation in 2024](https://www.theia.org/sites/default/files/2024-03/Further%20Fund%20Tokenisation%20-%20Achieving%20IF3%20Through%20Collaboration%20%20Mar24.pdf), signalling rising interest in blockchain-native asset vehicles and outlining pilots with regulators for tokenised fund use cases. However, large-scale UK launches remain rare - whilst US and EU peers’ debut billion-dollar products, no major British pension fund or asset manager has yet issued a tokenised product. Domestic momentum is still forming.

Yet despite progress, the UK lacks clear operational dates for its crypto regime - its treatment of stablecoins, neither fully e-money nor securities, confuses issuers. By contrast, MiCA and the GENIUS Act provide defined frameworks and most importantly regulatory guidance that issuers can work within. Indeed, whilst interested in launching their own stablecoins, banks and other UK regulated companies will, and cannot, without this regulatory clarity. And on the subject of banks, a 2025 start-up survey revealed that [50% of UK crypto and FinTech firms](https://www.forbes.com/sites/lawrencewintermeyer/2025/01/16/no-country-for-young-fintechs-the-uks-debanking-of-crypto-blockchain-and-web3/) were rejected when applying for bank accounts, underscoring persistent access barriers for the sector; high compliance burdens and unclear policies contribute to the problem. Moreover, unlike the [United States, which is updating its commercial code (such as UCC Article 12)](https://www.orrick.com/en/Insights/2022/10/The-Uniform-Commercial-Code-and-Digital-Assets-What-You-Need-to-Know) to recognise digital assets as controllable electronic records, the UK has not yet enacted a dedicated legal framework. Whilst UCC Article 12 enables clear rules on ownership, collateral and secured transactions, the UK continues to rely on proposed legislation and common law precedents - this leaves unresolved questions about digital asset ownership rights, enforceability and use as collateral. Furthermore, with Hong Kong, the UAE and the EU already onboarding institutional tokenisation at scale, the UK risks losing its first-mover advantage - jurisdictions with dedicated regulators or clearer laws are capturing market share.

In essence, the UK has all the ingredients to lead in tokenised finance: an engaged consumer base, a globally significant financial sector and the home of English and Welsh law with its highly respected and adopted globally and supportive policy initiatives. But it is the execution of these policies, not just their ambition, that will determine whether the UK captures the next wave of innovation.**** Tokenisation is no longer hypothetical - it is already reshaping financial markets. If the UK can bridge the gap between experimentation and full-scale adoption, it has a real opportunity to define the future of finance. If not, it risks ceding leadership to faster-moving jurisdictions.
