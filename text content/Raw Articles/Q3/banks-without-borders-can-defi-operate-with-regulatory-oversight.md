# Banks without borders: can DeFi operate with regulatory oversight? -

*By Jonny Fry | *

In a world where financial inclusion, autonomy and digital innovation are reshaping the global economy, decentralised finance (DeFi) has emerged as one of the most compelling and controversial forces of change. Born out of blockchain technology, DeFi challenges the centuries-old dominance of traditional banking institutions by enabling borderless, permissionless access to financial services. But can such a system thrive under the watchful eye of regulators? And how might Bitcoin-backed banking systems offer a middle ground between freedom and oversight?

DeFi refers to a suite of blockchain-based protocols that provide services such as lending, borrowing, trading and saving without intermediaries. But, unlike traditional banks that operate under strict licensing and jurisdictional constraints, DeFi platforms are inherently global and digital. Powered by smart contracts on public blockchains such as Ethereum and increasingly Bitcoin Layer-2 solutions, these blockchain-powered platforms operate 24/7, are non-custodial and offer transparency down to the code. With over [$114 billion in total value locked (TVL)](https://defillama.com/) the DeFi ecosystem has become more than merely a tech experiment. Projects such as [Aave](https://aave.com/), [Compound](https://compound.finance/) and [Uniswap](https://uniswap.org/) now rival traditional institutions in function, if not in scale, yet, the very features that make DeFi revolutionary (decentralisation, anonymity and immutability) also make it a regulatory puzzle. And whilst Ethereum dominates DeFi infrastructure, Bitcoin is not standing idle. The rise of Bitcoin-backed banking introduces another paradigm: asset-backed and transparent systems built on Bitcoin’s unmatched security and scarcity. However, unlike fiat currencies subject to inflation and policy manipulation, Bitcoin represents a non-sovereign, digitally scarce reserve asset. Platforms including [Ledn](https://ledn.io/) and [Unchained](https://unchained.com/) are pioneering Bitcoin-collateralised financial services - that is, offering interest accounts, collateralised loans and even hybrid banking services. Others, such as Xapo, are fully licensed institutions integrating traditional bank rails with Bitcoin-native infrastructure. Significantly, this convergence between DeFi principles and regulatory compliance represents a promising frontier: borderless banking with regulatory grounding.

However, governments and regulators are understandably wary of financial systems that operate outside their control. After all, DeFi protocols can facilitate money laundering, tax evasion and capital flight if left unchecked. But the question is not _whether_ DeFi should be regulated, it is _how_. In 2023, the FATF and OECD both released guidance on applying anti-money laundering (AML) and know your customer (KYC) rules to virtual asset service providers (VASPs). But applying such rules to code rather than corporations is an uphill battle. Consider decentralised exchanges (DEXs): who is liable if a smart contract facilitates illegal activity? The developer? The user? The DAO? And whilst there are questions, what is for certain - as the [Law Society](https://www.lawsociety.org.uk/topics/anti-money-laundering/suspicious-activity-reports) reminds us in the UK - is that individual and organisations (regulated or not) are required to report suspicious activity when it comes to potential money laundering concerns. Interestingly, other countries take different stances; the [EU’s MiCA regulation](https://www.merklescience.com/blog/is-defi-truly-exempt-from-mica-regulations#:~:text=What%20Does%20%E2%80%9CFully%20Decentralized%E2%80%9D%20Mean,constitutes%20a%20%22decentralized%20manner.%22) provides a clear legal framework for centralised crypto entities, but largely skirts full DeFi decentralisation; the US SEC continues to assert jurisdiction over DeFi platforms that resemble securities exchanges (with lawsuits and subpoenas increasing); meanwhile, jurisdictions such as Switzerland, Singapore and Dubai are exploring regulatory sandboxes to integrate DeFi within their systems, instead of stifling it.

To imagine DeFi operating with regulatory oversight, one must redefine how oversight itself works. Rather than relying on centralised enforcement, regulators could adopt regulatory nodes that monitor blockchain activity in real-time whereby identifying suspicious behaviour using AI and on-chain analytics. Protocols could implement opt-in compliance layers zero-knowledge proofs (ZKPs) - for instance, verify compliance without revealing personal private data. Projects such as [Chainalysis](https://www.chainalysis.com/), [TRM Labs](https://www.trmlabs.com/) and [CipherTrace](https://ciphertrace.com/) are already enabling this surveillance style model where transparency does not mean loss of privacy and compliance does not require intermediaries. Furthermore, regulated DeFi (sometimes called “RegFi”) is gaining traction. Protocols such as [Aave Arc](https://cointelegraph.com/news/aave-launches-its-permissioned-pool-aave-arc-with-30-institutions-set-to-join) are working exclusively with whitelisted institutions, merging DeFi’s efficiency with institutional standards. DeFi platforms do not merely challenge banks, they challenge the monopoly over the potential creation of money and payments, highlighting the tricky question of financial sovereignty vs. state power**.** Governments derive power, not only from law enforcement or taxation but from their control over monetary policy. By offering parallel financial systems that bypass capital controls, DeFi weakens the state's ability to enforce currency dominance especially in countries with hyperinflation, capital restrictions or political turmoil.**** This is why demand for decentralised assets is highest in nations such as Argentina, Turkey, Nigeria and Lebanon. DeFi, in such cases, is not about speculation - it is about survival. Bitcoin-backed banks such as [Galoy’s Stablesats](https://stablesats.com/) even offer synthetic dollar stability using BTC derivatives, giving users dollar exposure without holding USD or relying on traditional banks.

Meanwhile, to compete with DeFi and Bitcoin-based systems, governments are launching their own digital alternatives. Central bank digital currencies (CBDCs) - currently being piloted in over 100 countries - offer state-controlled digital cash with programmable features. But CBDCs raise concerns regarding privacy, censorship and surveillance. Privately issued stablecoins such as USDC and USDT remain crucial to DeFi’s infrastructure, acting as the de facto bridge between crypto and fiat. However, these tokens are centralised and dependent on those traditional banks vulnerable to both regulatory pressure and counterparty risk. In contrast, decentralised stablecoins such as [DAI](https://makerdao.com/en/) and new entrants such as [Ethena’s USDe](https://www.ethena.fi/) aim to maintain US$ parity without holding dollars. But, their resilience is constantly tested, especially during market volatility. However, the long-standing narrative of DeFi versus regulators is gradually shifting; instead of debating whether to permit decentralised finance, forward-looking jurisdictions are now exploring how to effectively co-exist with it. This change reflects a growing recognition that DeFi is not a passing trend, but a transformative force in the financial landscape. And, as this evolution unfolds, regulatory frameworks are expected to adapt in innovative ways.

One potential direction is the introduction of DeFi protocol licensing which would require developers or DAOs to register for compliance once their platforms reach certain usage thresholds. Alongside this, smart contract-level compliance could become standard, incorporating on-chain modules such as AML whitelists and sanctions filters directly into DeFi protocols. Collaborative efforts between regulators and blockchain analytics firms may also play a critical role in identifying and mitigating systemic risks through public-private monitoring models. Additionally, decentralised digital IDs could be used for purposes ranging from KYC and tax reporting to governance and voting within DAOs. This push toward integration is further reinforced by the rise of DeFi banking systems. These models blend the cryptographic security of blockchain technology with the practical needs of the real economy, so demonstrating that decentralisation and regulation are not inherently opposed but can, in fact, complement each other in building a more resilient financial system. DeFi and Bitcoin-backed banks are not simply alternative financial systems - they potentially represent an evolution of money itself. Hence, whilst the road ahead is filled with regulatory uncertainty and philosophical tension, it is clear that finance is no longer confined to physical branches, national borders or banking hours. Whether through open-source protocols or cryptographic reserves, these new systems are forcing a global conversation: not merely about how we regulate finance but who controls it, and why.

So, as regulation adapts and infrastructure matures, a hybrid model could emerge where the core principles of decentralisation coexist with practical frameworks of accountability. In such a world, the question will not be if DeFi and Bitcoin-backed banks can operate under oversight but how far they can go in reshaping the financial world as we know it.
