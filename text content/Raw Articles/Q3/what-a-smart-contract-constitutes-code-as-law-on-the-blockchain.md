# What a smart contract constitutes: code as law on the blockchain

*By Jonny Fry | *

Blockchain technology has emerged as one of the most revolutionary innovations in digital space that enables transparency, immutability and security in the process of recording and authenticating transactions. One of its most disruptive uses is the principle of the smart contract, which is programmed to execute agreements automating business logic and implements commercial rules without the use of intermediaries. This radicalisation has redefined the process of formation and execution of agreements in all kinds of industries, with the belief of the [principle of “code as law”.](https://ethereumclassic.org/why-classic/code-is-law/)

A smart contract is a computer code that dwells in a blockchain and automatically takes effect in an automatic manner when a set of specific conditions are completed. Such digital contracts operate similar to a flow chart - “if this happens, then do this….. if that happens, do another thing” - all pre-agreed and transparent for all parties to examine. After being deployed on a decentralised blockchain-powered platform, a smart contract can be executed 24/7 with no manual intervention. The main advantage of a smart contract is its independence; compared to traditional contracts, where code must be implemented and enforced manually or through third-party actions, smart contracts are purely code-based. They are[ autonomous](https://docs.massa.net/docs/learn/asc/intro), [non-repudiable](https://www.neumetric.com/non-repudiation-digital-transactions/), [unforgeable and trackable](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/smart-contracts.asp) by all parties within the network. Smart contracts help to achieve higher efficiency and develop a level of trust among the anonymous or remote parties by eliminating any dependence on centralised organisations. Instead, the programs are encoded in blockchain proprietary languages e.g. [solidity on the Ethereum blockchain](https://soliditylang.org/). They stand at the base of a variety of blockchain-based applications including decentralised finance (DeFI), non-fungible tokens (NFTs), CBDCs, stablecoins and even supply chain applications.

Of significance is that smart contracts possess unique properties which render them suitable for digital and decentralised space. Being fundamentally different to traditional contracts in several aspects, the most outstanding difference with smart contracts is automation. [Smart contracts](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/smart-contracts.asp) can be fulfilled automatically when events occur, whereas conventional contracts cannot be implemented automatically and the interventions of third parties are necessary - this disparity has a direct effect on efficiency and speed of execution. Another distinguishing element is transparency. Under conventional contracts, conflicts can arise as the different parties involved may understand some clauses differently. The two are also different in terms of [immutability](https://crypto.com/glossary/immutability#:~:text=immutability%20means%20that%20once%20data,data%20stored%20on%20the%20blockchain.). Traditional contracts can be edited, amended and even forged in some cases. However, in contrast, smart contracts, when created, cannot be changed - this renders them safe yet not flexible. Transparency is one of their defining traits, and decentralisation enhances their reliability further. Running distributed networks implies that no entity will have control over the execution process whereby mitigating the risk of manipulation or censorship. In addition, blockchain provides cryptographic protection and thus the results of the contracts cannot be tampered easily. Other significant features include the element of cost. Here the difference lies in the preferences. Conventional contracts have been associated with high overhead costs and legal fees whereas smart contracts result in saving money through automating processes and execution of a transaction and, in many instances, the removal of intermediaries. Furthermore, smart contracts reduce the expense of contract enforcement since they bypass intermediaries such as brokers, lawyers or escrow agents.

Nonetheless, obstacles still remain. The major difficulty lies in the technical complexities required in the designing, testing and deployment of smart contracts. Even minor bugs in the code may have serious implications and a smart contract is hard to change after implementation; this rigidity is problematic in a dynamic business environment where there is often the need to adjust the contracts. Legal and regulatory uncertainty is also a constraint.[ A legal framework ](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/389875866_Regulatory_Uncertainty_and_Smart_Contracts_A_Legal_Analysis_of_Corporate_Governance_Compliance)of enforcing smart contracts is not yet very clear in most jurisdictions and this ambiguity poses concerns regarding the method by which disputes are to be dealt with once some real-life problems arise or some stalemate emerges. Security is also a major concern as [defects in the code ](https://www.lcx.com/smart-contracts-security-challenges-explained/)can give way to intruders. The 2016 hack of the DAO and the $60 million of Ether stolen has raised concerns on how code audits are done, therefore, in order to counter this, developers will most frequently resort to intensive security audits and testing. Another problem is the usage of oracles, i.e. external sources of data which feeds systematic contracts. [Oracles](https://chain.link/education/blockchain-oracles) can be used to bridge the gap between off-chain and blockchain data because blockchains have no means of reading off-chain data by themselves. However, they also present centralisation risks since a compromised oracle may result in false data that may cause unintended contract execution - attempts to solve this problem include decentralised oracle networks.

Meanwhile, the application of smart contracts is wide-ranging, including in:

> · **finance** \- they are utilised within [escrow payments](https://101blockchains.com/escrow-smart-contract/) \- that is, money can only be released when particular conditions are fulfilled. The NFT marketplace makes direct transfer of ownership between the artists and the buyers possible via smart contracts which facilitates the subsequent payment of royalties to the creator on an on-going basis.
> 
> · **the insurance sector** \- smart contracts can advance the insurance activities by [automating the releases](https://legittai.com/blog/smart-contracts-in-insurance) in the event of completion of conditions. This minimises any disputes and ensures faster settlements. The advantage is felt in the supply chain management such that smart contracts monitor goods within the chain and automatically impose term compliance in delivery.
> 
> · **voting systems** \- represent another promising use case. Smart contracts are able to authorise the voting system to be secure, transparent and tamper-proof which can make the process by elections more reliable.
> 
> · **the gaming sector** \- smart contracts are utilised in the development and trade of digital assets, which automate decentralised economies across online platforms.

In many countries, the use of [blockchain and smart contracts](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s43545-024-01044-y#:~:text=Another%20challenge%20in%20cross%2Dborder,disputes%20compared%20to%20traditional%20litigation.) continues to lack legal and regulatory clarity whereby raising questions on enforceability and consumer protection - such ambiguities will inhibit faster uptake by enterprises and governments. Nonetheless, the future of smart contracts points toward enhanced integration with emerging technologies and research is underway to use[ artificial intelligence (AI) ](https://www.damcogroup.com/blogs/smart-contracts-in-real-estate#:~:text=One%20of%20the%20most%20exciting,transactions%20faster%20and%20more%20efficient.)to make smart contracts more receptive and capable of handling more complex situations in decision-making. Certainly, this overlap might allow them to increase utility in more dynamic implementations than mere conditional logic. Work is also being undertaken on [cross chain interoperability](https://chain.link/education-hub/cross-chain-smart-contracts) \- most smart contracts are currently isolated within their blockchain ecosystems which is a challenge, given there are [hundreds of blockchains in existence](https://ownrwallet.com/blog/how-many-blockchains-are-there-2024-ownr-wallet/). Mitigation of the possibility to communicate with different blockchains may open an era of cross-blockchain decentralised applications and therefore increase the number of businesses that can use them. On the regulation front, the world community is in discussion regarding global standards of recognition and enforcement of smart contracts. Clearing up legal frameworks would also allow giving due tenure to immutability of code against the backdrop of traditional legal principles of fairness and consumer rights.

Smart contracts are often heralded as the purest expression of blockchain’s promise, i.e. agreements that enforce themselves - immune to human bias, delay or manipulation. They take the ancient idea of contract law and strip it down to machine logic (“if this…. then that”) executed autonomously across a decentralised ledger. This shift is radical because it redefines trust: no longer vested in courts, lawyers or banks, but in code itself. Yet this very strength reveals the contracts’ fragility - code may be incorruptible, but it is not infallible. The DAO hack showed how a single vulnerability can cascade into multimillion-dollar losses, with no judge to appeal to and no safety net. Oracles, designed to bridge blockchains with real-world data, paradoxically reintroduce centralisation risks that smart contracts were meant to escape. Meanwhile, the law struggles to catch up, therefore can immutable code co-exist with centuries-old principles of fairness, equity and consumer protection? But despite these tensions, the trajectory is clear. Smart contracts are creeping into finance, insurance, supply chains and even governance whereby turning infrastructure, once dependent on paperwork, into automated logic. The true question is not whether smart contracts will spread, but whether societies can build the regulatory and ethical scaffolding to ensure that “code as law” serves humanity, rather than the other way around.
