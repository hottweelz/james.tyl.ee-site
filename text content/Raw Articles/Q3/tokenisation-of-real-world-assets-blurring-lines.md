# Tokenisation of real-world assets: Blurring Lines

*By Jonny Fry | *

For centuries, real-world assets and financial market representations were clearly distinguished. Deeds represented land. Certificates represented equity. A receipt proved ownership, even debt instruments such as bonds had coupons that were clipped once paid. Finance has historically relied on trusted brokers to evaluate, transmit, and enforce claims to physical assets. Today, that clarity is fading as we face a myriad of intermediaries clearing houses, custodians, administrators, lawyers and auditors all checking that transactions happen and who owes what. No wonder Delivery V Payment seems so elusive as all these various parties need to balance their books and signoff before funds are released and securities transferred.

Real World Assets ([RWAs](https://chain.link/education-hub/real-world-assets-rwas-explained)) such as real estate, stocks, bonds, art, and even agricultural produce are being digitally mirrored and in some cases, completely subsumed by programmable tokens on decentralised networks thanks to blockchain and Web3.0 technologies. Tokenisation enables the creation of a digital copy of an asset that trade and allow the transfer of value independently in Web3.0 ecosystems. This confluence redefines ownership, value, and trust philosophically and economically. As tokenisation progresses, real and digital become blurred. The asset's worth may eventually be unrelated to its physical form.

Simply put, tokenisation is generating a blockchain-based digital token that symbolizes real-world asset ownership. A real estate token may represent a fraction of a property and be legally linked to a title deed. This concept improves liquidity, offers fractional ownership, can democratise ownership of high value items such as real estate or private equity/debt funds, improve settlement speeds, and worldwide accessibility. Initial tokenisation is strongly tied to its real-world reference. Legal contracts, custodians, and regulations are needed to verify the token's asset representation. Tokenised assets take on life as they circulate, notably in decentralised finance (DeFi) systems 24/7 accessible to all provided they have access to the world wide web. Over time, the market focuses on the token, not the asset. A tokenised real estate can serve as stablecoin collateral, be traded in a liquidity pool, or be packaged into a synthetic product. In effect enabling homeowners to turn their house into a bank and theoretically buy a coffee by selling a tokenised part of their home. These abstractions create economic systems around tokens, not houses. The tokenised property's digital value seems to overshadow its underlying property.

Moving from physical to digital requires education. Traditional asset ownership is enforced by banks, notaries, land registries, and courts. Blockchain protocols, smart contracts, and decentralised networks i.e. code ensures asset transfer and payments are executed or not.

This is no cosmetic swap. Cryptography and transparency build blockchain trust, not discretion. Smart contracts that work properly can trade, enforce agreements, and pay out without people. This makes a token a financial entity with logic, not just an ownership record. A work of art token can automatically pay royalties to the artist when it is first sold and then be programmed so it pays a % of any subsequent sale when it changes hands in the secondary market. Code, not trustees, may enforce and distribute interest-bearing tokenised bonds. Automation cuts costs and friction, but more significantly, it separates the token from institutional enforcement and its real-world anchor. Oracles check real-world data for smart contracts, closing the gap. An oracle may predict gold prices, hotel occupancy, or even the weather triggering payments accordingly. Smart contracts can use this data to modify dividends or rebalance portfolios on-chain 24/7. Smart contracts, blockchains, and oracles are the new financial trust architecture. Tokens can join separate financial networks, eliminating intermediaries.

One of the clearest expressions of this shift is the use of tokenised RWAs as collateral in decentralised finance. DeFi platforms, originally dominated by crypto-native assets like ETH and USDC, are increasingly integrating tokenised RWAs such as real estate equity, invoices, music royalties, and carbon credits. Indeed, traditional organisations such as [EY are using stablecoins from Paxo](https://www.fstech.co.uk/fst/Paypal_uses_us_stablecoin_to_pay_ey_invoice.php) to settle invoices already. This inclusion is more than symbolic. It expands the collateral base of DeFi, stabilising its volatility-prone nature. But it also begins to digitally rematerialise real-world value within Web3.0. Once accepted as collateral, these tokens circulate within purely digital environment used to mint stablecoins, enter liquidity pools, or stake for governance rewards. Over time, their real-world origins fade from view. The token becomes primarily an economic actor in a digital world. Its value is no longer tied to the price of farmland or a container of coffee but to the digital services and protocols it can participate in. These second-order behaviours imbue the token with synthetic value beyond the original asset. In this sense, DeFi is not just integrating RWAs it is recontextualising them, folding them into new logics of liquidity, programmability, and composability. As more RWAs become tokenised and enter these systems, the concept of "real" financial value itself begins to evolve.

Ownership fractionalisation is also available through tokenisation. A tokenised building or picture can have hundreds or thousands of ownership tokens, each representing a little share. This fractionalisation might democratise. Retail investors can enter institutional-only industries like commercial real estate and fine art. Decentralised systems allow 24/7 worldwide buying, selling, and trading of these tokens without intermediaries. Fractionalised tokens often become speculative digital assets without any use or benefit. Owning a [tokenised hotel](https://www.hospitalityinvestor.com/technology/beginners-guide-tokenisation-hospitality-real-estate) gives you the right to speculate on its future worth, not to stay there. These tokens trade on a global, liquid digital marketplace based on demand and market sentiment rather than fundamentals. Sometimes the token's behaviour is unaffected by the actual asset. In a DeFi system, a token's yield may come from its [composability](https://www.contentful.com/blog/what-is-composability/#:~:text=Composability%20refers%20to%20the%20ability,to%20other%20fields%20as%20well.) rather than its asset appreciation. A synthetic asset, the token is digital and financial.

Despite this change, legal infrastructure still links many tokenised assets to their physical roots. Courts, contracts, and custodians must enforce token-realm links. Friction and centralized trust return to decentralised systems. Legal systems are evolving slowly. Switzerland, Singapore, and the UAE recognize tokenised securities and digital property rights. These hybrid solutions incorporate tokens in legally enforceable smart contracts that automatically enforce ownership and compliance. The digital token-physical world reality divide remains a fundamental conflict. What happens if token-linked real estate is destroyed or disputed? Imagine a warehouse full of tokenised gold whose custodian defaults. The fragility of linking immutable digital logic to changing real-world assets is shown by these edge situations. Progressively abstracting the token from the asset may work. These tokens may become synthetic derivatives, valued for their digital activity rather than their real-world foundation. Tokenised assets may become digital-native financial primitives, valued for their function in Web3 finance rather than their nominal worth, such as ETFs or futures.

In parallel, non-fungible tokens (NFTs) have become symbols of intellectual property, innovation, and digital culture. NFTs frequently start digitally, unlike RWAs. A generative art, digital music, or in-game object may not exist physically. The market values these digital artifacts, which may be purchased, traded, lent, and collateralised. Many of their values come from provenance, community, and scarcity, traditionally associated with art and collectibles. RWAs tokenise and NFTs acquire financial usefulness, bringing the two groups closer. Both tokenized land and digital art can be collateral. They can trade in the same liquidity pool. Both can support staking, pay royalties, and represent voting power. This new paradigm prioritises on-chain behaviour above asset origin. The difference disappears, creating a Web3.0-native asset layer.

As we look ahead, “real-world” assets may be outmoded. Digital relationships, computational logic, and network agreement will determine value in fully on-chain economies, not physical scarcity or institutional validation. Synthetic assets, digital tokens that mimic real-world instruments but are backed by other collateral show this tendency. [Synthetix](https://www.synthetix.io/) lets users trade synthetic equities, commodities, and currencies on-chain. Despite being unrelated, these assets act like real ones. Digital asset classes administered by decentralised organisations, driven by user engagement, and made completely of on-chain primitives will develop. These assets will be Web3.0 ecosystem natives, adapted to match its landscape. This is a sovereignty transition, not just financial progress. Nation-states, banks, and courts traditionally define and enforce property. Web3.0 is protocols, communities, and smart contracts. Tokens represent rights, existence, and worth. As more real-world assets are fractionalised, digitised, abstracted, and recomposed, the tangible-virtual line blurs. The new economy is programmable, composable, and digital.

The tokenisation of real-world assets (RWAs) represents a profound shift in how value, ownership, and trust are encoded and exchanged in modern finance. The boundary between the tangible and the digital is blurring. As assets become tokenised and enter decentralised financial systems, their value begins to reflect not their underlying physical form, but their utility within digital protocols, whether as collateral, governance rights, or yield-bearing instruments. This transformation is not just technical it is philosophical. It challenges the notion of what constitutes “real” value when a digital token of a house can be traded, staked, or used to mint a stablecoin in a DeFi ecosystem, all without the buyer ever stepping foot in the property. It forces us to ask whether these tokens still derive their worth from physical reality, or whether they are now economic actors in a fully digital domain, with their value shaped by code, liquidity, and network consensus rather than bricks and mortar.

As smart contracts automate functions once handled by courts, and oracles bridge on-chain logic with off-chain events, the entire apparatus of legal, financial, and institutional enforcement begins to migrate into code. Tokenisation promises unprecedented access, liquidity, and programmability but it also introduces new layers of abstraction and complexity. The very idea of ownership is evolving from a legal entitlement grounded in geography to a programmable right governed by protocols. In this emerging paradigm, the most valuable assets may not be those with the strongest legal claim, but those that are most composable, interoperable, and actively engaged in the digital economy.
