# UK’s global digital finance ambitions needs action not consultations

*By Jonny Fry | *

The UK has made bold declarations to become a global hub for digital finance yet, whilst policymakers have been busy drafting legislation that mirrors the US regulatory model, implementation has been slow. This disconnect between vision and execution risks pushing digital asset providers, investors and fintech innovators toward more markets that already have clearer regulatory guidance, such as the Middle East, Singapore, the EU and the US. In a global race for

regulatory leadership, time may prove more valuable than ambition. The UK government’s crypto ambition is no secret and, in April 2025, [HM Treasury published a draft statutory instrument](https://www.regulationtomorrow.com/eu/hmt-publishes-draft-legislation-for-regulating-cryptoassets/) to formally bring exchanges, wallet providers and stablecoin issuers under the Financial Services and Markets Act (FSMA) regulatory perimeter. The aim is to create a comprehensive, principles-based regime, so aligning with traditional financial regulations and recognising crypto as a financial product class. The draft rules also reflect a strong alignment with the US approach to digital asset regulation, particularly around classifying tokens as securities and placing obligations primarily on domestic issuers. Furthermore, as[ CoinGeek reports](https://coingeek.com/uk-drafts-rules-for-digital-assets-aligns-with-us-approach/), this strategic divergence from the EU’s more centralised MiCA regime suggests the UK is betting on flexibility, legal clarity and long-term competitiveness.

But, this is not simply regulatory posturing; the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) has acknowledged that[ 12% of UK adults now own crypto](https://www.fca.org.uk/news/press-releases/fca-finds-crypto-ownership-continues-rise-it-delivers-plans-regulate-crypto), equivalent to around 7 million people and up from 10% previously. Awareness is near universal at 93% with the[ average holding per investor rising to ~£1,842](https://www.fca.org.uk/publications/research/research-note-cryptoassets-consumer-research-2024) in 2024, reflecting steady growth in retail participation. Moreover,[ Gemini reports](https://cointelegraph.com/news/uk-outpaces-global-crypto-ownership-growth-in-2025-says-gemini) that UK crypto ownership jumped from 18% to 24% in one year - this is the fastest growth rate among major economies. Meanwhile, the [Crypto Roadmap](https://www.fca.org.uk/news/press-releases/fca-finds-crypto-ownership-continues-rise-it-delivers-plans-regulate-crypto) published by the FCA in late 2024 outlines upcoming consultations, future authorisation requirements and a multi-year rollout of the new regime. However, despite a strong regulatory blueprint, execution has lagged. The FCA has yet to set a firm go-live date for the regime, with [final rules not expected until 2026](https://www.lexology.com/library/detail.aspx?g=487b817f-4bb0-4faf-b257-7f7619725783) \- in practice, the market has received little more than consultations, discussion papers and roadmaps. Whilst the government has promised to legislate by the end of 2025, many business leaders say that delays are already costing the UK its first-mover advantage. Industry figures such as CryptoUK’s [CEO, Su Carpenter](https://cointelegraph.com/news/uk-crypto-regulation-lags-behind-mica-genius-act), have stated the UK’s framework is still “ _in its infancy and that the nation lags behind not just the EU and US, but also Dubai._ ” [Zopa CEO, Jaidev Janardana, has also remarked](https://www.pymnts.com/news/regulation/2025/crypto-and-fintech-execs-uk-needs-to-be-more-welcoming-to-industry) that Singapore, Hong Kong and the US are delivering far more agile regulatory frameworks, allowing their fintech ecosystems to thrive. Even supporters of the UK’s approach warn that strategy without execution is damaging confidence. A [June 2025 analysis](https://www.omfif.org/2025/06/uk-keeps-missing-the-boat-on-dlt-finance) by the OMFIF think-tank reported that the UK is “ _missing the boat_ ” on crypto due to persistent policy procrastination, and that regulatory ambiguity is eroding investor trust. So whilst the UK hesitates, the EU has pressed ahead. Its [Markets in Crypto-Assets (MiCA) regulation](https://www.esma.europa.eu/esmas-activities/digital-finance-and-innovation/markets-crypto-assets-regulation-mica) came into force in phases starting June 2024. By January 2025, major crypto-asset service providers were required to hold MiCA licences to continue operating in Europe. And, although only [12 CASPs and 10 e-money issuers](https://coincub.com/ranking/europe-crypto-report-2025/#:~:text=,the%20end%20of%20the%20year) were formally licensed in the early months, regulators anticipate that figure will rise to between 100 and 130 by the end of 2025.

MiCA's rollout has driven strong market activity.[ EU crypto trading volumes rose about 70% in Q1 2025](https://cointelegraph.com/news/crypto-flows-to-europe-under-mica-trump-us-retreat#:~:text=Germany%20leads%20in%20institutional%20infrastructure%2C,weight%20with%20strong%20payment%20connectivity), with greater institutional engagement. France alone saw a[ 175% spike in crypto activity in 2024](https://cointelegraph.com/news/crypto-flows-to-europe-under-mica-trump-us-retreat), and[ 24% of French adults now own crypto](https://www.chainalysis.com/blog/2024-western-europe-crypto-adoption/). Meanwhile, Central, Northern and Western Europe accounted for nearly[ $1 trillion in crypto flows in 2023–24](https://www.chainalysis.com/blog/2024-western-europe-crypto-adoption), with the UK alone representing $217 billion. As a result, companies that are focused on digital assets, such as Coinbase, OKX, Crypto.com and Gemini, have already secured MiCA-compliant registrations, often choosing Luxembourg, Malta or Ireland as their European base. [Coinbase recently announced](https://cointelegraph.com/news/coinbase-secures-mica-license-names-luxembourg-as-eu-headquarters) that it would make Luxembourg its EU headquarters after securing a MiCA licence and hence giving it regulatory passporting rights across all twenty-seven member states. Compared with the UK’s uncertain roadmap, MiCA provides clear rules on disclosures, market abuse, stablecoins and custody requirements. For digital asset businesses making decisions about expansion, MiCA offers what the UK currently does not: regulatory certainty. Meanwhile, the US has also gained momentum. Under its new administration in 2025, the White House issued an executive order creating a Strategic Bitcoin Reserve, and the Senate passed the [GENIUS Act](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/06/17/genius-stablecoin-bill-crypto.html). Therefore, assuming the House of Representatives also passes this act, it will introduce clarity for those companies wishing to issue stablecoins in the US. Meanwhile, the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) and Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC) have slowed enforcement actions, suggesting a more constructive regulatory tone. Certainly this US pivot toward innovation has strengthened the appeal of its markets for digital asset firms and, as [Reuters noted](https://www.reuters.com/sustainability/boards-policy-regulation/uk-sets-out-new-crypto-rules-date-wider-financial-services-strategy-2025-04-29/), although the UK’s draft regime aims to follow the US example, unlike the US, it has yet to operationalise that alignment.

Undoubtedly, these delays are already shaping business decisions. For example, despite its deep roots in the UK, Coinbase is focusing growth efforts on the EU and US markets. Other exchanges such as [Bybit ](https://cointelegraph.com/news/bybit-secures-mica-license-establishes-eu-hq-in-vienna)and[ OKX](https://thepaypers.com/cryptocurrencies/okx-launches-regulated-crypto-exchanges-in-europe--1274205) are rushing to secure EU regulatory authorisations, launching MiCA-compliant platforms to access European markets. Meanwhile, [UK fintech leaders warn](https://www.ainvest.com/news/uk-crypto-regulation-delays-risk-capital-outflow-innovation-lag-2506/) that regulatory delays are driving capital overseas and stifling innovation as the country risks falling behind more agile regimes in the EU and US. Legal advisors warn that asset managers and token issuers are choosing their domicile based on clarity, not sentiment. As one[ legal analysis](https://cointelegraph.com/news/crypto-domicile-eu-uk) notes, the UK may offer a promising framework eventually, but today’s regulatory clarity in the EU is far more bankable. And without a faster rollout, capital flight is a real risk as a director at Ripple noted in a[ PYMNTS interview](https://www.pymnts.com/news/regulation/2025/crypto-and-fintech-execs-uk-needs-to-be-more-welcoming-to-industry/): “ _Other jurisdictions have started to seize the opportunity. The UK needs to move from consultations to conclusions_.” The UK’s crypto draft rules are progressive, aligned with international standards and designed to integrate digital assets into its traditional financial system. But ambition is not a substitute for action. In global financial markets, speed and certainty win. If the UK craves to realise its potential as a global digital asset hub, it must accelerate the implementation timeline, resource the FCA for approvals, (as [85% of applications](https://www.vixio.com/insights/pc-fca-data-shows-85-percent-attempted-crypto-registrations-fail) to be a virtual asset services providers (VASP) fail. Regulation is needed to deliver clear guidance to firms now rather than regulated companies that wish to issue digital payment solutions having to wait. Until then, digital finance firms will continue to build their companies and employ staff in those jurisdictions where the rules are already in existence.
