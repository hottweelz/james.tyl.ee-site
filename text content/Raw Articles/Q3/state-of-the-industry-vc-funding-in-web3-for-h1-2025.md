# State of the industry: VC funding in Web3 for H1 2025

*By Jonny Fry | *

In H1 2025, crypto fundraising experienced a sharp rebound, driven by a wave of large-scale deals. According to CryptoRank, [Q1 2025 pulled in around $4.8 billion](https://cryptorank.io/insights/reports/crypto-fundraising-report-Q1-25) (led by Binance’s massive $2 billion raise), whilst [Q2 surged past $10 billion](https://cryptorank.io/insights/reports/crypto-fundraising-report-Q2-25). Buoyed by late-stage and public-market transactions such as Circle’s IPO, this brought the total for the half-year to roughly $15 billion under [CryptoRank’s](https://cryptorank.io/) broader methodology. Meanwhile, Galaxy Research (focusing strictly on private VC rounds) recorded 446 deals totalling $4.8 billion in [Q1](https://www.galaxy.com/insights/research/crypto-venture-capital-q1-2025#:~:text=In%20Q1%202025%2C%20venture%20capitalists,invested%20%242bn%20into%20Binance.) and 378 deals totalling about $2 billion in [Q2](https://www.galaxy.com/insights/research/crypto-blockchain-venture-capital-q2-2025), which implies approximately 824 pure VC deals and somewhere between $6.8–$7 billion in private venture funding for the half. These figures underscore a dual momentum: a revival of mega-deals in the spotlight and sustained early-stage activity behind the scenes. Not surprisingly, all this fund raising is resulting in Web3 transitioning from experiments and proof of works to actually hiring and scaling as can be seen from the graph below.

Capital flowed into specific sub-sectors with notable intensity and in Q1, infrastructure and CeFi tenders dominated deal volume whilst DeFi remained active by deal count. Q2 saw tokenised real-world assets (RWA) rise sharply, with headline raises such as [Securitize’s $400 million](https://thedefiant.io/news/cefi/securitize-launches-400-million-mantle-index-four-fund-btc-eth-sol-exposure-fund-7f5380c3) drive. Across deal sizes, Q1 featured many sub-$10 million rounds (comprising about two-thirds of deals), along with a handful of up to $50 million deals. By Q2, late-stage involvement deepened: 31 rounds of more than $50 million were recorded, accompanied by Securitize, Kalshi, World Financial and Circle’s IPO. The narrative across H1 is clear: crypto VCs are broadly re-risking early-stage innovation, whilst a new wave of large, revenue-backed late-stage financings is reshaping the fundraising landscape.

In H1 2025, early-stage crypto venture activity remained strong by deal count, especially in Q1, where seed rounds dominated despite headline-grabbing mega-deals such as [MGX’s $2 billion investment in Binance](https://www.binance.com/en-NG/square/post/21537240741545) \- the most active investors in 2025 are shown above. Furthermore, investor appetite at this stage has been fuelled by narratives around regulatory clarity, institutional adoption and compliance-ready infrastructure. New fund announcements, such as [Galaxy Ventures’ planned $180 million fund](https://cointelegraph.com/news/crypto-venture-fund-galaxy-ventures-i-tops-fundraising-goal) targeting blockchain payments and stablecoins and [Haun Ventures’ $1 billion dual-fund strategy](https://www.theblock.co/post/347600/haun-ventures-seeks-1-billion-to-fund-crypto-startups-report) (split between early and late-stage), reflected optimism for the sector’s innovation pipeline. By contrast, [Q2 saw](https://cryptorank.io/insights/reports/crypto-fundraising-report-Q2-25) a marked pivot toward late-stage and strategic funding, with 31 rounds of $50 million or more and a steep drop in sub-$1 million fund raises. Key themes driving this shift included revenue-backed business models, real-world asset tokenisation and CeFi scale-ups, whilst 82% of funded projects in 2025 so far raised without launching a token. Post-2023, regulatory pressure pushed projects toward equity/simple agreement for future equity (SAFE) rounds. Hybrid structures (e.g., equity + token warrants) became the norm, balancing investor protection and Web3-native upside.

These hybrid equity-token rounds combine SAFEs or equity with token warrants and have become standard in early-stage Web3 - [Avax](https://www.avax.network/about/blog/how-a-seasoned-investor-is-navigating-early-stage-web3-opportunities-in-2025) and [LDU Asia](https://lduasia.com/blogs/token-warrants-safts-and-safes-structuring-crypto-investments-the-smart-way) explain that this approach preserves traditional investor protections whilst offering upside in token economies. And, although quarterly breakdowns of hybrid usage remain undisclosed, the model is now a structural norm. Several major rounds and regional trends defined the half-year and, besides Binance’s record round, [late-stage winners in Q2](https://cryptorank.io/insights/reports/crypto-fundraising-report-Q2-25) included Kalshi ($185 million), World ($135 million) and World Liberty Financial ($125 million). Geographically, [Mexico overtook](https://cryptorank.io/news/feed/2ead7-mexico-leads-latam-funding-q2-2025-ai-fintech-data) Brazil in Latin American venture funding for the first time since 2012, raising $437 million in Q2 (led by a $170 million Series C for Klar) with late-stage and growth rounds in the region nearly doubling quarter-over-quarter. Fund activity and M&A also surged: Kraken’s $1.5 billion acquisition of NinjaTrader and MoonPay’s $175 million purchase of Helio contributed to over $6 billion in M&A volume in H1, tripling 2024’s total. These patterns underline a bifurcated market: early-stage remains vibrant for experimentation, whilst late-stage capital consolidates around proven, revenue-generating narratives.

Meanwhile, the mood in H1 2025’s venture market blends guarded optimism with hard-nosed realism with one seed-stage founder capturing the product-market pivot succinctly: “ _Stablecoins are perfect rails for yield distribution_ ,” [said Resolv Labs](https://www.coindesk.com/business/2025/04/15/resolv-labs-raises-usd10m-as-crypto-investor-appetite-for-yield-bearing-stablecoins-soars)’ CEO, Ivan Kozlov, whereby underscoring how tangible, revenue-driven narratives are replacing speculative token pitches. From the investor side, Flybridge’s [Chip Hazard](https://www.businessinsider.com/tech-investors-fear-yet-another-funding-chill-is-coming-2025-4#:~:text=%22To%20the%20extent%20you%20are,memo%20seen%20by%20Business%20Insider.&text=President%20Donald%20Trump's%20tariffs%20sparked,over%20the%20past%20two%20weeks.) advised portfolio companies: _“To the extent you are mid-stream in raising capital, get that closed as soon as possible. We repeat, close anything mid-stream ASAP” -_ a winter-tested tactic to lock in terms before macro or policy shifts undercut valuations. Even traditionally bullish voices such as [Pantera Capital](https://panteracapital.com/blockchain-letter/looking-past-tariff-volatility/) frame the current cycle as one where “ _headwinds may turn into tailwinds_ ”, but only with disciplined structures, staged exposure and conservative warrant terms in the face of persistent regulatory uncertainty. Outlier Ventures [pushes back](https://outlierventures.io/article/web3-fundraising-in-q1-2025-what-founders-investors-need-to-know/) on _“funding winter_ ” talk, instead calling it a rebalancing of founder expectations. Its guidance to founders is to “ _build something inevitable_ ” and prioritise “ _survival over speculation_ ”, meaning projects must ship, deliver real token utility, prove product-market fit _before_ fundraising, and be ready for compliance scrutiny. This discipline reflects a market where 67% of institutions made no investments in H1, [showing just how selective capital has become](https://rootdatalabs.medium.com/2025-h1-web3-industry-investment-research-report-ebd727792de1). In some cases, particularly for exchange listings on platforms such as Binance, grassroots community traction now outweighs institutional endorsement as a driver of access and credibility. Together, these perspectives paint a funding landscape where both sides of the table (founders and investors) are aligning around tangible value, operational proof and resilience over hype. Looking ahead to H2 2025 and 2026, the focus sharpens on utility, regulation and infrastructure maturity. Circle's landmark IPO ($1.1B raise, 168% first-day pop) offers a potential exit pathway, easing the VC liquidity crunch. Meanwhile, [Arche Capital](https://www.wsj.com/articles/two-crypto-investors-the-outlooks-improved-mostly-f255079a) and others point to sustained momentum driven by increasing regulatory clarity and deepening institutional engagement. Key themes include:

> · **infrastructure buildout** \- funding pivots decisively towards modular blockchains, zero-knowledge proofs (ZK), interoperability solutions and robust compliance tools, moving beyond speculative consumer apps. As Hack VC has [predicted](https://www.wealthmanagement.com/alternative-investments/top-3-predictions-for-2025-from-a-venture-capitalist): “ _2025 will be the year that the Web3.0 infrastructure layer begins to take shape.”_
> 
> · **AI-fintech convergence** \- over half of investors identify FinTech as the sector most susceptible to AI disruption (e.g., automated underwriting), accelerating investments at this intersection.
> 
> · **tokenisation acceleration** \- the success of stablecoins and early RWA projects paves the way for broader tokenisation of traditional finance (TradFi) assets.
> 
> · **sustainable models** \- heightened selectivity favours projects demonstrating clear utility, sustainable unit economics and enterprise-grade robustness. Insights4VC [forecasts](https://coinspaidmedia.com/news/venture-capital-investments-web3-expected-grow-2025/) Web3 venture investment could reach $18 billion for full-year 2025, up from $10-11 billion in 2024 whereby fuelling this maturation.

Web3 venture funding in H1 2025 has signalled a market in transition, from speculative hype to execution-driven capital. CryptoRank’s figures showed a split landscape: mega-deals such as Binance’s $2 billion raise and Circle’s $1.1B IPO pushed totals past $15 billion, whilst Galaxy’s stricter VC lens revealed $7 billion across 824 private deals. The duality captures both resilience and discipline: early-stage experimentation remains active, but late-stage capital increasingly gravitates toward revenue-backed, compliance-ready projects. Hiring trends, 15,000+ open roles, highlight that Web3 is moving from whitepapers to shipped products. Yet selectivity is fierce: 67% of institutions sat out H1 entirely, underscoring investor caution. Regional dynamics, from Mexico overtaking Brazil in Latin American VC to $6B in M&A, further illustrate consolidation. The narrative is shifting toward infrastructure, tokenised real-world assets and AI–fintech convergence, and the message is clear: the next Web3 cycle belongs not to speculation, but to utility, compliance and sustainable economics.
