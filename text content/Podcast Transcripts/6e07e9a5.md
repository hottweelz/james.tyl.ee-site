## Full Transcript

**Jonny:** Welcome to the Digital Bite Show. This week I'm delighted to be joined by Luke Conway, who's going to talk to us about tokenomics and financial crime. Luke, welcome to the show.

**Luke:** Thanks Jonny, great to be here.

**Jonny:** So Luke, this is a fascinating topic because I think there's a lot of misunderstanding about crypto and financial crime. Some people think crypto is all about money laundering, others think it's completely transparent and crime-free. What's the reality?

**Luke:** The reality is somewhere in between, Jonny. Blockchain technology is fundamentally transparent - every transaction is recorded permanently on a public ledger. But that doesn't mean criminals don't try to use it. What we're seeing is that crypto-related crime is actually quite trackable if you have the right tools and expertise.

**Jonny:** So it's actually easier to track than cash?

**Luke:** Much easier than cash. With cash, once it changes hands, it's gone. There's no trail. With crypto, even if someone tries to obfuscate their tracks through mixers or by moving funds through multiple wallets, there's always a record. Law enforcement and blockchain analytics companies are getting very sophisticated at following these trails.

**Jonny:** That's really interesting. So what about tokenomics - how does the economic design of a token affect its susceptibility to financial crime?

**Luke:** Great question. Tokenomics is essentially the economic model of how a token works - how it's distributed, what incentives exist, how supply is controlled. Poorly designed tokenomics can actually create opportunities for manipulation and fraud. For example, if a small group holds a huge percentage of the supply, they can manipulate the price. Or if there are no vesting periods for team tokens, insiders can dump their tokens on the market.

**Jonny:** So good tokenomics is actually a form of crime prevention?

**Luke:** Exactly. Well-designed tokenomics with transparency, fair distribution, proper vesting schedules, and clear utility creates a more legitimate project that's less attractive to bad actors. It's harder to manipulate a well-designed token than a poorly designed one.

**Jonny:** What are some of the red flags you look for when evaluating a project's tokenomics from a financial crime perspective?

**Luke:** Several things. First, anonymous team members - that's an immediate red flag. Second, unclear or absent vesting schedules. Third, tokens where the majority of supply is held by insiders. Fourth, projects that make unrealistic promises about returns. Fifth, lack of transparency about how funds will be used. And sixth, projects that seem to have no real utility or purpose beyond speculation.

**Jonny:** It sounds like a lot of it comes down to transparency and accountability.

**Luke:** Absolutely. The irony is that blockchain is a transparency technology, but many projects try to operate in opaque ways. The projects that succeed long-term are usually the ones that embrace transparency - who the team is, how the tokens are distributed, what the roadmap looks like, regular audits, clear communication with the community.

**Jonny:** What about regulatory compliance? How important is that in preventing financial crime in the token space?

**Luke:** It's critical. Regulations like KYC (Know Your Customer) and AML (Anti-Money Laundering) exist for good reasons. Projects that comply with these regulations from the start are much less likely to be used for financial crime. Some projects resist regulation, arguing it goes against the decentralized ethos of crypto, but the reality is that mainstream adoption requires regulatory compliance.

**Jonny:** So we're seeing a maturation of the space where the legitimate projects are separating from the illegitimate ones?

**Luke:** Definitely. The wild west days of crypto are ending. Regulators are paying attention, law enforcement has developed expertise, blockchain analytics tools are sophisticated, and institutional investors demand compliance. Projects that want to succeed long-term need to take financial crime prevention seriously from day one.

**Jonny:** What advice would you give to someone launching a token project who wants to get the tokenomics right and avoid financial crime issues?

**Luke:** First, be transparent. Make your team known. Second, work with legal and compliance experts from the beginning - don't bolt on compliance later. Third, design thoughtful tokenomics with fair distribution and proper incentives. Fourth, get security audits from reputable firms. Fifth, implement robust KYC/AML procedures. And sixth, communicate clearly and regularly with your community. Financial crime thrives in opacity and silence.

**Jonny:** That's excellent advice. Luke, this has been really informative. If people want to learn more about your work or get in touch, what's the best way?

**Luke:** LinkedIn is probably the easiest way to find me - just search for Luke Conway and tokenomics. I'm also active on Twitter where I post about these topics regularly.

**Jonny:** Fantastic. Well, thank you so much for joining us today Luke. Really valuable insights into a complex and important topic.

**Luke:** Thanks for having me, Jonny. It's been a pleasure.

**Jonny:** And thank you to everyone listening to the Digital Bite Show. We'll be back next week with another episode. Take care.
