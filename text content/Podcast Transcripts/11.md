We’ve seen tokenisation, stablecoins, NFTs, RWAs—call them what you like—but underneath it all sits one awkward question: who is actually looking after the keys, the access, and the legal rights? [file:73]  

[Jonny] To help us dig into this, I’m joined by [guest name], who spends most of their waking hours thinking about how to keep digital assets safe for institutions and serious investors. Welcome to the show. [file:73]  

[Guest] Thanks, Jonny. Great to be here. It’s an important conversation because the technology has raced ahead, but the way we think about **safekeeping** often hasn’t caught up. [file:73]  

[Jonny] Let’s start with the obvious: when people hear “safeguarding digital assets”, they often think “cold storage” and maybe a metal wallet in a safe. Is that still where the conversation is, or has it moved on? [file:73]  

[Guest] Cold storage is still one building block, but the conversation has moved well beyond “where is the private key?” into:  
- **Who is the legal custodian?**  
- **What happens in insolvency?**  
- **What are the operational controls around signing and transfers?**  
- **How do we prove, to a regulator or a court, that an asset was held and transferred correctly?** [file:73]  

[Guest] So yes, you still need secure key storage, but you also need governance, insurance, segregation of client assets, auditability and clarity of legal title. [file:73]  

[Jonny] In your article you split safeguarding into three pillars: **technology, process, and law**. Let’s take them one by one. Start with the technology: what actually matters? [file:73]  

[Guest] On the tech side, the basics are:  

- **Key‑management architecture** – Whether you use HSMs, MPC (multi‑party computation), hardware wallets or a combination, the design needs to minimise single points of failure and insider risk.  
- **Access controls** – Role‑based permissions, strong authentication, and the ability to enforce policy—e.g. no single person can move assets above a certain threshold.  
- **Segregation and monitoring** – Distinguishing omnibus addresses from segregated accounts, monitoring addresses for anomalies, and having real‑time visibility into balances and movements. [file:73]  

[Guest] A lot of high‑profile losses weren’t because the cryptography failed; they were because the **operational use** of keys was flawed. [file:73]  

[Jonny] So that leads nicely into process: controls, approvals, who can do what. What does good look like there? [file:73]  

[Guest] Good process looks a lot like good traditional‑finance operations, adapted for digital rails:  

- **Four‑eyes (or six‑eyes) principle** on significant transfers.  
- **Whitelisting** of counterparties and destinations, so assets can only move to pre‑approved addresses.  
- **Change‑management** procedures for adding new assets, chains or smart‑contract interactions.  
- **Incident‑response** runbooks for everything from lost credentials to chain forks or protocol exploits. [file:73]  

[Guest] Importantly, all of this needs to be documented and tested. Regulators, auditors and institutional clients will increasingly ask to see not just your tech stack, but your **SOC reports**, penetration‑testing results and operational runbooks. [file:73]  

[Jonny] And then the legal pillar—which, in my experience, is where a lot of projects have the biggest blind spot. People assume: “We’ve got the keys, therefore we’ve got the asset.” But as we’ve heard on other shows, that’s not always how courts see it. What are the key legal questions? [file:73]  

[Guest] Key questions include:  

- **Nature of the client relationship** – Are you a trustee, a bailee, a contractual custodian, or just a platform user under terms and conditions? That affects who owns what in insolvency.  
- **Segregation in law as well as in practice** – Are client assets clearly separated from the custodian’s own assets on the balance sheet and in the contractual documentation?  
- **Governing law and jurisdiction** – Which court will interpret the rights and obligations if something goes wrong? [file:73]  

[Guest] Recent cases in common‑law jurisdictions have begun to treat certain token positions as **property rights** capable of being held on trust, but the details vary, and your contracts need to be aligned with the most protective interpretation you can get. [file:73]  

[Jonny] So if I’m an asset manager or a family office, what should I be asking a prospective digital‑asset custodian? [file:73]  

[Guest] I’d focus on at least five questions:  

1. **Where and how are the keys stored?** (HSMs, MPC, geo‑distribution, offline/online mix.)  
2. **What is your legal model?** (Trust, custody agreement, omnibus vs segregated, insolvency treatment.)  
3. **What insurance coverage do you have, and what exactly does it cover?** (Theft, internal fraud, technology failure, etc.)  
4. **What audits and certifications do you have?** (SOC 1/2, ISO 27001, regulatory authorisations.)  
5. **How do you handle governance around new protocols, staking, DeFi interactions?** [file:73]  

[Guest] The more the custodian can answer in specifics instead of marketing language, the better. [file:73]  

[Jonny] In the piece you also touch on **staking and yield‑generating activities**—a big area of growth but also of risk. From a safeguarding perspective, what’s the right way to think about that? [file:73]  

[Guest] Staking and DeFi introduce **counterparty and protocol risk**. If your custodian stakes your assets or uses DeFi on your behalf, you need to understand:  

- Who is the legal counterparty—your custodian, or a protocol?  
- What happens if the protocol is exploited or slashed?  
- Are you comfortable that this activity fits within your mandate and regulatory perimeter? [file:73]  

[Guest] Many institutional clients are starting with **“custody‑only”** or very limited whitelisted staking, where risk is clearly evaluated and the legal model spells out who bears what. [file:73]  

[Jonny] You mention regulators. How are they thinking about safeguarding? Are we seeing convergence with how traditional custody is supervised? [file:73]  

[Guest] Yes, we’re seeing a convergence. Regulators are increasingly saying:  

- Digital‑asset custody should meet **equivalent or higher standards** than securities custody.  
- Client assets must be **segregated, reconciled and safeguarded** with robust organisational and technical measures.  
- Custodians must have appropriate **capital, governance and risk‑management frameworks**. [file:73]  

[Guest] In some jurisdictions, dedicated **crypto‑custody licences** or regimes are emerging; in others, existing banking or investment‑firm rules are being extended. Either way, the direction of travel is toward treating safekeeping of digital assets as a regulated financial‑market infrastructure function. [file:73]  

[Jonny] Looking forward, what do you see as the biggest challenge in “safeguarding the future” rather than just safeguarding today’s tokens? [file:73]  

[Guest] Two big challenges:  

1. **Complexity of smart‑contract‑based assets** – When rights and obligations are encoded in contracts that can be upgraded, paused, or governed by DAOs, we need new frameworks for what “custody” even means. Holding the key may not be enough if the contract can change under you. [file:73]  

2. **Interoperability and cross‑chain risk** – As value moves across chains via bridges, wrapped tokens and interoperability protocols, the potential failure points multiply. Safeguarding needs to expand from “on this chain” to “across this network of systems.” [file:73]  

[Guest] That’s why we emphasise not just technical controls but **ongoing risk assessment** and **governance** as core to safeguarding. [file:73]  

[Jonny] So if you had one piece of advice for boards and senior management who are approving digital‑asset strategies, what would it be? [file:73]  

[Guest] Treat safekeeping as a **first‑order design decision**, not an afterthought. Ask early:  

- Who will be our custodian?  
- What is our risk appetite?  
- How do we ensure that if something goes wrong, we can demonstrate that we took reasonable, industry‑standard steps to protect client and firm assets? [file:73]  

[Guest] Building that into your strategy from day one is much easier than trying to retrofit it around a live business. [file:73]
