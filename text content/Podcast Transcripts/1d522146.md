## Full Transcript

**Jonny:** Welcome to another episode of the Digital Bite Show. I'm Jonny Fry, and as always I'm joined by my co-host James Tylee from Georgia USA. This week we're discussing Trump's trade wars and what they mean for digital commerce, cross-border payments, and the crypto ecosystem.

**James:** Hey Jonny, hey everybody listening. You know, this is such a timely topic because we're seeing tariffs being threatened left and right, and it's going to have massive implications not just for traditional trade but for how money moves around the world digitally.

**Jonny:** Absolutely. So James, from your perspective in the US, what are you seeing and hearing about how these trade tensions might affect digital payments and crypto adoption?

**James:** Well, the interesting thing is that when you have tariffs and trade restrictions, money has to move more efficiently, not less. Companies are looking for ways to reduce friction in cross-border payments. They can't control the tariffs, but they can control how fast and how cheaply they move money to pay suppliers, especially in places like China, Mexico, Canada.

**Jonny:** That's a really good point. And what we're seeing here in Europe is that when traditional banking channels become more expensive or more complicated due to sanctions or trade restrictions, businesses are increasingly looking at stablecoins and crypto rails as alternatives.

**James:** Exactly. And you know what's fascinating? The Trump administration is very pro-crypto while also being very aggressive on trade. So you've got this interesting dynamic where they're making traditional trade harder but making crypto trade easier. It's almost like they're pushing people toward digital solutions.

**Jonny:** Yes, and I think that's intentional to some degree. If the US wants to maintain dollar dominance - which they clearly do - but they're also restricting traditional trade flows, then digital dollars in the form of stablecoins become more important, not less important.

**James:** Right, because a USDC payment from New York to Shanghai doesn't care about tariffs. The tariff is on the goods, not on the payment rail. So companies can at least reduce their payment costs and settlement times even if they can't avoid the tariff itself.

**Jonny:** And that brings us to an interesting point about regulatory arbitrage. We're seeing countries that are subject to US trade restrictions becoming more interested in non-dollar payment systems. China's digital yuan, for example, is being positioned partly as a way to circumvent dollar-based sanctions.

**James:** Yeah, but here's the thing - and I'm curious what you think about this Jonny - even with a digital yuan, China still needs dollars for international trade. Most commodities are priced in dollars. Most international contracts are denominated in dollars. So they can't really escape the dollar system entirely.

**Jonny:** You're absolutely right, but they can reduce their exposure. And that's what we're seeing with things like oil being priced in yuan, or trade agreements between countries that bypass the dollar entirely. It's not that the dollar is going away, it's that its dominance is being challenged in ways we haven't seen since Bretton Woods.

**James:** And crypto and digital assets are caught right in the middle of all this. Because on one hand, the US wants crypto to strengthen the dollar through stablecoins. On the other hand, other countries see crypto as a way to escape dollar dependence. It's going to be really interesting to see how this plays out.

**Jonny:** Absolutely. And for businesses caught in the middle - particularly small and medium enterprises that are trading internationally - they need solutions now. They can't wait for governments to figure this out. That's why we're seeing such rapid adoption of blockchain-based payment solutions in places like Latin America and Southeast Asia.

**James:** Yeah, and you know what's crazy? Some of these solutions are better than what traditional banks offer even without the trade war pressure. Faster, cheaper, more transparent. The trade wars are just accelerating adoption of something that was going to happen anyway.

**Jonny:** That's an excellent point. The technology was ready, the infrastructure was being built, but geopolitical tensions are acting as a catalyst for adoption. Companies that might have waited another 5 years to explore blockchain payments are being forced to explore them now.

**James:** So what do you think happens next, Jonny? Do we see more fragmentation with different currency blocs and different payment systems? Or does the efficiency of blockchain technology actually create more interoperability?

**Jonny:** I think we'll see both. In the short term, more fragmentation as countries try to protect themselves. But in the medium to long term, the technology enables interoperability in ways that traditional correspondent banking never could. You can have atomic swaps between different currencies, you can have cross-chain bridges, you can have programmable money that automatically converts at the best rate.

**James:** Yeah, I agree. And I think the companies that figure this out first - that build the infrastructure to move money efficiently across these fragmenting trade blocs - those are going to be incredibly valuable.

**Jonny:** Absolutely. Well, this has been a fascinating discussion. James, thank you as always for your insights from the American perspective.

**James:** Thanks Jonny, and thanks to everyone listening. This is one to watch closely because it's going to affect every business that trades internationally.

**Jonny:** Indeed. Thank you for listening to the Digital Bite Show. We'll be back next week with another episode. Until then, take care.
