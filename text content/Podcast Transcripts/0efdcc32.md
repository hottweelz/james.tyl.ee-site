## Full Transcript

**Jonny:** Welcome back to the Digital Bite Show. I'm Jonny Fry and as always, I'm joined by James Tylee from Georgia. This week we're discussing the Basel Committee's crypto regulations and what they mean for banks and crypto adoption.

**James:** Hey Jonny, hey everyone. This is a really important topic because the Basel Committee basically sets the rules for how banks manage risk globally. And now they're finally addressing how banks should handle crypto assets.

**Jonny:** Exactly. And for those who don't know, the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision is the group that creates international banking standards. When they speak, banks listen - because national regulators implement Basel standards into local law.

**James:** Right. So what are they saying about crypto?

**Jonny:** Well, the headline is that they're treating most crypto assets as very high risk. Under their proposed rules, banks would need to hold $1 of capital for every $1 of crypto exposure. That's essentially saying crypto is the riskiest asset class possible from a regulatory perspective.

**James:** Wow, that's pretty harsh. That's higher than the capital requirements for junk bonds or leveraged loans, right?

**Jonny:** Exactly. Much higher. For context, a typical corporate loan might require 8-12% capital backing. Investment grade bonds might require even less. But crypto? 100%. It's a one-to-one ratio.

**James:** So if a bank wanted to hold $100 million in Bitcoin, they'd need to set aside $100 million in capital to cover it?

**Jonny:** Precisely. Which obviously makes it uneconomical for most banks. The capital they set aside can't be used for lending or other activities. So the opportunity cost is enormous.

**James:** But there are some exceptions, right? I heard something about stablecoins being treated differently?

**Jonny:** Yes, and this is where it gets interesting. The Basel Committee is creating two buckets. Group 1 includes certain stablecoins and tokenized traditional assets - things that have real-world asset backing and meet certain criteria around stability and regulation. Group 2 is everything else - Bitcoin, Ethereum, most cryptocurrencies.

**James:** And Group 1 gets treated more favorably?

**Jonny:** Correct. Group 1 assets would be subject to normal risk-weighting rules similar to the underlying asset. So a tokenized treasury bond would be treated like a treasury bond. A properly regulated stablecoin backed by reserves might be treated similarly to cash or government securities.

**James:** That makes sense. So the regulators are basically saying "we're fine with blockchain technology applied to traditional assets, but native crypto assets scare us."

**Jonny:** That's exactly right. And you can understand their perspective - traditional assets have centuries of legal precedent, established custody arrangements, known risk profiles. Crypto assets are new, volatile, and the legal frameworks are still evolving.

**James:** Sure, but doesn't this create a problem? If banks can't hold crypto economically, then institutional adoption slows down, which means less liquidity, which means more volatility, which means regulators were right to be scared. It's a self-fulfilling prophecy.

**Jonny:** You've hit on exactly the criticism many in the crypto industry are making. They argue that these rules are preventing the very institutional adoption that would make crypto less volatile and more stable.

**James:** What about in the US specifically? Is the Fed going to implement these Basel rules?

**Jonny:** That's the billion dollar question, James. Traditionally, the US implements Basel standards, but there's been some discussion about whether they'll adopt these crypto rules as written. The Trump administration has been very pro-crypto, and there are members of Congress pushing back against overly restrictive regulations.

**James:** So we might see the US take a lighter touch than Europe or other jurisdictions?

**Jonny:** It's possible. And that could create interesting competitive dynamics. If US banks can hold crypto more easily than European banks, that could give US financial institutions a competitive advantage in the emerging digital asset space.

**James:** But it could also create regulatory arbitrage issues, right? Banks moving activities to friendlier jurisdictions?

**Jonny:** Absolutely. And that's something regulators hate. They want a level playing field. So there's going to be a lot of negotiation and coordination between different regulatory jurisdictions over the coming months and years.

**James:** What about crypto-native firms - the Coinbases and Krakens of the world? How do these rules affect them?

**Jonny:** Well, most of them aren't banks, so Basel rules don't directly apply. But many of them want to become banks or acquire banks precisely to have access to central bank payment systems and deposit insurance. These rules make that path more difficult and expensive.

**James:** So it's forcing a decision - either stay crypto-native without bank licenses, or become a bank but face these heavy capital requirements?

**Jonny:** Exactly. And we're seeing different companies make different choices. Some are pursuing banking licenses despite the challenges. Others are partnering with banks rather than becoming banks themselves. And still others are moving to friendlier jurisdictions.

**James:** This is all evolving so fast. What do you think happens over the next couple years?

**Jonny:** I think we'll see continued negotiation between the industry and regulators. The initial Basel proposals will likely be softened somewhat, especially for things like Bitcoin and Ethereum which have become quite established. We'll probably see more nuanced risk categories rather than the binary Group 1 / Group 2 approach. And I think stablecoins and tokenized securities will become increasingly mainstream in banking.

**James:** Yeah, I agree. The technology isn't going away, so regulators will have to find ways to accommodate it safely rather than trying to block it entirely.

**Jonny:** Well said. This is definitely a space to watch closely. Thanks for that discussion James.

**James:** Always a pleasure Jonny. Thanks everyone for listening.

**Jonny:** Indeed, thank you for joining us on the Digital Bite Show. We'll be back next week with another episode. Take care everyone.
