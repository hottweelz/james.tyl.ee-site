When I started my career on Wall Street nearly twenty years ago, I couldn't have imagined writing a book like this. This isn't a book about speculation or cryptocurrency hype. This is about infrastructure. This is about how money actually moves, how economies really function, and what happens when we have the technology to fundamentally reimagine both.

## Chapter 1: The World Wasn't Built for Me

Let me tell you something that's been on my mind for years: the United States is behind. And I know that bothers people. I'm American, I'm a tried and true New Yorker, and I'm about as patriotic as they come. But I'm also someone who's spent his entire life navigating systems not built for accessibility. I have Osteogenesis Imperfecta or "OI," a genetic bone disorder that prohibits me from walking and requires use of a wheelchair. That experience taught me something crucial: the best systems aren't built for the person with perfect conditions. They're built for the person with the most constraints. And that perspective is what I bring to this book.

I've been saying for nearly five years now—you guys, the people over in the UK and EU, you're ahead of us. You always do everything before we do.

Look at what the UK was doing with XRP while Americans were calling crypto the enemy of the state. Look at Dubai turning itself into a crypto paradise. Look at the Bank of England having regulatory clarity while we were fumbling around trying to figure out if Ethereum is a commodity or a security. Even eight years ago, we hated crypto. It was bipartisan hatred, which was actually impressive in its consistency. Democrats hated it, Republicans hated it, everybody hated it.

There's a story from New York—1940—that I keep coming back to. A press agent led an actual bull through a china shop as a publicity stunt. The whole point was to test the idiom. The result was surprising: the bull walked through without breaking a single thing. It was calm. It was just a bull in a room. But a bystander, terrified of what might happen, panicked and backed into a shelf. That's what broke the porcelain.

The damage was caused by fear. Not by the bull.

I think about that story every time someone calls blockchain "disruptive." Every senator who demanded hearings about what this technology *might* do. Every regulator who froze accounts, denied banking services to crypto companies, classified tokens inconsistently, and sent billions in capital fleeing to jurisdictions that weren't scared. Every pundit who wrote op-eds about the coming crypto catastrophe.

None of them protected what they were trying to protect. The SEC's enforcement paralysis didn't safeguard investors—it pushed a generation of builders to Switzerland, Dubai, and the Cayman Islands. The banks that refused to service crypto companies didn't stop crypto—they stopped themselves from participating in what came next. The seasoning periods, the account freezes, the jurisdictional confusion—that was the bystander, backing into shelves in a panic.

The technology just stood there. Waiting.

I know this feeling from my own life. Every time I rolled into a room, someone expected the bull. They'd pre-emptively clear a path, rearrange furniture, lower their expectations. They were bracing for damage. And I'd move through the room and nothing would break—because nothing was going to break—and I'd watch that same person trip over their own feet trying to get out of my way.

That's the story of blockchain in America. The disruption people feared was mostly self-inflicted.

But here's the thing about being behind—you learn from watching. And when you finally decide to move, you can move fast.

I spent my entire career watching how money moves. On Wall Street, I watched electronic money flow through the system. I built algorithms to predict markets. I worked under legends like Michael Bloomberg, who taught me that treating everyone equally—regardless of their circumstances—was what actually produced excellence.

A decent wheelchair—titanium models like a Quickie or TiLite—costs about six thousand dollars. In New York, you're jumping Long Island Rail Road gaps, subway gaps, curbs. There's no room for being gentle. You need top-dollar equipment and top-dollar earnings just to exist functionally.

When Mike interviewed me, he was pure New York about it: "I don't care if you walk, wheel, hop, skip, jump—do a great job." Then he looked at me and asked: "Does that bother you at all?"

I pointed down at the wheelchair. "If this is the thing that makes me money," I said, "...then that's why I'm in it."

He smiled. That was the final interview.

Without Mike, who refused to lower expectations, I'd probably be running a cash register at a Walmart. But he didn't adjust for me. He expected me at the office before the markets opened and staying until after they closed. He treated me like any other employee, which meant I became as capable as any other employee. The equation was simple: high expectations forced me to earn the money I needed to afford the equipment I needed to function in New York. That economic pressure is what made the difference.

That lesson stuck with me. And that's been part of my journey into digital assets—the realization that this technology can democratize access without lowering standards.

### When the System Made Me Feel Like a Criminal

So. 2018. I'm buying a house on the Gold Coast of Georgia using Bitcoin and Ethereum.

Now, let me be clear: this was complicated. Extremely complicated. But I did it because when there's a will, there's a way. I converted my Bitcoin and Ethereum into USDC, but I had to go through fiat to do it. And when I was moving that amount of money into fiat, into USDC, there was zero protection. They wanted to verify I had the money through a 30-day "seasoning period"—you know, where they make sure you're not a money launderer by forcing your money to sit in limbo for a month or more.

Picture this: a guy in a wheelchair, thick New York accent, full Sicilian energy, sitting in a Georgia real estate office trying to explain cryptocurrency to people who were looking at me like I'd just landed from another planet. I basically just kept insisting, loudly, that I was a normal person doing a normal thing. "This is my money. I own this money. It belongs to me." The banker looked at me like I'd handed him a live fish and asked him to process it.

At one point — and this actually happened — I looked at one of the legal guys across the table and said: "Uh... unless you *don't* want me to buy this house?"

That was the *"eh, no sir, we'll get through this"* moment. And we did.

The point is: I went through all those gymnastics to buy a house with crypto that was already legal tender in some countries. Think about that. The technology existed. Legal frameworks existed elsewhere. But in America, it was still treated like an anomaly, an edge case, something to be suspicious of.

That experience shaped how I think about digital assets. It's not about the speculation. It's about the infrastructure gaps. It's about the unnecessary friction in systems that should be frictionless.

## Chapter 2: What Constraint Teaches You

### You Know You've Arrived When It's No Longer About the Salary

Before Merrill Lynch, there was Bloomberg. You already know that part. What I haven't told you is what it felt like when Wall Street started competing *for me*.

You know you've arrived at a certain level when the negotiation stops being about money.

Merrill Lynch wanted me, and they put together a package that included something I hadn't expected: access to the private executive garage. Reserved spot, covered, secured — no subway gaps, no Long Island Rail Road platforms, no sidewalk to fight. For a guy navigating a titanium wheelchair through lower Manhattan every day, that wasn't a perk. That was infrastructure.

I thought that was a pretty good offer.

Then UBS came calling. They *really* wanted me. And their pitch was... creative.

I was living on Long Island at the time — Port Jefferson. UBS said: what if we handle the whole commute? Car waiting for you at the Port Jeff dock. Drive to the ferry. Cross to Connecticut. Get off the boat. Your other car is waiting. Drive to the office.

Door to dock to door. Two cars and a *boat*.

I just sat there for a second.

I knew I was doing something right when the competition stopped being about salary and started being about logistics. That was the moment I realized the wheelchair had never been the ceiling — it had just been something everyone else was worried about more than I was.

I told UBS: "Connecticut? That's not Wall Street."

Greed. Ego. Both, probably. But here's the thing I've thought about since — they built this entire elaborate solution around my wheelchair. Two cars and a boat. And the wheelchair was never actually the problem. *I* was never the problem. I was navigating Manhattan every day just fine. They were trying to fix something that wasn't broken.

Meanwhile, Merrill just gave me a garage.

More money, a parking spot, and I'm on the right island. Done.

### Seeing What Others Miss

When I wheeled into my first day at Merrill Lynch, I was the only person in the room using a wheelchair. More accurately, I was the only person the human resources department had explicitly processed through accessibility accommodations. There were probably other people with invisible disabilities—anxiety, dyslexia, hearing loss—but I was the visible anomaly. Ok, there were elevators — it's Manhattan, obviously. But my parking spot got painted blue. Everyone knew, before I arrived, that I was different. And I'd say the first eight hours revolved entirely around people swinging by my desk with: *"Ok, so... if you ever need anything..."*

Ugh.

What nobody anticipated was that my difference would become an advantage.

Here's what actually made me valuable at Merrill — and why UBS wanted me badly enough to arrange a boat.

FIX Protocol. If you know it, you know. There's a reason there's a FIX Bible — a literal reference document that practitioners live by. It's the messaging standard that makes electronic trading work: the language connecting brokers, exchanges, order management systems, and execution venues across every major market on earth. Getting it right is the difference between an order that executes and one that vanishes. Getting it *really* right — at ultra-low-latency, across international markets, in regulatory environments that share nothing in common — is genuinely hard. In the early 2000s, there weren't many people in the country who could do it at the level the Street needed.

I became one of them at Bloomberg. That's where I learned FIX the deep way. Turned out to be the most valuable skill I'd ever develop.

When Merrill brought me in, they had a project nobody had cracked: build an algorithmic trading platform for Brazil. From scratch. Complete architecture, design to production. Brazil's markets worked differently. Connectivity requirements worked differently. The regulatory environment worked differently. But underneath all of it — it was still FIX. And because it was FIX, and because I knew FIX cold, what looked like a unique challenge was really a translation problem I'd already solved.

We launched. It worked. And then the same architecture got adapted for more than 30 other countries.

Then I got assigned directly to Japan — to introduce Japan's *first* third-party hosted service for ultra-low-latency electronic trading. First. Ever. In Japan. Which sounds enormous. But again: FIX.

I was later told — and I want to be careful here, because I can't verify it and I'm not claiming credit for a $50 billion corporate decision — but I was *later told* that the Brazil platform, and what it represented about Merrill's global algorithmic trading capability, was one of the factors that made Bank of America want the firm during the 2008 crisis.

Bank of America paid roughly $50 billion for Merrill Lynch. The platform I designed from scratch was live in 30+ countries by the time that deal closed.

Here's the part that still makes me smile: it didn't stop at Wall Street. The major centralized crypto exchanges — Coinbase, Binance, others — they all use FIX Protocol too. Because internally, an order matching system is an order matching system. The market evolved, the assets changed, but the plumbing is the same plumbing.

And Changpeng Zhao — CZ, the founder of Binance — learned FIX alongside me during his time at Bloomberg.

*Thanks CZ.*

I was navigating lower Manhattan in a wheelchair the whole time. Nobody handed me that expertise. I just went and got it.

This is what disability teaches you: the next generation of infrastructure always starts as accessibility.

The internet was built by guys in labs, with full use of their bodies and minds and full time to dedicate. It wasn't built for accessibility. But then we added screen readers. We added keyboard navigation. We added captions. And suddenly we had infrastructure that didn't just work for disabled people—it worked better for everyone. A screen reader isn't just for blind users; it's useful when you're driving. Captions aren't just for deaf users; they're useful when you're in a loud bar.

The same pattern emerged in mobile. Smartphones were built for sighted people with ten working fingers. But the accessibility features—voice control, larger text, simple gestures—turned out to be the future of how everyone interacts with technology. The accessibility layer became the user experience.

Financial infrastructure is going through that transition now. The traditional banking system was built for a specific scenario: physical presence, specific hours, specific capabilities. That system is being disrupted by infrastructure that strips away those requirements. No branch hours. No geography. No requirement for a particular body or set of capabilities.

And the people who don't see it coming are the ones who designed the old system.

I did. I spent years optimizing that old system. Making it faster, more efficient, more sophisticated. But it was a local optimization. I was solving problems within a constraint that was about to become obsolete.

When I started working with blockchain and digital assets, I was recognizing something I'd already spent my whole life understanding: that the best infrastructure is the infrastructure that works for the person with the most constraints. Because that infrastructure will also work for everyone else.

That's the lens that disability gives you. It's not sympathy. It's not inspiration. It's pattern recognition. It's the ability to look at a system and ask: "What happens when conditions change? What happens when the system fails the person who needed it most? What happens when constraints shift?"

Those questions turned out to be the most important questions in finance.

## Chapter 3: Financial Systems and Single Points of Failure

### The Pattern Recognition

I spent the first half of my Wall Street career solving problems that didn't matter.

I say that without bitterness. Wall Street is full of smart people solving increasingly complicated problems within a system that's fundamentally sound. If you work in equities, you're optimizing the distribution of ownership. If you work in fixed income, you're optimizing the expression of interest rates. If you work in derivatives, you're optimizing the distribution of risk. All of this matters, in the sense that it moves money efficiently within the system. None of it matters, in the sense that the system itself might be wrong.

I was solving efficiency problems. Making the machine run faster. Better routing. Lower latency. Smarter algorithms. All the markers of success: promotions, compensation, respect. But I was also making the system more complex, more dependent on infrastructure that only worked if everything went perfectly.

Which meant I was also making it more fragile.

This is something disability teaches you immediately: redundancy isn't a luxury. It's a requirement.

In my daily life, nothing was simple. I needed Plan A and Plan B and Plan C. I needed backup systems for my backup systems. If a tool didn't work, I needed an alternative. If one mode of access failed, I needed another. I couldn't design my life around a single point of failure because a single point of failure meant I couldn't participate.

The financial system, increasingly, was a single point of failure. It was all-in on a specific infrastructure: electronic, centralized, dependent on a handful of nodes. If the electricity went out, the system stopped. If the internet broke, the system stopped. If the central authority decided to cut you off, the system stopped.

I didn't have to look far to see what a single point of failure looked like. I was at Bloomberg on September 11, 2001. I watched the towers come down from lower Manhattan. And then I watched the NYSE close — not for a day, not for the weekend, but for four full trading days. The most sophisticated, most liquid, most technologically advanced financial market in human history. Offline. Because one event took out the node it depended on. The system wasn't designed to fail gracefully. It was designed to work perfectly. And when perfect conditions ended, the system ended with them.

That lesson sat with me. But it took another event to make it personal.

May 6, 2010. I was at Merrill — Bank of America by that point, though I still called it Merrill, still call it Merrill — when the Dow dropped nearly a thousand points in about four minutes and then partially bounced back before anyone understood what had happened. Procter & Gamble briefly traded at $39. Accenture briefly traded at a penny. The initial theory was a "fat finger" — someone had typed billions instead of millions, and the algorithm didn't ask whether that was intentional. It just executed.

That afternoon, I was told: *"The bank will never allow this to happen again. James and his team will have it fixed by Monday."*

It was Friday.

I went home with no solution. I genuinely did not know what the answer was. There were a lot of smart people in that building, and none of us had it. Saturday night I was sitting on my couch watching Saturday Night Live — Weekend Update — and they did a bit on the flash crash. The host pointed out that if you try to delete a photo on your iPhone, the phone asks: *"Are you sure?"* But a trading system executing billions of dollars of orders didn't ask anything. It just went.

I leapt off the couch. *That's it.*

Confirmation logic. Order-level circuit breakers. A prompt — automated, instantaneous — that catches anomalous order size before execution, not after. Not a patch on the system. A fundamental change to how the system treated its own instructions.

We built it that weekend. Monday morning, my team walked in looking like heroes.

The punchline, of course, is that SNL saved us. Consumer software on a three-year-old smartphone had better human-error protection than the global equity markets. That's what a brittle system looks like when you examine it clearly: robust until the moment it isn't, and then catastrophic.

These weren't theoretical problems. They were happening constantly, and I was close enough to feel them. But I kept telling myself they weren't *my* problem. Wall Street serves the efficient market. The market moves money to where it's most valuable. If you're outside the market — if you're the country whose exchange closes, the person whose account gets frozen, the region with no bank branch at all — that's not an economics problem. That's a political one.

I believed that for longer than I should have. I believed it, I think, because believing it made the work feel clean. I was an engineer. I optimized systems. I didn't design who got access.

But I'd spent my entire life being the person the system wasn't designed for. I knew what it felt like to be told, implicitly, that your constraints were a political problem and not an engineering one. I'd just convinced myself that in finance — where I'd finally found a world that valued what I could do — the rules were different.

They weren't.

But I'd been outside systems my entire life. And I'd learned something crucial: the people outside the system often understand it better than the people inside it.

Disability forces you to see how systems work, because you can't rely on the default. A sighted person doesn't think about navigation; they just see where to go. A disabled person has to understand how navigation works—both how the space is designed and what alternatives exist. They have to hold multiple models of the same system in their mind simultaneously.

I learned to navigate the world through pure spatial reasoning. I learned to read environments and problem-solve on the fly. I learned to think in systems at a granular level, because my movement through those systems didn't work by default.

## Chapter 4: The Blockchain Insight

That skill—the ability to see how a system works at the component level—turned out to be exactly what I needed to understand blockchains.

Blockchains are just systems to solve the problem of: "How do we verify the truth of a transaction without needing a centralized authority?"

It's an accessibility question. It's asking: "What if the person verifying the transaction isn't in a position to trust the centralized authority? What if they're in a different country, or a different political situation, or a different economic context?"

The answer is: we distribute that authority. Everyone can verify. Everyone participates in the consensus mechanism. No single point of failure.

That's redundancy. That's the pattern I'd been living with.

And suddenly, it wasn't a personal accommodation anymore. It was global infrastructure.

When I was working on financial infrastructure, optimizing settlement times and routing efficiency, I was solving problems for people like me. People with access. People inside the system. People who could afford to wait for the system to serve them.

But when I moved to digital assets, I was solving problems for everyone else. For the person in a country with unstable currency. For the person politically disfavored by the central authority. For the person in a region without banking infrastructure.

For the person whose default mode of operating within a system didn't work.

The shift was profound. It wasn't about being a better engineer. It was about recognizing which problems mattered.

Disability teaches you pattern recognition at the system level. You learn to see:

- Where the single point of failure is
- Which constraints are real and which are artificial
- When the system is optimized for edge cases instead of common cases
- Where accessibility and resilience are the same thing
- When the system is elegant (works in multiple modes) versus brittle (works in one mode only)

These principles apply everywhere. They apply to financial infrastructure. They apply to supply chains. They apply to energy systems. They apply to governance.

The traditional financial system was optimized for efficiency under perfect conditions. Centralized authority, fast settlement, instant updates. Beautiful engineering, if conditions never degrade.

But conditions always degrade. And the system that's optimized for perfect conditions is usually the most vulnerable when conditions change.

This is the pattern I see now, looking at global finance. The systems are incredibly sophisticated and incredibly fragile. They're optimized for the happy path. And they're all connected, which means that when one fails, they all fail.

The people who understand disability already understand this. We've never had the luxury of assuming perfect conditions. We've never been able to build systems that work in one mode only.

Blockchain infrastructure, distributed systems, redundant verification, asymmetric cryptography—these aren't innovations in the technological sense. They're innovations in the accessibility sense. They answer the question: "How do we build financial systems that work when you can't rely on a centralized, trusted authority?"

And that's how I ended up in blockchain, speaking on panels, building infrastructure, thinking about money and protocol design.

Not because I became an expert in cryptography. But because I'd spent my whole life as an expert in how systems work when the default path fails.

### Raising Your Son

My son is neurotypical. He doesn't use a wheelchair. He has no chronic illness. He moves through the world in a way I never could, and probably never will.

But he's also my son, and that means he grew up watching me navigate systems that weren't designed for me. He watched me problem-solve. He watched me find alternatives. He watched me refuse to accept the frame that "this system is how it works, so you're the problem."

When he was younger, he asked why ramps were necessary. Not in a mean way. Just: "Why does that building have a ramp?" And I could've said, "Because I use it," which is true. Or I could've said, "Because people like me need it," which is also true.

Instead, I said: "Because if you designed the building right, the ramp wouldn't be a special feature. It would just be how you get into the building."

He was maybe eight. He thought about it. "So the building is badly designed?"

"Most buildings are. They were built assuming everyone could walk. That's a bad assumption."

He understood it immediately. And then he started noticing all the bad assumptions. The restaurant where you can't see the menu from a wheelchair. The playground where half the equipment is only accessible if you can climb stairs. The sidewalk with no curb cut, so a person in a wheelchair has to cross the street in the middle of traffic.

He didn't become a designer. He's not trying to fix the world. But he grew up with a different frame. He grew up thinking of accessibility as a design problem, not a special request.

When he talks to his friends, they notice. One of his friends is deaf. Another uses a mobility aid part-time. Another has severe anxiety. These aren't traumas in his worldview. They're just different context. His instinct is to problem-solve, not accommodate. "How do we do this so everyone can participate?"

That's not a special virtue. That's not inspiration porn. That's just: what if you were raised around someone who refused to accept that the system was fine the way it was?

I'm telling you this because it matters for what comes next in this book. This book is built on the premise that disability isn't separate from financial planning. It's central to it. That the same pattern recognition that lets you navigate a world not built for you is the same pattern recognition that lets you navigate financial systems that were designed before the world you're living in emerged.

And I'm telling you this because my son will someday read this, and I want him to know that I knew he was watching.

When I was building my cryptocurrency portfolio, I wasn't thinking, "This is a hedge against the financial system failing." I was thinking, "This is infrastructure that doesn't depend on me being in a specific place or having a specific set of capabilities." It solved a problem I'd been living with forever.

But it also solved a problem for people I'd never met. People in countries with currency crises. People who couldn't access banking. People who needed financial movement and couldn't get it from the institutions.

When I talked about digital assets at conferences and on podcasts, I wasn't talking about a speculative investment. I was talking about infrastructure. I was talking about: What does it look like if we build financial systems for the people the current system fails, instead of assuming everyone can access the perfect system?

My son heard that. And I think he understood, at some level, that I wasn't just talking about money. I was talking about the same thing I'd been teaching him his whole life: if you build for the person with constraints, you end up with a system that works better for everyone.

That's the inheritance I wanted to leave him. Not wealth. Not status. But a way of thinking about systems: always ask, "What happens when conditions change? What happens when someone can't access this the way it was designed? What if we had to rebuild this without assuming perfect conditions?"

Those are the questions that matter in a changing world.

That's the lens I developed, living in a body that didn't match the infrastructure I was born into.

And now I'm trying to help redesign the infrastructure itself.

## Chapter 5: Infrastructure Built for the Edge Case

### Cyber FM: The Accessibility Lens in Action

I want to show you what happens when you take the pattern recognition disability taught me and actually build something with it.

In 2007, I was on Wall Street and every major bank had its own independent band. Guys in the office who could play guitar, drums, keyboards—we'd spend weekends seeing each other perform. But during the week, we had no way to share that music. So a group of us fintech guys built software to let the bands share their tracks internally. It was just a tool to stay connected to the music we loved while working the markets.

Then 2008 happened. The financial crisis hit. And instead of abandoning the idea, we asked a different question: what if we made this a business?

But here's where the accessibility lens mattered. When I looked at the music industry—particularly independent artists—I saw the same constraint problem I'd been living with my entire life. A band in Lagos couldn't get their music heard globally without navigating 20+ different country registration systems. A musician in Manila had to register with Filipino performing rights organizations, then American ones, then European ones. Each system required separate paperwork, separate compliance, separate payments. The fragmentation was the barrier. The system wasn't built wrong—it just wasn't built *for* the musician who had constraints.

So we didn't build another centralized system. We built Cyber FM as a distributed platform. Independent artists register once. Globally. No paperwork in 20 countries. No waiting for payments from fragmented performing rights organizations. One ledger, one token system, instant payouts. And we built in listener rewards so the people who discover new artists get compensated too.

Today, 2.3 million people listen to Cyber FM monthly. Artists from 140 countries are registered. Most of them have never stepped foot in a major label office. They're earning crypto tokens for every listen, globally, without a single registration form.

That's what happens when you design for the person with the most constraints. You end up with infrastructure that works for everyone.

## Chapter 6: Payments Are the First Domino

### From Pattern Recognition to Global Infrastructure

When I founded Cyber FM, I wasn't thinking about blockchain. I was thinking about the same constraint problem I'd been navigating my entire life: fragmentation.

Musicians couldn't access global markets because they had to navigate fragmented systems. Banks can't move money efficiently because they're trapped in fragmented legacy infrastructure. Countries are trying to control money flows through fragmented regulatory frameworks they built decades ago.

The pattern is always the same: **the system works great if you fit the mold perfectly. It breaks completely if you don't.**

Blockchain isn't the solution because it's magical. It's the solution because it removes the fragmentation through a single, distributed ledger that everyone can access simultaneously. When we implemented this at Cyber FM, we weren't doing something revolutionary—we were doing something obvious. One global standard instead of 20 local ones.

The same principle is now moving through the entire financial system, and it's about to reshape how money moves globally.

### Understanding What's Really Happening

Around the middle of 2025, I wrote an article that became the basis for many conversations: "Are We Seeing the Dollarisation of the UK?" The title seems provocative until you understand what I actually mean.

I'm not predicting some American invasion or hostile takeover. I'm describing an economic inevitability based on simple human behavior: money goes where there's liquidity.

There are approximately $250 billion in market cap among stablecoins globally right now. That's not a small number. But when you're talking about the entire UK economy, the European economy, or the global economy, it's actually quite modest. What matters isn't the total size—it's the trajectory and what it represents.

Here's the simple math:

**The USD Advantage:**
- Everyone understands what a US dollar is
- Oil is priced in dollars
- Stablecoins backed by the US dollar have massive liquidity
- The US Treasury market can absorb enormous amounts of capital
- There's regulatory clarity (finally) in the United States

**The Alternative Currencies' Challenge:**
- The Euro stablecoin didn't work
- The Yen stablecoin didn't work
- Sterling acceptance is limited
- Local currencies in high-inflation countries provide zero confidence

### What We're Actually Seeing

I call this "dollarisation"—using the British spelling intentionally, to be specific about the geography—but what's really happening is simpler: capital is becoming more efficient about where it lives.

When I was in London doing financial work, we operated out of Queen Victoria Street—that's the Bloomberg terminal area, the Canary Wharf equivalent for everyone. Money was already flowing there at enormous volumes. But it was all electronic money, legacy banking system money.

Now we have digital money. Programmable money. Money that moves peer-to-peer without intermediaries. And when you have that innovation, it changes everything about how capital flows.

Think about a 15-year-old kid in London. A generation ago, he'd be trading on the stock exchange and priced everything in pounds. Today, he's trading Bitcoin, and he understands the US dollar better than his grandparents do. He's trading Doge. If he's really into it, he's trading Trump Coin. His entire financial worldview is denominated in dollars and digital assets.

That's not sinister. That's just what happens when the technology changes and older alternatives aren't keeping pace.

### The Property Connection

This is where it gets visceral—where people feel it in their actual lives.

I worked with David Parsons and Vilia Longo on the "Dollarization Benefits for London Property" article. The premise is straightforward: overseas buyers—Americans, Middle East investors, international capital seeking UK real estate—increasingly think in dollars. USDT, USDC, these are the currencies they hold. These are the currencies they think in.

When you're buying a £10 million property in Knightsbridge or a country estate in the Cotswolds, foreign exchange costs matter. Transaction delays matter. And if you can buy directly with stablecoins you already hold, bypassing the whole conversion process, why wouldn't you?

Bird & Bird, the law firm, did a study showing that there's a growing trend of stablecoin and non-fiat acceptance in real estate transactions. Their research suggested this could increase marketing budgets by 15-20% to encourage property acquisitions in London—because sellers realize they can access a much larger pool of buyers if they accept digital US dollars.

But here's the problem: the legacy banking system isn't set up for this. When I bought my property in 2018, the infrastructure didn't exist. Now it's being built.

### The "Seasoning" Problem

Let me explain something technical that affects billions in transactions: the banking practice called "seasoning."

When you move money between banks in the traditional system for a large transaction, they make it sit for 30, 60, 90 days. The official reason is fraud prevention. The practical effect is that it prevents the transaction from completing until they've verified everything.

Imagine you're buying a £50 million building in London. You've agreed on a price with the seller. You send the money. Now your money sits in limbo for 60 days. Meanwhile:
- The property market moves
- The price of the building relative to other assets changes
- You're both in legal liability if something changes
- No one can close the deal until the money "seasons"

This is in 2025. We have blockchain technology. We have instant settlement. And we're still seasoning money like it's some financial equivalent of basting a turkey.

With stablecoins and proper infrastructure—like what London Digital Escrow is building—that entire process happens in minutes or hours. The transaction is legally binding. It's insured through Lloyd's of London. It's completed.

That's the infrastructure gap I'm talking about. And once people feel that gap being closed, capital flows toward the solution.

### Why This Matters Beyond Property

The property angle is easy to understand, but the real impact is broader.

If people are thinking in dollars to buy property, they're thinking in dollars for other things too. They're not going to price Bitcoin in pounds. They're thinking about everything in the most liquid, most stable, most widely understood currency available.

And yes, that currency is the US dollar. Whether I like it or not, whether I want that to be true or not, that's what the market is choosing.

### The Multi-Money-Verse Problem

The Bank of England talked about something called the "multi-money-verse." The idea is that having different forms of money circulating creates inefficiency and regulatory challenges.

A pound in your bank account is different from a pound in cash. It's different from a pound on your credit card. It's theoretically the same value, but it behaves differently. It has different properties.

An that becomes a thousand times more complicated when you layer in stablecoins, central bank digital currencies, cryptocurrency, and everything else.

The problem the Bank of England and other regulators face is: how do you actually enforce regulations when money exists in multiple forms simultaneously?

You can't. Not effectively. So instead of fighting it, the smart move—and the UK is making this move—is to work with it. To create regulatory frameworks that accommodate multiple forms of money.

But here's the paradox: the more forms of digital money that exist, and the more frictionless they become, the more citizens naturally gravitate toward the most liquid, most useful form. Which, right now, is USD stablecoins.

## Chapter 7: Stablecoins and the New Dollar Layer

### The Genius Act Changes Everything

In the United States, we finally passed the Genius Act. And then we're now working on the Clarity Act. These aren't just regulatory pieces—they're recognition of a fundamental shift.

The Genius Act mandates federal framework for stablecoins with 100% backing in cash, treasuries, bonds, or other approved assets. It's not different from what Tether allegedly does, or what Circle's USDC does. But now it's law. It's codified. It's backed by the federal government's authority.

And this changes everything.

When the US finally moves, and when it moves toward crypto and digital assets, it tends to move decisively. For years, we were hostile. Now we've flipped.

The bull never moved. We just finally stopped being afraid of it.

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent is talking about three to four trillion dollars in stablecoins. The biggest banks in the world are forming consortiums to create G7-compatible stablecoins.

This isn't fringe stuff anymore. This is the absolute core of how global finance is reorganizing.

### What Stablecoins Actually Are (And Aren't)

Here's where I need to be pedantic because the terminology matters.

People use the word "stablecoin" but I think it's misleading. These coins aren't "stable"—they're "pegged." A stablecoin is pegged to something else. Usually the US dollar. Tether (USDT) is pegged to the dollar. USDC from Circle is pegged to the dollar. These are pegged coins.

Bitcoin, in my view, is actually more stable than those stablecoins because Bitcoin is consistent with itself. I don't care what Bitcoin's price is. Bitcoin is doing what Bitcoin does, consistently, every single day. It's not promising to be worth $1. It's not pegged to anything. It's just Bitcoin.

So when we talk about stablecoins as infrastructure, we're really talking about tokenized versions of existing currencies—mostly the US dollar—that can move on blockchains at the speed of digital networks instead of at the speed of banking legacy systems.

### The Three Trillion Dollar Question

Scott Bessent projected that we could end up with three to four trillion dollars in stablecoins globally. Think about that number. That's approaching the size of entire national economies.

If you have that much value moving on stablecoins, moving peer-to-peer, settling instantly, with no intermediaries, you've fundamentally changed how finance works.

And you won't be able to un-do it. There's no going backward to a system where we wait 60 days to season money.

### The Role of Backing

Everyone asks: what backs a stablecoin?

For USDC, it's US Treasuries, cash, and other specified assets held by Circle. For the federal stablecoin framework under the Genius Act, it would theoretically be treasury securities, bonds, and government-approved assets.

But here's where it gets interesting: the US government has 37 trillion dollars in sovereign debt right now. THAT'S the backing. The entire US Treasury market is the backing. And that's almost unlimited backing when you think about the number of stablecoins we could theoretically issue.

Contrast that with some other countries, and you start to understand why dollar-based stablecoins dominate. The US has the debt, the regulatory infrastructure, and the economic confidence to back vast quantities of stablecoins.

Some countries don't. Some countries have high inflation. Some countries don't have the institutional depth.

So if you're a person holding money in Argentina, Venezuela, Turkey, or any high-inflation environment, having access to stablecoins backed by US assets isn't some imperious American expansion—it's literally access to a more stable system than what your own government provides.

That's not colonialism. That's a better product winning.

### Stablecoins vs. Central Bank Digital Currencies

There's a big debate right now: should governments issue their own central bank digital currencies (CBDCs) or should they let private companies issue stablecoins?

Trump doesn't want a Fed-controlled CBDC because he understands something important: a CBDC wouldn't influence interest rates the way he wants them influenced. But stablecoins—if people are moving large amounts of capital into them, into short-dated US Treasuries backing those stablecoins—THAT influences interest rates. That's real monetary policy effect.

So Trump's push toward stablecoins isn't accidental. It's strategic. It recognizes that private market solutions, properly regulated, might actually work better than government-issued digital currencies.

The Bank of England is thinking similarly. There's a realization dawning in central banks: having a monopoly on money might not be the best thing anymore. Competition might actually benefit the system.

You might think a book about financial infrastructure and blockchain has nothing to do with accessibility. But I've learned something over my lifetime: the person with the most constraints in a system usually understands that system better than anyone else.

### USDP: Stablecoins Backed by Property

David Parsons developed something called USDP—US Dollar Property. Instead of being backed by US Treasury bonds, USDP would be backed by physical real estate.

The idea is elegant: roughly 60% of the currency in the US and Europe is created from property through mortgages. We're just recreating that model differently. But instead of mortgages creating currency (where a bank issues money against property), USDP would involve direct ownership of property backing a token.

Imagine you own a million-pound house in the UK. Under the USDP model, I could give you £10,000 in USDP and receive 1% ownership of your home. You don't pay me rent. The property value goes up and down. When you eventually sell or leave, my name would be on the title and I'd be paid out.

That's tokenization. That's fractional ownership. That's property as collateral for a currency.

But here's what makes this accessibility-driven innovation: **it solves the constraint problem of property ownership.**

Traditional property ownership requires you to be able to access a bank, secure a mortgage, prove your creditworthiness, be physically present in your home country, wait months for approval. It's built for people with stable documentation, stable geography, and access to the traditional financial system.

USDP bypasses those constraints. You don't need a bank. You don't need a mortgage. You don't need traditional credit history. You need a smart contract and a wallet. Someone in Nigeria can own a fraction of London property. Someone in El Salvador can own property in New York. Someone with zero banking history can be on the property deed.

The wealth gap in the US isn't just wage gap; it's property ownership gap. Homeownership builds intergenerational wealth. But you need capital up-front, which creates a barrier for people without family money or access to credit. Tokenization removes that barrier by allowing fractional entry.

You don't need £500,000 to own part of a £5 million property anymore. You need £5,000. Or £50,000. Or £500. You buy the fraction that matches your capital.

That's not redistribution. That's frictionless access. That's what accessibility looks like in property markets.

The advantage over traditional Treasury-backed stablecoins: you're getting actual physical assets, not IOUs promising future payment. You're getting real estate—bricks and mortar—not government promises. And it works for people who previously couldn't access the property market at all.

The challenge: it only works in jurisdictions with sophisticated property law. Which is probably why David chose the UK to build the prototype. English property law has had a thousand years to evolve. It knows how to handle property transfers and title disputes. The infrastructure exists to support this kind of decentralized ownership because English law has already solved the hard problem of managing shared property claims.

### Real-World Tokenization: Music Rights and the Creator Economy

Let me give you a concrete example of tokenization that's already working, that I built, that solves a $14.6 billion problem.

In 2021, the global music streaming economy generated $14.6 billion in revenue. That's massive. But the system for distributing that revenue to artists is fundamentally broken. Here's why:

**The Fragmentation Problem**

If you're an independent artist, you have to register with:
- ASCAP or BMI in America
- PRS in the UK
- GRS in Germany
- Phil's Cap in the Philippines
- Different organizations in every country where your music might be heard

Each registration requires:
- Separate paperwork
- Separate compliance requirements
- Separate payment schedules (some pay monthly, some yearly)
- Different fee structures (they all take a cut)
- No unified tracking of where your music is being played

And here's the kicker: most streaming happens across borders. Your song gets played in Nigeria, Japan, and Russia simultaneously. But you don't find out for months, and the revenues are fragmented across three different payment systems that don't talk to each other.

**The Tokenization Solution**

At Cyber FM, we created something called a Distributed Ledger Performance Rights Organization (DLPRO). Here's how it works:

An independent artist registers **once**. Not in 20 countries. Not with 20 different organizations. Once. On a blockchain-based ledger.

When their music is played on Cyber FM, which broadcasts to 140+ countries simultaneously, a token representing that performance is instantly created on the ledger. That token contains:
- Who played it (which artist)
- Where it was played (country/region)
- When it was played (timestamp)
- How many listeners participated

The artist is compensated **immediately** in cryptocurrency. Not in 6 months. Not after the PRO takes their cut and processes the payment. Immediately.

We also created a separate token for listeners. Every person who listens earns tokens for participating. This aligns incentives: artists want to be heard, listeners want to discover music, and they both get rewarded in real-time.

**Why This Matters at Scale**

When MasterCard was doing $6.3 trillion in transactions, Ethereum was handling $6.2 trillion in transactions. Same scale. Same capability. But with Ethereum, the transactions were peer-to-peer, instant, and required no intermediaries.

Now apply that to music rights. The $14.6 billion streaming industry could operate in real-time with **zero fragmentation**. No ASCAP waiting for your payment. No regional delays. No cut taken by middlemen who are processing paperwork instead of adding value.

But here's what's really important: this isn't just efficiency. This is **accessibility**.

Independent artists in developing countries now have the exact same access to global revenue as artists signed to major labels. The constraint—"you need to be established enough to navigate 20 registration systems"—is removed. Anyone can register. Everyone gets paid the same way, simultaneously, globally.

The old system worked perfectly if you were a major artist with lawyers and accountants navigating multiple jurisdictions. It worked terribly if you were a solo musician trying to get your music heard without spending years in bureaucratic hell.

We tokenized the rights. We removed the fragmentation. And suddenly, the infrastructure works for everyone.

This is happening across every industry right now. Real estate. Healthcare. Energy. Labor. Supply chains. Wherever there was fragmentation sold as "necessary complexity," tokenization is creating single, distributed ledgers that work for the users the old system excluded—and therefore work better for everyone.

### The Velocity of Money Problem: Access vs. Waiting

Here's something that doesn't get discussed enough: money velocity matters. But it matters not just for macroeconomics—it matters for individual access to capital.

If you get paid monthly, you're waiting 30 days to move that money to your next transaction. If you're living paycheck to paycheck, those 30 days matter. You might miss an investment opportunity. You might need the money to move faster to cover an emergency. That's a constraint.

But think deeper: traditional payments are asynchronous and centralized. You send money, it sits in a queue, a bank processes it, days pass, it arrives. You have no visibility. You can't do anything with your money until the system says you can.

Tokenization enables programmable, instant settlement. Smart contracts can execute instantaneously when conditions are met. You don't wait. You don't have intermediaries deciding when your money moves. Your capital is accessible to you immediately.

In a monthly paycheck system, your money velocity is roughly 12 per year (12 transactions per unit of money across 12 months).

But imagine: your employer pays you daily via smart contract. Your payment hits your wallet instantly, 24/7/365. Your money could move immediately into investments, savings, or consumption. Your velocity becomes 365+.

That sounds esoteric. It's not. Here's why: for people with no financial margin, daily payment access changes everything. You're not holding money for 30 days hoping you'll have enough when the bill is due. You get paid, immediately invest a portion, have certainty about what's available. You can take advantage of market opportunities that currently require capital sitting idle.

For people in high-inflation countries, daily payment cycles mean your money doesn't lose value sitting in a bank account for a month. You can move it to stablecoins or investments immediately.

For gig workers (which is 1 in 4 American workers now), waiting 30 days between project completion and payment is devastating. Programmatic instant settlement means they can complete work, get paid, and reinvest or survive—all in the same day.

This isn't economic theory. This is accessibility. This is whether someone with constrained cash flow can actually participate in economic life or whether they're locked out by payment system architecture.

### The Practical Use Case: Shipping—Solving Constraints in Real-Time

I'll give you a concrete example of how programmable money and tokenization solve real-world constraints that existing systems simply can't address.

This isn't an edge case. This is happening thousands of times a day across global shipping. It's a massive constraint on every transaction.

Now imagine: IoT sensors on the goods confirm delivery. Smart contracts confirm all parties agree on final terms. The moment those conditions are met—instantly—payment settles from an escrow account to all parties simultaneously, in their preferred currencies, via blockchain infrastructure.

No delays. No renegotiation. No sitting and waiting.

You've just eliminated billions in unnecessary delays and transaction costs. You've removed the constraint that was making the system inefficient. **And here's the key insight: you didn't need to change the rules or the relationships or who gets paid. You just removed the waiting.**

Multiply that across shipping (which handles 80% of global goods), across supply chains everywhere, across insurance claims and derivatives settlement and cross-border transactions—you're talking about removing constraints from every financial artery in the global economy.

That's tokenization's real power. It's not making things "more advanced." It's removing the friction that made systems wait-dependent.

### Tokenization Is Already Happening: Enterprise Adoption

Here's what makes me confident this isn't theoretical: major enterprises are already doing this.

**Starbucks Odyssey** is their Web3 loyalty program. They have 30 million loyalty program members who account for 56% of their total revenue. Instead of a physical punch card or even a simple digital app, they're tokenizing the loyalty experience. It's not about the token itself—it's about what tokenization enables: members can trade, transfer, and own their loyalty rewards. Instead of being locked into Starbucks-only redemption, you can actually own something portable.

The constraint they removed: member lock-in. Instead of "you must spend your points at Starbucks," members now have fungible assets they can use, transfer, or hold.

**HSBC and other major banks** aren't just talking about blockchain—they're issuing tokenized assets. HSBC created tokenized gold in Hong Kong for retail investors. Swarm (a regulated German exchange) created tokenized gold backed by Brinks. These aren't crypto companies doing this. These are the institutions people have trusted for centuries, now adopting tokenization because it solves real constraint problems.

**PayPal created a USD-backed stablecoin** on the Solana blockchain. They recognized something simple: in traditional banking, when you deposit money, it becomes the bank's money. They can lend it out (rehypothecate it), creating leverage that can hurt you if they fail—as happened with Silicon Valley Bank and Silvergate. PayPal said "No, we're going to back every dollar 1:1 and not lend it out." Then they put it on blockchain for programmable, instant settlement instead of waiting for SWIFT transfers.

The constraint they removed: counterparty risk and settlement time.

**The NBA created NFTs of highlight moments**—a natural extension of the baseball cards kids used to trade. This isn't "JPEG monkeys." This is digital proof of ownership for collectible moments, tradeable instantly, held in your wallet.

**Nike, Adidas, and Burberry** have all launched Web3 experiences with digital collectibles. Again, these aren't speculative crypto projects. These are brands using tokenization to create new experiences and engagement mechanisms.

What all of these have in common: they're not trying to replace existing systems. They're removing specific constraints and creating new value. Starbucks isn't replacing their point-of-sale system. They're making loyalty more fungible. HSBC isn't replacing all banking. They're making gold ownership more accessible to regular people. PayPal isn't replacing ACH transfers. They're offering instant settlement alternatives.

**This is what enterprise tokenization looks like: solving real constraints for real customers in real use cases.**

It's not speculation. It's constraint-removal in action.

### Why Accessibility Is the Real Story of Tokenization

I want to step back and be explicit about something that ties all of this together.

Tokenization isn't a technology story. It's a constraint story. Every successful tokenization use case I've researched—whether it's Brazil's Pix system, India's UPI, energy IoT sensors, property fractional ownership, shipping settlement, or enterprise loyalty programs—solves a constraint that made the old system inaccessible to someone.

Pix was built because the traditional banking system was inaccessible to Brazil's poor. UPI was built because the formal banking system was inaccessible to India's rural population. Energy tokenization solves the constraint of centralized utilities denying market access to small producers. Property tokenization solves the constraint of capital requirements locking people out of wealth-building. Gold tokenization solves the constraint of storage and insurance costs locking people out of commodity ownership. Enterprise loyalty tokenization solves the constraint of vendors trying to lock customers in.

The pattern is consistent: **accessibility-driven innovation beats optimized-for-the-comfortable innovation every single time.**

Because when you remove constraints, you expand the market. And when you expand the market, everyone benefits.

This is why I think the people who dismiss tokenization and digital assets fundamentally misunderstand what's happening. They think this is about speculation or hype or replacing banks. It's none of those things.

This is about removing constraints from systems that became brittle and exclusive because they were optimized for perfect conditions. And the people who understand constraints—who've spent their lives having to navigate systems that weren't built for them—usually see this transition first.

## Chapter 11: The Trillion Dollar Question

### Why Bitcoin Belongs in the US Reserve

One of my more controversial arguments: the United States should hold Bitcoin (and possibly other cryptocurrencies) as a strategic reserve, similar to how we hold gold.

This isn't about speculation. This isn't about me being a Bitcoin maximalist. It's about redundancy and resilience.

Here's what I learned from living in a body that doesn't match standard infrastructure: **redundancy isn't a luxury. It's a requirement.**

You can't build a system that depends on one point of failure. Because if that point fails, you have nothing. I learned this in my daily life. I need Plan A and Plan B and Plan C. I need backup systems for my backup systems. Because a single point of failure means I can't function.

The US financial system currently has a single point of failure: the US dollar. Now, the dollar is strong and has massive backing—I'm not predicting its collapse. But what if something happens? What if there's a crisis? What if another power moves to undermine it?

Bitcoin is a hedge. It's not alternative money. It's redundancy. It's saying: we believe in the dollar, but we're also holding something that doesn't depend on any government, any central bank, any single point of control.

That's not paranoia. That's good system design.

Compare this to El Salvador's strategic thinking: if the US wants something from us, we have leverage if we're also holding crypto. We're not entirely dependent on the dollar. We have redundancy.

Multiply that to national strategy: if the US holds a strategic reserve of Bitcoin and other cryptocurrencies, we're saying to the world: "Our financial infrastructure doesn't depend on any single asset or any single authority. We have redundant systems. We have backup plans. We're resilient."

That's more credible than saying "Trust the dollar because we say so."

El Salvador was the first. The Czech Republic followed. Dubai positioned itself as a crypto hub. Canada has Bitcoin ETFs. The UK eventually got regulatory clarity. And what was America doing? Fumbling around trying to figure out if crypto is evil.

Then the Trump administration came in and asked a simple question: if cryptocurrency is the future of finance, don't we want to be holding some of it?

And the answer is: yes, probably.

### The Confiscated Bitcoin Problem: From Accident to Strategy

Here's the tricky part: the US already has about 200,000 Bitcoin. But it's Bitcoin we confiscated. We didn't accumulate it strategically. We took it from people involved in criminal proceedings. We're holding it as seized assets.

Think about what that actually means. We criminalized the technology. We treated Bitcoin like contraband—confiscated, locked away, labeled dangerous—as though the asset itself was the problem. We were so convinced the bull was going to wreck the place that we threw it in a cage. And then, slowly, we started looking through the bars and thinking: wait. That's actually a valuable animal. And it's been standing there calmly the whole time.

We didn't protect anything by fighting it. We just delayed our own participation.

Now, should we put seized assets into a reserve? That's a different moral and strategic question. In my view, we should have acquired Bitcoin legitimately as part of a planned strategic reserve, not just be accumulating confiscated stuff by accident.

But the fact is, we have it. And one way to think about it: we've effectively been given options on the future of digital infrastructure. Why not acknowledge that gift and use it strategically?

The alternative reason people oppose this: they're worried the government will dump it, and that's a legitimate concern. Which is why, if we do this, we need to treat crypto reserves the way we treat gold reserves. Put it away. Treat it as a strategic hedge, not a trading asset. Make a credible commitment that it's not being sold or used for short-term gain.

From an accessibility standpoint, this is about building resilience. A financial system that relies on a single reserve asset (the dollar) is a financial system with a single point of failure. A financial system that holds multiple reserve assets—dollars, gold, crypto—is a financial system that works even when conditions deteriorate.

That's not about being prepared for doomsday. That's about building infrastructure that works under multiple scenarios, not just perfect conditions.

### Why This Actually Matters

Here's the thing that caught my attention and that Jonny pointed out: if other countries and other exchanges are holding massive amounts of crypto, and if tokenization is real, then the infrastructure that those cryptocurrencies run on becomes critical infrastructure.

If Bitcoin and Ethereum are the rails for global digital assets, then having quantities of those assets in your national reserves isn't speculation—it's infrastructure investment.

It's like saying, "Shipping lanes are critical infrastructure, so we should own some ships." Or, "Money needs to be able to move digitally, so we should own some of the digital transport."

### The Precedent: El Salvador and National Strategy

El Salvador was the first country to make Bitcoin legal tender. This wasn't crazy—it was strategic. The President of El Salvador realized: if the US wants something from us, we have leverage if we're also holding crypto. We're not entirely dependent on USD.

Now, multiply that strategy to a nation-level. If the US holds a strategic reserve of Bitcoin and other cryptocurrencies, we're positioned at the center of the global crypto economy. We're holding assets that the world increasingly needs.

And if, at some point, Bitcoin or other cryptocurrencies become global reserve assets (which many believe will happen eventually), then we'll have positioned ourselves as major holders from the very beginning.

### Gold Reserves as the Model

Think about US gold reserves. We hold about 147 million ounces of gold, worth hundreds of billions of dollars. We built an entire building in Tennessee to store some of it. We don't touch it. It's a strategic reserve.

Why can't we treat crypto the same way? Hold it. Secure it. Make it part of our national treasure. Don't speculate with it. Use it as a hedge against global economic uncertainty.

### The Tap-o-the-Iceberg: Why This Matters for Everything Else

A strategic crypto reserve isn't just about having crypto. It's about sending a signal: we believe digital assets are essential to the future economy. We're committing to them. We're positioning ourselves at the center.

When you commit to that, everything else follows. If the US holds crypto in reserve, then the Clarity Act makes sense. Then stablecoin regulations make sense. Then tokenization becomes more palatable to regulators because the foundational belief has shifted: digital assets aren't a threat, they're essential.

## Chapter 10: Global Financial Inclusion

### Who Understands Constraints Wins the Infrastructure Game

This is the part that really keeps me up at night, but not for the reasons people usually think.

The conventional narrative is: "China is winning. The US is behind. It's a race to see who dominates crypto."

But that's thinking about it wrong. It's not a race to dominate crypto. It's a race to see who understands how to build inclusive, accessible financial infrastructure.

And here's the thing: the people building the most accessible financial systems aren't the ones trying to dominate anything.

Dubai didn't become a crypto hub by crushing competition. It became a crypto hub by asking a simple question: "What constraints do the traditional financial system create?" And then: "What would financial infrastructure look like if we removed those constraints?"

The answer: it would work for people excluded from traditional banking. People without formal identification. People in regions without banking infrastructure. People with capital but without credit history. People with international movement needs.

So Dubai said: we'll build for them. And what happened? The capital came.

Brazil asked the same question about payment systems. "What constraints does our traditional banking system create?" And then: "What if we built a system that worked for favela residents, for people without identification, for rural populations?"

The answer was Pix. And now 76% of transactions happen through it.

India asked the same thing about money transfer and payments. "What if we built a system that worked for someone in the countryside with zero technological experience?" The answer was UPI. Now 13 billion transactions monthly.

These countries aren't trying to dominate global finance. They're solving constraints. And the infrastructure they build ends up being so useful that it becomes the standard.

### The Real Competition: Infrastructure Philosophy

America's problem isn't that we're behind China in some abstract sense. Our problem is that we're fragmented in our philosophy.

We have the OCC, the SEC, the CFTC, state regulators, all with different rules. Each regulator is optimizing for something different. Consumer protection. Market structure. Tax collection. Anti-money-laundering.

But nobody's asking: "What are the constraints in our financial system that could be removed?"

China has a different problem: state control means coordination, but it also means exclusion. The system works for approved participants and nobody else.

The US could have the best of both: coordination **and** inclusivity. We could ask: "What constraints can we remove?" And then: "Who gets excluded by the current system?" And then: "How do we build infrastructure that works for them?"

That's not a race problem. That's a philosophy problem.

El Salvador gets this. Dubai gets this. Barbados gets this. They're building financial infrastructure by removing constraints, and it's working.

### London vs. New York vs. Everywhere Else

London has been the financial center of the world for foreign exchange trading. $7 trillion a day in FX trading happens in London. About 38% of global FX volume.

New York is maybe 18%.

London knows property law. London knows financial regulation. London knows how to build global financial infrastructure.

But London isn't the only player anymore.

Dubai has made a conscious choice to build accessible financial infrastructure. Austin, Texas is being positioned as the crypto capital of America (and I have thoughts about whether that's where it should be, but the fact that a US city is positioning itself as "progressive on crypto" means something). Switzerland is a contender. Malta is a contender. Barbados is a contender.

This isn't really a zero-sum game, but it is a competition for talent, capital, and infrastructure expertise. And whoever builds the most accessible, constraint-removing infrastructure will likely be the center of global digital asset finance for the next 20 years.

The difference is: if you're building to exclude, you get a temporary advantage until someone else builds to include. And then everyone moves to the inclusive system.

### The Dollarization Feedback Loop Through an Accessibility Lens

Here's my thesis: if the US positions itself properly, we don't have to "make" the world use dollars. The market will make that choice because we've built the most accessible infrastructure.

USD stablecoins will dominate not because of American imperialism, but because they have the most liquidity, the most clarity, and the most accessibility.

Here's the feedback loop:

USD stablecoins are accessible (no proof of identity required, works in every timezone, instant settlement).

Accessibility attracts more users.

More users attract developers who want to build on the infrastructure.

More developers build more applications.

Better applications make the system more useful.

More useful systems attract more capital.

More capital attracts institutional interest.

Institutional interest attracts regulatory clarity.

Regulatory clarity attracts more developers and more compliance-oriented institutions.

And the cycle continues.

But you have to start with accessibility. You have to start by asking: "Who is currently excluded from this financial system?" And then: "How do we change the infrastructure so they're not excluded?"

The Trump administration taking a bet on crypto, on stablecoins, on digital assets—that might be the philosophical shift that matters. Not because Trump is a crypto guy. But because it opens the conversation: "What if we built this for accessibility instead of control?"

### What Happens If We Don't Move First

Conversely, if the US doesn't make accessibility the foundation of our digital asset philosophy, capital and talent will migrate to places that do.

Looking at history: whoever controls the financial infrastructure controls enormous economic power. The British Empire controlled finance for centuries and used that power to project influence globally. The US took over post-WWII and built the system that works even now.

But infrastructure dominance only lasts if you're solving the problems people actually have. If you're building systems for perfect conditions and someone else is building for real-world constraints, eventually everyone uses the real-world system.

If we fumble the digital asset transition by over-regulating, by protecting incumbent banks, by optimizing for control instead of access, we're ceding that power to whoever gets it right.

And I don't want that to be China. I don't want it to be the EU through failure to move. I want the US to earn it by building something genuinely better.

## Chapter 12: When Infrastructure Changes, Everything Changes

### Building Infrastructure for Constraint-Solving Means Building for Everyone

The UK and US are working on what's being called a Transatlantic Digital Asset Treaty. The concept is elegant: if the UK recognizes American digital asset regulations and standards, and the US reciprocates, we've created a framework for global digital asset infrastructure.

It's not perfect. It's not the whole world. But it's built on something important: the understanding that removing constraints—not adding control—is how you build lasting infrastructure.

English law is used globally for contracts and disputes. American financial markets are the most developed. Combined, they could build a standard for global digital asset infrastructure. But only if the standard is based on **removing constraints** rather than protecting incumbents.

### Why This Actually Matters for Individual People

All of this institutional stuff might seem abstract. Let me make it concrete by thinking about constraints.

If programmable money becomes standard, you could get paid daily instead of monthly—**removing the constraint of waiting for payment**.

If property becomes tokenized, you could buy fractional ownership of diversified real estate globally, instantly, without an intermediary—**removing the constraint of capital requirements and geography**.

If carbon credits become tradeable, you could monetize your personal environmental impact—**removing the constraint of being locked out of carbon markets**.

If data becomes tokenizable, you could actually own and profit from your own data instead of working for platforms that profit from it—**removing the constraint of having zero agency over your data**.

If work becomes tokenized with verifiable reputation, a developer in any country can build credentials that follow them globally—**removing the constraint of being locked into one geography's credentialing system**.

But none of this works without the infrastructure. And the infrastructure only gets built if governments and financial institutions commit to removing constraints rather than maintaining control.

The difference between an accessible system and an exclusive one isn't technical. It's philosophical.

### The Blockchain Infrastructure Competition: Beyond Ethereum Dominance

Here's something most people don't realize: the blockchain space itself is undergoing massive infrastructure shifts. Ethereum is dominant now, but that dominance is being challenged by blockchains built for different problems.

According to Blockworks' analysis, there are about 25,000 active developers building blockchain applications. Ethereum has about 5,000 of them—which seems like dominance until you realize that means 20,000 developers are building on alternative blockchains, many of which are newer and built for specific problems Ethereum wasn't designed for.

One example: Minima. I mentioned this because I'm a car guy and it resonates with me. Minima has 180,000 nodes—more than any other blockchain in the world. Why? Because it runs on people's laptops, on smartphones, **in cars**.

This isn't theoretical. Minima is building the Internet of Things infrastructure. Imagine car-to-car communication where your vehicle automatically receives warnings: "There's a cyclist coming around the corner," or "There's a pothole in the road ahead." That communication happens through a distributed blockchain network that lives in your car. No central authority. No single point of failure. Just peer-to-peer vehicle alerts.

And it works because Minima was designed from the ground up for that constraint—to run locally on distributed devices without needing massive server farms.

Minima just signed a partnership with WeTransfer (80 million customers). They're not trying to be "Ethereum killers." They're solving a specific problem that Ethereum's design doesn't handle well—local, device-based, distributed computing without central servers.

Here's the principle: **specialized blockchains designed for specific problems outcompete general-purpose blockchains in those domains.**

Ethereum dominates general-purpose smart contract platform space right now. But you'll increasingly see:
- Blockchains specialized for supply chain (provenance, traceability)
- Blockchains specialized for IoT (device-to-device communication)
- Blockchains specialized for privacy
- Blockchains specialized for real-time settlement
- Blockchains specialized for energy grids, autonomous vehicles, medical devices

The developer ecosystem reflects this. Ethereum's 5,000 developers are being diluted as specialized chains attract developers building solutions for specific problems.

This matters because it means the infrastructure winners in the next 10 years might not be the current dominant chains. They'll be whoever builds constraint-solving infrastructure for the specific problems that matter. Just like it happened in finance: whoever understood accessibility won (Brazil, India, Dubai), not whoever had the biggest existing system (legacy banking).

The same dynamic is happening at the blockchain level.

### The Acceleration We're Seeing in Constraint-Removal

From multiple conversations and industry developments as of March 2026, I'm seeing real movement on constraint-removal:

- **Multiple major banks** are actually building stablecoin infrastructure (removing the constraint of "banks can't issue programmable money")
- **G7 consortia** forming around digital assets standards (removing the constraint of regulatory fragmentation)

- **Real-world asset tokenization happening** especially in real estate (removing the constraint of fractional property ownership)
- **Central banks reconsidering digital assets** not as threats but as infrastructure (removing the constraint of "only governments can issue currency")

This isn't talk anymore. This is constraint-removal happening in real-time. And it's accelerating because people who are excluded from traditional systems finally have alternatives.

That's the real story.

Some things are going to happen regardless of what anyone wants:

## APPENDIX B: Glossary of Terms

**Stablecoin**: A cryptocurrency token designed to maintain a stable value, typically pegged to a fiat currency like the US Dollar. Examples: USDC, USDT.

**CBDC**: Central Bank Digital Currency - a digital version of fiat currency issued directly by a central bank.

**Tokenization**: The process of converting physical or intangible assets into digital tokens on a blockchain.

**Smart Contract**: Self-executing code on a blockchain that automatically executes when predetermined conditions are met.

**Dollarization**: The adoption of a currency (in this case, USD stablecoins) as the primary medium of exchange in an economy beyond its country of origin.

**RWA**: Real-World Assets - physical assets like real estate, commodities, or securities that are represented as tokens on a blockchain.

**Liquidity**: The ease with which an asset can be bought or sold in a market.

**Genius Act**: US legislation establishing a federal framework for stablecoin deployment with specific backing and regulatory requirements.

**Clarity Act**: Proposed US legislation to provide regulatory clarity for cryptocurrency and blockchain technologies.

**USDP**: US Dollar Property - a theoretical stablecoin backed by tokenized real estate rather than government bonds.

**Programmable Money**: Digital currency with built-in logic that can automatically execute transactions based on predetermined rules via smart contracts.

# PART 2: THE LENS DEVELOPS

## Chapter 6: Seeing What Others Miss

When I walked into my first day at Goldman Sachs, I was the only person in the room using a wheelchair. More accurately, I was the only person the human resources department had explicitly processed through accessibility accommodations. There were probably other people with invisible disabilities—anxiety, dyslexia, hearing loss—but I was the visible anomaly. The elevators had been retrofitted. A parking spot had been reserved. My desk had been positioned. Everyone knew, before I arrived, that I was different.

What nobody anticipated was that my difference would become an advantage.

For years, I thought disability was simply a personal constraint to manage. You adapt. You work harder. You find workarounds. That's what my parents taught me, and that's what I taught myself. But sitting in those glass-walled conference rooms on the 42nd floor, watching senior bankers make decisions about financial infrastructure, I realized something else: they were designing systems for people like them, with the visual cortex they had, the mobility they had, the assumptions they had.

They were blind to entire categories of friction.

I'd notice things that others filtered out. A transaction flow that made sense on a desktop but created impossible decision trees on a mobile phone. A process that worked fine if you had uninterrupted time to complete it but fell apart if your attention was fragmented. A system that assumed a single point of access—a branch, an office, a place—when millions of people couldn't reliably get to any single place.

In Wall Street's case, they were designing for the trader, the portfolio manager, the institutional client—people with dedicated workstations, dedicated teams, dedicated time. They weren't designing for the person trading from bed, or from a rural area, or from a country with unreliable infrastructure. They certainly weren't designing for the person doing three things simultaneously: trading, managing health logistics, living life in a body that made some decisions time-critical.

I didn't articulate it that way at first. I just noticed inefficiencies. A risk system that didn't degrade gracefully when you couldn't see all the information at once. A settlement process that required phone calls to complete, which meant it was broken for anyone with hearing loss or transcription delays. A mobile app that looked designed for a 27-year-old with perfect vision and two functioning hands.

These weren't edge cases. These were massive population segments. And the systems weren't designed for them not out of malice, but out of invisibility. The designers had never needed to solve those problems, so they never thought about them.

Here's what disability teaches you: everyone's going to hit a constraint eventually. It might be age. It might be an injury. It might be a situation where your normal capabilities aren't available—traveling without good internet, dealing with a temporary injury, living through a crisis where normal structure collapses. The people who understand how to design for constraints become the people who understand how to build systems that work when conditions change.

That's valuable on Wall Street. But it's radical in finance.

Traditional finance works because the system is rigid. You go to the bank during bank hours. You have a certain account structure. You follow certain rules. The system assumes a stable context: you're in one place, with one body, with one set of capabilities, at one specific time. If you deviate—if you're somewhere else, or your situation changes, or you enter a crisis—the system stops working.

I watched this collapse for millions of people during COVID. People who'd been fine within the static finance system suddenly needed remote banking. Needed asynchronous processes. Needed flexibility. The whole infrastructure that had seemed so stable suddenly revealed itself as a house of cards designed for a very specific scenario.

But I'd been living in that crisis every day. I'd been solving remote access problems. I'd been designing workarounds for rigid systems. I'd been finding pockets of flexibility in structures designed to be closed.

So when DeFi started emerging—when blockchains began enabling financial transactions without requiring you to be in a specific place, at a specific time, during specific hours, with a specific set of capabilities—I recognized it immediately as an accessibility infrastructure that nobody was talking about.

This is what disability teaches you: the next generation of infrastructure always starts as accessibility.

The internet was built by guys in labs, with full use of their bodies and minds and full time to dedicate. It wasn't built for accessibility. But then we added screen readers. We added keyboard navigation. We added captions. And suddenly we had infrastructure that didn't just work for disabled people—it worked better for everyone. A screen reader isn't just for blind users; it's useful when you're driving. Captions aren't just for deaf users; they're useful when you're in a loud bar.

The same pattern emerged in mobile. Smartphones were built for sighted people with ten working fingers. But the accessibility features—voice control, larger text, simple gestures—turned out to be the future of how everyone interacts with technology. The accessibility layer became the user experience.

Financial infrastructure is going through that transition now. The traditional banking system was built for a specific scenario: physical presence, specific hours, specific capabilities. That system is being disrupted by infrastructure that strips away those requirements. No branch hours. No geography. No requirement for a particular body or set of capabilities.

And the people who don't see it coming are the ones who designed the old system.

I did. I spent years optimizing that old system. Making it faster, more efficient, more sophisticated. But it was a local optimization. I was solving problems within a constraint that was about to become obsolete.

When I started working with blockchain and digital assets, I was recognizing something I'd already spent my whole life understanding: that the best infrastructure is the infrastructure that works for the person with the most constraints. Because that infrastructure will also work for everyone else.

That's the lens that disability gives you. It's not sympathy. It's not inspiration. It's pattern recognition. It's the ability to look at a system and ask: "What happens when conditions change? What happens when someone can't access this the way it was designed to be accessed? What happens when constraints shift?"

Those questions turned out to be the most important questions in finance.

## Chapter 7: The Pattern Recognition

I spent the first half of my Wall Street career solving problems that didn't matter.

I say that without bitterness. Wall Street is full of smart people solving increasingly complicated problems within a system that's fundamentally sound. If you work in equities, you're optimizing the distribution of ownership. If you work in fixed income, you're optimizing the expression of interest rates. If you work in derivatives, you're optimizing the distribution of risk. All of this matters, in the sense that it moves money efficiently within the system. None of it matters, in the sense that the system itself might be wrong.

I was solving efficiency problems. Making the machine run faster. Better routing. Lower latency. Smarter algorithms. All the markers of success: promotions, compensation, respect. But I was also making the system more complex, more dependent on infrastructure that only worked if everything went perfectly.

Which meant I was also making it more fragile.

This is something disability teaches you immediately: redundancy isn't a luxury. It's a requirement.

In my daily life, nothing was simple. I needed Plan A and Plan B and Plan C. I needed backup systems for my backup systems. If a tool didn't work, I needed an alternative. If one mode of access failed, I needed another. I couldn't design my life around a single point of failure because a single point of failure meant I couldn't participate.

The financial system, increasingly, was a single point of failure. It was all-in on a specific infrastructure: electronic, centralized, dependent on a handful of nodes. If the electricity went out, the system stopped. If the internet broke, the system stopped. If the central authority decided to cut you off, the system stopped.

I watched it happen repeatedly from my desk. A country in crisis. Electricity unavailable. The bank closed. And millions of people couldn't access their own money.

Or: a person flagged by automated systems for suspicious activity. No recourse. No appeals process. Just: your account is frozen. You have no money.

Or: a region in geographic or political isolation. No bank branches. No financial system. Just cash, if you could find it.

These weren't theoretical problems. They were happening constantly. But they weren't my problem—that's what I told myself. Wall Street serves the efficient market. The market moves money to where it's most valuable. If you're outside the market, that's not an economics problem; it's a political one.

But I'd been outside systems my entire life. And I'd learned something crucial: the people outside the system often understand it better than the people inside it.

Disability forces you to see how systems work, because you can't rely on the default. A sighted person doesn't think about navigation; they just see where to go. A disabled person has to understand how navigation works—both how the space is designed and what alternatives exist. They have to hold multiple models of the same system in their mind simultaneously.

I learned to navigate the world through pure spatial reasoning. I learned to read environments and problem-solve on the fly. I learned to think in systems at a granular level, because my movement through those systems didn't work by default.

That skill—the ability to see how a system works at the component level—turned out to be exactly what I needed to understand blockchains.

Blockchains are just systems to solve the problem of: "How do we verify the truth of a transaction without needing a centralized authority?"

It's an accessibility question. It's asking: "What if the person verifying the transaction isn't in a position to trust the centralized authority? What if they're in a different country, or a different political situation, or a different economic context?"

The answer is: we distribute that authority. Everyone can verify. Everyone participates in the consensus mechanism. No single point of failure.

That's redundancy. That's the pattern I'd been living with.

And suddenly, it wasn't a personal accommodation anymore. It was global infrastructure.

When I was working on financial infrastructure, optimizing settlement times and routing efficiency, I was solving problems for people like me. People with access. People inside the system. People who could afford to wait for the system to serve them.

But when I moved to digital assets, I was solving problems for everyone else. For the person in a country with unstable currency. For the person politically disfavored by the central authority. For the person in a region without banking infrastructure.

For the person whose default mode of operating within a system didn't work.

The shift was profound. It wasn't about being a better engineer. It was about recognizing which problems mattered.

Disability teaches you pattern recognition at the system level. You learn to see:

- Where the single point of failure is
- Which constraints are real and which are artificial
- When the system is optimized for edge cases instead of common cases
- Where accessibility and resilience are the same thing
- When the system is elegant (works in multiple modes) versus brittle (works in one mode only)

These principles apply everywhere. They apply to financial infrastructure. They apply to supply chains. They apply to energy systems. They apply to governance.

The traditional financial system was optimized for efficiency under perfect conditions. Centralized authority, fast settlement, instant updates. Beautiful engineering, if conditions never degrade.

But conditions always degrade. And the system that's optimized for perfect conditions is usually the most vulnerable when conditions change.

This is the pattern I see now, looking at global finance. The systems are incredibly sophisticated and incredibly fragile. They're optimized for the happy path. And they're all connected, which means that when one fails, they all fail.

The people who understand disability already understand this. We've never had the luxury of assuming perfect conditions. We've never been able to build systems that work in one mode only.

Blockchain infrastructure, distributed systems, redundant verification, asymmetric cryptography—these aren't innovations in the technological sense. They're innovations in the accessibility sense. They answer the question: "How do we build financial systems that work when you can't rely on a centralized, trusted authority?"

And that's how I ended up in blockchain, speaking on panels, building infrastructure, thinking about money and protocol design.

Not because I became an expert in cryptography. But because I'd spent my whole life as an expert in how systems work when the default path fails.

## Chapter 8: Raising Your Son

My son is neurotypical. He doesn't use a wheelchair. He has no chronic illness. He moves through the world in a way I never could, and probably never will.

But he's also my son, and that means he grew up watching me navigate systems that weren't designed for me. He watched me problem-solve. He watched me find alternatives. He watched me refuse to accept the frame that "this system is how it works, so you're the problem."

When he was younger, he asked why ramps were necessary. Not in a mean way. Just: "Why does that building have a ramp?" And I could've said, "Because I use it," which is true. Or I could've said, "Because people like me need it," which is also true.

Instead, I said: "Because if you designed the building right, the ramp wouldn't be a special feature. It would just be how you get into the building."

He was maybe eight. He thought about it. "So the building is badly designed?"

"Most buildings are. They were built assuming everyone could walk. That's a bad assumption."

He understood it immediately. And then he started noticing all the bad assumptions. The restaurant where you can't see the menu from a wheelchair. The playground where half the equipment is only accessible if you can climb stairs. The sidewalk with no curb cut, so a person in a wheelchair has to cross the street in the middle of traffic.

He didn't become a designer. He's not trying to fix the world. But he grew up with a different frame. He grew up thinking of accessibility as a design problem, not a special request.

When he talks to his friends, they notice. One of his friends is deaf. Another uses a mobility aid part-time. Another has severe anxiety. These aren't traumas in his worldview. They're just different context. His instinct is to problem-solve, not accommodate. "How do we do this so everyone can participate?"

That's not a special virtue. That's not inspiration porn. That's just: what if you were raised around someone who refused to accept that the system was fine the way it was?

I'm telling you this because it matters for what comes next in this book. This book is built on the premise that disability isn't separate from financial planning. It's central to it. That the same pattern recognition that lets you navigate a world not built for you is the same pattern recognition that lets you navigate financial systems that were designed before the world you're living in emerged.

And I'm telling you this because my son will someday read this, and I want him to know that I knew he was watching.

When I was building my cryptocurrency portfolio, I wasn't thinking, "This is a hedge against the financial system failing." I was thinking, "This is infrastructure that doesn't depend on me being in a specific place or having a specific set of capabilities." It solved a problem I'd been living with forever.

But it also solved a problem for people I'd never met. People in countries with currency crises. People who couldn't access banking. People who needed financial movement and couldn't get it from the institutions.

When I talked about digital assets at conferences and on podcasts, I wasn't talking about a speculative investment. I was talking about infrastructure. I was talking about: What does it look like if we build financial systems that work for the person with the most constraints, instead of assuming everyone can access the perfect system?

My son heard that. And I think he understood, at some level, that I wasn't just talking about money. I was talking about the same thing I'd been teaching him his whole life: if you build for the person with constraints, you end up with a system that works better for everyone.

That's the inheritance I wanted to leave him. Not wealth. Not status. But a way of thinking about systems: always ask, "What happens when conditions change? What happens when someone can't access this the way it was designed? What if we had to rebuild this without assuming perfect conditions?"

Those are the questions that matter in a changing world.

That's the lens I developed, living in a body that didn't match the infrastructure I was born into.

And now I'm trying to help redesign the infrastructure itself.
